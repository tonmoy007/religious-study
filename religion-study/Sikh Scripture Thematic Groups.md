# Sikh Scripture Thematic Groups

A reference guide to the major thematic groups that run through the corpus of Sikh scripture — the Guru Granth Sāhib (Ādi Granth, compiled 1604, finalised 1708), the Dasam Granth (attributed to Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, early 18th c.), the Sarbloh Granth, the Vārāṅ of Bhāī Gurdās (c. 17th c.), and the vast Janam Sākhī and Gurbilās narrative traditions (c. 17th–19th c.). Each group links texts that share a common subject, doctrinal theme, or narrative pattern. Use this note to navigate Sikh scripture by theme and to see how different texts — composed over three centuries in a distinctive blend of Sant Bhāṣā dialects (Old Punjabi, Braj, Khari Boli, Persian, and Arabic) — engage the same questions from the standpoint of Gurmat (the Guru's teaching).

> [!note] How the groups were built
> The major scriptures of the Sikh tradition — the Guru Granth Sāhib (the living Guru, the *Śabad-Gurū*), the Dasam Granth (the Book of the Tenth Guru), and the ancillary narrative and didactic works — were grouped by their dominant doctrinal themes, narrative function, and literary genre. Many texts belong to multiple groups — for example, the Japjī Sāhib is simultaneously a Nām Simran text, a Mūl Mantra exposition, an articulation of Hukam, a meditation on the Five Virtues, and a liberation-teaching. The groups below consolidate these into navigable themes. Cross-references to the [[Sikh Stories MOC]] and [[Sikh Figures MOC]] link thematic groups to narrative and figure traditions. Because the Sikh tradition recognises the Guru Granth Sāhib as the ultimate authority, Gurmukhī/Ang (page) references are given where possible. The Dasam Granth is cited with caution — its authorship is debated — but it remains doctrinally significant for the Khālsā tradition.

## 1. Ik Oaṅkār (The One God)

The foundational doctrine of Sikhism — the oneness of God. The *Mūl Mantra* ("Root Formula") that opens the Guru Granth Sāhib is the most important theological statement in the Sikh tradition: *Ik Oaṅkār Sat Nām Karatā Purakh Nirbhao Nirvair Akāl Mūrat Ajūnī Saibhaṃ Gur Prasād* ("There is One God, Truth by Name, the Creator, without fear, without enmity, the Timeless Being, not incarnated, self-existent, by the Guru's grace"). This single statement — repeated at the beginning of every major section of the Guru Granth Sāhib — establishes Sikh theology as rigorously monotheistic, non-incarnational, and non-anthropomorphic. God is both transcendent (*Nirguṇa*, without qualities) and immanent (*Sarguṇa*, with qualities), though both descriptions are ultimately inadequate to the Infinite.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1** (Japjī Sāhib, Mūl Mantra) | The primary revelation of the divine Name (*Nām*): Ik Oaṅkār as the irreducible starting-point of all Sikh theology |
| **Japjī Sāhib** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1–8) | 38 paurīs (stanzas) expanding the Mūl Mantra: the oneness of God as the ground of all reality — "There is one light among all beings, one wind that blows through all" (paurī 18) |
| **Sukhmanī Sāhib** (Gurū Arjan, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 262–296) | 24 ashtapaḍīs (octaves) celebrating the One who is "beyond colour and form, beyond caste and lineage" — the devotional experience of the One God |
| **Āsā dī Vār** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 462–475) | The morning hymn that contrasts the One Creator with the multiplicity of human religious claims: "Some call Him Rām, some call Him Khudāi — He is the One, may He guide us" |
| **Siddha Goṣṭ** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 938–946) | A debate with Nāth yogīs: God is not found in ascetic postures but in the realisation of the One within — "There is one light, one breath, in all beings" |
| **Dasam Granth** (Jāp Sāhib, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh) | A 199-stanza hymn continuing the Mūl Mantra tradition: "The One is the only One — no one else exists" (stanza 1); the *Cakkī* section expands the names of the One across Indic and Islamic vocabularies |

**Core texts:** Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1 (the Mūl Mantra — the irreducible core of Sikh identity); Japjī Sāhib (the daily recitation that enacts the Mūl Mantra)

**See also:** [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Gurū Nānak, Gurū Arjan, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh; [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Tawḥīd in Islam, Brahman in Hinduism

## 2. Nām Simran (Meditation on the Divine Name)

The central practice of Sikh spirituality — the loving remembrance (*simran*) of the divine Name (*Nām*). Nām Simran is not intellectual recitation but a total orientation of consciousness toward God, achieved through *japana* (repetition with awareness), *kīrtan* (congregational singing), and the constant remembrance of God in daily life. The Gurus teach that the Nām is both the means and the goal — it is the Name of God and God's own presence accessible to the devotee. The entire Guru Granth Sāhib is, in one sense, a single extended Nām Simran text.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Japjī Sāhib** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1–8) | The paradigm of Nām Simran: "By chanting the Nām one is saved, by chanting the Nām one swims across" (paurī 4); the *japana* (meditative repetition) as the essential practice |
| **Sukhmanī Sāhib** (Gurū Arjan, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 262–296) | "The peace-bestowing jewel of the Nām" — the entire composition is a sustained meditation on the power of the divine Name: "The Nām is the essence of all scriptures; the Nām is the support of the earth" |
| **Rahiras** (evening prayer, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 8–13, 266) | The daily evening liturgy: "Nām is the treasure that never runs out" — the Nām as the true wealth of the soul |
| **Sohaṇ Rā dī Vār** (night hymn, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 875–877) | The bedtime prayer: "The Nām is the boat to cross the world-ocean" — Nām Simran as the only means of liberation |
| **Ānand Sāhib** (Gurū Amar Dās, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 917–922) | The ecstatic song of Nām realisation: "Through the Nām, bliss wells up — O my mind, meditate on the Nām!" |
| **Vārāṅ of Bhāī Gurdās** (Vār 1, 4, 9, 21) | The authoritative commentary on Nām Simran: "The Nām is the medicine for the disease of egotism — the Nām is the remedy for all ills" (Vār 9, paurī 1) |

**Core texts:** Japjī Sāhib (morning), Sukhmanī Sāhib (anytime), Rahiras (evening) — the daily cycle of Nām Simran

**See also:** [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → Gurū Nānak's Four Journeys (spreading the Nām); [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Bhāī Mardānā (the constant companion in Nām Simran)

## 3. The Guru (The Divine Teacher)

The doctrine of the Guru is the organising principle of Sikh theology and history. The term *Guru* (from Sanskrit *guru*, "weighty," "honourable") refers first to the divine Teacher — God as the Original Guru — then to the ten human Gurus through whom the divine light (*jot*) was transmitted, and finally to the Guru Granth Sāhib as the eternal Śabad-Guru (Word as Guru). The line of ten Gurus — from Gurū Nānak (1469–1539) to Gurū Gobind Siṅgh (1666–1708) — is understood as one single light in ten bodies; the physical death of each Guru was simply the passing of the *jot* to the next. At the end of his life, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh declared the Guru Granth Sāhib the permanent Guru: "The Guru is the Granth; the Granth is the Guru."

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1** (Mūl Mantra ending: *Gur Prasād*) | "By the Guru's grace" — the entire Sikh path begins with the Guru's favour; grace is received through the Guru's teaching |
| **Siddha Goṣṭ** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 938–946) | The definition of the true Guru: "One who knows the One, who instils the One in others — that is the Guru" — the Guru as the revealer of the One |
| **Rāmakalī Sad** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 923–924) | "The Guru and the disciple are one — there is no difference between them" — the spiritual identity of Guru and disciple |
| **Vār Rāmakalī** (Gurū Rām Dās, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 953–958) | The Guru as the spiritual physician: "The Guru is the physician, the Nām is the medicine" |
| **Bachitar Nāṭak** (Dasam Granth, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh) | The autobiographical portion: "I did not wish for kingship, but I was born for the suffering of others" — the Guru's self-understanding as the servant of God and humanity |
| **Prem Sumārag** (attributed to Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, early 18th c.) | The Sikh polity: the Guru as both spiritual teacher and political sovereign |
| **Janam Sākhī tradition** (especially Bhāī Bālā and Puratan Janam Sākhīs) | The biographies of Gurū Nānak that establish the pattern of the Guru's life as a teaching: the Guru is recognised by his words, not his birth |

**Core texts:** Guru Granth Sāhib (the Śabad-Guru itself), Bachitar Nāṭak (the Tenth Guru's self-narrative), Janam Sākhīs (the Guru as narrated presence)

**See also:** [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → The Ten Gurus (each carrying the same *jot*); [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → The Installation of Gurū Aṅgad, The Compilation of the Ādi Granth

## 4. Haumai (Ego)

*Haumai* (from *ham* + *mai* — "I, me") is the root illness of the soul in Sikh theology — the sense of a separate, self-willed ego that experiences itself as distinct from God. Haumai is the primal separation, the original sin of the soul not against God's law but against God's reality: the soul's forgetting that its existence is borrowed from the One. The entire spiritual path of Sikhism is the progressive dying to haumai and the awakening to Hukam (divine will). The Guru Granth Sāhib contains the most extensive analysis of haumai in any Indian religious text.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Japjī Sāhib** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1–8) | Paurī 15–19 diagnose haumai: "Through haumai, one wanders lost in doubt; through haumai, one clings to duality" — the ego's imprisonment in the cycle of birth and death |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 124–125** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Mājh) | The most concentrated analysis of haumai in the entire Granth: "Haumai comes from the Lord — the Lord Himself creates it, and the Lord Himself removes it" — the paradox of the ego as both from God and the obstacle to God |
| **Sukhmanī Sāhib** (Gurū Arjan, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 262–296) | Ashtapaḍī 6: "When haumai departs, then the Nām is obtained" — the removal of the ego as the condition for divine union |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 617–620** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Āsā) | "Haumai is a deep and chronic disease — but it is also the medicine, when one follows the Guru's teaching" — the transformative paradox: haumai healed by turning the ego toward God |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 750–751** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Sūhī) | The three types of haumai: *rajas* (active egotism), *tamas* (dark ignorance), *sattva* (even spiritual pride becomes haumai if it claims separate existence) |
| **Bhāī Gurdās, Vār 4** | "All beings are afflicted by haumai — without the Guru, no one is cured" — the Guru as the healer of the egoic disease |

**Core texts:** Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 124–125 (the definitive Gurbanī on haumai); Japjī Sāhib paurī 15–19 (the accessible teaching)

**See also:** [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Gurū Nānak (the first to diagnose haumai); [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Anattā in Buddhism, Nafs in Sufism

## 5. Hukam (Divine Order)

*Hukam* (Perso-Arabic *hukm*, "command," "order") is the Sikh term for the divine will that permeates and governs all existence. To accept Hukam is to live in alignment with reality; to resist it is to remain trapped in haumai. Hukam is not a distant decree but the intimate order of the cosmos — the laws of nature, the moral law inscribed in the heart, and the specific circumstances of each life. The Sikh path is not a struggle *against* fate but a willing surrender *to* the divine will — an active, joyful acceptance, not a passive fatalism.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Japjī Sāhib** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1–8) | Paurī 2: "By His Hukam, bodies are formed; by His Hukam, greatness is attained; by His Hukam, some are high and some low; by His Hukam, some are blessed and some suffer" — the totality of Hukam's scope |
| **Japjī Sāhib, Paurī 3** | "By His Hukam, one understands Hukam — O Nānak, the Lord is beyond description" — Hukam can only be known through itself: submission is the organ of understanding |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1** (Slok of Japjī) | "The One who made the world, who ordained the world — that One alone knows" — Hukam as the unsearchable wisdom of God |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 150–151** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Āsā) | "Whatever pleases You, that alone is good" — the Sikh equivalent of the Islamic *radītu billāhi rabban* (I am pleased with God as my Lord) |
| **Āsā dī Vār** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 462–475, stanzas 15–16) | "If one could please the Lord through flattery, the whole world would try — but the Lord is pleased by truth alone" — Hukam as the standard of truth |
| **Sukhmanī Sāhib** (Gurū Arjan, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 262–296, Ashtapaḍī 4) | "One who accepts Hukam merges in the Lord" — the union of the soul with God through surrender |
| **Gurū Nānak's final sermon** (recorded in Janam Sākhīs) | "The Lord's Hukam is sweet to me" — Gurū Nānak's death as the final act of submission to Hukam |

**Core texts:** Japjī Sāhib paurī 2–3 (the foundational statement on Hukam); Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 150–151 (the acceptance of the divine will)

**See also:** [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → Gurū Nānak's Birth and Early Life (the first lesson in Hukam), The Martyrdom of Gurū Arjan (the supreme example of accepting Hukam)

## 6. Kirat Karo (Honest Work) & Vaṇḍ Chakko (Sharing)

Two of the three pillars of Sikh ethics (alongside Nām Japo — meditating on the Name). *Kirat Karo* — earn an honest living; *Vaṇḍ Chakko* — share what you have with others. Sikhism rejects the traditional Indian renunciatory ideal: the householder (*gṛhastha*) is the preferred location of spiritual practice. Work is not a distraction from spirituality but its expression — provided it is honest, ethical, and done in the awareness of God. Sharing is the natural outflow of the recognition that everything belongs to God; the Sikh is a trustee, not an owner.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1245** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Sūhī) | "They alone are pure who earn by hard work and give some to others" — the definition of true purity: honest labour and charity |
| **Āsā dī Vār** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 462–475, stanza 5) | "One who eats what he earns by his own labour, and gives some in charity — O Nānak, that is the true path" — the threefold formula |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1012** (Gurū Amar Dās, Rāg Rāmakalī) | "The Gurmukh works and earns, and gives some in charity — this is the way of life" — the Gurmukh (one turned toward the Guru) as the model of ethical labour |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 395–396** (Gurū Rām Dās, Rāg Gūjrī) | "Those who do not earn their own living, they are parasites on the Lord's earth" — the strong critique of idleness and mendicancy |
| **Vārāṅ of Bhāī Gurdās** (Vār 5, paurī 11) | "The true Sikh earns by honest means, recites the Nām, and shares with the needy" — the three duties summarised |
| **Rahitnāmā literature** (18th c. Khālsā codes) | The prohibition on stealing, cheating, gambling, and taking food from dishonest sources — the Khālsā code enforces *kirat karo* as a community discipline |

**Core texts:** Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1245 (the definitive verse); Āsā dī Vār stanza 5 (the classic formulation)

**See also:** [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → Gurū Nānak and Bhāī Mardānā (the wandering labourers), The Creation of the Khālsā (the new ethic of the sovereign householder)

## 7. Sevā (Selfless Service)

*Sevā* (Sanskrit *sevā*, "service") is one of the most important Sikh practices — service to others performed without expectation of reward, recognition, or even thanks. Sevā is the direct expression of love for God: because God dwells in all beings, serving another is serving the Divine. In Sikh practice, sevā takes concrete forms: carrying water for the *saṅgat* (congregation), cleaning the shoes of visitors, washing dishes in the *laṅgar* (community kitchen), offering first aid, and countless other humble tasks. The Gurus themselves set the example — Gurū Nānak insisted on serving food to travellers, and Gurū Amar Dās required every visitor, including the Mughal emperor, to sit and eat together.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 17–18** (Gurū Nānak, Japjī Sāhib, paurī 28) | "The greatest service is to serve the Lord's creation" — the definition of sevā as the summit of the spiritual life |
| **Sukhmanī Sāhib** (Gurū Arjan, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 262–296, Ashtapaḍī 16) | "The servant who serves without pride — that one is dear to the Lord" — humility as the hallmark of true sevā |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 286** (Gurū Arjan) | "Sevā is the true worship; without sevā, even the most elaborate rituals are hollow" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1013–1014** (Gurū Amar Dās, Rāg Rāmakalī) | "Those who perform sevā and meditate on the Lord — their faces shine in the court of the Lord" |
| **Bhāī Gurdās, Vār 21** | "Sevā is the real prayer; the act of service is itself the chanting of the Nām" — the identity of service and worship |
| **Janam Sākhī of Gurū Nānak** (Sākhī: "The Stone That Floated") | When the stone from the mosque did not float, Gurū Nānak insisted the carpenter be paid first — sevā means honesty and justice before piety |
| **Gurū Gobind Siṅgh's autobiography** (Bachitar Nāṭak, Dasam Granth) | "I came into the world for the sevā of the saints; I am the servant of the Khālsā" — the Tenth Guru's self-definition as servant |

**Core texts:** Japjī Sāhib paurī 28 (the foundational mandate), Bhāī Gurdās Vār 21 (the identification of sevā with worship)

**See also:** [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → Gurū Amar Dās and the Test of Humility (sevā as the test of a true Sikh), Laṅgar and the Egalitarian Meal; [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Bhāī Mardānā (the model of loyal service)

## 8. The Five Evils (Pañj Chor)

The five vices that the Sikh must overcome through the Guru's grace: *kām* (lust), *krodh* (anger), *lobh* (greed), *moh* (attachment), and *ahaṅkār* (pride). These are called the *Pañj Chor* — the "five thieves" — that rob the soul of its innate purity. They arise from haumai and perpetuate the cycle of birth and death. The Gurus analyse each evil with psychological precision and prescribe the antidote: the Nām, the Saṅgat (holy congregation), and the constant discipline of self-awareness.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 279** (Gurū Arjan, Sukhmanī Sāhib, Ashtapaḍī 4) | "Lust, anger, greed, attachment, and pride — these five are the thieves that plunder the soul" — the definitive listing |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 601** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Āsā) | "The five evils are the five demons that torment the soul — but through the Guru's word, they are conquered" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 219–220** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Mājh) | A sustained meditation on each evil: "Lust burns like fire; anger darkens the mind; greed is an insatiable ocean; attachment binds like chains; pride is a wall of stone" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 932–933** (Gurū Amar Dās, Rāg Rāmakalī) | "One who conquers the five thieves — that one is the true warrior" — spiritual battle as the real *dharam yudh* |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 386** (Gurū Rām Dās, Rāg Gūjrī) | The antidote: "When the Nām enters the heart, the five thieves flee — like darkness fleeing from light" |
| **Dasam Granth** (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, Chandi dī Vār) | The demonic forces in the Chandi narrative are allegorised in Khālsā tradition as the five evils: the Khālsā must slay them within themselves before fighting external enemies |
| **Rahitnāmās** (Khālsā codes of conduct) | The prohibitions mirror the five evils: no adultery (kām), no revenge (krodh), no theft or fraud (lobh), no intoxication (breaking moh to worldly identity), no shaving or disrespect to the Guru (conquering ahaṅkār) |

**Core texts:** Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 279 (Sukhmanī Sāhib) — the classic listing; Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 219–220 (the full treatment)

**See also:** [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → The Battle of Chamkaur (the Khālsā as warriors who conquered the inner five evils); [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → The Five Hindrances in Buddhism, The Seven Deadly Sins in Christianity

## 9. The Five Virtues

The positive qualities cultivated through the Guru's grace: *sat* (truth), *santoṣ* (contentment), *dayā* (compassion), *dhīraj* (patience), and *nimratā* (humility). These are the fruits of Nām Simran — not acquired by effort alone but received as the ego diminishes. The Five Virtues are the Sikh answer to the question "What kind of person should I become?" — a person of truthfulness, inner peace, active compassion, unshakeable patience, and genuine humility.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Japjī Sāhib** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1–8, paurī 16) | The virtues listed through their opposites: "Truth, contentment, compassion — these are the essence of the path" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 299** (Gurū Arjan, Sukhmanī Sāhib, Ashtapaḍī 7) | "Truth is higher than everything; but higher still is truthful living" — the virtue of *sat* as active truthfulness |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 51–52** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Sīrī Rāg) | "Contentment is the mother of all virtues; without contentment, the mind wanders in a thousand directions" — *santoṣ* as the root of spiritual stability |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 276** (Gurū Arjan, Sukhmanī Sāhib) | "Compassion is the greatest religion" — *dayā* as the universal dharma, echoing the Jain principle and extending it beyond non-violence to active kindness |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 288** (Gurū Arjan) | "Patience is the key to liberation" — *dhīraj* as the endurance that makes all other virtues possible |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 470** (Gurū Nānak, Āsā dī Vār) | "Humility is the true sword; without it, all other weapons are useless" — *nimratā* as the Khālsā's inner weapon |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 15** (Gurū Nānak, Japjī Sāhib, paurī 24) | The five virtues as the elements of *sach khaṇḍ* (the realm of truth): they are the qualities of the liberated person |

**Core texts:** Japjī Sāhib paurī 16 and 24 (the programmatic statements); Sukhmanī Sāhib Ashtapaḍī 7 (the virtue of truth)

**See also:** [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Gurū Tegh Bahādur (the embodiment of *dhīraj* and *nimratā* in martyrdom); [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → The Pāramitās in Buddhism, The Fruits of the Spirit in Christianity

## 10. Satsang (Holy Congregation)

*Satsang* (Sanskrit *sat* = truth + *saṅgha* = assembly) — the holy congregation of those who gather to sing the Lord's praises, meditate on the Nām, and hear the Guru's words. In Sikhism, the *saṅgat* (the congregation) is the body of the Guru; the Guru is present wherever two or three are gathered in the Nām. Satsang is both a practice (the weekly *dīwān* in the *gurdwārā*) and a theological principle: the individual cannot be saved in isolation; the path to God runs through the community of seekers.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 272** (Gurū Arjan, Sukhmanī Sāhib, Ashtapaḍī 3) | "The Lord is found in the Saṅgat — without the Saṅgat, there is only darkness" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 675** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Vadhans) | "Where the Saṅgat gathers and the Name is sung — there the Lord Himself descends" — the theology of divine presence in the congregation |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 127** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Mājh) | "The Saṅgat is the second form of the Guru — the Guru is the Saṅgat, the Saṅgat is the Guru" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 6** (Gurū Nānak, Japjī Sāhib, paurī 13) | "The Saṅgat is the place of purification — join the Saṅgat and sing the Lord's praises" |
| **Vārāṅ of Bhāī Gurdās** (Vār 21, paurī 1) | "The Saṅgat is the mirror in which the soul sees itself — without the Saṅgat, one cannot recognise one's own faults" |
| **Gurū Gobind Siṅgh's Hukamnāmās** | The commands to the Khālsā: "Wherever five Sikhs gather, there the Guru is present" — the *Pañj Piāre* (Five Beloved Ones) as the foundational Saṅgat |

**Core texts:** Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 272 (Sukhmanī Sāhib); Bhāī Gurdās Vār 21 (the doctrinal commentary)

**See also:** [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → The Creation of the Khālsā (the Saṅgat as the sovereign body), Laṅgar and the Egalitarian Meal (the Saṅgat as the site of equality); [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → The Pañj Piāre (the model Saṅgat)

## 11. Māyā (Illusion/Attachment)

*Māyā* in Sikhism is not the philosophical illusion of Advaita Vedānta (the unreality of the world) but the *attachment* to worldly things that obscures the vision of God. The world itself is real — it is God's creation and therefore good — but the soul's entanglement in it through desire, possessiveness, and forgetting is māyā. The Gurus use the term *māyā* primarily as a moral and spiritual concept: it is the power that separates the soul from God, the veil of attachment that must be pierced by the Guru's grace.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1** (Gurū Nānak, Japjī Sāhib, paurī 1) | "One cannot know Him through intellect — but through the Guru's grace, the veil of māyā is lifted" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 124** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Mājh) | "Māyā is the bride of the Lord — but the soul is deceived by her beauty and forgets the Groom" — the metaphor of māyā as a seductress |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 602–603** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Āsā) | "Māyā is like the shadow of a cloud — it seems to follow you, but when you chase it, it retreats" — the insubstantiality of worldly attachment |
| **Sukhmanī Sāhib** (Gurū Arjan, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 262–296, Ashtapaḍī 21) | "One who sees the world as a dream, who does not cling to it — that one is liberated even while alive" — *jīvan mukta* as freedom from māyā |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 918** (Gurū Amar Dās, Ānand Sāhib) | "The Lord's māyā is sweet to the ego — but the Guru shows that it is poison" |
| **Dasam Granth** (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, Chandi dī Vār) | The goddess Chandi is both māyā (the creative power of God) and its destroyer — the paradox of the world as both real instrument and potential trap |

**Core texts:** Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 124 (the classic Gurbanī on māyā); Sukhmanī Sāhib Ashtapaḍī 21 (the liberated soul beyond māyā)

**See also:** [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Māyā in Vedānta, Duniyā in Sufi thought

## 12. Liberation (Mukti/Jīvan Mukt)

Liberation in Sikhism — *mukti* (freedom from the cycle of birth and death) — is not a post-mortem achievement but a state to be realised *in this life* (*jīvan mukta* — liberated while living). The liberated soul, while still embodied, experiences union with God, sees the One in all beings, and lives in a state of perpetual bliss and service. Death is then only the dissolution of the body; the soul merges in God like a drop in the ocean. Sikhism rejects the Advaitin monism of total identity — the soul remains distinct enough to enjoy union — and also rejects theistic dualism of eternal separation: the soul is "of God and in God."

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Japjī Sāhib** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1–8, paurī 27) | "One who knows the Lord in this life — that one is liberated. Nānak sings the praises of such a one" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 112–113** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Mājh) | "Liberation is not in the forest, nor in the mountains — it is in the heart that has surrendered to the Guru" |
| **Sukhmanī Sāhib** (Gurū Arjan, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 262–296, Ashtapaḍī 17) | "The *jīvan mukta* — the liberated one — is the true king, living in the world yet untouched by it" — the classic description of liberated living |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 633–634** (Gurū Rām Dās, Rāg Āsā) | "Liberation is not gained by renouncing the world — liberation is found through the Guru's word, while living in the world" — the householder's path |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 275** (Gurū Arjan, Sukhmanī Sāhib) | "The liberated soul is like a lotus in the water — it lives in the world but is not wetted by it" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 930** (Gurū Amar Dās, Rāg Rāmakalī) | "One who dies while living — that one knows the secret of liberation." The *jīvat maranā* (dying while alive) — the death of the ego before physical death |
| **Dasam Granth** (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, Zafarnāmā) | Liberation is not escape but the courage to stand for truth: "When all other means fail, it is righteous to unsheath the sword" — the liberated soul acts freely |

**Core texts:** Sukhmanī Sāhib Ashtapaḍī 17 (the fullest description of the *jīvan mukta*); Japjī Sāhib paurī 27 (the essence)

**See also:** [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Gurū Arjan (the *jīvan mukta* as martyr), Gurū Tegh Bahādur (the liberated soul who dies for others)

## 13. The Guru Granth Sāhib as Living Guru

The most distinctive doctrine in Sikhism: the scripture IS the living Guru. At the end of his life (1708), Gurū Gobind Siṅgh declared that after him there would be no human Guru — the *Śabad* (Word) embodied in the Guru Granth Sāhib would be the Guru. The Granth is not merely an authoritative text; it is the literal presence of the Guru — the body (*śarīr*) of the ten Gurus. In every *gurdwārā*, it is installed on a throne (*takhtt*) under a canopy, fanned with a *chaurī* (fly-whisk) as a mark of sovereignty, and addressed as *Satgurū* (True Guru). Before any decision — personal, communal, or political — a *Hukamnāmā* (a random opening) is taken as the Guru's direct command.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib throughout** (Ang 1–1430) | The Granth itself is the primary "text" for this group — its structure, its *rāg* arrangement, its multilingual composition |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 966** (Gurū Arjan, Rāg Rāmakalī) | "The Word is the Guru; the mind, the disciple — through the Word, the mind merges in the Lord" — the *Śabad-Gurū* doctrine |
| **Gurū Amar Dās, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1216** | "The Word is the Guru; through the Word, one attunes to the Lord" |
| **Gurū Gobind Siṅgh's declaration** (1708, recorded in Gurū Siṅgh's *Gurū Bilās* and Sainapati's *Srī Gurū Sobhā*) | "The Guru is the Granth; the Granth is the Guru. In it are all the teachings; the body of the Guru is the Granth" |
| **Bhāī Gurdās, Vār 1, paurī 45** | "The Guru wrote the Word, the Word became the Guru — the Word is the one without fear, the Word is the one without enmity" |
| **Hukamnāmā practice** (oral tradition, but rooted in Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1) | The *vāk lāī* (taking the Guru's command) — the Guru speaks directly to the seeker through the opened page |
| **Gurbilās literature** (especially *Srī Gurū Sobhā*, 1711) | The earliest accounts of the installation of the Guru Granth Sāhib as the eternal Guru: the ceremony at Nānded in 1708 |

**Core texts:** Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 966 (the founding sūtra of the Śabad-Gurū); Gurbilās accounts of the 1708 succession

**See also:** [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → The Compilation of the Ādi Granth, The Installation of the Guru Granth Sāhib; [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Gurū Arjan (the compiler), Gurū Gobind Siṅgh (who installed the Granth as Guru)

## 14. Laṅgar (Community Kitchen)

*Laṅgar* — the free, egalitarian community kitchen attached to every gurdwārā — is one of the most visible and beloved Sikh institutions. Everyone, regardless of religion, caste, gender, or social status, sits together on the floor and eats the same simple meal (*dāl, rotī, sabzī*, and *kṛpāḍ*). Laṅgar is not merely a charitable scheme but a theological practice: it is the enactment of the equality of all humans before God, the destruction of caste pride, and the concrete expression of *sevā* and *vaṇḍ chakko*. The institution was established by Gurū Nānak when he used the capital given to him by his father for a business venture to feed hungry saints — and was formalised by Gurū Amar Dās, who insisted that everyone who came to see him must first eat in the laṅgar.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1245** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Sūhī) | "They alone are true who earn by the sweat of their brow and share what they have" — the theological basis of laṅgar |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 347** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Gūjrī) | "The Lord's kitchen is always full — the Lord Himself provides for all who come" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 609** (Gurū Amar Dās) | "First seat them in the laṅgar, then show them the Guru" — the priority of feeding over preaching |
| **Janam Sākhī tradition** (Sākhī of Gurū Nānak at Saidpur / Eminabad) | The foundational laṅgar narrative: Gurū Nānak feeds the holy men with his father's money; the father complains, but Nānak says "This is the true business — the Lord's business" |
| **Bhāī Gurdās, Vār 21** | "The laṅgar is the Guru's table — whoever eats there becomes pure" |
| **Rahitnāmās** (Khālsā code) | The requirement for every gurdwārā to maintain a laṅgar; the prohibition on making any distinction of caste or status in the laṅgar; the rule that all sit together on the floor |

**Core texts:** Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1245 (the ethical foundation); Janam Sākhī of Saidpur (the founding narrative)

**See also:** [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → Gurū Nānak and Bhāī Mardānā (the first laṅgar), Laṅgar and the Egalitarian Meal; [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Gurū Amar Dās (the institutionaliser of laṅgar)

## 15. Equality and Social Justice

Sikhism was born in a society rigidly stratified by caste (*jāt pāt*), gender hierarchy, and religious discrimination. From its beginnings, the Sikh Gurus proclaimed the radical equality of all humans. The Guru Granth Sāhib is one of the first scriptures in world history to explicitly reject caste, condemn the oppression of women, criticise religious hypocrisy, and insist that God is found not in temples, mosques, or pilgrimages but in truthful living and justice for the poor. The Gurus did not merely preach equality — they institutionalised it through laṅgar, through the rejection of Brahminical ritual, through the elevation of women to the administration of the Saṅgat, and finally through the Khālsā, where a barber and a Brahmin were initiated as equals.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 15** (Gurū Nānak, Japjī Sāhib, paurī 24) | "In the realm of truth, the formless One is seen — there, caste has no meaning; only truth matters" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 349** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Gūjrī) | "The same light is in all beings; the One is in all. How can caste be real when the One is everywhere?" |
| **Āsā dī Vār** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 462–475, stanza 4) | "False are the claims of caste; false is the pride in one's lineage" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 611** (Gurū Amar Dās, Rāg Āsā) | "One who eats with everyone, who treats all as equals — that one knows the Guru's path" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 73** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Sīrī Rāg) | "Women give birth to kings and emperors — why call them inferior?" — the classic Gurbanī on gender equality |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 137** (Gurū Amar Dās) | "All beings are equal in the eyes of the Lord" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1128** (Bhagat Kabīr's Bānī) | "The Brahmin is a Brahmin by deeds, not by birth — the same light is in all" (echoed and amplified by the Gurus) |
| **Rahitnāmās** (Khālsā codes) | The prohibition on caste distinctions among the Khālsā: all initiated Sikhs share the surname Siṅgh (lion) for men and Kaur (princess) for women, erasing all caste markers |
| **Dasam Granth** (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, Chandi dī Vār) | The allegory of the goddess Chandi destroying the demon-army of caste-class hierarchy — the Khālsā as the army of social justice |

**Core texts:** Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 349 and 611 (the doctrinal rejection of caste); Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 73 (the equality of women)

**See also:** [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → The Creation of the Khālsā (the first initiation of equals), The Martyrdom of Gurū Arjan (justice for the common person); [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Bhagat Kabīr, Bhagat Nāmdev (the Bhaktas who inspired the Gurus' egalitarianism)

## 16. Sufi & Bhakti Vocabulary

The Guru Granth Sāhib is a unique multilingual scripture that draws freely on the vocabulary of both Islam and Hinduism, while subsuming all terminology under the Sikh framework of Ik Oaṅkār. The Gurus use *Allāh*, *Rasūl* (messenger), *Khudāi* (God), *Ādam* (Adam), *Jibrīl* (Gabriel) alongside *Rām*, *Kṛṣṇa*, *Govind*, *Hari*, *Vedas*, *Purāṇas* — but always with the same meaning: the One God. This is not syncretism but *synthesis from above* — every name points to the same Reality. The *Bhagat Bānī* (the hymns of the fifteen Bhaktas and Sūfīs included in the Guru Granth Sāhib) constitutes the most extraordinary ecumenical act in religious history: the inclusion of Hindu Bhaktas (Kabīr, Nāmdev, Ravidās, etc.) and a Muslim Sūfī (Śaikh Farīd) in a sacred scripture that is also distinctly Sikh.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 885** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Āsā) | "Some call Him Rām, some call Him Khudāi — He is the One; may He guide us" — the explicit identification of Islamic and Hindu names for God |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1083** (Gurū Arjan) | "Do not speak of Hindus or Muslims — speak of the human. All are the same" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 142** (Bhagat Kabīr, Rāg Bhairon) | "Allāh is the same as Rām in the Vedas — the One who created the universe is beyond all names" |
| **Śaikh Farīd's Bānī** (Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 488–498) | The Sūfī verses of Farīduddīn Ganj-e-Shakar (12th c.) — the first Sūfī poetry included in a non-Muslim scripture: "Farīd, do not turn your face from anyone — the Lord is in every heart" |
| **Bhagat Kabīr's Bānī** (Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 479–490, 929–941, 1124–1138) | The most voluminous Bhagat Bānī: Kabīr's radical critique of both Hindu and Muslim orthodoxy — "What does the mullāh know of God who reads the kalima without understanding?" |
| **Bhagat Nāmdev's Bānī** (Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 484–487, 525–526, 727–729) | The Marathi Bhakta who sings of Kṛṣṇa and Hari — but the Gurus read his devotion as devotion to the One |
| **Bhagat Ravidās's Bānī** (Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 345–347, 486, 658–659) | The cobbler-saint whose hymns on the City Without Sorrow (*Begampurā*) became an inspiration for Sikh social vision |
| **Dasam Granth** (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, the *Kṛṣṇa Avatār* and *Rudra Avatār* sections) | Gurū Gobind Siṅgh writes hymns in the voice of a devotee of the goddess (Chandi) and of Kṛṣṇa — while also writing a Zafarnāmā (Letter of Victory) in Persian to the Mughal emperor, quoting the Qur'an |

**Core texts:** Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 885 (the "Some call Him Rām, some call Him Khudāi" verse — the most quoted Sikh ecumenical statement); Bhagat Kabīr's Bānī (the most extensive Bhagat Bānī)

**See also:** [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Bhagat Kabīr, Śaikh Farīd, Bhagat Nāmdev, Bhagat Ravidās; [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Bhakti Movement, Sufi Islam, the Sant Tradition (Nirguṇa Bhakti)

## 17. Death, Afterlife and Karma

Sikh eschatology is distinct from both Hindu and Muslim models. The soul (*ātman*) is a part of God, created by God, and destined to return to God. Karma (*karam*) operates as the law of moral causation that determines the cycle of birth and death (*chaurāsī kā chakkar* — the wheel of 84 lakh births). But karma is not ultimate — the Guru's grace (*nadar, prasād*) can break the karmic chain. The soul, purified by Nām Simran, the Guru's teaching, and selfless service, merges in God (*joti jot samāvaṇā*). The afterlife is not eternal heaven or hell but further births — though the Gurus also speak of *dargah* (the divine court) where the soul is judged as a metaphor for inner realisation.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Japjī Sāhib** (Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1–8, paurī 22) | "The soul comes and goes by the Lord's Hukam — we can only submit to the One who understands" — karma and rebirth under the sovereignty of Hukam |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 163–164** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Āsā) | "The body is a field; karma is the seed; the Lord gives the harvest" — the metaphor of karmic agriculture |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 315–316** (Gurū Arjan, Sukhmanī Sāhib, Ashtapaḍī 11) | "As one sows, so one reaps — the soul wanders in the cycle of 84 lakh births until it finds the Guru" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 730** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Āsā) | "Death is the wedding day — the bride (the soul) goes to meet the Groom (God)" — dying as union, not loss |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 887–888** (Gurū Rām Dās, Rāg Āsā) | "Those who have remembered the Nām — their faces shine in the court of the Lord" — the *dargah* as the metaphor of divine justice |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 936** (Gurū Amar Dās, Rāg Rāmakalī) | "The soul does not die, it does not burn — it merges in the Lord like water in water" |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 565** (Bhagat Kabīr, Rāg Āsā) | "Kabīr says: the world is a drama — the soul and God are one; realise this and be free" |
| **Bhāī Gurdās, Vār 21** | "Karma binds the soul; the Nām frees it. Without the Guru, the chain of karma is unbreakable" |

**Core texts:** Japjī Sāhib paurī 22 (the framework of karma under Hukam); Sukhmanī Sāhib Ashtapaḍī 11 (the classic cycle-of-births text)

**See also:** [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Bhāī Gurdās (the commentator on karma); [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Karma in Hinduism, Karma in Buddhism, Judgement in Islam

## 18. The Khālsā and Rehit (Code of Conduct)

The Khālsā ("the pure") — the community of initiated Sikhs created by Gurū Gobind Siṅgh at Vaisākhī 1699. The initiation ceremony (*Amrit Sañchār* — nectar initiation) was a revolutionary religious event: the Guru asked for five volunteers willing to give their heads, and initiated them (the *Pañj Piāre* — Five Beloved Ones) from a single pot of sweetened water stirred with a double-edged sword (*khaṇḍā*). He then bowed to them as the Guru's own body. The Khālsā is bound by the *Rahit* (code of conduct): the Five Ks (*kakkār* — *keś* [uncut hair], *kaṅghā* [comb], *kirpān* [ceremonial sword], *kaṛā* [steel bracelet], *kacchera* [cotton undergarment]), the prohibitions (*kurehat* — no tobacco, no halal/kosher meat, no adultery, no shaving or cutting hair), and the positive duties of daily recitation (the five *banīs*), service, and defence of the oppressed.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Dasam Granth** (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, *Amrit Kīrtan* section) | The hymns recited at the Amrit ceremony: the *Jāp Sāhib*, *Tav-Prasād Savaiye*, and *Chaupai Sāhib* — the initiation liturgy itself |
| **Dasam Granth** (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, *Chandi dī Vār*) | The martial hymn that instilled the Khālsā warrior ethos: "The sword is the soul of the brave — they alone are worthy who die for righteousness" |
| **Bachitar Nāṭak** (Dasam Granth, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh) | The autobiographical section recounting the creation of the Khālsā: "When I sent the five to their deaths, they came back — I then realised they were the Beloved Ones" |
| **Rahitnāmā literature** (Chaupa Siṅgh, Dayā Siṅgh, Prahlād Siṅgh, 18th c.) | The earliest written codes of Khālsā conduct: the *Rahitnāmā* of Chaupa Siṅgh (c. 1740), the *Rahitnāmā* of Dayā Siṅgh (c. 1750), the *Tankhāh-nāmā* (book of penalties) |
| **Gurū Gobind Siṅgh's Hukamnāmās** | Direct letters from the Tenth Guru to Sikh communities across North India: the commands to maintain the Five Ks, to keep the Rehit, to avoid caste distinctions |
| **Srī Gurū Sobhā** (Sainapati, 1711) | The earliest narrative of the Khālsā creation outside the Dasam Granth: "The Guru called for the five, and the five arose — they were the five sparks of the divine light" |
| **Sūraj Prakāś** (Bhāī Sāntokh Siṅgh, 1843) | The massive 19th-century compilation that standardised the Khālsā creation narrative for the later tradition |

**Core texts:** Dasam Granth (Amrit Kīrtan section — the initiation liturgy); Rahitnāmā of Chaupa Siṅgh (the oldest Rehit code); Srī Gurū Sobhā (the earliest historical account)

**See also:** [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → The Creation of the Khālsā; [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → The Pañj Piāre, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, Bhāī Chaupa Siṅgh, Māī Bhāgo (a female Khālsā warrior)

## 19. Dharam Yudh (Righteous War)

The Sikh doctrine of just war — *dharam yudh* (righteous battle) — is one of the most developed theologies of defensive violence in any religious tradition. It was formulated in response to the Mughal persecution of the Sikhs under Jahāngīr (martyrdom of Gurū Arjan, 1606), Shāh Jahān (Gurū Har Gobind's battles), and especially Aurangzeb (torture and execution of Gurū Tegh Bahādur, 1675). The principle is simple: force may only be used in defence of the innocent and the oppressed, when all other means have failed. The warrior-saint (*sant-sipāhī*) is the Khālsā ideal — one who is internally pure (the saint) and externally ready to defend righteousness (the soldier). The sword is never the weapon of aggression but the "dagger of mercy" (*kṛpāṇ*), used to protect the defenceless.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 579** (Gurū Nānak, Rāg Āsā) | "When the hand is stained with the blood of the oppressor, it is still pure" — the moral justification of defensive violence |
| **Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 1082–1083** (Gurū Arjan, Rāg Gūjrī) | "The sword that protects the poor is the sword of God" — the divine mandate for the use of force |
| **Dasam Granth** (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, *Chandi dī Vār*) | The most important Sikh text on dharam yudh: the goddess Chandi's battle against the shape-shifting demons — allegorising the righteous war against tyranny and falsehood |
| **Dasam Granth** (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, *Zafarnāmā*, Letter to Aurangzeb, 1707) | The ethical framework of dharam yudh in the Guru's own words: "When all other means have failed, it is righteous to unsheath the sword" — the most quoted line in Sikh political thought |
| **Dasam Granth** (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, *Bachitar Nāṭak*) | The autobiographical narrative of battles: the Guru's account of the battles against the hill chiefs and the Mughals, always framed as defensive |
| **Sūraj Prakāś** (Bhāī Sāntokh Siṅgh) | The 19th-century compendium that narrates the battles of Gurū Har Gobind and Gurū Gobind Siṅgh with the full theology of dharam yudh — the Khālsā as the army of the timeless One |
| **Gurū Har Gobind's Mīrī-Pīrī doctrine** (from Janam Sākhī and Gurbilās traditions) | The Guru wearing two swords: one representing *mīrī* (temporal power), the other *pīrī* (spiritual authority) — the union of the two realms as the Khālsā's domain |
| **Kurānet (Gurmat interpretation)** | The Sikh rejection of pacifism as a path — the Guru Granth Sāhib contains no blanket condemnation of war, only of unjust war |

**Core texts:** Dasam Granth — Zafarnāmā (the letter to Aurangzeb — the most concise statement of dharam yudh); Chandi dī Vār (the allegorical theology); Bachitar Nāṭak (the historical record)

**See also:** [[Sikh Stories MOC]] → The Battle of Chamkaur, The Martyrdom of Gurū Arjan, The Martyrdom of Gurū Tegh Bahādur; [[Sikh Figures MOC]] → Gurū Har Gobind, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, Banda Siṅgh Bahādur

## 20. The Lives of the Gurus as Scripture

Sikhism has a vast narrative literature — the *Janam Sākhīs* (birth-stories of Gurū Nānak), the *Gurbilāses* (accounts of the Gurus' lives and battles), and the *Bachitar Nāṭak* (autobiography of Gurū Gobind Siṅgh) — that functions as scripture in a secondary but crucial sense. These texts are not canonical in the same way as the Guru Granth Sāhib — they do not hold the authority of the Śabad-Guru — but they transmit doctrine through narrative, shape Sikh identity through the remembered lives of the Gurus, and provide the interpretive key to many of the theological concepts in the Granth. The Gurus' lives — their births, travels, marriages, battles, and martyrdoms — are themselves *teaching events*; the narrative literature preserves and transmits these events as living scripture.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Puratan Janam Sākhī** (c. 16th–17th c., recension of Bhāī Bālā tradition) | The earliest connected biography of Gurū Nānak: the birth, the four journeys (*udāsīs*), the ascension to God's court, the teaching encounters with the Nāth yogīs, Sūfīs, kings, and commoners |
| **Bhāī Bālā Janam Sākhī** (contested 17th c. recension) | The most popular Janam Sākhī tradition: 57 sākhīs that give Gurū Nānak's life as a series of teaching episodes — "Every moment of the Guru's life is a sermon" |
| **Miharbān Janam Sākhī** (c. 1658, by Sodhī Miharbān) | The most theologically sophisticated Janam Sākhī: presents Gurū Nānak as the incarnation of the divine Word — the earthly life as the unfolding of the eternal Nām |
| **Bachitar Nāṭak** (Dasam Granth, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh) | The only autobiographical scripture in the Sikh canon: the Tenth Guru's chronicle of his own birth, lineage, battles, and mission — "I did not wish for kingship" — the Guru's life as the enactment of the divine will |
| **Srī Gurū Sobhā** (Sainapati, 1711) | The earliest Gurbilās: the life of Gurū Gobind Siṅgh from 1696 to 1708, including the creation of the Khālsā, the battles, and the Guru's final journey — written by a contemporary poet of the Guru's court |
| **Gurū Bilās Pātshāhī 6** (c. 1780, attributed to Kesar Siṅgh Chhībbar) | The life of Gurū Har Gobind (sixth Guru): the Mīrī-Pīrī doctrine embodied in narrative — the first Guru to take up arms, his battles against the Mughals, the construction of the Akāl Takht |
| **Gurū Bilās Pātshāhī 10** (Sukhā Siṅgh, 1797) | The most influential Gurbilās of Gurū Gobind Siṅgh: the full narrative of the Guru's life from his birth in Patna through the creation of the Khālsā to the final installation of the Guru Granth Sāhib |
| **Sūraj Prakāś** (Bhāī Sāntokh Siṅgh, 1843) | The grandest Sikh narrative work: 55,000 verses covering all ten Gurus' lives — the single most important post-Granth Sikh text. Not canonical but influential in shaping popular Sikh piety |
| **Vārāṅ of Bhāī Gurdās** (especially Vār 1) | Vār 1 is a condensed biography of Gurū Nānak in 45 paurīs — "The Guru was the lamp that lit the world" — perhaps the oldest surviving biographical text of the first Guru |
| **Gurti Siṅgh's Gurū Bilās** (1726) | A verse-history of the Sikh tradition from Gurū Nānak through the 1710s — bridges the Janam Sākhī and Gurbilās genres |

**Core texts:** Puratan Janam Sākhī (the oldest Gurū Nānak biography), Bachitar Nāṭak (the Tenth Guru's autobiography), Srī Gurū Sobhā (the earliest Khālsā-era narrative), Sūraj Prakāś (the encyclopedic narrative)

## How to Use These Groups

**To find texts by theme:** Browse the group headings above and follow the references to the key Gurbanī passages, Dasam Granth sections, and narrative sources. For example, group 2 (Nām Simran) will take you from the Japjī Sāhib through the Sukhmanī Sāhib to the daily prayer liturgy.

**For thematic study:** Read a group sequentially — for instance, trace the theme of Haumai (group 4) from Japjī Sāhib paurī 15–19 (the foundational diagnosis), through Guru Granth Sāhib, Ang 124–125 (the definitive analysis), to Bhāī Gurdās Vār 4 (the commentarial tradition).

**For cross-referencing notes:** When writing about a topic (e.g., "liberation in Sikh thought"), consult group 12 (Liberation) to find all relevant doctrinal texts, then group 20 (The Lives of the Gurus as Scripture) for the narrative embodiments of liberation, and group 18 (The Khālsā and Rehit) for the discipline that leads to liberation.

**To understand individual texts:** Each text's cross-reference in the groups above explains *why* it belongs to that theme. Many texts appear in multiple groups — the Japjī Sāhib, for instance, appears in groups 1, 2, 4, 5, 9, 12, and 17 — reflecting its role as the seed-text of the entire Sikh theological system.

**Paired study:** Use alongside [[Quran Thematic Groups]], [[Bible Thematic Groups]], [[Hindu Scripture Thematic Groups]], [[Buddhist Scripture Thematic Groups]], and [[Jain Scripture Thematic Groups]] to compare how the world's major scriptures treat shared themes like monotheism, meditation, ego, karma, ethics, community, and liberation.
