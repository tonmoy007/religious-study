# 🍛 Laṅgar (The Sikh Free Kitchen)

> *"Sabh-nāṁ kā sāhib-u ek-u hai — sabh nāṁ kī Laṅgar ek-u hai."*
> *"There is One Master of all — there is one Laṅgar for all."*
> — **Gurū Nānak**, Āsā kī Vār (Ādi Granth, p. 473)

## Definition

**Laṅgar** (ਲਙ੍ਗਰ — from Sanskrit *aliṅgāra*, "kitchen") is the Sikh tradition of the **community free kitchen** — a self-sustained, vegetarian meal served at no cost to every visitor regardless of religion, caste, class, gender, or nationality. More than a charitable kitchen, Laṅgar is a core institution of Sikhism, a lived enactment of **Vaṇḍa Chakō** (sharing with others). Every gurdwārā in the world operates a Laṅgar, and the largest — at the Golden Temple in Amritsar — feeds over 100,000 people daily.

## Etymology

| Term | Origin | Meaning |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Laṅgar** (ਲਙ੍ਗਰ) | Sanskrit *aliṅgāra* (अलिङ्गार) | "Kitchen, place for cooking" — also glossed as *a-liṅga* ("bodiless"), implying the kitchen serves beyond bodily distinctions of caste |
| **Vaṇḍa Chakō** (ਵੰਡ ਛਕੋ) | Punjabi *vaṇḍṇā* ("divide") + *chakṇā* ("taste/eat") | "Share and consume together" — the theological imperative underlying Laṅgar |
| **Sēvā** (ਸੇਵਾ) | Skt. *sevā* ("service") | Selfless voluntary service — the framework within which every Laṅgar operates |

## Historical Origins

### The Kartarpur Foundation (c. 1520–1539)

Laṅgar originates with **Gurū Nānak** (1469–1539) at **Kartarpur** ("City of the Creator") on the Ravi River (now Punjab, Pakistan). After his nine-year travels (*udāsīs*), Gurū Nānak settled at Kartarpur, adopted the householder life, and instituted a communal farm and kitchen that was radically egalitarian:

- **Open to all** — Hindu, Muslim, low-caste, high-caste, all sat together in a single row (*pañṅgat*)
- **Vegetarian by default** — simple *dāl*, *roṭī*, *sabzī* that anyone could eat without ritual restrictions
- **Prepared through shared labour** — Gurū Nānak himself fetched water, chopped vegetables, and served food alongside his followers
- **Self-funded** — the village contributed to a common fund; no one was turned away for inability to pay

A famous anecdote: when the wealthy chief Mālik Bhoi offered Gurū Nānak a lavish feast on golden plates, the Guru refused, stating he would only eat from the *saṅgat* (community) — earning him the derisive title *Laṅgarī* ("kitchen-man") from the orthodox. He embraced it.

### Institutionalisation under Gurū Aṅgad (1539–1552)

Gurū Aṅgad formalised the kitchen into an institution:

1. **Continuous operation** — the Laṅgar ran every day without break
2. **Caste abolition** — he decreed that anyone could serve and anyone could eat; no one could meet the Guru without first sitting in the Laṅgar
3. **Physical labour as discipline** — Gurū Aṅgad personally drew water for the kitchen, establishing *sēvā* as essential spiritual practice
4. **Scriptural mandate** — he composed verses elevating shared eating as worship

### The Peṅgh Rule under Gurū Amar Dās (1552–1574)

Gurū Amar Dās established the most famous structural rule of Laṅgar — the **Peṅgh** regulation:

> **"Pehalē paṅgat, pīchai saṅgat"** — *"First the common kitchen, then the congregation."*

Under this rule, **no one** — not even the Mughal Emperor Akbar or the most venerated elder — could meet the Guru without **first eating in the Laṅgar**. All visitors sat on the floor in a single row — kings and paupers shoulder to shoulder.

When Emperor **Akbar** visited Goindvāl, he was required to eat Laṅgar before meeting the Guru. Impressed, he offered a *jāgīr* (land grant), which the Guru declined — accepting only that the land be tax-free for the farmers. Gurū Amar Dās also appointed **masands** (community leaders) to oversee Laṅgar operations across growing Sikh centres, mandating *dasvandh* (one-tenth of income) to sustain the institution.

## Core Qualities of Laṅgar

| Quality | Practice | Principle |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| **Free** | No charge under any circumstance | God's grace is unconditional |
| **Vegetarian** | No meat, eggs, or animal products | Food acceptable to all traditions |
| **Egalitarian seating** (*pañṅgat*) | All sit on the floor in straight rows | Caste, class, gender distinctions dissolve |
| **Prepared through *sēvā*** | Cooked and served entirely by volunteers | Work is worship; humility through service |

---

## Theological Meaning

### 1. Equality

The *pañṅgat* is the physical icon of **Ik Oaṅkār** — the Oneness of God. If one God created all humans, no human is intrinsically higher or lower. The *jāt-pānt* (caste system) dissolves in the shared act of eating.

> *"Gurū kī Laṅgar vich sabbh-e samāṇ-ī / Jāṇā kūṛhā vakh ṭhaṇ-ī"*
> *"In the Guru's kitchen, all are equal / The false distinctions of birth are set aside."*
> — **Bhāī Gurdās**, Vārāṅ

### 2. Humility

Guru after Guru personally served food — Gurū Nānak drawing water, Gurū Aṅgad fetching wood, Gurū Amar Dās washing dishes. **Leadership is service**. The etymological link between *sēvā* and *sikkhī* is intentional — one cannot be a Sikh without serving.

### 3. Rejection of Caste

Laṅgar is the single most effective institutional weapon against caste discrimination in South Asia. By compelling upper-caste, Dalit, Muslim, and Western visitors to eat side by side with food that touches each other's plates, Laṅgar performs a radical social levelling that textual doctrine alone cannot achieve.

### 4. Vaṇḍa Chakō — The Economy of Grace

The kitchen models a gift economy: no one pays, everyone receives, and the community's collective generosity — through *dasvandh*, donations, and volunteer labour — sustains the entire operation. Surplus is shared with the wider community, especially during famine, flood, or crisis.

### 5. The Guru's Table

Laṅgar is understood as **Gurū kī Laṅgar** — the Guru's own table. To eat there is to be a guest of the Guru, transforming a simple meal into an act of spiritual union (*saṅgat*).

## Daily Operations in Gurdwārās Worldwide

### The Daily Cycle

| Time | Operation |
|------|-----------|
| **04:00–07:00** | Volunteers arrive; wash and chop vegetables; *dāl* and *roṭī* preparation begins in large steel cauldrons (*degh*) |
| **08:00–10:30** | First meal after morning prayers |
| **10:30–14:00** | Main lunch surge — up to 4,000–5,000 per sitting in large gurdwārās |
| **16:00–18:00** | Evening *shābād kīrtan* concludes; Laṅgar resumes |
| **18:00–23:00** | Continuous evening service; final deep cleaning |
| **23:00–04:00** | All-night service (*Laṅgar chauṅkī*) at larger gurdwārās on *Guru-purabs* |

### The Volunteers

All labour is voluntary (*sēvā*). Volunteers include local Sikh families on rotating shifts, pilgrims, youth groups, and non-Sikh visitors. Roles include:

- **Sabzī-vālā** — vegetable chopping
- **Roṭī-vālā** — dough kneading and flatbread rolling
- **Dāl-vālā** — lentil cooking in large cauldrons
- **Pauṇṭhī-vālā** — serving — walking the rows with buckets of food
- **Bhāṇḍā-vālā** — dish washing (steel *thālī* and *kāṇi*)
- **Saṛkā-vālā** — floor washing between sittings

### The Meal

A standard Laṅgar meal is simple, nutritious, and universally acceptable:
- **Dāl** (lentil stew) — *māṇh kī dāl* or *moñg dāl* tempered with cumin and ginger
- **Roṭī** (wholewheat flatbread) — rolled fresh in the kitchen
- **Sabzī** (mixed vegetable curry) — seasonal vegetables in light onion-tomato gravy
- **Kheer** (rice pudding) — on special occasions
- **Chā** (mild spiced milk tea) — served at the end

Strict vegetarianism is universal — no meat, fish, eggs, or animal rennet.

## Largest Laṅgar Operations

### Golden Temple (Shrī Harmandir Sāhib), Amritsar

| Statistic | Figure |
|-----------|--------|
| **Daily meals served** | 100,000–150,000 |
| **Peak days (Vaisākhī, Gurpurabs)** | 300,000–500,000 |
| **Daily roṭī production** | ~40,000–50,000 |
| **Daily dāl production** | 8,000–10,000 litres |
| **Permanent volunteers** | 350–400 (rotating) |
| **Annual operating cost** | ~₹180 crore (~USD 22 million) — entirely from donations |

The kitchen operates **24 hours a day, 365 days a year**, never shutting — not during the 1984 Operation Blue Star siege, when volunteers hauled food through gunfire.

### Other Major Langars Worldwide

| Gurdwārā | Location | Daily Meals |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Shrī Harmandir Sāhib** | Amritsar, Punjab | 100,000–150,000 |
| **Gurdwārā Bangla Sāhib** | Delhi | 50,000–60,000 |
| **Gurdwārā Sīs Gañj Sāhib** | Chandni Chowk, Delhi | 30,000–40,000 |
| **Gurdwārā Darbār Sāhib** | Kenosha, WI, USA | 10,000–15,000 |
| **Gurdwārā Rakāb Gañj** | Delhi (near Parliament) | 25,000–30,000 |
| **Gurdwārā Bidāraṇ Sāhib** | Sri Lanka | 5,000–8,000 |

An estimated **25,000–30,000 gurdwārās worldwide** each operate a Laṅgar, collectively serving **over 5 million free meals every day**. During disasters — earthquakes (Nepal 2015, Turkey–Syria 2023), floods (Kerala 2018, Pakistan 2022), the COVID-19 lockdowns — the Sikh Laṅgar network mobilises faster than most government relief agencies.

## Contemporary Challenges

| Challenge | Nature | Response |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Industrialisation** | Pressure to use machine-made *roṭī*; loss of hand-made quality | Most gurdwārās maintain hand-rolled *roṭī* as spiritual practice |
| **Donor fatigue** | Rising ingredient costs (₹50,000/day at Golden Temple) | *Dasvandh* culture; diaspora remittances; non-Sikh donors |
| **Hygiene regulation** | Western food safety laws require professional kitchens | Retrofitting; *sēvādār* training; jurisdictional exemptions |
| **Caste persistence** | Reports of Dalit Sikhs served separately at some rural langars | SGPC and Akāl Takht repeatedly condemn and excommunicate offenders |
| **Pandemic disruptions** | COVID-19 forced shift from sit-down to takeaway/tiffin | Contactless service; home delivery for elderly |
