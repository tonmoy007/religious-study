# ☬ Khālsā (The Pure Ones)

> *"The Khalsa is my very self. In the Khalsa, I live. In the Khalsa, I act. In the Khalsa, I am victorious."*
> — **Gurū Gobind Siṅgh** (attributed tradition)

## Definition

**Khālsā** (ਖ਼ਾਲਸਾ — from Persian *khālis*, "pure, genuine, belonging to the king") is the body of initiated Sikhs who have undergone **Khaṇḍē dī Pāhul** (baptism of the double-edged sword) and commit to the Five Ks, the Rahit (code of conduct), and daily Banī recitation. The Khalsa is simultaneously a spiritual order, a military brotherhood, a sovereign body politic, and — in Sikh theology — the living embodiment of the Guru (*Gurū Panth*).

---

## Etymology

| Term | Origin | Meaning |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Khālsā** | Persian *khālis* / Arabic *khāliṣ* | "Pure, exclusive, the king's own" — in Mughal administration, *khālsa* land was directly under the emperor. Gurū Gobind Siṅgh repurposed it to mean **the Guru's own** — the direct possession of the Eternal One. |
| **Siṅgh** | Skt. *siṃha* | "Lion" — surname adopted by all Khalsa men at Vaisākhī 1699 |
| **Kaur** | Skt. *kumārī* | "Princess" — surname adopted by all Khalsa women |
| **Gurū Panth** | Skt. *patha* + Pers. *panth* | Doctrine: the Guru resides in the collective body of the Khalsa |

## Historical Origins

### Founding Event: Vaisākhī 1699

The Khalsa was created on **30 March 1699** at **Anandpur Sāhib** by Gurū Gobind Siṅgh:

1. **The Five Head-Offerings**: The Guru called for heads. Five men from five castes and regions stepped forward — Dayā Rām (Khatri, Lahore), Dharam Dās (Jat, Hastināpur), Mohkam Chand (Chhīmba/tailor, Dwarka), Sāhib Chand (Nāī/barber, Bidar), Himmat Rāi (Jhīvar/water-carrier, Puri). They became the **Pañj Piāre** (Five Beloved Ones).

2. **The Baptism**: The Guru prepared Amrit in an iron bowl by stirring water with a double-edged sword (*khāṇḍā*) while reciting five Banīs. He administered it to the Pañj Piāre, then **they baptised him** — establishing the radical egalitarian principle that the Guru resides in the collective.

3. **The Proclamation**: The Guru declared **Rāj Karegā Khālsā**, gave the community the Five Ks, the Rehit, the surnames Siṅgh and Kaur, and the doctrine that after him the Guru would reside in the **Gurū Granth** (Scripture) and the **Gurū Panth** (Community).

### Preceding Context

- **1469–1606**: Sikh growth from Gurū Nānak to Gurū Arjan; compilation of the Ādi Granth
- **1606**: Martyrdom of Gurū Arjan — the Panth recognises the need for political self-defence
- **1606–1644**: Gurū Har Gobind's **Mīrī-Pīrī** doctrine (temporal + spiritual authority)
- **1675**: Martyrdom of Gurū Tegh Bahādur — defence of religious freedom becomes a Sikh imperative
- **1675–1699**: Gurū Gobind Siṅgh's fortification of Anandpur, martial poetry, defensive battles

## The Five Ks (*Pañj Kakār*)

Every Khalsa Sikh wears five articles of faith, each beginning with **K** (ਕ) in Gurmukhī:

| K | Article | Purpose |
|---|---------|---------|
| **Keś** (ਕੇਸ) | Uncut hair (bound in *dastār* / turban) | Acceptance of God's will; visible identity; rejection of caste-based tonsure |
| **Kaṅghā** (ਕੰਘਾ) | Small wooden comb in the hair | Cleanliness, order, discipline |
| **Kirpān** (ਕਿਰਪਾਨ) | Ceremonial sword | Defence of the weak; protection of justice; symbol of sovereignty |
| **Kachh** (ਕਛ) | Knee-length undergarment | Modesty, readiness, sexual restraint |
| **Kaṛā** (ਕੜਾ) | Steel bracelet (right wrist) | Bound to the Guru and God; symbol of the Infinite; restraint from evil |

## Initiation: Khaṇḍē dī Pāhul

- **Vessel**: Iron bowl (*sarābloh*) — no clay, brass, or silver
- **Contents**: Water with sugar crystals (*patāse*) — steel + sweetness
- **Five Banīs recited**: Japjī Sāhib (Gurū Nānak), Jāp Sāhib (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh), Tīā Sāhib (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh), Baintī Chaupāī (Gurū Gobind Siṅgh), Anand Sāhib (Gurū Amar Dās) — recited while stirring with the *khāṇḍā*
- **Administration**: Amrit drunk five times from cupped hands; sprinkled on eyes and hair
- **Vows**: Commit to Five Ks, Rehit, rejection of caste, superstition, and idolatry

## The Rahit (Code of Conduct)

### Four Cardinal Prohibitions (*Kurahit*)

1. **Keś-nashī** — Cutting any bodily hair
2. **Halālī** — Eating meat killed by Muslim ritual slaughter (Khalsa eats *jhaṭkā* — single-stroke kill)
3. **Paradārishanī** — Adultery or sexual misconduct
4. **Tambākū** — Use of tobacco or any intoxicant

### Positive Obligations

| Practice | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| ***Amrit-velā*** | Rise before dawn for prayer and meditation |
| ***Nitnem*** | Daily recitation of the five prescribed Banīs |
| ***Sēvā*** | Selfless service — cleaning, cooking, serving in the gurdwārā |
| ***Vaṇḍ chakṇā*** | Sharing earnings; *dasvandh* (one-tenth of income) to the community |
| ***Dharam-yuddh*** | Fight tyranny and injustice, even at cost of one's life |
| ***Sarbat dā bhālā*** | Prayer for the well-being of all humanity |

## The Doctrine of Gurū Panth

The most radical innovation of Vaisākhī 1699: after Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, the Guru resides in two forms:
- **Gurū Granth** — the scripture as eternal spiritual Guru
- **Gurū Panth** — the Khalsa as the living, temporal Guru

Any **five** baptised Sikhs can convene a **Gurmatā** (the Guru's intention) in the presence of the Gurū Granth Sāhib. Majority decisions are binding — establishing **theocracy without a monarch**: sovereignty flows from the scripture through the collective.

---

## Rāj Karegā Khālsā

> **"Rāj karegā Khālsā — ākī rahe na koi. Khwār hovē sabbh milāinge — bachē śrān jō hōi."**
> *"The Khalsa shall rule — no opponent shall remain. Divided they perish — united, the kingdom is theirs."*

Proclaimed at Vaisākhī 1699, this asserts the Khalsa as a **sovereign community** with a divine mandate. The **Nishān Sāhib** (saffron flag bearing the *khaṇḍā*, *chakkar*, and two *kirpāns*) flown over every gurdwārā is the banner of this unrevoked claim.

---

## The Khalsa as Sant-Sipāhī

The Khalsa unites two identities usually kept separate:

| Aspect | Saint (*Sant*) | Soldier (*Sipāhī*) |
|--------|---------------|--------------------|
| **Practice** | Prayer, meditation, scripture | Combat training, readiness to die |
| **Posture** | Humility, submission to God | Courage, assertiveness, defence of justice |
| **Goal** | Liberation (*mukti*) | Righteousness (*dharam*) |

Both are simultaneous requirements: spirituality without self-defence is incomplete; violence without spiritual discipline is barbaric.

## Demographics and Global Distribution

Of the estimated **25–27 million Sikhs** worldwide:

| Region | Approx. Sikh Population | Notes |
|--------|-------------------------|-------|
| **India (Punjab)** | ~16 million | Sikh-majority state (~58%); Khalsa is the dominant institutional form |
| **Rest of India** | ~4–5 million | Delhi, Haryana, Rajasthan, Chandigarh, Maharashtra |
| **Diaspora** | ~3–4 million | Canada (~770K), UK (~520K), USA (~280K), Australia (~125K), Malaysia (~100K), Italy (~70K); smaller in Singapore, Kenya, UAE, Germany, NZ |

Approximately **15–40%** of all Sikhs are estimated to be *amritdhārī* (baptised Khalsa), with proportions varying by region and generation. The Khalsa remains the normative ideal of Sikh identity even for those who have not taken formal initiation.

## Theological Framework

1. **Akal Purakh kī Fauj** — The Army of the Timeless One; God's instrument for establishing righteousness
2. **Gurū kā Bēṭā / Bēṭī** — Every Khalsa is reborn through Amrit into the Guru's own family
3. **Khaḷsā Mērī Jāt̤** — "The Khalsa is my caste"; caste abolished and replaced by Khalsa identity
4. **Sarbat dā Bhālā** — The Khalsa prays for the well-being of all creation, not only Sikhs

## Historical Legacy

- **1708–1716**: Banda Siṅgh Bahādur leads first Khalsa rebellion, establishes short-lived Sikh state
- **1716–1765**: **Misl** period — Khalsa confederacies resist Mughal and Afghan invasions
- **1765–1849**: Maharaja Raṇjīt Siṅgh unites Misls, creates the Sikh Empire
- **1849–1947**: British annexation; the Khalsa survives through the Singh Sabhā revival
- **1947**: Partition — mass displacement; Khalsa becomes central to Sikh political identity
- **1984–present**: Operation Blue Star, Khalistan movement, and globalisation of the Khalsa diaspora
