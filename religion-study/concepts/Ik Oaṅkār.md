# Ik Oaṅkār (ੴ — The One Supreme Reality)

**Ik Oaṅkār** (Gurmukhī: ੴ, also transliterated *Ik Oṅkār* or *Ek Oṅkār*) is the opening phrase of the Mūl Mantra (the foundational creed of Sikhism) and the first two words of the Gurū Granth Sāhib. It means **"One Supreme Reality"** or **"One Primal Sound/Being"** — the assertion of a single, all-pervading, formless God. Ik Oaṅkār is the bedrock of Sikh theology; every hymn, every teaching, and every practice in the Sikh tradition flows from this declaration of divine oneness.

| Key Fact | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Script | Gurmukhī conjunct form: ੴ (Ik + Oaṅkār merged) |
| Transliteration | *Ik Oaṅkār* (preferred), *Ek Oṅkār*, *Ek Ongkār* |
| Meaning | "One Supreme Reality" — *Ik* = one, *Oaṅkār* = the Primal Sound/Manifestation |
| First Appearance | Opening of the Mūl Mantra in the Japjī Sāhib (Gurū Nānak) |
| Usage | First two words of the Gurū Granth Sāhib; recurring throughout Sikh scripture |

---

## Linguistic Analysis: Ik + Oaṅkār

The compound **Ik Oaṅkār** breaks into two elements, each carrying deep semantic weight.

### *Ik* (੧ / ਇਕ)

*Ik* is the Gurmukhī numeral and adjective for **one** — not merely the integer 1 but the philosophical concept of absolute, non-dual unity. It implies:
- **Uniqueness**: God is singular, without second (*advitīya*).
- **Indivisibility**: The divine cannot be divided, partitioned, or multiplied.
- **Exclusivity**: No other being or principle shares God's ultimate status.

Gurū Nānak's choice of *Ik* over the Sanskrit *eka* signals the vernacular, accessible character of Sikh scripture — the theological revolution is couched in common speech.

### *Oaṅkār* (ਓਅੰਕਾਰ)

*Oaṅkār* derives from Sanskrit **oṃkāra** (ॐकार) — the sacred syllable **Oṃ** (प्रणव) + *kār* (a suffix meaning "maker" or "the sound itself"). In the Upaniṣadic tradition, *Oṃ* is the primordial vibration from which the universe emanates, the sound-symbol of Brahman.

However, Gurū Nānak **reconfigures** the term radically:
- He removes the nasalisation of *Oṃ* (the *candrabindu*), producing *Oaṅkār* rather than *Oṅkār* — a pointed departure from Hindu usage.
- *Oaṅkār* retains the sense of "the Primal Sound/Manifestation" but strips it of all association with a personal, incarnate deity or ritualistic chanting.
- The *kār* suffix may also carry the sense of "the one who utters/creates" — thus *Oaṅkār* is **the Self-Existent Being who utters Himself into manifestation**.

> **Scholarly note**: Dr. Ratandeep Singh (and others) argue that *Oaṅkār* should be understood as *Oa* (a locative intensifier) + *Aṅkār* — meaning "the One who pervades the entire creation through His mark/sign" — though this remains a minority view.

## Theological Significance: The Mūl Mantra's Foundation

Ik Oaṅkār is the first word of the **Mūl Mantra** (Root Mantra), which opens the Japjī Sāhib and, by extension, the entire Gurū Granth Sāhib. The full mantra reads:

> **ੴ ਸਤਿ ਨਾਮੁ ਕਰਤਾ ਪੁਰਖੁ ਨਿਰਭਉ ਨਿਰਵੈਰੁ ਅਕਾਲ ਮੂਰਤਿ ਅਜੂਨੀ ਸੈਭੰ ਗੁਰ ਪ੍ਰਸਾਦਿ ॥**
> **Ik Oaṅkār Sat Nām Kartā Purakh Nirbha'u Nirvair Akāl Mūrat Ajūnī Saibhaṅ Gur Prasād.**
> "One Supreme Reality, Truth is the Name, the Creator-Person, without fear, without enmity, the Timeless Being, beyond birth, self-existent — by the Guru's grace."

Every subsequent attribute in the Mūl Mantra is a predicate of **Ik Oaṅkār**. The structure is:

| Element | Meaning | Theological Implication |
|---------|---------|------------------------|
| Ik Oaṅkār | One Supreme Reality | Primary assertion of absolute monotheism |
| Sat Nām | Truth is the Name | God's essence is truth; the divine name *is* the divine reality |
| Kartā Purakh | Creator-Person | God is both transcendent Creator and immanent Person |
| Nirbha'u | Without fear | God is sovereign over all; nothing threatens Him |
| Nirvair | Without enmity | God is utterly impartial, without hatred or favouritism |
| Akāl Mūrat | Timeless Form | God transcends time and death |
| Ajūnī | Beyond birth | God is not subject to reincarnation or embodiment |
| Saibhaṅ | Self-Existent | God is uncaused and self-sustaining |
| Gur Prasād | By the Guru's grace | Knowledge of God comes through divine grace mediated by the Guru |

## Pronunciation Debate: Oaṅkār vs. Oṅkār

A significant orthographic and theological question divides scholars: is the second element **Oaṅkār** (with a clear *a* vowel after the *O*) or **Oṅkār** (with a nasalised *O*, i.e., *Oṃkār*)?

| Position | Transliteration | Basis | Theological Thrust |
|----------|----------------|-------|-------------------|
| **Traditional Sikh** | *Oaṅkār* (ਓਅੰਕਾਰ) | Early manuscripts (including the Kartarpur Bir of 1604) write the conjunct clearly as ੴ, which is *Ik* + *Oaṅkār* | Deliberate departure from Hindu *Oṃ*; asserts a uniquely Sikh concept |
| **Revisionist / Hindu-leaning** | *Oṅkār* / *Oṃkār* | Some later commentators read the symbol as equivalent to *Oṃkār* | Sees continuity with Upaniṣadic *Oṃ*; God as the primal sound |
| **Modern scholarly consensus** | *Oaṅkār* | Paleographic evidence from the Gurū Granth Sāhib's earliest manuscripts shows the full spelling *Oaṅkār* (ਓਅੰਕਾਰ) when written out (not as the conjunct symbol) | Gurū Nānak intentionally modified the term to mark a new theological framework |

The **Gurū Granth Sāhib itself** resolves the ambiguity: when the word appears in full orthography (e.g., in the *vaar* of Bhāī Gurdās or in certain *śabads*), it is consistently spelled **ਓਅੰਕਾਰ** (*O-a-ṅ-kār*), with the *a* vowel clearly present between the *O* and the *ṅ*. This strongly supports the traditional Sikh pronunciation *Oaṅkār*.

## The Unique Gurmukhī Conjunct Form (ੴ)

The symbol **ੴ** is a **conjunct** (a merged ligature) unique to Gurmukhī script, appearing only at the beginning of the Gurū Granth Sāhib and in Sikh iconography. It combines:

- **੧** — the Gurmukhī numeral *Ik* (one)
- **ਓ** — the Gurmukhī letter *Ūṛā* representing the syllable *O* (the start of *Oaṅkār*)

The two characters are fused into a single compound graph: ੧ + ਓ → ੴ. This visual unity is itself a theological statement — the oneness of God is not merely asserted verbally but **inscribed graphically** as a single, inseparable character.

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Unicode | U+0A74 (Gurmukhī Ek Oṅkār) |
| Components | Gurmukhī numeral 1 (੧) + letter Ūṛā (ਓ) |
| Usage | Opening of the Gurū Granth Sāhib; Khālsā flag (Nishān Sāhib); Sikh templates and letterhead; religious art |
| Status | The most universally recognised symbol of Sikhism, equivalent to the Christian cross or the Islamic shahādah in iconic function |

## Ik Oaṅkār and Interfaith Theology

### Relationship to Islamic *Tawḥīd*

The assertion of divine unity in Ik Oaṅkār bears a strong structural resemblance to **tawḥīd** (توحيد), the Islamic doctrine of God's oneness (Qur'an 112: *Qul Huwa Allāhu Aḥad* — "Say: He is God, the One").

| Comparison | Ik Oaṅkār | Tawḥīd |
|------------|-----------|--------|
| Core claim | God is one, unique, without second | God is one (*aḥad*), unique, without partner |
| Anthropomorphism | Rejected — God is *nirbha'u* (fearless) and *nirvair* (without enmity) | Rejected — God is *lā yushbihuhu shay'* (nothing resembles Him) |
| Mediation | Grace comes through the *Guru* (divine guide/word) | Guidance comes through the *Qur'an* and *Rasūl* (prophet) |
| Immanence | God is simultaneously transcendent (*Kartā Purakh*) and immanent (*Sat Nām*) | God is transcendent (*al-ʿAlī*) yet close (*aqrab min ḥabl al-warīd*) |

However, where tawḥīd emphasizes God's **utter transcendence** (*tanzīh*) to the point of apophatic theology, Sikh theology tends toward a **qualified cataphaticism** — God can be known through *Nām* (the Divine Name) and through the Guru's grace. The *Kartā Purakh* ("Creator-Person") element of the Mūl Mantra admits a personal, relational dimension that some Islamic schools (especially Ashʿarī and Sufi) also affirm, but the Sikh emphasis on *śabad* (divine sound/word) as a medium of communion finds a closer parallel in Sufi *samāʿ* (auditory contemplation) and the Quranic *kalām Allāh*.

### Relationship to Hindu *Advaita* and *Bhakti*

The relationship between Ik Oaṅkār and Hindu theology is complex, blending both continuity and rupture.

**With Advaita Vedānta:**
- **Similarity**: Both assert a single ultimate Reality (Brahman / Ik Oaṅkār) that transcends all attributes and is the ground of all being.
- **Difference**: Advaita's Brahman is strictly *nirguṇa* (without qualities) and known through *jñāna* (knowledge); Ik Oaṅkār is both *nirguṇa* (transcendent) and *saguṇa* (immanent as Nām), and known through *nām simran* (meditative remembrance) and *gurprasād* (grace). Sikhism rejects *māyā* as illusion in the Advaitin sense — the world is real, created by God, and a stage for ethical and devotional life.
- **Difference**: Advaita culminates in *mokṣa* as the realisation of identity (*aikya*) between *ātman* and Brahman. Sikhism maintains an eternal distinction between the soul and God; the goal is loving union (*sanjog*), not ontological identity.

**With Bhakti Tradition:**
- **Continuity**: The medieval *sant paramparā* (including Gurū Nānak's contemporaries Kabīr, Nāmdev, and Ravidās) had already challenged ritualism, caste hierarchy, and the exclusive authority of the Vedas. Gurū Nānak stands squarely within this devotional turn.
- **Distinctiveness**: While bhakti saints often directed devotion toward a personal *avatāra* (Kṛṣṇa, Rāma), Gurū Nānak redirects all devotion to the **formless One** (*nirākār*). The *sagunī* (with-form) dimension is not an incarnation but the *Śabad* — the divine Word as revealed through the Guru.

| Comparison | Ik Oaṅkār (Sikh) | Advaita Brahman | Islamic Tawḥīd |
|------------|------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Ultimate Reality | One, self-existent, personal-immanent | One, impersonal, attributeless | One, transcendent, personal |
| Creation | Real, God-willed, ethically significant | Illusory (*māyā*), ultimately unreal | Real, God-created, purposeful |
| Path to Realisation | Nām simran, Gurprasād, Sevā | Jñāna (knowledge), renunciation | *Īmān* (faith), *Ṣalāh* (prayer), *Taṣawwuf* (purification) |
| Goal | Loving union with God (not identity) | Liberation (*mokṣa*) as identity with Brahman | *Jannat* (Paradise) / *Riḍwān* (divine pleasure) |
| Avatāra / Incarnation | Rejected — the Guru is a human channel, not God embodied | Avatāras as manifestations of Brahman; accepted in *saguṇa* bhakti | Rejected — Jesus is a prophet, not God incarnate |

## Ik Oaṅkār in Sikh Practice

Ik Oaṅkār is not a mere doctrinal formula — it permeates every dimension of Sikh life.

| Domain | Expression |
|--------|-----------|
| **Scripture** | Recited daily as part of Japjī Sāhib (morning *nitnem*); visually present at the opening of every *rāga* section in the Gurū Granth Sāhib |
| **Liturgy** | The *ardās* (formal prayer) opens with *Ik Oaṅkār* before the invocation of the Gurū Granth Sāhib |
| **Iconography** | ੴ appears on the Nishān Sāhib (Khālsā flag), on gurdwārā architecture, on Sikh seals and publications |
| **Life-cycle** | Recited at birth, marriage, and death ceremonies — the individual's life is bracketed by the affirmation of divine unity |
| **Daily remembrance** | *Ik Oaṅkār* is chanted or meditated upon as a *mūl mantra* — a seed-sound that encapsulates all theology |

The theological rhythm is one of **return**: the soul, separated from God by *haumai* (ego), contemplates *Ik Oaṅkār* to realign with the primal unity from which it came. In this sense, Ik Oaṅkār functions not only as a creed but as a **soteriological key** — the Name that, when remembered, dissolves illusion and reunites the devotee with the One.

## Comparative Table: Ik Oaṅkār, Tawḥīd, and Advaita Brahman

| Aspect | Ik Oaṅkār | Islamic Tawḥīd | Advaita Brahman |
|--------|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Number** | One (Ik) | One (*aḥad*) | One (*ekam eva advitīyam*) |
| **Positive attributes** | Yes — *Sat, Kartā, Nām* | Yes — *al-Raḥmān, al-Karīm*, etc. | No — *neti neti*; ultimately *nirguṇa* |
| **Personal** | Yes (*Kartā Purakh*) | Yes (Allāh as personal) | No (impersonal) |
| **Creation** | Real; divine expression | Real; divine act | Illusory (*māyā*) |
| **Means of knowledge** | Guru's grace + Nām simran | Revelation (Qur'an) + reason | *Śravaṇa, manana, nididhyāsana* |
| **Soteriology** | Union with God's will (*hukam*) | Submission (*islām*) to God's will | Realisation of identity (*mokṣa*) |
| **Scripture** | Gurū Granth Sāhib (divine Word) | Qur'an (God's literal speech) | Upaniṣads, Brahma Sūtras, Gītā (revealed insight) |
