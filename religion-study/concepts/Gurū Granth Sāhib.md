# Gurū Granth Sāhib (Ādi Granth)

> *"The Word, the Guru, and the congregation are one. Let this be the Guru of the Sikhs — where the Word is, there is the Guru."*
> — **Gurū Arjan**, declaration at the installation of the Ādi Granth, Harimandir Sāhib, 1604 CE

## Overview

The **Gurū Granth Sāhib** (Punjabi: ਗੁਰੂ ਗ੍ਰੰਥ ਸਾਹਿਬ, "the Guru in scripture form") — also known as the **Ādi Granth** (the "first" or "primal" book) — is the central religious scripture of Sikhism. It is not merely a sacred text but is venerated as the **living, eternal Guru** (*śabad-gurū*) of the Sikhs: a personification of divine wisdom whose authority supersedes that of any human teacher. Sikhs regard the Gurū Granth Sāhib as the ultimate spiritual authority, the court (*darbār*) of the Guru, and the direct embodiment of the Divine Word (*śabad*).

## Compilation History

### First Compilation: Gurū Arjan (1601–1604 CE)

The Ādi Granth was compiled by **Gurū Arjan** (1563–1606), the fifth Sikh Guru, between 1601 and 1604 CE at **Amritsar**, in the precincts of the Harimandir Sāhib (the Golden Temple). The urgent need for a definitive canon arose from three crises:

| Crisis | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Spurious compositions** | Poets and rivals composed hymns falsely attributed to the early Gurus |
| **Rival collections** | **Prithi Chand** (Gurū Arjan's elder brother) circulated his own *pothīs* with interpolated hymns |
| **Oral drift** | Hymns changed as singers forgot or embellished exact wordings across the growing Sikh community |

Gurū Arjan, a poet-theologian of remarkable range (author of the *Sukhmani Sāhib*, the *Bārahmāha*, and the *Lāvān* marriage hymns), personally supervised every stage of compilation. He selected hymns through a rigorous process of **recitation, authentication, comparison, and dictation**, with **Bhāī Gurdās** (1551–1637) serving as scribe (*lipikār*). The work was done **publicly** at Harimandir Sāhib, witnessed by the congregation — a liturgical act, not a secret editorial project.

The completed Granth — the **Kartārpurī Bīr** (the original manuscript, now preserved in Kartārpur, Punjab) — was installed at the Harimandir Sāhib in **August 1604** (Bhādon Sudī 1st, Samvat 1661). Gurū Arjan bowed before it, establishing the protocol that the Granth would henceforth be treated as the Guru's living presence.

### Second Expansion: Gurū Gobind Siṅgh (1706–1708 CE)

A century later, **Gurū Gobind Siṅgh** (1666–1708), the tenth Guru, added the hymns of **Gurū Tegh Bahādur** (the ninth Guru, his father) to the Ādi Granth. This expanded version — now called the **Gurū Granth Sāhib** — includes:

- The full contents of the 1604 Ādi Granth
- The 115 hymns and 57 *slokas* of Gurū Tegh Bahādur
- Additional *slokas* by Gurū Gobind Siṅgh (at Damdamī Sāhib, c. 1706, he also dictated the entire existing text to Bhāī Manī Siṅgh, ensuring accurate transmission)

### The Declaration of 1708

In **October 1708**, at **Nanded** (Maharashtra), shortly before his death, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh declared the **end of the human Guru lineage**:

> *"Sab sikkhāṇ kō hukam hai: Gurū māṇiyo Granth."*
> *"All Sikhs are commanded: recognise the Granth as the Guru."*

This was a revolutionary event in world religious history: a living spiritual authority voluntarily transferred all future authority to a scripture, making the Word itself the Guru. From that moment, no human could claim the Guruship; the Gurū Granth Sāhib alone holds that office.

## Physical Description: The 1430 Āṅgs

The standard Gurū Granth Sāhib comprises exactly **1,430 pages** (called *āṅgs* — "limbs" or "pages"). Each page is numbered and printed identically across all editions, allowing universal reference. The text is written in **Gurmukhī** script (literally "from the Guru's mouth"), which Gurū Aṅgad standardized but which the Granth elevated to the sacred script of Sikhism.

A hand-copied Gurū Granth Sāhib (*saroop*) is traditionally written on hand-made paper with black ink and red headings, bound between wooden covers, and wrapped in fine cloths (*rumālās*). No two handwritten *saroops* are identical in calligraphy, but the text must be letter-perfect.

## Internal Structure

The Gurū Granth Sāhib is organized in a sophisticated liturgical architecture:

### 1. Prefatory Section

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Mūl Mantra** | The opening creed: *Ik Oaṅkār* — the foundational statement of God's nature |
| **Japjī Sāhib** | Gurū Nānak's seminal composition, the morning prayer of every Sikh |
| **So Dar** and **So Purakh** | Evening prayers (Rehrās Sāhib) |
| **Sohilā** | Night-time bedtime prayer (Kīrtan Sohilā) |

### 2. The Main Body: 33 Ragas

The heart of the Granth is arranged by **rāga** (musical mode), making it a liturgical songbook for congregational singing (*kīrtan*). The 31 + 2 (combined) rāgas are:

| # | Rāga | # | Rāga | # | Rāga |
|---|------|---|------|---|------|
| 1 | Āsā | 12 | Baḍahaṅsā | 23 | Sūhī |
| 2 | Gūjrī | 13 | Sorathi | 24 | Bilāval |
| 3 | Devagandhārī | 14 | Dhanāsarī | 25 | Gaunḍ |
| 4 | Bihāgarā | 15 | Jaitsrī | 26 | Rāmkālī |
| 5 | Vaḍahaṅs | 16 | Toḍī | 27 | Nata |
| 6 | Mājh | 17 | Bairāṛī | 28 | Mālī Gaura |
| 7 | Gaurī | 18 | Tilaṅg | 29 | Māru |
| 8 | Kedārā | 19 | Sūhī | 30 | Tukhārī |
| 9 | Bhairon | 20 | Bilāval | 31 | Kedārā |
| 10 | Basant | 21 | Gaurī | 32 | Prabhātī |
| 11 | Sārang | 22 | Āsā | 33 | Jaijāvantī |

> Note: The exact ordering and grouping conventions vary slightly between recensions; the principle of rāga-based arrangement is universal.

### 3. Internal Hierarchy within Each Rāga

Within each rāga section, hymns are arranged by:

1. **Author** — in a fixed hierarchy: the Sikh Gurus in chronological order (Nānak, Aṅgad, Amar Dās, Rām Dās, Arjan, Tegh Bahādur), followed by the Bhagats (saint-poets), then Bhātts (bards)
2. **Length** — *Chaupadas* (4-stanza hymns), *Asta-padīs* (8-stanza hymns), then longer compositions
3. **Musical form** — Chhants, Vārs (ballads), and other specific forms

### 4. Closing Sections

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Slokas of Gurū Nānak** | Epigrammatic couplets on key themes |
| **Slokas of Gurū Amar Dās** | Theological distillations |
| **Mundāvaṇī** | "The Seal" — Gurū Arjan's closing hymn declaring the treasury complete |
| **Rāgmālā** | A list of rāgas appended at the end (debated as integral or later addition) |

## Three Major Thematic Sections

Scholars often divide the Gurū Granth Sāhib's contents into three broad thematic streams:

1. **Kīrā (Praise)** — Hymns glorifying the One Nām, the divine attributes, and the Guru's grace
2. **Upadeś (Instruction)** — Ethical teachings, warnings against ego (*haumai*), the Five Evils (*pañj chor*), and the path to liberation
3. **Bisarām (Consolation)** — Hymns of comfort and hope for the suffering soul, especially in the *Sukhmani Sāhib* and the *Bārahmāha* (twelve-month) poems

## Languages of the Gurū Granth Sāhib

The Gurū Granth Sāhib is a **multi-lingual anthology** of extraordinary linguistic range. No single language dominates:

| Language | Description | Usage |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| **Sant Bhāṣā** | A blended literary language of Punjabi, Braj Bhasha, and Khari Boli (western Hindi) | The predominant language — a *sādhukhaṛī* (common speech) accessible across north India |
| **Punjabi** | The local dialect of the Punjab region | Heavy in the Gurus' own hymns, especially Gurū Nānak |
| **Braj Bhasha** | The literary language of the Krishna-bhakti tradition | Common in the hymns of the Bhagats (Kabīr, Nāmdev) |
| **Persian** | The Mughal court language, learned by the Gurus | Gurū Nānak's *Slokas* in Persian; the *Mūl Mantra* exists in a Persian version (*Mūl Mantra* in Persian script is preserved in some manuscripts) |
| **Arabic** | Used sparingly in Sufi-influenced passages | Select phrases from Islamic theology (e.g., *Allāh*, *Rabb*) |
| **Marathi** | The language of Maharashtra | Hymns of Bhagat Nāmdev |
| **Multani (Saraiki)** | The dialect of south-western Punjab | Occasional passages, especially in Bhagat Farīd |
| **Sindhi** | Language of Sindh | Traces in some Bhagat compositions |
| **Awadhi** | Eastern Hindi from Awadh region | Hymns of Bhagat Rāmānand |
| **Sanskrit** | Classical Indian language | Select verses and philosophical terms |
| **Hindi (Khari Boli)** | The emerging lingua franca | Kabīr and Raidās compositions |

This linguistic diversity was intentional: the Gurū Granth Sāhib was designed to be understood by ordinary people across the diverse linguistic landscape of medieval India, not locked in a priestly language.

## The Authors: 6 Gurus and 15 Bhagats

The Gurū Granth Sāhib contains the compositions of **36 authors** in total: 6 Sikh Gurus, 15 Bhagats (saint-poets from Hindu and Muslim backgrounds), and 15 Bhātts (bards) and others.

### The Six Gurus

| Guru | Lifespan | Hymns in Granth | Key Compositions |
|------|----------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Gurū Nānak** (1st) | 1469–1539 | 974 hymns | Japjī Sāhib, Āsā dī Vār, Slokas, Pāṭī |
| **Gurū Aṅgad** (2nd) | 1504–1552 | 62 slokas | Short slokas on humility and service |
| **Gurū Amar Dās** (3rd) | 1479–1574 | 907 hymns | Ānand Sāhib, Slokas, Vārān |
| **Gurū Rām Dās** (4th) | 1534–1581 | 679 hymns | Lāvān (wedding hymns), Vārān |
| **Gurū Arjan** (5th) | 1563–1606 | ~2,218 hymns | Sukhmani Sāhib, Bārahmāha, Mundāvaṇī |
| **Gurū Tegh Bahādur** (9th) | 1621–1675 | 115 hymns, 57 slokas | Saloks, hymns on detachment from the world |
| *Gurū Gobind Siṅgh (10th)* | 1666–1708 | None (his hymns are in the Dasam Granth) | He added Gurū Tegh Bahādur's hymns but did not include his own |

> Note: Gurū Gobind Siṅgh composed the **Dasam Granth** as a separate collection; he deliberately excluded his own works from the Gurū Granth Sāhib, maintaining the Ādi Granth as the primary Guru and keeping his compositions as supplementary.

### The Fifteen Bhagats

The inclusion of **non-Sikh saints** was Gurū Arjan's radical theological innovation: truth is not the property of any single religion. Each Bhagat was vetted for alignment with the core teaching of *Ik Oaṅkār* (One God), *Nām japṇa* (meditation on the Name), and rejection of caste and idolatry.

| Bhagat | Background | Hymns | Key Themes |
|--------|-----------|-------|------------|
| **Kabīr** (d. 1518) | Muslim weaver, Varanasi | ~237 hymns | Rejection of both Hindu and Muslim orthodoxy; inner devotion over ritual |
| **Bābā Farīd** (d. 1265) | Chishtī Sufi, Punjab | ~130 slokas | Sufi longing for the Beloved; humility |
| **Nāmdev** (c. 1270–1350) | Hindu calico-printer, Maharashtra | ~61 hymns | Bhakti through daily work; God in all things |
| **Ravidās (Raidās)** (c. 1450–1520) | Hindu tanner, Varanasi | ~40 hymns | Caste-less devotion; God as the ultimate refuge for the lowly |
| **Trilochan** (c. 13th–14th c.) | Hindu Brahmin, Maharashtra | ~5 hymns | Detachment from worldly bonds |
| **Pīpā** (c. 15th c.) | Hindu Rajput king, Rajasthan | ~1 hymn | Renunciation of kingship for devotion |
| **Benī** (c. 15th c.) | Hindu, unknown region | ~3 hymns | Critique of hypocrisy in both ascetics and householders |
| **Rāmānand** (c. 1400–1476) | Hindu ascetic, Varanasi | ~1 hymn | Teacher of Raidās and Kabīr; devotion transcending caste |
| **Dhannā** (c. 15th c.) | Hindu Jat farmer, Rajasthan | ~3 hymns | Simple farmer's devotion |
| **Sain** (c. 15th c.) | Hindu barber, Rewa | ~1 hymn | Devotion in humble service |
| **Sūrdās** (c. 15th c.) | Hindu blind bard, Braj region | ~1 hymn | (different from the famous *Sūr Sāgar* author) |
| **Parmanand** (c. 15th c.) | Hindu, unknown | ~1 hymn | Devotional praise |
| **Bhīkhan** (c. 16th c.) | Hindu saint, Awadh | ~2 hymns | Renunciation |
| **Jaidayv** (c. 12th c.) | Hindu poet, author of *Gīta Govinda* | ~2 hymns | The earliest author in the Granth |
| **Sundardās** (c. 16th c.) | Hindu ascetic | ~1 hymn | Moral instruction |

> Note: Some lists count 15 Bhagats; the exact number depends on whether Sundardās and a few minor figures are included. The standard enumeration is **15 Bhagats**: Kabīr, Farīd, Nāmdev, Raidās, Trilochan, Benī, Pīpā, Rāmānand, Dhannā, Sain, Sūrdās, Parmanand, Bhīkhan, Jaidayv, and one additional (Sundardās or Mīrābāī in some traditions — though Mīrābāī's inclusion is contested; the standard Gurū Granth Sāhib does not include her).

### The Bhātts, Bards, and Others

| Authors | Number | Role |
|---------|--------|------|
| **Bhātts** (bards) | 11 poets (Kalhār, Salāh, Sādhah, Chhajjū, etc.) | Panegyrics praising the Gurus |
| **Gurū Nānak's attendants** | Various | A few slokas |

## Daily Liturgical Function

The Gurū Granth Sāhib is not a library text studied in private — it is the **living centre of Sikh worship** (*dīvān*), read aloud, sung, and consulted daily in every Gurdwārā.

### Nitnem (Daily Regimen)

| Time | Composition | Source |
|------|------------|--------|
| **Dawn (Amrit Velā)** | Japjī Sāhib, Jaap Sāhib, Tav-Prasād Savaiye (the latter two from the Dasam Granth) | The Sikh's personal morning meditation |
| **Evening** | Rehrās Sāhib (So Dar, So Purakh, hymns) | End-of-day congregational prayer |
| **Bedtime** | Kīrtan Sohilā (Sohilā hymns) | Night-time prayer |

### Akhand Pāṭh

The entire Gurū Granth Sāhib can be read continuously in a non-stop **48-hour recitation** (*Akhand Pāṭh*) by a relay of readers — a common practice at festivals, life-cycle events, and before major decisions. A *Sadhāran Pāṭh* (non-continuous) takes 7–10 days.

### Kīrtan

The dominant Sikh worship form is **kīrtan** — the singing of hymns from the Gurū Granth Sāhib in their prescribed rāgas, accompanied by traditional instruments (*tabla*, *harmonium*, occasionally *rabāb* or *dilrubā*). The medieval Sikh tradition maintained rāga-based singing strictly; modern Gurdwārās often use harmonium and lighter rāgas, though rigorous traditional *kīrtan* survives.

### Hukamnāmā (Daily Command)

A central daily ritual in every Gurdwārā: the Granth is opened at a random page, and the first hymn on the left-hand page is read aloud as the **Hukamnāmā** (the Guru's command for the day). The congregation listens standing, and the verse is treated as the Guru's direct guidance to the community.

## Ritual Reverence: The Guru as Living Presence

The Gurū Granth Sāhib is the only scripture in world religious history that is formally treated as a **living person** — a legal "person" in Sikh polity, enthroned, attended, and honoured with protocols befitting a sovereign.

### Parkash (Opening Ceremony)

Each morning, the Gurū Granth Sāhib is brought out in a ceremonial procession — carried on a head-supported cushion, preceded by a ceremonial whisk (chaurī), and placed on a raised platform (manjī-sāhib / takht) under a canopy (chananī). The *chaurī* is waved over it as a mark of sovereignty. Ardas (formal prayer) is recited, the Granth is opened, and the Hukamnāmā is taken.

### Sukhasan (Closing Ceremony)

Each evening, the Gurū Granth Sāhib is closed after the Kīrtan Sohilā prayer. Ardas is offered, the Granth is wrapped in clean rumālās, and carried on the head to the *sukhasan* (resting room), where it is placed on a bed as if for the night. No one may sit higher than the Granth; the entire congregation sits on the floor.

### Rules of Conduct (Gurū Granth Sāhib)

| Protocol | Practice |
|----------|----------|
| **Head covered** | All persons in the presence of the Granth must cover their heads |
| **No shoes** | Shoes are removed before entering the Guru's presence |
| **Sit on floor** | No chairs or raised seating in the dīvān hall |
| **Prostration** | Sikhs bow and touch their forehead to the floor before the Granth |
| **Chaurī service** | The whisk is waved during kīrtan and reading as an act of honour |
| **No idle talk** | The Guru's court is a space of reverence; unnecessary speech is avoided |
| **Offerings** | Devotees present monetary or material offerings (*dasvandh*) |
| **Menstruating women** | Traditional practice discourages handling the Granth during menstruation (contested in modern Sikh discourse) |
| **Purity** | The reader must bathe before reading; the Granth is never placed on the ground |

## Theological Status as Living Guru

### The Śabad-Gurū Doctrine

The theological foundation of the Gurū Granth Sāhib's authority is the identity between the **Word** (*śabad*) and the **Guru**. Sikh theology holds that:

1. The Guru is not a body but the divine **light** (*jot*) passed from Nānak to his successors, and ultimately into the Word
2. The *śabad* (the divine utterance) is the essential Guru — the human teacher is the vehicle
3. When the Word is gathered into scripture, the scripture IS the Guru

This is formalized in Gurū Arjan's declaration:

> *"Vāhigurū jī kā Khālsā, Vāhigurū jī kī Fateh — Gurū māṇiyo Granth."*

And in the final decree of 1708, the Guru's authority was fully transferred:

> *"Sab sikkhāṇ kō hukam hai: Gurū māṇiyo Granth."*

### Legal and Constitutional Status

In contemporary Sikhism, the Gurū Granth Sāhib:

- Is the **supreme spiritual authority** in all matters of doctrine and worship
- **Consults on all major decisions** through the Hukamnāmā
- Is the **final authority** in the Sikh code of conduct (*Sikh Rehat Maryādā*, 1945)
- Is treated as a **juridical person** — in 2000, the Supreme Court of India recognized the Gurū Granth Sāhib as a "legal entity" for purposes of property and trust (a unique status for a scripture)
- No **human Guru** has existed since 1708; any claimant is declared a heretic (*tankhāhiyā*)
- The **Pañj Piāre** (Five Beloved Ones) and the **Akāl Takht** exercise temporal authority, but only as servants of the Granth

### Comparison with Other Scriptural Traditions

| Aspect | Gurū Granth Sāhib | Qu'ran | Bible | Vedas |
|--------|------------------|--------|-------|-------|
| **Status** | Living Guru (personified) | Revealed word of God | Inspired word of God | Revealed scripture (Śruti) |
| **Authorship** | 36 named human authors (Gurus & Bhagats) | Single divine author (through Prophet) | Multiple human authors, divinely inspired | Rishis (seers), apauruṣeya (impersonal) |
| **Language** | Multi-lingual (10+ languages) | Classical Arabic | Hebrew, Greek, Aramaic | Vedic Sanskrit |
| **Authority** | Supersedes all living leaders | Principal authority (with Hadith) | Principal authority (with Tradition) | Supplemented by smriti |
| **Musical form** | Liturgical songbook (rāga-based) | Chanted recitation | Sung in parts (Psalms) | Chanted in specific meters |

## The Gurū Granth Sāhib in Sikh Identity

The Gurū Granth Sāhib shapes virtually every dimension of Sikh life:

| Dimension | Role of the Gurū Granth Sāhib |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| **Theology** | Every doctrine — Ik Oaṅkār, Nām Simran, Haumai, Hukam, Sevā, Laṅgar — is derived from its hymns |
| **Ethics** | The hymnody teaches the Five Virtues (truth, contentment, compassion, patience, humility) and warns against the Five Evils |
| **Worship** | Kīrtan (congregational singing) is the normative form; no rituals, idols, or pilgrimages are prescribed |
| **Life-cycle** | Birth, marriage, and death ceremonies centre on the Granth: Lāvān (wedding hymns), Akhand Pāṭh (after death) |
| **Community** | Every Gurdwārā is defined by the presence of the Granth; a building without the Granth is not a Gurdwārā |
| **Politics** | The Akāl Takht issues edicts (*hukamnāmās*) in the name of the Guru Granth Sāhib |
| **Culture** | The Gurmukhī script, Punjabi language, and Sikh musical tradition all derive from the Granth |

## Significance

The Gurū Granth Sāhib stands as one of the most remarkable achievements in world religious literature — a **closed canon** compiled by two living Gurus (Gurū Arjan in 1604, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh in 1708) that then **superseded the human Guru lineage entirely**. It is simultaneously:

- A **liturgical songbook** for congregational worship in 33 rāgas
- A **multi-lingual anthology** embracing Hindu, Muslim, and Sikh voices across three centuries
- A **theological treatise** systematizing the core teachings of Sikhism
- A **living Guru** treated with the full protocol of a royal court
- A **constitutional document** that regulates Sikh polity and practice
- A **model of interfaith openness** that includes saints from traditions other than its own

> *"The Guru's Word is the Guru himself — there is no difference between them. The Word is the true guru, the Word is the true teaching."*
> — **Gurū Arjan**, Rāga Mājh, p. 41
