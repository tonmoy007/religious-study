# The Problem of Evil

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → Philosophy & Theology

The strongest argument against classical **theism**: if God is all-powerful,
all-knowing, and all-good, why does evil and suffering exist? Central to the
[[Abrahamic Traditions]]; reframed entirely by [[Dharmic Traditions]].

## The Logical Problem (Epicurus / Mackie)
A claim of **contradiction** among:
1. God is omnipotent.
2. God is omnibenevolent.
3. Evil exists.

> "Is God willing to prevent evil, but not able? Then he is not omnipotent.
> Is he able but not willing? Then he is malevolent." — attributed to Epicurus

**Status**: Largely answered by **Plantinga's Free Will Defense** — it's *possible*
that God couldn't create free creatures capable of moral good without also
permitting the possibility of moral evil. This shows the set is not strictly
contradictory. Most philosophers now agree the *logical* problem is defeated;
the debate has shifted to the *evidential* version.

## The Evidential Problem (Rowe, Hume)
A weaker but tougher claim: even if not contradictory, the *amount* and
*gratuitousness* of suffering make God's existence **improbable**.
- Rowe's example: a fawn trapped in a forest fire, suffering terribly, with no observing consciousness to learn from it — pointless suffering.
- The challenge: what outweighing good could possibly justify *this much*, *this kind* of suffering?

**Status**: Considered the stronger version; no consensus defeat. The main responses are skeptical theism (we can't judge God's reasons) and the greater-good theodicy (soul-making, etc.).

## Types of Evil
- **Moral evil** — caused by free agents (cruelty, war, injustice).
- **Natural evil** — disasters, disease, predation — harder to pin on free will.
- **Animal suffering** — a distinct and acute form: pre-human suffering that cannot be explained by free will or soul-making (since animals lack moral agency). Darwinian evolution entails vast amounts of animal pain across deep time — a challenge to any theodicy.

## The Problem of Hell
A distinct but related problem: if God is omnipotent and omnibenevolent, and
creates souls knowing they will be eternally damned, is that compatible with
perfect goodness?

| Response | Claim |
|---|---|
| **Traditional hell** (eternal conscious torment) | Most difficult to defend; "the problem of hell" is arguably worse than the problem of evil |
| **Annihilationism** (conditional immortality) | The wicked cease to exist — no infinite suffering, but loss of existence |
| **Universal reconciliation** (apocatastasis) | All souls are eventually saved (Origen, von Balthasar, Hart) — no permanent loss |
| **C.S. Lewis's "Locked from inside"** | Hell is the soul's own choice; God respects libertarian freedom to reject good |

The problem of hell is particularly acute because it involves *eternal* suffering
for *finite* wrongdoing — a proportionality problem.

## Theodicies (Defenses of God)
| Theodicy | Core idea | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|---|
| Free Will Defense | Evil is the price of genuine freedom | Explains moral evil well | Doesn't cover natural evil or animal suffering |
| Soul-Making (Irenaeus / Hick) | Suffering builds moral/spiritual character | Empirically plausible for some suffering | Seems to justify horrific excess; doesn't cover animal suffering |
| Augustinian | Evil is privation of good (not a thing); result of the Fall | Metaphysically elegant | Relies on contested theology; doesn't work with evolutionary timeline |
| Skeptical Theism | We can't expect to grasp God's reasons | Logically strong | Risks undercutting all moral knowledge — if we can't tell pointless evil from justified, how can we judge evil at all? |
| Cross & Redemption | God enters into suffering in Christ — not a *solution* but a *solidarity* | Theologically deep; doesn't explain but transforms | Not a *theodicy* in the strict sense (not a justification) — more a pastoral response |
| Process Theodicy | God is not omnipotent; God *persuades* but cannot coerce — evil is metaphysical risk of a free creation | Preserves God's goodness; addresses natural evil | Many find a non-omnipotent God too radical a departure from classical theism |

## God After Auschwitz — The 20th-Century Rupture
The Holocaust poses a unique challenge that broke theodicy language for many:
- **Elie Wiesel**: "Never shall I forget... the smoke... the faces of the children."
- **Hans Jonas**: the Holocaust forces us to reconsider divine omnipotence — God is *suffering, not all-powerful*.
- **Post-Holocaust theology**: some Jewish thinkers (Jonas, Rubenstein) argue that we must either abandon theodicy or abandon the classical God.
- **Christian reckoning**: Moltmann's *The Crucified God* — God is not a distant monarch but a co-sufferer on the cross.

## Non-Theistic Reframings
Each tradition reframes the question so thoroughly that it becomes a *different question*:

| Tradition | Reframing | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Hinduism (karma + rebirth)** | Suffering is the working-out of past actions (*karma*) across multiple lives; no omnipotent God is on trial | The problem dissolves into a cosmic justice system — but the *origin* of karma itself is unexplained |
| **Buddhism** | Suffering (*dukkha*) is the first Noble Truth — the *starting datum*, not a problem about God | The task is *cessation*, not explanation. If no God, no problem of evil |
| **Jainism** | Karmic particles adhere to the soul; strict dualism | Every soul is solely responsible for its own suffering |
| **Dualism** (Manichaean, Zoroastrian) | Good and evil are co-eternal cosmic principles | God's goodness is preserved, but metaphysical dualism raises its own puzzles (who created the two?) |
| **Stoicism** | The cosmos is rational and ordered; suffering is indifferent to virtue | Not an *evil* to be explained but a *test* to be endured |
| **Epicureanism** | The gods are distant and indifferent to human affairs | No problem of evil — the gods are not all-good by hypothesis |

## Why It's Pivotal
This is the hinge between belief and unbelief for many people, and a perfect lens
for comparing how different traditions handle the same brute fact of suffering.
It connects directly to [[Religious Ethics and Meaning]] (how do we find meaning
in suffering?) and to [[Arguments Concerning God]] (it is the strongest argument
*against*).

## Open Questions
- Does skeptical theism actually undercut the evidential problem, or does it make moral knowledge impossible?
- Is the problem of hell logically distinct from the problem of evil, or a special case?
- Does evolutionary biology make the problem of evil *worse* because of the vast animal suffering entailed by natural selection?
- Can a *disaster* be redemptive, or does the attempt to find meaning in it insult the victims?
