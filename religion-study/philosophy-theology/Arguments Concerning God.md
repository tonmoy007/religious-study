# Arguments Concerning God

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → Philosophy & Theology

The classic arguments **for** the existence of God — and their major
**objections**. A neutral survey: the point is to understand the structure of
each argument and where it's vulnerable. See [[Philosophy of Religion]] for the
broader framing and [[Religious Epistemology]] for the epistemic status of these
arguments.

## 1. Cosmological Arguments
**Claim**: The existence/contingency of the universe requires a first cause or
necessary being.
- **Kalam** (al-Ghazali, revived by Craig): whatever begins to exist has a cause; the universe began; therefore it has a cause.
- **Aquinas' Five Ways** (motion, causation, contingency): a chain of dependence needs an unmoved mover.
- **Leibniz**: the Principle of Sufficient Reason — why is there something rather than nothing?
- **Objections**: Why must the cause be God? Why can't the universe itself be the brute fact? (Hume, Russell). The Kalam is also vulnerable to quantum physics (virtual particles may begin uncaused) and the question "what caused the first cause?"

## 2. Teleological (Design) Arguments
**Claim**: Order, complexity, or fine-tuning points to a designer.
- **Paley's watchmaker** — design implies a designer.
- **Fine-tuning** — the cosmic constants (gravitational constant, cosmological constant, etc.) are improbably life-permitting.
- **Objections**: Darwinian evolution explains biological design without a designer (the strongest blow ever struck against natural theology); the multiverse hypothesis as an alternative to fine-tuning; Hume's *Dialogues* (if the universe is like a machine, the designer might be a team of finite, imperfect gods).
- **Anthropic principle**: we observe fine-tuning because we *are here* to observe it — no explanation needed. (Contested: does the anthropic principle explain or explain *away*?)

## 3. Ontological Arguments
**Claim**: God's existence follows from the very concept of God — a purely *a priori* argument.
- **Anselm** (Proslogion): God = "that than which nothing greater can be conceived"; existence in reality is greater than existence in the mind alone; therefore God exists in reality.
- **Plantinga's modal version**: a maximally great being (one that is omnipotent, omniscient, and morally perfect in every possible world) either exists necessarily or is impossible; if such a being is *possibly* necessary, it exists actually.
- **Objections**: Kant — "existence is not a predicate" (it adds no new property to a concept); Gaunilo's "perfect island" parody (the logic would prove any maximally perfect thing); critics challenge whether the concept of a necessary being is coherent.

**Why it survives**: after Kant's critique was thought to be fatal, the 20th-century modal revival (Plantinga, Gödel, Hartshorne) showed the argument in much stronger form — though it remains the most counter-intuitive of the theistic arguments.

## 4. Pascal's Wager — The Pragmatic Argument
Not a proof but a **decision-theoretic** argument for belief:
- If God exists, believers gain infinite reward; atheists suffer infinite loss.
- If God does not exist, believers lose finite pleasure; atheists gain finite pleasure.
- The expected value of belief is infinite; of disbelief, finite/infinite — so *it is rational to believe*.

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "Many gods" problem — which God to wager on? | Refined wagers try to handle this (multi-option decision matrices) |
| We cannot *choose* belief at will | You can put yourself in the way of belief — practice, community |
| Intellectual dishonesty | Pragmatic rationality is still a kind of rationality |

## 5. Moral Arguments
**Claim**: Objective moral values and duties require a transcendent ground.
- **Kant**: morality necessarily leads to the postulates of God, freedom, and immortality (practical reason).
- **Adams**: Divine commands constitute moral obligations — but refined to avoid Euthyphro.
- **Objective values as evidence** (Lewis, Swinburne, Zagzebski): if moral facts exist, theism explains them better than naturalism.
- **Objections**: The **Euthyphro dilemma** — is good commanded because it is good (autonomous morality) or good because commanded (arbitrariness)? Secular ethics (utilitarianism, Kantian deontology, virtue ethics) claim to ground morality without God. See [[Religious Ethics and Meaning]].

## 6. Argument from Religious Experience
- **Swinburne's Principle of Credulity**: if it seems to a subject that X is present, then probably X is present — absent defeaters. Religious experience thus gives *prima facie* evidence.
- **Alston's doxastic practice approach**: the practice of forming beliefs from religious experience is as justified as sense-perception — both are socially established, uneliminable, and yield consistent outputs.
- **James's pragmatic approach**: judge experiences by their fruits (moral transformation, spiritual insight), not their causes.
- **Objections**: Religious experiences are culturally constructed; conflicting experiences across traditions cancel each other out; they are better explained by psychology/neurology (TLE, hallucinogens).
See [[Religious Epistemology]] for deeper treatment.

## Arguments AGAINST / For Atheism
- **The Problem of Evil** — the heavyweight; see [[The Problem of Evil]].
- **Divine hiddenness** (Schellenberg, 1993): a perfectly loving God would ensure that all who are capable of relationship with God are in a position to do so; reasonable non-belief persists; therefore no such God exists.
- **Naturalist parsimony** — science provides increasingly unified explanations *without* invoking God (Occam's razor favors the simpler ontology).
- **Incoherence objections** — the classical divine attributes (omnipotence + omniscience + omnibenevolence + freedom) may be mutually inconsistent.
- **Evolutionary debunking of religion** — religious belief may be a by-product of evolved cognitive mechanisms (agency detection, theory of mind) rather than tracking genuine truth.

## Cumulative Case & Inference to the Best Explanation
Many contemporary philosophers of religion (Swinburne, Plantinga) don't rely on a single argument but on a **cumulative case**: individual arguments are weak on their own, but together they form a coherent web that makes theism a better explanation of the data than naturalism.

| Data to explain | Theistic explanation | Naturalistic explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Existence of universe | A necessary being created it | Brute fact or quantum fluctuation |
| Cosmic fine-tuning | A designer set the constants | Multiverse or chance |
| Objective morality | Grounded in God | Emergent social construct |
| Religious experience | Perception of the divine | Psychological projection |
| Consciousness | Reflective of a mental source | Neural epiphenomenon |

## Summary Table
| Argument | Type | Strength | Main weakness |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cosmological | A posteriori | Intuitive; addresses "why anything?" | Leap to *God* specifically |
| Teleological | A posteriori | Empirical hook (fine-tuning) | Evolution; multiverse |
| Ontological | A priori | Purely logical | Suspicious "magic from definition" |
| Pascal's Wager | Pragmatic | Avoids epistemic stalemate | "Many gods" problem |
| Moral | A posteriori | Grounds objective ethics | Euthyphro dilemma |
| Experience | A posteriori | First-person evidential weight | Conflicting interpretations |

## Open Questions
- Is a cumulative-case theism actually stronger than its weakest link, or is the web only as strong as the best argument?
- Does the fine-tuning argument survive if we take radically different forms of life (carbon-based or not) into account?
- Is the "many gods" objection to Pascal's Wager fatal, or can it be resolved?
