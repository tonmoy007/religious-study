# Philosophy of Religion

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → Philosophy & Theology

The **philosophical examination** of religious concepts, claims, and experience —
using reason and argument rather than appeal to authority. It asks not "what does
tradition X teach?" but "are these claims coherent, justified, true?"

## Core Questions
1. **Does God (or an ultimate reality) exist?** → [[Arguments Concerning God]]
2. **If God is good and powerful, why evil?** → [[The Problem of Evil]]
3. **Can religious claims be known or only believed?** (faith vs. reason) → [[Religious Epistemology]]
4. **What is religious experience, and is it evidence?** (William James, mysticism) → [[Religious Epistemology]]
5. **Is religious language meaningful?** (logical positivism, Wittgenstein)
6. **Do morality and meaning depend on God?** → [[Religious Ethics and Meaning]]

## Faith and Reason — The Central Tension
| Position | Claim | Representative |
|---|---|---|
| Rationalism | Belief needs rational justification | Aquinas, Leibniz, Swinburne |
| Fideism | Faith stands above/independent of reason | Kierkegaard, Tertullian, Barth |
| Evidentialism | Believe only on sufficient evidence | Clifford, Hume, Russell |
| Reformed epistemology | Belief in God can be "properly basic" | Plantinga, Wolterstorff, Alston |

## Religious Language — Can We Speak of God?
A deep meta-question that shadows every other debate:

| School | Claim | Key figure |
|---|---|---|
| **Verificationism** | Religious statements are *cognitively meaningless* — they cannot be verified empirically | Ayer, logical positivists |
| **Falsification** | Religious believers never let anything count against their claims — so the claims say nothing | Flew's "parable of the gardener" |
| **Language-games** | Religious language has its own grammar and criteria of meaning within the form of life; it's *meaningful* but not pseudo-scientific | Wittgenstein (+ Phillips, D.Z.) |
| **Analogy** | We speak of God analogically (not univocally or equivocally) — predicate terms stretch but don't break | Aquinas |
| **Symbol** | Religious language is *symbolic*, not literal — it participates in the reality it points to | Tillich, Ricoeur |
| **Non-cognitivism** | Religious utterances express attitudes, commitments, or emotions rather than making truth-claims | Braithwaite, Hare (blik) |

The stakes: if religious language is meaningless, *all* the other debates collapse. If analogical or symbolic, we need a theory of how such language can be truth-apt.

## Religious Experience — Evidence or Illusion?
A major source of religious belief that raises epistemological questions:

| Thinker | Contribution | Key idea |
|---|---|---|
| **William James** | *The Varieties of Religious Experience* | Pragmatic criterion: experiences judged by their fruits, not their origins |
| **Rudolf Otto** | *The Idea of the Holy* | The *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* — the numinous as a *sui generis* category of experience |
| **W.T. Stace** | *Mysticism and Philosophy* | Distinguishes extrovertive (unity-in-diversity) vs introvertive (pure consciousness) mysticism |
| **Swinburne** | Principle of credulity | If an experience seems veridical, it probably is — absent defeaters |
| **Alston** | *Perceiving God* | Religious experience is a form of perception with its own doxastic practice, as justified as sense-perception |
| **Katz / Constructivists** | Against perennialism | Mystical experience is shaped by the mystic's concepts, language, and tradition — no raw, unmediated experience |

**The challenge**: are these experiences veridical perceptions of the divine, or merely subjective psychological phenomena?

## Religious Pluralism — One Truth or Many?
If multiple traditions make conflicting truth-claims, how many can be "right"?

| Position | Claim | Proponent |
|---|---|---|
| **Exclusivism** | Only one tradition is true; others are false | Traditional orthodoxy (most religions in their classical forms) |
| **Inclusivism** | One tradition is fully true, but others contain partial truth and can save | Karl Rahner's "anonymous Christians" |
| **Pluralism** | Major traditions are different but equally valid responses to the same ultimate Reality | John Hick's "Copernican revolution" — the Real *an sich* vs. as experienced through cultural lenses |
| **Perennialism** | All traditions point to the same transcendental truth — the *philosophia perennis* | Aldous Huxley, Frithjof Schuon |
| **Particularism** | Traditions are incommensurable; no neutral standpoint exists to compare them | Lindbeck's cultural-linguistic model |

**Critique of pluralism** (from exclusivists and particularists): Hick's move blunts the actual historical truth-claims of traditions, reducing doctrines to metaphors.

## Non-Western Philosophy of Religion
The field has historically been Western-centric. Important correctives:

- **Buddhist anti-realism about the self** (anātman) reframes the Western soul/body problem and debates about personal immortality.
- **Advaita Vedānta** (Śaṅkara): the ultimate reality (Brahman) is non-dual, beyond predication — a direct challenge to theistic personalism.
- **Mīmāṃsā**: a rigorously atheistic school of Vedic ritualism that grounds ethics and meaning *without* a creator God.
- **Confucian philosophy of ritual (li)** — questions whether religious practice precedes belief and whether "religion" is even the right category.
- **Islamic philosophy of religion**: al-Ghazālī's critique of causality, Avicenna's contingent/necessary distinction, Ibn Rushd's harmony of faith and reason.
- **Daoist critiques of language**: the *Dao De Jing* opens by declaring the Dao ineffable — a profound reflection on religious language.

For specific traditions, see: [[Dharmic Traditions]], [[Abrahamic Traditions]], [[East Asian and Indigenous Traditions]].

## Methods
- Conceptual analysis, logical argument, thought experiments, phenomenology.
- Increasingly in dialogue with **cognitive science of religion** and **evolutionary psychology**.
- **Feminist philosophy of religion** (Anderson, Jantzen): critiques classical theism as patriarchal and re-centers embodiment, relationality, and practice.

## Open Questions
- Is the term "religion" itself a Western construct that distorts non-Western traditions?
- Can a pluralist consistently hold that all traditions are salvific while also affirming that contradictory truth-claims are real contradictions?
- Does the cognitive science of religion *explain away* religious belief or just explain it?
- Is religious language ultimately metaphorical, or can it be genuinely referential?
