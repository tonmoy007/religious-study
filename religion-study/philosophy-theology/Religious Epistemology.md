# Religious Epistemology

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → Philosophy & Theology

The branch of philosophy that asks: **Can religious belief be rationally justified? Under what conditions is it knowledge rather than mere opinion or wishful thinking?** This note sits between [[Philosophy of Religion]] (the field overview) and [[Arguments Concerning God]] (specific arguments) — it focuses on the *epistemic status* of religious claims.

## The Central Questions
1. Is religious belief **rational**? What does rationality even mean here?
2. Is it **knowledge** (justified true belief) or something else (faith, trust, commitment)?
3. Does religious belief need **evidence** — or can it be "properly basic"?
4. Should the **risk of error** deter belief, or is the **risk of missing truth** equally important?

## Faith, Belief, and Knowledge — Key Distinctions

| Term | Meaning | Contrast |
|---|---|---|
| **Propositional faith** | Belief *that* God exists | Ordinary belief — can be true or false |
| **Fiducial faith** | Trust *in* God | Relational — more like trusting a person than believing a proposition |
| **Doxastic** | Related to *doxa* (belief) | The standard epistemic category |
| **Knowledge (*epistēmē*)** | Justified true belief (classically) | Requires truth + justification + belief |
| **Assent** | Mental agreement to a proposition | May be weaker than full belief |

## The Great Evidentialist Challenge

### Clifford's Principle (1877)
> "It is wrong always, everywhere, and for anyone, to believe anything upon insufficient evidence."

**Implication**: religious belief without sufficient evidence is *morally* wrong — we have a duty to proportion belief to evidence.

**Response (James, "The Will to Believe", 1896)**: Clifford's principle is self-defeating when:
1. The question is *forced* (cannot be avoided by suspending judgment — avoiding belief is itself a decision).
2. The question is *live* (genuinely meaningful options).
3. The question is *momentous* (the stakes are high).

In such cases, James argues, we have the right to choose belief on *passional* grounds — not because evidence compels us, but because the risk of *missing truth* by excessive caution may be worse than the risk of error.

## Reformed Epistemology — Belief Without Evidence

The most significant 20th-century challenge to evidentialism. **Key claim**: belief in God can be "properly basic" — justified without inferential evidence, just like belief in other minds, the external world, or the past.

### Plantinga's Project (1983–2000)
- Warranted Christian Belief (trilogy): argues that Christian belief is *warranted* (not just justified) if it is produced by properly functioning cognitive faculties aimed at truth.
- **The *sensus divinitatis***: Calvin's notion that humans have an inbuilt sense of the divine — a faculty that, when functioning properly, produces belief in God in appropriate circumstances (the starry sky, a moral conviction, a sense of guilt).
- If the *sensus divinitatis* is part of our original design plan, and it is damaged by sin (Calvin) but partially repaired by grace, then theistic belief can be *warranted* even without arguments.

### Objections to Reformed Epistemology
| Objection | Reply |
|---|---|
| **Great Pumpkin objection**: by this logic, any belief (including absurd ones) could be "properly basic" | Proper basicality has criteria: it must be unavoidable, universally present in some form, and not controverted by evidence |
| **It doesn't convince non-believers** | Reformed epistemology is a *defense*, not a *proof* — it aims to show that theistic belief is *epistemically permissible*, not mandatory |
| **It insulates belief from criticism** | Basic beliefs can still be defeated by counter-evidence; they are not incorrigible |

## Religious Experience as Evidence

Can a subjective experience constitute evidence for God? This bridges [[Philosophy of Religion]] and epistemology proper.

### Swinburne's Principle of Credulity
> "If it seems to a subject that X is present, then probably X is present — absent defeaters."

This is a principle we accept in ordinary perception. Why not in religious experience? If someone seems to be conscious of God, we should take it *prima facie* as genuine.

### Alston's Perceptual Model (Perceiving God, 1991)
- Religious experience functions as a **doxastic practice** (a socially established way of forming beliefs) — analogous to sense-perception.
- Sense-perception cannot be *non-circularly* justified either; we accept it because it is practically unavoidable, socially shared, and yields consistent outputs.
- The Christian mystical practice (CMP) has the same epistemic status as sense-perception: it is rational to engage in it unless it is shown to be unreliable.

### Defeaters

| Defeater | Response |
|---|---|
| **Conflicting experiences** (different traditions report incompatible truths) | Alston: the practice is *Christian* mystical practice — other practices are separate doxastic practices, assessed on their own terms |
| **Psychological explanation** (religious experience as wish-fulfillment, temporal lobe epilepsy) | Swinburne: the origin of a belief doesn't determine its truth; God could work through natural processes |
| **Cultural construction** (mystics experience what their tradition trains them to) | Alston: this is true of all perception — concepts shape *what* we perceive, but don't prove the perception is of nothing real |

## The Problem of Disagreement

Religious disagreement is *pervasive* and *persistent*. What epistemic implications does this have?

| Position | Claim |
|---|---|
| **Conciliationism** | When epistemic peers disagree, each should reduce confidence in their own view (Feldman, Christensen) — religious believers should become agnostic |
| **Steadfast view** | You can reasonably maintain your belief even in the face of disagreement if you have independent reason to think the other side is mistaken (Plantinga, van Inwagen) |
| **Total evidence view** | The disagreement itself is evidence that must be weighed alongside the original evidence for belief |

**The challenge for theism**: given that reflective, intelligent, well-informed people disagree about God, can either side claim *knowledge* or just *faith*?

## Feminist and Social Epistemology of Religion
Standard epistemology tends to assume an isolated rational knower. Critiques:

- **Relational knowing**: religious knowledge is often *communal* and *testimonial* — we receive it from a tradition, not by private reasoning.
- **Standpoint theory**: one's social location shapes what one can know about God — marginalized perspectives may see aspects the powerful miss (e.g., liberation theology's "preferential option for the poor" as an epistemic principle).
- **Idolatry critique** (Jantzen, Daly): classical theistic epistemology projects male power onto God — a genuinely liberatory religious epistemology must be embodied, relational, and aware of its own biases.

## Table: Mapping the Major Positions

| Position | Core claim | Rational to believe? | Representative |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Classical evidentialism** | Only with sufficient evidence | Depends on evidence | Clifford, Russell |
| **Pragmatism** | Right to believe on passional grounds when evidence runs out | Yes, if the option is live, forced, momentous | James |
| **Reformed epistemology** | Belief can be properly basic | Yes, without evidence | Plantinga, Wolterstorff |
| **Fideism** | Faith transcends/opposes reason | Yes, but *not* because of reason | Kierkegaard, Barth |
| **Wittgensteinian fideism** | Religious language has its own grammar | Yes, within its form of life | Phillips, D.Z. |
| **Skepticism** | Religious belief cannot be justified | No — or suspend judgment | Hume (arguably), new atheists |
| **Critical rationalism** | Religious claims can be tested | Yes, but some may be falsified | Swinburne (Bayesian) |

## Open Questions
- Is Reformed Epistemology a genuine defense of religious belief or a sophisticated way to insulate it from criticism?
- Does the problem of peer disagreement make religious knowledge impossible, or can it be overcome?
- Is "faith" a virtue (trusting commitment) or a vice (believing without evidence)?
- Can the Principle of Credulity distinguish between genuine religious experience and hallucination, or does it prove too much?
