# Religious Ethics and Meaning

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → Philosophy & Theology

How religions ground **morality**, the **good life**, and **ultimate meaning** —
and whether any of these actually require God. Connects the conceptual work of
[[Philosophy of Religion]] to lived practice across traditions.

## Does Morality Need God?
| Position | Claim | Proponents | Challenge |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Divine Command Theory** | Right actions = those commanded by God | Duns Scotus, Ockham, Adams | Euthyphro dilemma |
| **Natural Law** | Morality is built into human nature, discoverable by reason | Aquinas, Aristotle (secular root) | Does "nature" really prescribe? Is/ought gap |
| **Reformed Epistemology of Ethics** | Moral knowledge is *sensus divinitatis* — we know God through conscience | Calvin, Plantinga | Doesn't convince non-theists |
| **Secular ethics** | Ethics grounded in reason, well-being, social contract, or evolution | Kant, Mill, Singer, Hume | Can it explain *objective* moral obligations? |

### The Euthyphro Dilemma — The Classic Challenge
> "Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods?" — Plato

- **Horn 1** (Divine command makes it right): morality becomes arbitrary — if God commanded cruelty, it would be good.
- **Horn 2** (God commands it because it's right): goodness exists independently of God — so God is not the source.

**Responses**:
- **Modified divine command** (Adams): God's necessary goodness means God *wouldn't* command cruelty — the commands are constrained by God's character.
- **Natural law** (Aquinas): sidesteps the dilemma — God created human nature with an objective telos, and reason discerns the good in it.
- **Theistic grounding** (Zagzebski, Swinburne): objective moral facts exist *because* they are grounded in God's necessary nature — they are neither arbitrary nor independent.

## Visions of the Good Life Across Traditions
| Tradition | Highest good | Cardinal virtue(s) | Path |
|---|---|---|---|
| Christianity | Union with God, love (*agape*) | Faith, hope, love (theological); prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance (cardinal) | Grace, sacraments, discipleship |
| Islam | Submission (*islām*), justice, paradise | Taqwā (God-consciousness), adl (justice), sabr (patience) | Five Pillars, righteous deeds, *jihād al-nafs* (struggle against the self) |
| Judaism | Covenant faithfulness, *tikkun olam* | Tzedakah (justice/charity), chesed (loving-kindness) | Torah study, mitzvot, prayer |
| Buddhism | Liberation (*nirvāṇa*) from suffering | Wisdom (*prajñā*), compassion (*karuṇā*), ethical conduct (*śīla*) | Eightfold Path, meditation, *bodhicitta* |
| Hinduism | *Moksha* (liberation); fulfilling *dharma* | Satya (truth), ahimsa (non-violence), dharma (duty) | Bhakti (devotion), jñāna (knowledge), karma (action) yoga |
| Confucianism | Social harmony; virtue (*ren*) | Rén (benevolence), yì (righteousness), lǐ (ritual propriety) | Self-cultivation, filial piety, rectification of names |
| Daoism | Harmony with the Dao | Wu-wei (effortless action), spontaneity, simplicity | Non-striving, aligning with nature |
| Aristotelian (secular cousin) | Flourishing (*eudaimonia*) | Wisdom, courage, temperance, justice | Virtue practice, practical reason |

## Sin, Vice, and the Shadow Side
Traditions don't just define the good — they diagnose what *blocks* it:

| Tradition | Diagnosis of evil | Remedy |
|---|---|---|
| Christianity | Sin: rebellion, pride, concupiscence | Grace, repentance, atonement |
| Islam | *Dhulm* (wrongdoing), forgetfulness (*ghafla*) | Remembrance (*dhikr*), submission |
| Buddhism | The Three Poisons: greed, hatred, ignorance | The Eightfold Path — wisdom, ethics, meditation |
| Hinduism | *Avidyā* (ignorance of one's true nature) | *Jñāna* (knowledge) and *bhakti* (devotion) |
| Confucianism | Loss of *ren* — failure of self-cultivation | Ritual (*lǐ*), rectification, education |

## Religious Existentialism — Meaning in the Margins
A distinct current within religious ethics that emphasizes **subjectivity, choice, and crisis**:

| Thinker | Key idea |
|---|---|
| **Kierkegaard** | The "leap of faith" — authentic religious existence requires a passionate, irrational commitment that transcends the ethical (the teleological suspension of the ethical in *Fear and Trembling*) |
| **Dostoevsky** | "If there is no God, everything is permitted" — but also the radical freedom of the *Brothers Karamazov* |
| **Paul Tillich** | "Ultimate concern" — religion is not a belief in gods but the state of being grasped by what matters unconditionally; courage to be despite anxiety |
| **Martin Buber** | The *I-Thou* relationship — meeting God in the encounter with other persons |
| **Gabriel Marcel** | The distinction between *problem* (to be solved) and *mystery* (to be participated in) — religious meaning is not a puzzle we crack but a relation we enter |
| **Simone Weil** | Affliction (*malheur*) as the site of encounter with God; attention as the form of love |

## Comparative Bioethics — Religion Meets Modernity
Religions increasingly engage with moral questions that ancient texts could not have anticipated:

| Issue | Christian (varies) | Islamic | Jewish | Buddhist |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Abortion** | "Sanctity of life" — generally opposed (some exceptions) | Ensoulment at 120 days — graded prohibition | Not murder but significant; permitted for maternal health | Against killing; intent matters — varies by tradition |
| **Euthanasia** | Generally opposed (sanctity / suffering has meaning) | Forbidden — life belongs to God | Prohibited, but passive withdrawal debated | Intent and compassion — active euthanasia problematic |
| **Genetic engineering** | Permitted for therapy; concerns about enhancement | Permitted for therapy; *tawakkul* (trust in God) considered | Permitted for healing; ethical limits debated | Permitted if reduces suffering; intention matters |

## Meaning and Mortality
- Religions characteristically answer: *Why am I here? What happens at death? What is my place in the cosmos?*
- **Salvation / liberation** narratives give suffering and death a frame (see [[The Problem of Evil]]).
- Secular and existentialist alternatives (Camus — Sisyphus as hero; Frankl — meaning through suffering and work) ask whether meaning can be **made** rather than received.
- **The question of absurdity** (Nagel, Camus): is the religious answer a flight from the absurd, or a genuine encounter with it?
- **Viktor Frankl** (*Man's Search for Meaning*): meaning can be found in every situation — even suffering — through attitude, love, and work.

## Shared Ethical Core?
Many traditions converge on a **"Golden Rule"** (reciprocity) and on virtues like
compassion, honesty, and humility — a key data point for [[Connections Between Religious Texts]]
and for debates about moral universals.

| Tradition | Version of the Golden Rule |
|---|---|
| Christianity | "Do unto others as you would have them do unto you" (Matthew 7:12) |
| Judaism | "What is hateful to you, do not do to your neighbour" (Hillel / Talmud) |
| Islam | "None of you has faith until he loves for his brother what he loves for himself" (Hadith) |
| Buddhism | "Hurt not others in ways that you yourself would find hurtful" (Udānavarga 5:18) |
| Hinduism | "This is the sum of duty: do nothing to others which would cause you pain if done to you" (Mahābhārata) |
| Confucianism | "Do not do to others what you do not want done to yourself" (Analects 15:24) |

Whether these represent a universal moral grammar or merely convergent evolution is a live debate.

## Open Questions
- Does the Euthyphro dilemma actually refute divine command theory, or does the modified version (grounded in God's nature) escape it?
- Is the Golden Rule evidence for a common moral core, or is it vague enough to accommodate very different ethical systems?
- Can meaning be *made* (existentialist) or must it be *received* (religious)? Or both?
- Is religious existentialism a coherent third way between dogmatic orthodoxy and secular atheism?
