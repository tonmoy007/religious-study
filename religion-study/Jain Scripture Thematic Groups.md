# Jain Scripture Thematic Groups

A reference guide to the major thematic groups that run through the corpus of Jain scripture — the Śvetāmbara and Digambara Āgamas (compiled c. 5th–6th c. CE, though orally transmitted from Mahāvīra's time, c. 6th–5th c. BCE), the Tattvārtha Sūtra (c. 2nd–5th c. CE, the only text accepted by all Jain sects), and the post-canonical classics (c. 1st–13th c. CE). Each group links texts that share a common subject, doctrinal theme, or narrative pattern. Use this note to navigate Jain scripture by theme and to see how different texts — separated by centuries, sectarian affiliation, and language — engage the same questions.

> [!note] How the groups were built
> The major scriptures of the Jain tradition (the Śvetāmbara and Digambara Āgamas, the Tattvārtha Sūtra, and the post-canonical Sanskrit, Prakrit, and Apabhraṃśa classics) were grouped by their dominant doctrinal themes, narrative function, and literary genre. Many texts belong to multiple groups — for example, the Ācārāṅga Sūtra is simultaneously a monastic discipline text, a non-violence treatise, a source for Mahāvīra's biography, and a meditation manual. The groups below consolidate these into navigable themes. Cross-references to the [[Jain Stories MOC]] and [[Jain Figures MOC]] link thematic groups to narrative and figure traditions. Because the Jain canon differs between the Śvetāmbara and Digambara traditions, texts specific to one school are noted; the Tattvārtha Sūtra, accepted by both, appears in nearly every group.

## 1. The Life of Mahāvīra

The biographical narrative of Vardhamāna Mahāvīra — the 24th Tīrthaṅkara — from his celestial existence in the Puṣpottara heaven, through his descent into the womb of Queen Triśalā, his birth, renunciation, twelve years of asceticism, kevala (omniscience), forty-year teaching ministry, and final liberation (nirvāṇa). No single text tells the complete story; it is assembled from Āgama passages, late Śvetāmbara biographies, and the Digambara purāṇic tradition.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** (Book 2, Śvetāmbara) | The earliest biographical material: Mahāvīra's renunciation, his ascetic ordeals, his encounters with hostile villagers and animals — raw, unsentimental, archaic |
| **Kalpa Sūtra** (Bhadrabāhu, Śvetāmbara) | The classic Śvetāmbara biography: the five auspicious events (*kalyāṇakas*) — conception, birth, renunciation, omniscience, liberation — with the famous account of Mahāvīra's birth dreams |
| **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (Hemacandra, 12th c.) | The most comprehensive biography: Mahāvīra's previous births, his life in full narrative detail, the establishment of the fourfold Saṅgha, and the gradual spread of the Jain community |
| **Ādīpurāṇa** (Jinasena, 9th c., Digambara) | The Digambara account of the first Tīrthaṅkara (Ādinātha), with Mahāvīra's life contextualised within the broader cycle of Tīrthaṅkara biographies — the source for the Digambara version |
| **Mahāvīracaritra** (Gunabhadra, 9th c., Digambara) | A dedicated Digambara biography of Mahāvīra, covering the events of his life from a Digambara perspective, including his omniscience and liberation |
| **Bhagavatī Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 5) | Numerous dialogues between Mahāvīra and his disciples (especially Gautama) that embed biographical fragments — his family, his contemporary teachers, his travels |
| **Jñātādharmakathāḥ** (Śvetāmbara Upāṅga 2) | Stories featuring Mahāvīra as interlocutor: biographical episodes interwoven with moral narratives |

**Core texts:** Ācārāṅga Sūtra Book 2 (the earliest stratum), Kalpa Sūtra (the classic Śvetāmbara version), Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra (the fullest narrative)

**See also:** [[Jain Stories MOC]] → The Birth of Mahāvīra, The Great Renunciation, The Twelve Years of Asceticism, The Attainment of Kevala; [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Mahāvīra, Gautama Indrabhūti, Triśalā

## 2. The Lives of the Tīrthaṅkaras

The biographies of the twenty-four Tīrthaṅkaras — the ford-makers who revive the Jain faith in each descending half-cycle of time (*avasarpinī*). From Ṛṣabhanātha (Ādinātha), who founded the Jain tradition at the beginning of the present epoch, to Mahāvīra, the most recent. The Tīrthaṅkaras are born as humans, attain omniscience through asceticism, and teach the eternal path of liberation.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Kalpa Sūtra** (Bhadrabāhu, Śvetāmbara) | Biographies of all 24 Tīrthaṅkaras: each follows the same five *kalyāṇaka* pattern — a formulaic structure that establishes the typology of a Tīrthaṅkara's life |
| **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (Hemacandra) | The lives of all 24 Tīrthaṅkaras interwoven with the biographies of the other 63 Śalākāpuruṣas — the definitive Śvetāmbara compendium |
| **Mahāpurāṇa** (Jinasena, 9th c., Digambara) | The Digambara purāṇa covering the lives of the first Tīrthaṅkara (Ādinātha) in Book 1; completed by Gunabhadra as the Uttarapurāṇa covering Tīrthaṅkaras 2–24 |
| **Ādīpurāṇa** (Jinasena) | The life of Ṛṣabhanātha (Ādinātha), the first Tīrthaṅkara — his establishment of society, kingship, agriculture, and the arts; the Digambara model of the Tīrthaṅkara as civilising hero |
| **Uttarapurāṇa** (Gunabhadra) | The continuation of Jinasena's Mahāpurāṇa: Tīrthaṅkaras Ajita through Mahāvīra, plus the other Śalākāpuruṣas |
| **Sāmācārī Śataka** (Śvetāmbara) | A Prakrit text cataloguing the key events, symbols, and attributes of each Tīrthaṅkara — used in ritual and iconographic contexts |
| **Tiloyapaṇṇattī** (Yativṛṣabha, Digambara) | Cosmography with Tīrthaṅkara geography: where each was born, where they attained omniscience and liberation, embedded in cosmic description |

**Core texts:** Kalpa Sūtra (Śvetāmbara), Mahāpurāṇa/Uttarapurāṇa (Digambara), Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra (the unified tradition)

**See also:** [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Ṛṣabhanātha, Pārśvanātha, Mahāvīra, The 24 Tīrthaṅkaras; [[Jain Stories MOC]] → The Tīrthaṅkara Cycle

## 3. Non-Violence (Ahiṃsā)

The cardinal virtue of Jainism — *ahiṃsā paramo dharmaḥ* (non-violence is the supreme dharma). Jain ahiṃsā is the most rigorous non-violence doctrine in any Indian tradition: it encompasses not only killing but also harming, in thought, word, or deed, any living being — from five-sense beings to the single-sense *ekendriya* beings (earth, water, fire, air bodies, and plants). This group gathers the texts that define, justify, and apply ahiṃsā across all spheres of life.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** (Book 1, Śvetāmbara) | The foundational Jain scripture on ahiṃsā: "All beings desire to live, none wish to die" — the first systematic articulation of non-violence as the core of the ascetic path; detailed rules against harming earth-bodies, water-bodies, fire-bodies, air-bodies, and plants |
| **Sūtrakṛtāṅga** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 2) | Defends ahiṃsā against competing traditions — especially the Vedic sacrificial tradition and the violence inherent in Brahminical ritual — arguing that animal sacrifice is the greatest evil |
| **Daśavaikālika Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara Mūla Sūtra) | A concise manual of ascetic conduct: "Injuring living beings is the root of all evil" — practical instructions for walking, sitting, eating, and speaking without harming any living being |
| **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (ch. 7, Umāsvāti/Umāsvāmin) | The theoretical framework: ahiṃsā as the first and foremost *vrata* (vow), the hierarchy of living beings (*jīvasthāna*), the states of mind (*bhāva*) that turn a physical act into a violent one or a non-violent one |
| **Yogaśāstra** (Hemacandra, 12th c.) | The layperson's comprehensive guide: ahiṃsā explained for householders as a graded practice — the *aṇuvrata* (small vow) version of non-violence adapted for worldly life |
| **Upāsakadaśāḥ** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 7) | Stories of eleven exemplary laymen (*śrāvakas*) who practised ahiṃsā in their domestic and professional lives — including the famous layman Ānanda, a wealthy merchant who transformed his business to avoid harm |
| **Santipada or "Papaṅkuddāhara" texts** (Digambara) | Digambara śrāvakācāra literature on ahiṃsā: how a layperson can avoid the five types of violence (intentional, protective, occupational, preparatory, and incidental) |

**Core texts:** Ācārāṅga Sūtra Book 1 (foundational), Tattvārtha Sūtra ch. 7 (theoretical), Daśavaikālika Sūtra (practical)

**See also:** [[Jain Stories MOC]] → Ahiṃsā Narratives; [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Ahiṃsā in Buddhist and Hindu Traditions

## 4. Karma Theory

The Jain theory of karma is unique among Indian traditions: karma is not a metaphysical principle of moral causation but a *material* substance — subtle particles of matter (*karma-pudgala*) that flow into the soul (*āsrava*) due to passionate activity, bind to it (*bandha*), and colour it (*leśyā*). The goal is to stop the influx, shed accumulated karma, and achieve total purity. Jain karma theory is the most elaborate and mechanistic in all of Indian philosophy.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (ch. 8, Umāsvāti/Umāsvāmin) | The definitive canonical account: the eight types of karma (four *ghātiyā* — obstructive — and four *aghātiyā* — non-obstructive), the 148 sub-types, the processes of *āsrava* (influx), *bandha* (bondage), *saṃvara* (stoppage), *nirjarā* (shedding) |
| **Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama** (Puṣpadanta & Bhūtabali, Digambara, c. 2nd c. CE) | The "Scripture in Six Parts" — the oldest Digambara Āgama text, devoted entirely to karma theory. The *karma-prakṛti* section catalogues every variety of karma with its duration, intensity, and fruition |
| **Kaṣāya Pāhuda** (Guṇadhara, Digambara) | "The Treatise on Passions" — the companion to Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama: how the four passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed) drive karma bondage; the 25 constituent factors of passion (*kaṣāya*) |
| **Karmagrantha** (Śvetāmbara, c. 6th–7th c.) | Six treatises systematising Śvetāmbara karma theory: the nature of karmic matter, the bonding process, the temporal duration of each karmic type, the geography of karmic fruition |
| **Pañcāstikāya** (Kundakunda, Digambara, c. 1st–2nd c. CE) | The five cosmic substances — soul, matter, space, time, and the principles of motion and rest — karma as the relationship between soul and matter; a crucial bridge between ontology and karma theory |
| **Gommaṭasāra** (Karmakāṇḍa, Nemicandra, Digambara, 10th c.) | The definitive Digambara karma compendium: the mechanics of bondage and liberation in verse, with auto-commentary — used by both Digambara and Śvetāmbara scholars |
| **Karmaprakṛti** (Śvetāmbara) | A Prakrit text listing all 148 sub-types of karma with their characteristics — a foundational reference text for Śvetāmbara karma study |
| **Pravacanasāra** (Kundakunda) | The interaction of karma with the soul from the *niścaya* (transcendental) perspective: ultimately karma is a *mithyātva* (wrong belief) phenomenon that dissolves when right knowledge dawns |

**Core texts:** Tattvārtha Sūtra ch. 8 (the accessible introduction), Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama (the Digambara foundation), Karmagrantha (the Śvetāmbara systematisation)

**See also:** [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Umāsvāti, Kundakunda, Nemicandra; [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Karma in Hinduism and Buddhism

## 5. The Nature of the Soul (Jīva)

The Jain concept of the soul (*jīva*) is the cornerstone of the entire system: an eternal, conscious, immaterial substance that is intrinsically perfect but bound by karma. The soul is characterised by *cetanā* (consciousness), *upayoga* (cognitive activity), *amūrtatva* (formlessness), and *pramāṇa* (extension co-extensive with the body it occupies). Liberation (*mokṣa*) is the realisation of the soul's innate purity.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (ch. 2, Umāsvāti) | The classic definition: the soul is distinguished from non-soul (*ajīva*) by consciousness; the qualities of the soul — infinite knowledge, infinite perception, infinite bliss, and infinite energy in its pure state; the 14 *guṇasthānas* as stages of the soul's purification |
| **Samayasāra** (Kundakunda, Digambara) | "The Essence of the Self" — the single most important Jain text on the nature of the soul from the transcendental (*niścaya*) perspective: "I am pure consciousness, distinct from the body, mind, and karma"; the soul's intrinsic purity (Kundakunda's *śuddhātman*); the distinction between the conventional (*vyavahāra*) and real (*niścaya*) self |
| **Pañcāstikāya** (Kundakunda) | The five cosmic substances, with the soul as the first; the soul's spatial extension, its relationship with karma, and its embodiment in different realms of existence |
| **Pravacanasāra** (Kundakunda) | The essence of the teaching: the soul's innate omniscience obscured by karma; the means by which the soul realises its own nature — right faith, right knowledge, right conduct |
| **Niyamasāra** (Kundakunda) | "The Essence of the Doctrine": the soul's own nature as the final authority; the path of self-realisation as a return to the soul's intrinsic nature; the threefold path (*ratnatraya*) as the soul's self-actualisation |
| **Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama** (Jīvasthāna section) | The Digambara classification of 14 *jīvasthānas* (types of souls): from the one-sense beings (earth, water, fire, air, plants) through five-sense beings, to liberated souls (*siddhas*) |
| **Dravyasaṃgraha** (Nemicandra) | A concise verse summary of Jain ontology: the soul as one of six substances (*dravyas*); the soul's qualities (*guṇas*) and modes (*paryāyas*); the distinction between the worldly soul (*saṃsārī jīva*) and the liberated soul (*mukta jīva*) |

**Core texts:** Samayasāra (Kundakunda's masterpiece — the deepest Jain exploration of the self), Tattvārtha Sūtra ch. 2 (the systematic foundation)

**See also:** [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Kundakunda, Nemicandra; [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Ātman in Hindu Philosophy, Anattā in Buddhism

## 6. Anekāntavāda & Syādvāda (Many-Sidedness & Conditional Predication)

Jainism's distinctive contribution to Indian philosophy: the doctrine that reality has infinite aspects (*anekānta*) and that any statement about reality is only conditionally true (*syād-vāda* — "maybe" or "in some respect"). The seven-fold predication (*saptabhaṅgī*) maps the logical space of any proposition: "in some sense it is, in some sense it is not, in some sense it is both, in some sense it is indescribable," etc. This is not relativism but a sophisticated ontology of complexity.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (Umāsvāti) | The foundational canonical source: the distinction between *pramāṇa* (valid knowledge) and *naya* (partial standpoint); the seven viewpoints (*nayavāda*) — *naigama* (teleological), *saṃgraha* (generic), *vyavahāra* (practical), etc.; every judgement must be qualified by *syāt* (in some respect) |
| **Anekāntajayapatākā** (Haribhadra Sūri, Śvetāmbara, 8th c.) | "The Victory Banner of Anekānta" — the definitive Śvetāmbara defence of anekāntavāda: a logical refutation of one-sided views (*ekānta*), a demonstration that all philosophical systems are partial truths; includes a famous dialogue with Buddhist and Brahminical opponents |
| **Syādvādamañjarī** (Mallavādī Kṣamāśramaṇa, Śvetāmbara, 11th c.) | "The Blossom of Syādvāda" — a sophisticated treatment of the seven-fold scheme (*saptabhaṅgī*), arguing that every proposition has exactly seven logical possibilities; includes a defence against the charge that syādvāda is self-refuting |
| **Pramāṇanayatattvāloka** (Vādideva Sūri, Śvetāmbara, 12th c.) | "The Light of Truth on Pramāṇa and Naya" — the most comprehensive Jain work on epistemology: the validation of different knowledge sources, the relationship between *pramāṇa* (valid cognition) and *naya* (standpoint), a systematic exposition of all seven *nayas* |
| **Sanmatitarka** (Siddhasena Divākara, Śvetāmbara, 5th c.) | "The Logic of the True Doctrine" — one of the earliest systematic treatments: the two main *nayas* — *dravyārthika* (substantial viewpoint) and *paryāyārthika* (modification viewpoint) — as the key to reconciling apparently contradictory scriptural statements |
| **Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya** (Haribhadra) | "A Compendium of Six Philosophies" — Haribhadra applies anekāntavāda to the six major Indian philosophical systems (Buddhist, Nyāya, Sāṃkhya, Jain, Vaiśeṣika, Cārvāka), showing each has partial truth within the Jain framework |
| **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** (Book 1) | Although pre-dating the formal philosophy, the Ācārāṅga contains the germinal insight: "Those who praise one-sided views are not free from attachment" |
| **Nayacakra** (Devasena, Digambara, 10th c.) | The Digambara systematisation of *nayavāda*: a wheel of standpoints showing how each *naya* applies to different domains of discourse |

**Core texts:** Tattvārtha Sūtra (canonical foundation), Anekāntajayapatākā (the great defence), Sanmatitarka (the early systematisation)

**See also:** [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Haribhadra, Siddhasena Divākara, Vādideva Sūri; [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Tolerance and Pluralism in Indian Philosophy

## 7. The Path to Liberation (Mokṣa Mārga)

The soteriological framework of Jainism: liberation (*mokṣa*) as the complete separation of the soul from all karmic matter, achieved through the three jewels (*ratnatraya*) — right faith, right knowledge, right conduct. The path is graded for ascetics and householders, and is described in terms of the 14 *guṇasthānas* (stages of spiritual development) and the 11 *pratimās* (stages of lay perfection).

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (Umāsvāti) | The canonical blueprint: "Right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct are the path to liberation" (1.1); the progressive stages of the path; the distinction between *bhavya* (capable of liberation) and *abhavya* (eternally incapable) souls |
| **Samayasāra** (Kundakunda) | The path from the *niścaya* viewpoint: liberation is the soul recognising its own purity — not a journey to a new state but a removal of what obscures the soul's eternal perfection |
| **Gommaṭasāra** (Jīvakāṇḍa, Nemicandra) | The Jīvakāṇḍa (Soul Section) of the Gommaṭasāra: the journey of the soul through the 14 *guṇasthānas*, the timing and mechanics of liberation, the description of the liberated soul (*siddha*) and its state of eternal bliss |
| **Yogaśāstra** (Hemacandra, 12th c.) | The path as a graded practice for householders: the twelve *vratas* (vows), the *pratimās* (stages), and the progressive purification of conduct leading toward monastic initiation |
| **Ratnakaraṇḍa Śrāvakācāra** (Samantabhadra, Digambara, 5th–6th c.) | "The Jewel-Box of Lay Conduct" — the most influential Digambara guide for lay Jainism: the path of the *śrāvaka* (lay practitioner) from initial faith to the brink of monastic life |
| **Niyamasāra** (Kundakunda) | The path governed by the self's own nature: the supreme discipline is self-awareness; the supreme restraint is the cessation of all karmic activity; liberation as the natural state of the purified soul |
| **Dravyasaṃgraha** (Nemicandra) | A concise systematic overview: the path in terms of the seven *tattvas* (fundamental categories) — soul, non-soul, influx, bondage, stoppage, shedding, liberation |
| **Bṛhat Siddhi** (Vādirāja, Digambara) | A Digambara treatise on the accomplishment of liberation: the meditative practices, the yogic disciplines, and the final breakthrough into omniscience and liberation |

**Core texts:** Tattvārtha Sūtra (the canonical outline), Samayasāra (the interior path), Ratnakaraṇḍa Śrāvakācāra (the lay path)

**See also:** [[Jain Stories MOC]] → The Liberation of Mahāvīra, The Attainment of Kevala; [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Siddhas (Liberated Souls)

## 8. The Three Jewels (Ratnatraya)

The triple path of *samyag darśana* (right faith), *samyag jñāna* (right knowledge), and *samyag cāritra* (right conduct) — the three together constitute the path to liberation. No one jewel is sufficient alone; all three must be cultivated simultaneously. This is the most fundamental doctrinal formulation in Jainism, repeated in every text and commentary.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (ch. 1, Umāsvāti) | The locus classicus: sūtra 1.1 — "Samyag darśana-jñāna-cāritrāṇi mokṣamārgaḥ" (Right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct are the path to liberation); the mutual dependence of the three; the eight limbs of right faith |
| **Samayasāra** (Kundakunda) | The three jewels from the transcendental perspective: right faith is conviction in the self, right knowledge is awareness of the self, right conduct is absorption in the self — all three are aspects of the soul's own nature |
| **Pravacanasāra** (Kundakunda) | The three jewels as the essence of Mahāvīra's teaching: faith in the *tattvas* as taught by the Jina, knowledge of their true nature, and conduct that accords with that knowledge |
| **Daśavaikālika Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara Mūla Sūtra) | A practical ascetic text that grounds the three jewels in daily monastic life: "The *sādhu* who possesses all three jewels is a true Jaina" — right conduct as the visible expression of faith and knowledge |
| **Yogaśāstra** (Hemacandra) | The three jewels as the basis of all Jain practice: without right faith, knowledge is barren; without right knowledge, faith is blind; without right conduct, both are fruitless |
| **Dravyasaṃgraha** (Nemicandra) | The three jewels defined in ontological terms: faith in the true nature of the six *dravyas* (substances), knowledge of their modes (*paryāyas*), conduct that refrains from harming any of them |
| **Niyamasāra** (Kundakunda) | The supremacy of the three jewels: even a layperson who possesses all three is on the path; even an ascetic who lacks them is not. The three jewels as the universal criterion of Jain identity |

**Core texts:** Tattvārtha Sūtra 1.1 (the foundational sūtra), Samayasāra (the deepest exposition), Daśavaikālika Sūtra (the practical code)

**See also:** [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → The Three Jewels compared with the Three Refugees in Buddhism, the Three Paths in Hinduism

## 9. The 14 Guṇasthānas (Stages of Spiritual Purification)

The fourteen stages of spiritual development (*guṇasthānas*) that map the soul's journey from the state of total delusion (mithyātva) to the state of perfect liberation (ayoga kevalin). This is Jainism's unique taxonomy of spiritual progress — a ladder (*śreṇi*) that the soul climbs by gradually eliminating karmic impurities.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (ch. 9, Umāsvāti) | The canonical listing of the 14 stages: from *mithyā-dṛṣṭi* (wrong belief) through the various levels of vow-observance and passion-subdual to *sayoga-kevalin* (omniscience with activity) and *ayoga-kevalin* (omniscience without activity, the threshold of liberation) |
| **Gommaṭasāra** (Jīvakāṇḍa, Nemicandra) | The most detailed exposition of the guṇasthānas: the specific karmas that are destroyed at each stage; the duration a soul may remain at each stage; the types of souls that ascend and those that fall back |
| **Pañcāstikāya** (Kundakunda) | The relationship between the guṇasthānas and the soul's innate qualities — each stage corresponds to a level of obscuration of the soul's four infinite qualities (*ananta catuṣṭaya*) |
| **Dravyasaṃgraha** (Nemicandra) | A condensed account: the guṇasthānas as the progressive purification of the soul's substance (*dravya*) from karmic contamination |
| **Nandī Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara) | The Śvetāmbara enumeration: the 14 guṇasthānas with their defining characteristics — which passions are present, which types of *saṃjñā* (cognition) are operative, and what *dhyāna* (meditation) is possible at each stage |
| **Mūlācāra** (Digambara monastic code) | The guṇasthānas as the framework for monastic discipline: the rules of conduct appropriate to each stage, the transgressions that cause a fall to a lower stage |

**Core texts:** Tattvārtha Sūtra ch. 9 (canonical), Gommaṭasāra (detailed), Pañcāstikāya (philosophical context)

**See also:** [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Nemicandra, Umāsvāti; [[Buddhist Scripture Thematic Groups]] → The 4 Jhānas and 8 Samāpattis (a parallel progression scheme)

## 10. Jain Cosmology & Universe

The Jain universe is uncreated and eternal (*anādi* and *ananta*), consisting of three worlds (*triloka*) — the upper world (*urdhva loka*) of heavens, the middle world (*madhyaloka*) of human and animal realms, and the lower world (*adho loka*) of hells. The cosmos is shaped like a human standing with hands on hips (*loka-puruṣa* or *loka-ākāśa*). Time moves in cycles (*kāla-cakra*) of ascending (*utsarpiṇī*) and descending (*avasarpinī*) half-cycles.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (chs. 3–4, Umāsvāti) | The canonical cosmography: the shape and dimensions of the universe; the *loka-ākāśa* (cosmic space) vs. *aloka-ākāśa* (transcendental space); the three worlds; the continents and oceans of the middle world — Jambūdvīpa, Dhātakīkhaṇḍa, Puṣkaradvīpa |
| **Trilokasāra** (Nemicandra, 10th c., Digambara) | "The Essence of the Three Worlds" — the definitive Digambara cosmography: detailed measurements of every region, the number and types of beings in each realm, the celestial vehicles (*vimānas*), the geography of births and deaths |
| **Tiloyapaṇṇattī** (Yativṛṣabha, Digambara, 6th c.) | "The Description of the Three Worlds" — an encyclopedic Prakrit text (5,600 verses): the geography of Jambūdvīpa, its mountains, rivers, and regions; the 24 Tīrthaṅkaras' birthplaces mapped onto it; the heavens and hells with their precise dimensions |
| **Lokavibhāga** (Śarvavarman, Digambara, 5th c.) | "The Division of the Cosmos" — a comprehensive cosmography that also incorporates astronomical calculations: planetary motions, time cycles, and the calculation of cosmic time |
| **Saṃgrahaṇī Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara) | A Śvetāmbara manual of cosmography: the structure of the universe drawn in mandalic diagrams; the islands and oceans; the heavens arranged in tiers; the hells arranged in layers |
| **Jambūdvīpaprajñapti** (Śvetāmbara Upāṅga 6) | A detailed description of Jambūdvīpa (the central continent) — its geography, its inhabitants, its *kṣetras* (regions), and the eighty-four lakh (8,400,000) villages said to exist within it |
| **Sūryaprajñapti & Candraprajñapti** (Śvetāmbara Upāṅgas 4–5) | Astronomical texts: the motions of the sun and moon, their chariots, the calculation of day and night, the length of months and years — Jain astronomy embedded in the Āgama tradition |
| **Jīvājīvābhigama** (Śvetāmbara Upāṅga 1) | "The Treatise on the Living and the Non-Living" — a catalog of the cosmos in terms of the fundamental categories of substance: the distribution of souls across the realms, the elements, and the forms of non-soul matter |

**Core texts:** Tattvārtha Sūtra chs. 3–4 (the introduction), Trilokasāra (the Digambara compendium), Jambūdvīpaprajñapti (the Āgama source)

**See also:** [[Jain Stories MOC]] → The Shape of the Universe; [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Cosmos in Buddhism and Hinduism

## 11. Monastic Discipline & Ethics

The rule of life for the Jain mendicant — the most demanding ascetic code in any Indian tradition. The *sādhu* (monk) and *sādhvī* (nun) take five great vows (*mahāvratas*): non-violence, truthfulness, non-stealing, celibacy, and non-possession. The monastic discipline extends to every detail of movement, eating, speech, and mental activity, governed by elaborate codes of *sāmācārī* (proper conduct) and *pratikramaṇa* (repentance rituals).

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 1) | The oldest and most authoritative Jain monastic code: the complete rule of life — how to walk, beg, accept food, speak, use the toilet, and spend the night without harming any living being; the earliest surviving ascetic manual in any Indian tradition |
| **Daśavaikālika Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara Mūla Sūtra) | The essential monastic handbook: ten chapters covering the ten *dharmas* (forbearance, modesty, straightforwardness, truthfulness, purity, restraint, austerity, renunciation, non-attachment, celibacy); daily recited by Śvetāmbara monks |
| **Cheda Sūtras** (Śvetāmbara) | The six disciplinary codes (*cheda* = cutting, referring to cutting off part of the penance for transgressions). **Niśītha** (on expiations for offences); **Vyavahāra** (procedures for dealing with transgressions); **Bṛhatkalpa** (seniority, hierarchy, and group conduct); **Daśāśrutaskandha** (rules for the rainy-season retreat); **Śrī Niśītha** (penances for minor offences); **Jītakalpa** (rules for the use of alms and possessions) |
| **Mūla Sūtras** (Śvetāmbara) | The four foundational texts: **Daśavaikālika** (ascetic conduct), **Uttarādhyayana** (30 instructive chapters on monastic life), **Āvaśyaka** (daily obligatory duties), **Daśāśrutaskandha** (rainy-season conduct and confession) |
| **Mūlācāra** (Digambara, Vattakera) | The foundational Digambara monastic code: the conduct (*ācāra*) of the Digambara monk — the rules of nudity, the possession of only a peacock-feather whisk and a water-gourd, the practice of *āhāra-dāna* (accepting food) |
| **Kalpa Sūtra** (Sāmācārī section, Bhadrabāhu) | The rules of *sāmācārī* (proper deportment): how monks interact during the rainy-season retreat (*cāturmāsa*), seniority, bowing, seating, and group recitation |
| **Niśītha Cūrṇi** (Śvetāmbara) | A commentary on the Niśītha Sūtra that preserves case law — real and hypothetical scenarios testing the boundaries of the monastic rules |
| **Yogaśāstra** (Hemacandra, ch. 3–5) | The Śvetāmbara monk's daily code: the five *samitis* (carefulness in walking, speaking, taking alms, handling objects, and bodily functions) and the three *guptis* (restraints of mind, speech, and body) |

**Core texts:** Ācārāṅga Sūtra (the oldest and most authoritative), Daśavaikālika Sūtra (the daily code), Mūlācāra (the Digambara code)

**See also:** [[Jain Stories MOC]] → The Ācārāṅga's Ascetic Narratives; [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Bhadrabāhu, Hemacandra, Vattakera

## 12. Lay Ethics & Vows

The Jain path for the householder (*śrāvaka* / *śrāvikā*): adapting the rigorous Jain ethic of non-violence and renunciation to the demands of worldly life. The twelve *aṇuvratas* (minor vows) — five *aṇuvratas* (lesser versions of the great vows), three *guṇavratas* (merit-augmenting vows), and four *śikṣāvratas* (training vows) — form the layperson's code. The *śrāvakācāra* (lay conduct) literature is one of the richest genres in Jain scripture.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Upāsakadaśāḥ** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 7) | "Ten Chapters on Laymen" — stories of eleven exemplary lay followers (the *upāsakas*), each illustrating a specific vow or virtue: the wealthy merchant Ānanda who gave up his business to practise non-violence; the layman Kāmadeva who attained *kevala* as a householder |
| **Ratnakaraṇḍa Śrāvakācāra** (Samantabhadra, Digambara, 5th–6th c.) | The most influential Digambara lay manual: the 12 *aṇuvratas* in systematic detail; the *pratimās* (11 stages of lay perfection); the daily duties of a Jain householder — worship, study, charity |
| **Yogaśāstra** (Hemacandra, 12th c.) | The Śvetāmbara lay masterpiece: the twelve vows explained for the King Kumārapāla — includes the ethics of business, war, and governance from a Jain perspective; the duties of a layperson toward ascetics |
| **Śrāvakācāra texts** (Digambara tradition) | A genre of texts: most notably **Cāmuṇḍarāya's Caritra-sāra** (10th c.), **Āśādhara's Sāgāra-dharmāmṛta** (13th c.), and **Henraja's Śrāvakācāra** — each adapting the vows to changing social and economic conditions |
| **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (ch. 7, Umāsvāti) | The theoretical framework of the lay vows: the distinction between *mahāvratas* (for monks) and *aṇuvratas* (for laypeople); the five *aticāras* (transgressions) for each vow |
| **Dharma Ratna** (Śvetāmbara) | A medieval Prakrit text on lay Jaina ethics: the etiquette of receiving ascetics, the qualifications for a true *śrāvaka*, the conduct of a layperson during the *paryuṣaṇā* festival |
| **Puṇya-śravana-kathā** (Śvetāmbara) | Story collections illustrating the fruits of lay vows: narratives of laypeople who attained heaven or liberation through faithful observance of the *aṇuvratas* |

**Core texts:** Upāsakadaśāḥ (the Āgama foundation), Ratnakaraṇḍa Śrāvakācāra (the Digambara handbook), Yogaśāstra (the Śvetāmbara classic)

**See also:** [[Jain Stories MOC]] → The Upāsakadaśāḥ Stories; [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Samantabhadra, Hemacandra, Cāmuṇḍarāya

## 13. The 63 Illustrious Men (Śalākāpuruṣas)

The great figures of the Jain universal history: 24 Tīrthaṅkaras (ford-makers), 12 Cakravartins (universal emperors), 9 Baladevas (balarāmas), 9 Vāsudevas (vishṇus), and 9 Prativāsudevas (anti-vishṇus) — the 63 *Śalākāpuruṣas* or "Illustrious Men." Their lives, intertwined across births and ages, form the grand narrative of Jain sacred history. The *purāṇic* literature of Jainism is largely the telling of these 63 lives.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (Hemacandra, 12th c.) | "The Lives of the 63 Illustrious Men" — the single most important Jain narrative text: the complete biographical cycle of all 63, structured as a series of past-life connections that link them together; includes the famous story of Nemi's renunciation, the tales of Rāma and Kṛṣṇa as Jain heroes |
| **Mahāpurāṇa** (Jinasena, 9th c., Digambara) | The Digambara *Ādipurāṇa* (life of Ādinātha) and the *Uttarapurāṇa* (the remaining lives) — together covering the Śalākāpuruṣas from the Digambara perspective; the authoritative Digambara version of the universal history |
| **Ādīpurāṇa** (Jinasena) | The biography of Ādinātha, the first Tīrthaṅkara, who was also the first king, lawgiver, and teacher of civilisation — establishes the framework for the entire Śalākāpuruṣa system |
| **Uttarapurāṇa** (Gunabhadra) | The continuation: Tīrthaṅkaras from Ajita to Mahāvīra; the Cakravartins; the Baladeva, Vāsudeva, and Prativāsudeva cycles (including the Jain versions of the Rāma and Kṛṣṇa stories) |
| **Śalākāpuruṣa Carita** (other works) | Various independent caritas (biographies) of individual figures: the **Nemicarita** (Nemi's story), the **Rāmacarita** (Jain Rāmāyaṇa), the **Harivaṃśapurāṇa** (Jain Mahābhārata/Kṛṣṇa narrative) |
| **Paumacariya** (Vimala Sūri, Śvetāmbara, c. 3rd c. CE) | The earliest Jain Rāmāyaṇa — Rāma as a Jain hero, Rāvaṇa as a character facing karmic consequences; predates Hemacandra's version by centuries |

**Core texts:** Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra (the Śvetāmbara compendium), Mahāpurāṇa (the Digambara compendium), Paumacariya (the earliest Jain Rāmāyaṇa)

**See also:** [[Jain Stories MOC]] → The Jain Rāmāyaṇa, The Jain Mahābhārata, The Story of Nemi and Rājul; [[Jain Figures MOC]] → The 63 Śalākāpuruṣas

## 14. Logic, Epistemology & Debate

Jain philosophy made major contributions to Indian logic (*pramāṇa-śāstra*) and epistemology: the theory of *pramāṇa* (valid cognition) distinguished from *naya* (partial standpoint), the doctrine of *syādvāda* (conditional predication), and the sophisticated defence of anekānta against Buddhist logicians (Dignāga, Dharmakīrti) and Brahminical schools. The Jain logicians were among the most rigorous thinkers in medieval India.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Pramāṇasamuccaya** (Siddhasena Divākara, Śvetāmbara, 5th c.) | "A Compendium of Valid Cognitions" — the foundational Jain work on logic: the definition of *pramāṇa*, the division into *pratyakṣa* (direct perception) and *parokṣa* (indirect knowledge), the refutation of Buddhist and Nyāya theories of perception |
| **Pramāṇanayatattvāloka** (Vādideva Sūri, Śvetāmbara, 12th c.) | "The Light of Truth on Pramāṇa and Naya" — the most comprehensive Jain epistemology: five types of *pramāṇa* (perception, inference, testimony, analogy, and presumption), the seven *nayas*, and a detailed critique of Dharmakīrti's logic |
| **Nyāyāvatāra** (Siddhasena Divākara) | "The Inception of Logic" — a short but highly influential verse text on the nature of inference (*anumāna*), the logic of syllogism, and the fallacy types; widely studied as a first text in Jain logic |
| **Anekāntajayapatākā** (Haribhadra Sūri, 8th c.) | The logical defence of anekānta: the refutation of *ekānta* (one-sided) views using formal argumentation; the demonstration that all philosophical positions are partial truths that can be reconciled through *nayavāda* |
| **Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya** (Haribhadra) | "A Compendium of Six Philosophies" — a unique text that applies Jain logical method to other schools: Buddhism, Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika, Sāṃkhya, Mīmāṃsā, and Cārvāka are each presented, praised for their partial truths, and transcended from a Jain standpoint |
| **Vādanyāya** (Akalaṅka, Digambara, 8th c.) | "The Method of Debate" — the definitive Digambara work on logic: a sophisticated engagement with Dharmakīrti's *Pramāṇavārttika*; the defence of *syādvāda* against Buddhist critiques; the logic of sevenfold predication |
| **Tattvārtha Rājavārtika** (Akalaṅka) | Akalaṅka's commentary on the Tattvārtha Sūtra, which is as much a work of logic as of commentary — expands the sūtras into a full epistemological system |
| **Pramāṇavārttika** (Dharmakīrti, Buddhist) — as debated | The Jain logicians (especially Vādideva Sūri and Akalaṅka) wrote their works in sustained dialogue with Dharmakīrti's *Pramāṇavārttika*; understanding Jain logic requires reading both sides of this debate |

**Core texts:** Pramāṇasamuccaya (early systematisation), Pramāṇanayatattvāloka (comprehensive), Nyāyāvatāra (introductory)

**See also:** [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Siddhasena Divākara, Haribhadra, Akalaṅka, Vādideva Sūri; [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Buddhist Logic (Dignāga, Dharmakīrti), Nyāya Logic

## 15. Yoga & Meditation

Jain meditation (*dhyāna*) and yoga are distinct from the Hindu and Buddhist yogic traditions. Jain yoga is oriented toward the purification of the soul from karmic matter through the four types of *dhyāna*: *ārta-dhyāna* (distressful meditation) and *raudra-dhyāna* (wrathful meditation) — both to be avoided; *dharma-dhyāna* (righteous meditation) and *śukla-dhyāna* (pure white meditation) — the two that lead to liberation. The 12 *bhāvanās* (reflections) and 25 *mārgaṇāsthānas* (stations of inquiry) structure the meditative path.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Yogaśāstra** (Hemacandra, 12th c.) | The great Śvetāmbara yoga manual: the 12 *bhāvanās*, the *prekṣā* (insight) meditation system, the ethics of yoga, the stages of yogic practice leading to *śukla-dhyāna*, and the final attainment of liberation |
| **Yogabindu** (Hemacandra) | "The Drop of Yoga" — a supplementary text to the Yogaśāstra: the nature of yogic concentration, the removal of mental impurities, the experience of the soul's innate bliss |
| **Yogadṛṣṭisamuccaya** (Haribhadra, 8th c.) | "A Compendium of Yogic Perspectives" — a unique comparative work on yogic experience: Haribhadra surveys eight systems of yoga (Jain, Buddhist, Hindu, etc.) and demonstrates that they all converge in their higher stages, while maintaining the Jain framework as the most complete |
| **Dhyānaśataka** (Śvetāmbara, c. 6th c.) | "One Hundred Verses on Meditation" — the classic Śvetāmbara meditation text: the objects of meditation, the postures, the contemplations on the nature of the soul, the progress through the *guṇasthānas* |
| **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** (Book 1) | The earliest description of Mahāvīra's own meditative practice: "Abandoning all sense-pleasures, he meditated upon the self; he knew the knowable but did not desire the desirable" — the foundation of Jain meditation theory |
| **Sthānāṅga Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 3) | A numerical encyclopaedia containing lists relevant to meditation: the four types of *dhyāna*, the 12 contemplations, the 25 *mārgaṇāsthānas* (stations of inquiry for self-examination) |
| **Bhagavatī Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 5) | Dialogues on the nature of *śukla-dhyāna*: Mahāvīra describes the four stages of pure meditation to Gautama — the progressive dissociation of the soul from karmic activity |
| **Samayasāra** (Kundakunda) | Meditation as the realisation of the soul's identity: the supreme meditation is the awareness "I am pure consciousness" — beyond all postures, techniques, and objects |
| **Dhyāna-vatī** (Digambara) | A Digambara meditation treatise: visualisation practices, the *kāyotsarga* (standing meditation) posture, and the contemplation of the *siddha* (liberated souls) |

**Core texts:** Yogaśāstra (the Śvetāmbara classic), Dhyānaśataka (the concise manual), Yogadṛṣṭisamuccaya (the comparative perspective)

**See also:** [[Jain Stories MOC]] → Mahāvīra's Meditation Under the Sāla Tree; [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Yoga in Hinduism, Meditation in Buddhism

## 16. Caste, Society & Kingship

Jainism originated in a society deeply structured by caste and kingship. The Jain tradition developed its own social philosophy: Tīrthaṅkaras are born in the Brahmin or Kṣatriya varna depending on the age; the earliest Jain texts show Mahāvīra and his disciples engaging with kings, merchants, and Brahmins; and the later purāṇic literature offers a Jain theory of the origin of society, kingship, and occupational groups.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (Hemacandra) | The Jain theory of the *varṇas* (social classes): how Ṛṣabha (Ādinātha) established the four varnas; the duties of each class from a Jain perspective; the stories of kings who patronised Jainism and those who opposed it |
| **Ādīpurāṇa** (Jinasena) | The foundational myth of civilisation: Ṛṣabha as the first king, lawgiver, and teacher of arts — the establishment of kingship, the institution of private property, the founding of agriculture, and the formation of the varna system as part of divine order |
| **Sūtrakṛtāṅga** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 2) | Critical dialogues with Brahmins: Mahāvīra's rejection of Brahminical claims to superiority by birth; the argument that virtue, not caste, determines a person's worth — "The Jain sage is the true Brahmin" |
| **Bhagavatī Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 5) | Detailed descriptions of the social world of Mahāvīra's time: the kings, merchants, and Brahmins who visited him; the cities and regions; the social hierarchy and Mahāvīra's interactions with each level |
| **Kumārapāla Carita** (Hemacandra) | The biography of King Kumārapāla of Gujarat (12th c.), Hemacandra's royal patron: the first and most famous Jain king of medieval India; his conversion to Jainism, his establishment of ahiṃsā as state policy, his enforcement of Jain vows across the kingdom |
| **Nāyādhammakahāo** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 6) | Stories of merchants, courtesans, kings, and commoners — providing a rich picture of Jain lay society in the early centuries CE; the economic ethics of Jain merchants |
| **Vikramacarita** (Jain recensions) | The Jain versions of the Vikramāditya stories: the ideal Jain king, his justice, his patronage of ascetics, his use of Jain principles in governance |

**Core texts:** Ādīpurāṇa (the origin of society), Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra (the social order), Kumārapāla Carita (the historical example)

**See also:** [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Ṛṣabhanātha, Kumārapāla; [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → Caste in Hindu Dharmaśāstra

## 17. Women & Gender

The status of women in Jainism is one of the most debated topics in the tradition, dividing the Śvetāmbara and Digambara schools sharply. The Śvetāmbara tradition holds that women can attain liberation (*mokṣa*) and become ascetics (nuns, *sādhvīs*), and the Āgamas preserve verses of awakened nuns. The Digambara tradition maintains that women cannot attain liberation without being reborn as men, because nudity (a requirement for a Digambara monk) is not possible for women, and the female body cannot generate the necessary ascetic heat (*tapas*).

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** (Book 2) | Rules for nuns: the extra restrictions on female ascetics — they must travel in groups, cannot wander alone, must be supervised by male monks; the earliest evidence of the monastic regulation of women |
| **Sūtrakṛtāṅga** (Śvetāmbara) | Dialogues on the capacity of women for spiritual attainment: some passages appear to support the possibility of women's liberation; others reflect the patriarchal assumptions of the milieu |
| **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (Hemacandra) | The stories of women: Queen Triśalā (Mahāvīra's mother), the nun Candanā (chief nun), Queen Mṛgāvatī, Rājul (Nemi's betrothed) — women as spiritual seekers, teachers, and exemplars of Jain virtue |
| **Nāyādhammakahāo** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 6) | Stories of virtuous laywomen: the merchant's wife who converted her husband; the courtesan who became a nun; the queen who protected the Saṅgha — the most extensive Āgama source on women's religious lives |
| **Therāvalī** (Śvetāmbara) | The lineage of the Jain Saṅgha: includes the names and traditions of prominent early nuns; the establishment of the *sādhvī* order |
| **Śvetāmbara-Digambara debate texts** | A genre of polemical literature: Śvetāmbara texts argue *strī-mukti* (women's liberation) from Āgama sources; Digambara texts argue *strī-aparādhya* (the impossibility of women's liberation) on the basis of the body, nudity, and karma |
| **Stri-mukti-prakaraṇa** (Śvetāmbara, c. 12th c.) | "A Treatise on Women's Liberation" — the Śvetāmbara defence: women can attain kevala (omniscience) as women; citing the examples of Mallī (the 19th Tīrthaṅkara in Śvetāmbara tradition) and Rājamatī |
| **Kaṣāya Pāhuda** (Digambara) | The five *dhātus* (elements) theory: used in Digambara arguments to show that the female body lacks the capacity for *śukla-dhyāna* — used as a proof text by Digambara opponents of women's liberation |

**Core texts:** Ācārāṅga Sūtra Book 2 (the rules), Nāyādhammakahāo (the narratives), Stri-mukti-prakaraṇa (the Śvetāmbara defence)

**See also:** [[Jain Stories MOC]] → Mallī's Story, Rājul's Devotion, Candanā the Chief Nun; [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Mahāvīra's Mother Triśalā, Mallī (19th Tīrthaṅkara), the Nuns (Sādhvīs)

## 18. Debate with Other Traditions (Interfaith Polemic)

Jainism developed in constant dialogue — and often sharp debate — with Buddhist, Brahminical (Mīmāṃsā, Nyāya), Ājīvika, and materialist schools. The Āgamas preserve Mahāvīra's debates with rival ascetics; the later philosophical literature systematically refutes the positions of other traditions while often incorporating their insights through the lens of anekāntavāda.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Sūtrakṛtāṅga** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 2) | The earliest Jain polemical text: sustained critique of the Vedic sacrificial tradition (against *ahiṃsā*), the Ājīvika doctrine of fatalism (niyati), the Buddhist doctrine of momentariness (kṣaṇikatva), and materialist (Cārvāka) denial of the soul |
| **Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya** (Haribhadra, 8th c.) | The most famous Jain work on other traditions: a survey of six philosophical systems (Buddhism, Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika, Sāṃkhya, Mīmāṃsā, Cārvāka) presented fairly and then shown to be partial truths within the Jain framework — Haribhadra's *anekānta* approach to interfaith dialogue |
| **Pramāṇanayatattvāloka** (Vādideva Sūri, 12th c.) | The sustained refutation of Dignāga's and Dharmakīrti's Buddhist logic: the one-to-one engagement with Buddhist epistemology that determined the shape of later Jain logic |
| **Anekāntajayapatākā** (Haribhadra) | A defence of the Jain position against 21 specific objections from Buddhist and Nyāya opponents — arranged as a formal debate with the opponent's view stated and then refuted |
| **Samaṇasuttaṃ** (Jain reply to Buddhist critiques) | A late compilation but important: gathers passages from Jain Āgamas that respond to specifically Buddhist critiques — the Jain answers to Buddhist arguments about anattā (no-self), karma, and the validity of ascetic practice |
| **Tattvārtha Rājavārtika** (Akalaṅka, Digambara, 8th c.) | Akalaṅka's massive commentary on the Tattvārtha Sūtra is as much a refutation of Buddhist and Brahminical alternatives as an exposition of Jain doctrine — especially the defence of soul (*jīva*) against Buddhist no-self |
| **Dvātriṃśikā** (Siddhasena Divākara) | Thirty-two verses defending the Jain position on key points: the validity of Jain scripture, the existence of the soul, the nature of omniscience — addressed to a non-Jain audience |
| **Uttarādhyayana Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara Mūla Sūtra) | Chapters recounting debates between Jain ascetics and non-Jain teachers: the story of Kesi and Sūri (a dialogue between two Jain sects, not interfaith); the debate between the Jain monk and the Brahmin (ch. 25) |

**Core texts:** Sūtrakṛtāṅga (early polemic), Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya (comparative survey), Pramāṇanayatattvāloka (the debate with Buddhist logic)

**See also:** [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] → The Six Philosophical Schools, Buddhist-Jain Debates on the Self

## 19. Narratives & Parables (Story Literature)

Jain story literature is vast and deeply influential — second only to the Jātaka tradition in Indian narrative literature. The Jain *kahā* (story) tradition includes *dharma-kathā* (moral stories), *ākhyāna* (legends), *avadāna* (heroic tales of lay virtue), and *prabandha* (historical narratives). Jain stories are always didactic: they illustrate the workings of karma, the fruits of virtue, and the importance of right faith.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Nāyādhammakahāo** (Śvetāmbara Aṅga 6) | "Stories of Righteous Conduct" — the most important Āgama story collection: narratives of laymen and laywomen who practised the Jain dharma in challenging circumstances; the tale of the merchant who gave up his wealth; the story of the courtesan who chose renunciation |
| **Jñātādharmakathāḥ** (Śvetāmbara Upāṅga 2) | "Stories of Knowledge and Righteousness" — further Āgama narratives: the story of Kāli (a virtuous nun), the tale of Jamālī (Mahāvīra's rebellious nephew), and moral fables embedded in teachings |
| **Antaḥkṛtadaśāḥ** (Śvetāmbara Upāṅga 8) | "Ten Chapters on Those Who Conquered Death" — stories of ascetics who achieved liberation through intense penance; narratives of spiritual heroism and triumph over karmic bondage |
| **Anuttaraupapātikadaśāḥ** (Śvetāmbara Upāṅga 9) | Stories of those reborn in the highest heavens: laypeople and ascetics whose good karma led to celestial rebirth; the descriptions of heavenly rewards for virtuous conduct |
| **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (Hemacandra) | The greatest Jain story collection: embedded within the biographies are hundreds of sub-stories — past-life tales, moral parables, and karmic case histories that illustrate the law of karma across multiple lifetimes |
| **Vivāgasuya** (Śvetāmbara Upāṅga 10) | "Stories of Karmic Ripening" — narratives focused entirely on the workings of karma: how good and bad deeds ripen in specific circumstances; the most direct narrative treatment of karmic causation in the Āgamas |
| **Āvadāna literature** (Śvetāmbara & Digambara) | The Jain avadāna genre: short narratives of laypeople who performed a single act of great virtue and reaped immediate karmic rewards — the Jain equivalent of Buddhist avadāna literature (e.g., the stories of Śreṇika, the layman who fed a monk) |
| **Cariyas of the Śalākāpuruṣas** | Independent biographies (caritas) of individual figures: **Paumacariya** (Jain Rāmāyaṇa, Vimala Sūri), **Vasudevahiṇḍī** (Travels of Vasudeva, a frame-tale collection), and the **Dhammaparikkhā** (stories illustrating the superiority of Jain dharma) |

**Core texts:** Nāyādhammakahāo (the Āgama foundation), Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra (the narrative universe), Vivāgasuya (karmic narratives)

**See also:** [[Jain Stories MOC]] (the narrative vault); [[Jain Figures MOC]] → The 63 Śalākāpuruṣas, the Jain merchant-heroes of the Nāyādhammakahāo

## 20. Temple Ritual, Worship & Festivals

The devotional and ritual dimension of Jainism — temple worship (*pūjā*), the installation of images (*pratiṣṭhā*), the daily obligations of monks and laypeople (*āvaśyaka*), and the major festivals (Paryuṣaṇā, Mahāvīra Jayantī, Dīpāvalī). Contrary to the stereotype that Jainism is only ascetic and philosophical, the temple and ritual traditions are among the richest in Indian religious culture.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Pratiṣṭhā texts** (Śvetāmbara & Digambara) | Manuals for the consecration of Jain images: the *Pratiṣṭhāpāṭha* (Śvetāmbara) and the *Pratiṣṭhātilaka* (Digambara) — detailed rituals for installing a Jina image, the mantras, the offerings, the purification of the priest and the icon |
| **Āvaśyaka Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara Mūla Sūtra) | The six daily obligatory duties of the monk and the devout layperson: *sāmāyika* (equanimity), *caturviṃśatistava* (praise of the 24 Tīrthaṅkaras), *vandana* (veneration of ascetics), *pratikramaṇa* (confession of faults), *kāyotsarga* (standing meditation), *pratyākhyāna* (renunciation for the day) |
| **Caityavandana Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara) | The daily hymn of praise to the Jinas, recited before temple images: "Praise to the Arhats, the Bhagavāns… who have destroyed the four kinds of obstructive karma" — the most frequently recited liturgical text in Śvetāmbara Jainism |
| **Nandī Sūtra** (Śvetāmbara) | A Śvetāmbara Upāṅga on the philosophical meaning of worship: the five types of *pūjā*, the merits of *dravya-pūjā* (material worship) vs. *bhāva-pūjā* (mental worship), the correct performance of temple rites |
| **Kalpa Sūtra** (Bhadrabāhu) | The rituals of the Paryuṣaṇā festival: the eight-day period of confession, fasting, and scripture recitation in the rainy season — the most important festival in the Śvetāmbara calendar; the Kalpa Sūtra is recited during Paryuṣaṇā |
| **Pañca-Kalyāṇaka-pratiṣṭhā texts** | Manuals for the five-life-consecration festival: installing a Jina image by ritually re-enacting the five auspicious events (conception, birth, renunciation, omniscience, liberation) of a Tīrthaṅkara's life |
| **Pūjā manuals** (Śvetāmbara & Digambara) | Hundreds of medieval and early-modern manuals: the *Aṣṭaprakārī Pūjā* (eight-fold worship), the *Snātra Pūjā* (anointment of the Jina image), the *Pañcāmṛta Kalasha* (five-nectar consecration) |
| **Bhakti literature** (from c. 12th c. onward) | Hymns (*stotras*), devotional poems (*stavanas*), and songs (*kīrtanas*) in Prakrit, Sanskrit, and Gujarati: the *Bhaktāmara Stotra* (Mānatuṅga, 7th c.), the *Kalyāṇamandira Stotra*, and the compositions of Ānandaghanajī (17th c.) — the flowering of Jain devotional poetry |

**Core texts:** Āvaśyaka Sūtra (daily ritual foundation), Kalpa Sūtra (festival ritual), Bhaktāmara Stotra (devotional masterpiece)

**See also:** [[Jain Stories MOC]] → The Paryuṣaṇā Story Cycle, The Installation of the Jina Image; [[Jain Figures MOC]] → Bhadrabāhu, Mānatuṅga

## 21. The Tattvārtha Sūtra as a Cross-Cutting Text

A special section devoted to the Tattvārtha Sūtra — the only Jain scripture accepted by all sects (Śvetāmbara, Digambara, and the later sub-sects) and the single most important philosophical text in the tradition. Authored by Umāsvāti (c. 2nd–5th c. CE, also known as Umāsvāmin), the Tattvārtha Sūtra is a systematic summary of Jain doctrine in ten chapters of terse sūtras, with auto-commentary. It cuts across every thematic group in this guide.

| Chapter | Chapters & Themes Covered |
|---------|-------------------------|
| **1. Faith & Knowledge** | The path of liberation: right faith (*samyag darśana*), right knowledge (*samyag jñāna*), right conduct (*samyag cāritra*) — the Three Jewels (Group 8); the eight limbs of right faith; the hierarchy of knowledge (*mati, śruta, avadhi, manaḥparyāya, kevala*); the sources of valid knowledge (Group 14) |
| **2. The Soul (Jīva)** | The nature of the soul: characteristics, categories, the distinction between worldly and liberated souls; the 14 *guṇasthānas* (Group 9); the 4 states of existence (Group 7) |
| **3. The Lower & Middle Worlds (Adho Loka & Madhya Loka)** | Cosmography: the hells (7 levels), the geography of Jambūdvīpa (Group 10); the types of infernal beings; the human realm and the *karmabhūmi* (Group 16) |
| **4. The Upper World (Ūrdhva Loka)** | The heavens: the 16 heavens of the kalpas and the 9 graiveyaka heavens; the celestial beings (*devas*) and their lifetimes; the *siddha-śilā* (the abode of liberated souls) (Group 10) |
| **5. The Non-Living Categories (Ajīva)** | The five *astikāyas* (extended substances): *dharma* (motion), *adharma* (rest), *ākāśa* (space), *pudgala* (matter), *jīva* (soul); plus *kāla* (time) as a sixth substance; the distinction between substance (*dravya*), quality (*guṇa*), and mode (*paryāya*) (Groups 5, 10) |
| **6. Karma: Influx (Āsrava)** | The causes of karmic influx: the 42 *āsravas* — the body, speech, mind, the 5 senses, the 4 passions, and the 28 types of activity; how the soul becomes karmically bound (Group 4) |
| **7. Vows & Ethics** | The *mahāvratas* (great vows for ascetics) and the *aṇuvratas* (minor vows for laypeople); the hierarchy of living beings (*jīvasthāna*); the practice of *ahiṃsā* (Group 3); the two types of conduct — *sāgāra* (householder) and *anāgāra* (ascetic) (Groups 11, 12) |
| **8. Bondage & Stoppage (Bandha & Saṃvara)** | The mechanism of karmic bondage: the 4 modes (*prakṛti*, *sthiti*, *anubhāga*, *pradeśa*); the stoppage of karma through *samiti* (carefulness), *gupti* (restraint), *dharma* (virtues), *bhāvanā* (reflections), *pariṣaha-jaya* (endurance of afflictions), and *cāritra* (conduct) (Group 4) |
| **9. Shedding of Karma (Nirjarā)** | The final three chapters: the shedding of karma by asceticism (*tapas*), the 6 external and 6 internal austerities (Group 11); the 14 *guṇasthānas* in detail (Group 9); the process of *śukla-dhyāna* (Group 15) |
| **10. Liberation (Mokṣa)** | The attainment of liberation: the 4 types of *dhyāna* (Group 15); the 4 *ghātiyā* karmas destroyed; the qualities of the liberated soul; the *siddha* as the perfected being in eternal bliss at the summit of the universe (Groups 7, 9, 10) |

**Core text:** The Tattvārtha Sūtra in its entirety is the single most important cross-cutting text in the Jain tradition. Use the chapter breakdown above to navigate its coverage across all 20 thematic groups.

## How to Use These Groups

**To find texts by theme:** Browse the group headings above and follow the references to the key Āgamas, sūtras, and later classics. For example, group 4 (Karma Theory) will take you from the Tattvārtha Sūtra ch. 8 through the Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama to the Karmagrantha.

**For thematic study:** Read a group sequentially — for instance, trace the theme of ahiṃsā (group 3) from the Ācārāṅga Sūtra Book 1 (the oldest expression), through the Tattvārtha Sūtra (the theoretical framework), to the Yogaśāstra (the lay application).

**For cross-referencing notes:** When writing about a topic (e.g., "karma in Jain thought"), consult group 4 (Karma Theory) to find all relevant doctrinal texts, then group 19 (Narratives & Parables) for the karmic story literature, and group 21 (Tattvārtha Sūtra) for the systematic overview.

**To understand individual texts:** Each text's cross-reference in the groups above explains *why* it belongs to that theme. Many texts appear in multiple groups — the Ācārāṅga Sūtra, for instance, appears in groups 1, 3, 11, 15, and 17 — reflecting its role as biography, non-violence scripture, monastic code, meditation text, and gender regulation.

**Paired study:** Use alongside [[Quran Thematic Groups]], [[Bible Thematic Groups]], [[Hindu Scripture Thematic Groups]], and [[Buddhist Scripture Thematic Groups]] to compare how the world's major scriptures treat shared themes like non-violence, karma, cosmology, monasticism, and the path to liberation.
