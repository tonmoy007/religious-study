# Connections Between Religious Texts

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → Sacred Texts & Their Connections
> The conceptual framework for the evidence-based comparisons in [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]].

How do texts influence, echo, or parallel one another across traditions? This note lays out the **types of connection**, the **major intertextual relationships** between world scriptures, and the **methodological guardrails** for studying them honestly.

---

## 1. Three Types of Connection

Every parallel between two religious texts falls into one of three categories. Keeping them distinct is the first discipline of comparative study.

### A. Genealogical (Direct Borrowing)

One text demonstrably draws on another — through translation, adaptation, allusion, or explicit citation.

**Examples:**
- The Qur'an's retelling of biblical figures (Abraham, Moses, Mary, Jesus) — the Qur'an *knowingly* engages the Biblical tradition, assuming the listener knows the earlier stories and correcting, condensing, or reinterpreting them.
- The New Testament's use of the Tanakh (Septuagint) — Matthew's Gospel cites Hebrew prophecy as fulfilled in Jesus ("Out of Egypt I called my son").
- The Lotus Sutra's echoes of earlier Buddhist Agama texts.
- The Book of Mormon's linguistic and narrative dependence on the KJV Bible.

**Key signal:** chronological and geographic plausibility of contact.

### B. Diffusion (Cultural Transmission)

Ideas spread along trade routes, through empire, or via missionary contact — not as a direct text-to-text relationship but as motifs, concepts, or practices that travel between cultures.

**Examples:**
- The Golden Rule appearing across traditions — from Confucius (5th c. BCE) to Hillel (1st c. BCE) to the Gospels and Hadith. Likely transmitted along the Silk Road rather than independently invented each time.
- Hellenistic influence on early Buddhist art (the anthropomorphic Buddha image) and on Jewish mysticism (Philo's Logos).
- Sufi mystical vocabulary borrowing from Neoplatonic and Hindu sources.
- Apocalyptic literature (Enoch, Daniel, Revelation, Syriac Bahira legend) as a genre that spread across the Near East.

**Key signal:** the parallel is conceptual rather than textual — same idea, different words — and appears along known historical contact zones.

### C. Independent / Archetypal

Similar forms arise from shared human psychology, not contact. Flood myths, dying-and-rising gods, purity laws, and creation narratives appear worldwide — often with no plausible pathway of transmission.

**Examples:**
- Flood narratives in Mesopotamia (Gilgamesh), the Bible (Noah), the Qur'an (Nuh), Greece (Deucalion), India (Manu), and indigenous American traditions. The basic structure (divine displeasure → single survivor → repopulation) is too widespread to all be genealogical.
- The "hero's journey" pattern (Joseph Campbell's monomyth) across cultures.
- Cosmic-tree or world-mountain motifs (Yggdrasil, Mount Meru, the Cross as cosmic axis).

**Key signal:** the parallel is structural or archetypal, the cultures had no known contact, and close textual analysis reveals divergent details that make genealogical dependence unlikely.

> [!warning] Parallelomania
> Samuel Sandmel (1962) coined this term: the habit of treating every surface similarity as evidence of borrowing. Before claiming a connection, ask: *Could this have arisen independently? Is the similarity at the level of idea, structure, or wording? What is the direction of influence?*

## 2. Major Intertextual Relationships

### Abrahamic Family (Genealogical Chain)

The Qur'an explicitly positions itself as a **confirmation** (*musaddiq*) of earlier scriptures (2:41, 3:3, 5:48) — "sent down before it." This creates a dense intertextual web:

| Relationship | Nature | Key Example |
|---|---|---|
| Qur'an ↔ Bible (OT/NT) | Retelling, correction, abbreviation | [[Joseph and Yusuf - A Figure Traced]] — Genesis 37–50 condensed into a single surah with theological reframing |
| Qur'an ↔ Jewish tradition | Legal and narrative parallels | Dietary laws, prophetic stories, the role of scripture — mediated orally (the *Isra'iliyyat* tradition) |
| NT ↔ Tanakh (LXX) | Prophecy-fulfillment framework | Matthew's formula quotations; Paul's typological reading of Exodus |
| Hadith ↔ Qur'an | Amplification and contextualization | [[Hadith and the Qur'an]] — prophetic practice (*Sunnah*) as the living interpretation of the Book |
| Talmud ↔ Tanakh | Legal and narrative commentary | The Mishnah and Gemara as "oral Torah" unpacking written Torah |

### Dharmic Cross-Pollination (Diffusion and Independent Development)

| Relationship | Nature | Key Example |
|---|---|---|
| Vedas → Upanishads | Internal development: from ritual hymn to philosophical speculation | The Brahman-Atman identity as a metaphysical deepening of Vedic sacrifice |
| Upanishads → Gita | Synthesis: Upanishadic monism meets devotional theism | Krishna as the Supreme Brahman — the personal face of the impersonal Absolute |
| Buddhism ↔ Hinduism | Shared vocabulary, divergent metaphysics | *Karma*, *dharma*, *samsara*, *moksha/nirvana* — same words, radically different meanings |
| Buddhism → East Asian traditions | Diffusion via the Silk Road | Chan/Zen as a fusion of Buddhist meditation with Daoist naturalism; Buddhist influence on Neo-Confucianism |

### Comparative Structures Across Families

| Feature | Abrahamic Pattern | Dharmic Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Revelation mode | Prophetic speech ("Thus says the Lord") | Visionary insight or heard hymn (shruti) |
| Canon | Closed (fixed list of books) | Open or layered (Vedic corpus, commentarial traditions) |
| Author function | Divine or prophetic author named | Often anonymous or attributed to sages of antiquity |
| Central tension | Law vs. grace / faith vs. works | Knowledge (jnana) vs. devotion (bhakti) vs. action (karma) |

### Mythological & Ancient Traditions

- **Mesopotamian influence on the Bible** — Enuma Elish and Genesis 1; Gilgamesh flood and Noah; Hammurabi and Mosaic law. Genealogical: Israel inherited and transformed Canaanite/Mesopotamian literary culture.
- **Egyptian influence on Hellenistic religion** — Isis-Osiris mysteries as precursors to Christian sacramental theology; the Weighing of the Heart and judgment after death; Hermetic corpus as Graeco-Egyptian synthesis.
- **Norse and Indo-European parallels** — Comparative mythology (Georges Dumézil's trifunctional hypothesis): priest-warmer-farmer tripartition found in Norse, Roman, Hindu, and Iranian myth.

## 3. Methods for Honest Comparison

### The Three-Question Test

For any parallel between Text A and Text B:

1. **What is shared?** — Identify the parallel at the right level of specificity, one tradition at a time."Do both have a flood" is too broad. "Both have a single righteous man warned by a deity to build a vessel" is specific enough to be meaningful.

2. **What is changed?** — The divergence is often more revealing than the similarity. The Qur'an adds the raven to the Cain/Abel story ([[First Murder - Cain and Abel]]): what does that change about the moral?

3. **Why?** — Propose a genealogical, diffusion, or archetypal explanation. Be explicit about uncertainty.

### Avoiding the Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Antidote |
|---|---|
| *Parallelomania* (Sandmel) | Demand multiple points of correspondence at the verbal, not just thematic, level |
| *Confirmation bias* | Seek out verses that *contradict* your hypothesis, not just those that support it |
| *Chronological carelessness* | Check dating of texts; later texts can't influence earlier ones (unless redacted) |
| *Anachronistic terms* | "Monotheism" in the Vedas? "Trinity" in the Tanakh? Define terms before applying them |
| *Over-synthesis* | Not all religions say the same thing; the differences are as important as the similarities |

### Recommended Framework

> **Describe first, compare second, explain third.**

1. Understand each text on its own terms (its genre, date, community, purpose).
2. Set the parallels side by side at the level of wording or structure.
3. Propose the *most economical* explanation — that is, the one requiring the fewest assumptions — for the connection.

## 4. Key Scholars & Schools

| Scholar | Contribution |
|---|---|
| **Samuel Sandmel** | Coined "parallelomania"; warned against superficial comparisons (1962) |
| **Michael Fishbane** | Inner-biblical exegesis — how the Hebrew Bible interprets itself (*Biblical Interpretation in Ancient Israel*) |
| **Angelika Neuwirth** | Qur'an as late antique text in conversation with Biblical and Syriac traditions |
| **John Wansbrough** | *Quranic Studies* — argued the Qur'an emerged from a sectarian milieu in dialogue with Jewish and Christian scripture |
| **Wilfred Cantwell Smith** | "Scripture" as a relational category: a text becomes scripture when a community treats it as such |
| **Mircea Eliade** | Archetypal patterns (sacred vs. profane, cosmic tree, eternal return) across traditions |
| **Georges Dumézil** | Indo-European trifunctional hypothesis; comparative mythology |
| **Frits Staal** | Rigorous critique of comparison: "ritual is a structure, not a message" — meaning is secondary to form |
| **Wendy Doniger** | Comparative mythology as a tool for understanding human concerns, not just historical borrowing |
| **Huston Smith** | Perennialist approach: the world's religions as different paths up the same mountain (controversial but influential) |

## 5. Open Questions

- *The Qur'an and the New Testament Apocrypha* — How much of the Qur'an's distinct Jesus material (speaking in the cradle, clay birds) derives from the Infancy Gospel of Thomas and the Protoevangelium of James? (See [[Jesus and Isa - A Figure Traced]].)
- *The Bible and the Book of Mormon* — Is the Book of Mormon an instance of genealogical (early American author consciously imitating KJV style) or independent (divine revelation) intertextuality? The textual evidence strongly favors the former.
- *Buddhist influence on early Daoist scripture?* — The *Huahu jing* (Scripture of the Conversion of the Barbarians) claims the Buddha was a manifestation of Laozi — a conscious intertextual claim in the opposite direction.
- *Is the "shared vocabulary" of the Dharmic traditions (karma, dharma) meaningful comparison or misleading homonymy?* — The same words carry almost opposite senses across Buddhist and Hindu contexts.
