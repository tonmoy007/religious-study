## The Major Scriptures
| Tradition | Primary text(s) | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| Judaism | Tanakh; Talmud | Law, narrative, prophecy + commentary |
| Christianity | Bible (Old + New Testament) | Narrative, letters, gospels |
| Islam | Qur'an; Hadith | Recited revelation + prophetic tradition |
| Hinduism | Vedas, Upanishads, Gita, epics | Hymn, philosophy, narrative |
| Buddhism | Tripitaka, sutras | Discourses, monastic rules |
| Sikhism | Guru Granth Sahib | Devotional poetry (a living Guru) |
| Daoism | Dao De Jing, Zhuangzi | Aphorism, parable |
| Confucianism | Analects, Five Classics | Sayings, ritual, history |

## How Texts Function (varies enormously)
- **Revealed vs. composed** — the Qur'an as literal divine speech vs. the Vedas as "heard" (shruti) vs. texts seen as humanly authored but inspired.
- **Oral vs. written** — many scriptures were memorized and recited long before being written (Vedas, early Buddhist canon, Qur'an).
- **Canon formation** — *which* books count was often a long, contested process (e.g., the Christian New Testament canon).
- **Translation** — some traditions resist it (Qur'anic Arabic is the "real" Qur'an); others embrace it (Christianity).

## Ways to Read Scripture
- **Literal / historical** — what did it mean to original audiences?
- **Allegorical / symbolic** — deeper spiritual meanings.
- **Legal** — deriving law and practice (halakha, sharia).
- **Mystical** — Kabbalah, Sufi tafsir, Vedantic exegesis.

## Scholarly Tools (Academic Study)
- **Historical-critical method** — dating, authorship, sources (e.g., the Documentary Hypothesis for the Torah; the Synoptic Problem for the Gospels).
- **Philology & textual criticism** — reconstructing earliest texts from manuscripts.
- **Form & redaction criticism** — units of tradition and how editors shaped them.

> These methods describe texts historically; they bracket (set aside) the
> question of divine authorship — see method note in [[Religion Study MOC]].
