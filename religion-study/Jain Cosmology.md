# Jain Cosmology

**Jain cosmology** describes an eternal, uncreated universe (*loka*) shaped like a cosmic man (*loka-puruṣa*) standing with arms akimbo — the narrow waist marking the middle realm, the broad chest and head forming the heavens, and the spreading legs enclosing the hells. Unlike theistic cosmologies, there is no creator god: the universe has always existed and will always exist, undergoing endless cyclical time.

## The Three Worlds (Triloka)

The *loka-puruṣa* body comprises three tiers:

| World | Sanskrit | Region of the Body | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Upper World | *Ūrdhva Loka* | Chest & head | Heavens — multiple celestial planes where gods (*devas*) reside, including *siddhaśilā* at the very apex |
| Middle World | *Madhya Loka* | Waist | The human realm — a flat disc of concentric continents and oceans where humans, animals, and plants live |
| Lower World | *Adho Loka* | Legs & feet | Hells (*naraka*) — seven or more subterranean planes of suffering, each progressively darker and more painful |

The three worlds are filled with eternal substances (*dravyas*): soul (*jīva*), matter (*pudgala*), space (*ākāśa*), motion (*dharma*), rest (*adharma*), and time (*kāla*). Beyond the *loka* lies the non-universe (*aloka*) — infinite, empty, and devoid of any substance.

## Siddhaśilā — The Realm of the Liberated

At the very crown of the upper world, beyond the highest heavens, lies **siddhaśilā** (also called *īṣatprāgbhāra*), the crescent-shaped abode of the *siddhas* — liberated souls who have shed all karma and attained eternal bliss, omniscience, and formless perfection. Souls that ascend here never fall back into the cycle of rebirth.

## Madhya Loka — The Middle Realm

The human world is a series of concentric ring-shaped continents (*dvīpas*) separated by ring-shaped oceans (*samudras*).

| Continent | Ocean | Key Features |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| **Jambūdvīpa** | Lavaṇa-samudra (Salt Ocean) | Innermost continent; contains Bharatavarṣa (India), Airavatavarṣa, and Mahāvideha — the only region where Tīrthaṅkaras are born |
| Dhātakīkhaṇḍadvīpa | Kāloda-samudra (Dark Ocean) | Second continent |
| Puṣkaravaradvīpa | Puṣkara-samudra | Third continent |
| (Six continents total) | — | Beyond these lie the three and a half continents (*sāḍhe dvīpa*) known in later texts |

At the centre of Jambūdvīpa stands **Mount Meru** (also *Sumeru*), the cosmic axis around which the sun, moon, and stars revolve. The human realm is sprinkled with lakes, rivers, and forests described in extraordinary geographical detail in the *Jambūdvīpaprajñapti*.

## Time Cycles (Kālacakra)

Time is conceived as a rotating wheel with alternating descending and ascending half-cycles:

- **Avasarpinī** (descending arc) — a progressive decline in human virtue, longevity, and well-being through six epochs (āra / kāla). We are currently in the *duṣamā-suṣamā* (5th epoch) of a descending arc.
- **Utsarpinī** (ascending arc) — a mirror-image rise back to perfection, also through six epochs.

Each full cycle — one *avasarpinī* plus one *utsarpinī* — is one *kālacakra*. Twenty-four Tīrthaṅkaras appear in each half-cycle to revive the Jain faith when it becomes necessary.

| Epoch (Āra) | Avasarpinī (Descending) | Utsarpinī (Ascending) |
|-------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| 1st | Suṣamā-suṣamā — utmost bliss, no death | Utmost bliss returns |
| 2nd | Suṣamā — moderate bliss | Moderate bliss |
| 3rd | Suṣamā-duṣamā — mixed | Mixed |
| 4th | Duṣamā-suṣamā — more suffering | More suffering |
| 5th | Duṣamā (current epoch) — mostly suffering, Tīrthaṅkaras appear | Mostly suffering |
| 6th | Duṣamā-duṣamā — extreme suffering, chaos | Extreme suffering, then upturn |

## No Creator, No Beginning

A central tenet: the universe is **anādi** (beginningless) and **ananta** (endless). It was not created by any god or cosmic agent — it simply *is*, governed by the impersonal laws of karma, space, and time. The Jain position rejects both theistic creation (Brahmā, Īśvara) and Buddhist momentariness; the universe's fundamental constituents (the six *dravyas*) are eternal, though their configurations perpetually change.

## Key Sources

- ***Tattvārtha Sūtra*** (c. 2nd–5th cent. CE) by Umāsvāmi — Chapter 3 (triloka description), chapters on time and the cosmic structure.
- ***Trilokasāra*** (c. 10th cent. CE) by Nemicandra — The definitive Digambara manual on cosmology, with precise measurements of the universe.
- ***Jambūdvīpaprajñapti*** (c. 3rd–4th cent. CE) — Śvetāmbara canonical text detailing the geography of Jambūdvīpa.
- ***Sūryaprajñapti*** & ***Candraprajñapti*** — Canonical texts on solar and lunar movements within the cosmic timetable.
