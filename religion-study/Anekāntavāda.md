# Anekāntavāda (The Doctrine of Many-Sidedness)

**Anekāntavāda** (Skt. *an-eka-anta-vāda* = "non-one-sided doctrine") is the Jain metaphysical principle that reality is complex and cannot be adequately captured by any single standpoint. It is the ontological foundation for [[Syādvāda]] and one of the most distinctive contributions of Jain philosophy.

## The Parable of the Blind Men and the Elephant

The classic illustration: several blind men touch different parts of an elephant — trunk, tusk, ear, leg, tail — and each describes the whole elephant based on his limited experience. Each account is true *from a certain standpoint*, yet none captures the full reality. Error arises not from partial truth but from mistaking the part for the whole.

## The Naya Theory: Standpoints of Knowledge

Jain epistemology elaborates anekāntavāda through *nayas* — valid but partial standpoints from which reality can be described.

### Three Primary Nayas (Āgamic)

| Naya | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Naigama** | Teleological, purpose-oriented | Calling a man a "cook" because he is cooking, though cooking is a transient state |
| **Saṃgraha** | Generic / universal | "All beings are *jīva*" — true at the generic level |
| **Vyavahāra** | Practical / conventional | "This pot is red" — everyday empirical description |

### The Seven Nayas (Classical System)

Expanded by Siddhasena Divākara and Umāsvāmi, divided into:

**Substantive (Dravyārthika) — permanent aspect:**
1. **Naigama** — teleological standpoint
2. **Saṃgraha** — generic/class standpoint
3. **Vyavahāra** — practical/particular standpoint

**Modal (Paryāyārthika) — changing aspect:**
4. **Ṛjusūtra** — momentary state (like a snapshot)
5. **Śabda** — grammatical/verbal distinctions
6. **Samabhirūḍha** — etymological differences among synonyms
7. **Evambhūta** — a thing described only when performing its function (e.g., "a pot is a pot only when it holds water")

Each *naya* is valid in its domain; absolutising any one yields *nayābhāsa* (fallacy of a standpoint).

## The Intellectual Virtue of Non-Absolutism

Anekāntavāda is an ethical stance as much as a metaphysics. It demands intellectual humility, tolerance of opposing views, and — crucially — recognition that dogmatic assertion (*ekānta*) is a form of intellectual violence (*hiṃsā*). This distinguishes it from mere relativism: not "anything goes," but every valid claim occupies a legitimate standpoint that must be honoured.

## Interfaith Dialogue: Haribhadra's Ecumenism

The 8th-century Jain Ācārya **Haribhadra Sūri** applied anekāntavāda to inter-religious understanding in the *Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya* ("Compendium of Six Philosophies") and *Anekāntajayapatākā*. He argued that each rival school (Buddhism, Nyāya, Sāṃkhya, etc.) grasps a genuine aspect of reality from its own *naya*; conflict arises when a school absolutises its standpoint. The Jain position is uniquely comprehensive because it explicitly incorporates multiple *nayas* without absolutising any single one.

## Key Sources

- ***Tattvārtha Sūtra*** (c. 2nd–5th cent. CE) by Umāsvāmi — Foundational text; discusses *naya* and *pramāṇa* in chapters 1–2.
- ***Sanmatitarka*** (c. 5th cent. CE) by Siddhasena Divākara — Definitive work on the seven-*naya* system.
- ***Anekāntajayapatākā*** (c. 8th cent. CE) by Haribhadra Sūri — Polemical defence of anekāntavāda.
- ***Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya*** by Haribhadra Sūri — Sympathetic compendium of six Indian philosophies.

## Comparative Notes

**Buddhist *catuṣkoṭi*:** Nāgārjuna's tetralemma — (1) it is, (2) it is not, (3) both, (4) neither — aims to *deconstruct* all standpoints to reveal emptiness (*śūnyatā*). Anekāntavāda *affirms* multiple standpoints as simultaneously valid. The catuṣkoṭi is a dialectical ladder to be discarded; *nayas* are a permanent epistemic framework.

**Hindu *neti neti* ("not thus, not thus"):** The Upaniṣadic method (especially *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad*) negates all attributes to reach the ineffable Brahman. Both traditions agree no description exhausts reality, but they move in opposite directions: *neti neti* strips away standpoints to reach the unqualifiable absolute; anekāntavāda multiplies standpoints to capture the richness of the relative world.

**Non-scepticism:** Anekāntavāda is not doubt about knowledge — it is a **positive epistemological pluralism**. Knowledge is always from a standpoint, and full knowledge (*kevala jñāna*) is precisely the capacity to see all *nayas* simultaneously.
