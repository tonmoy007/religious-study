# Anāthapiṇḍika

> The greatest patron of the Buddha's dispensation — a wealthy merchant of Sāvatthī whose name means "feeder of the helpless." He bought the Jetavana Grove, covered it with gold coins, and gifted it to the Saṅgha — the monastery where the Buddha spent nineteen rainy seasons.

|--------|-----------|

| **Jetavana** | [[Anāthapiṇḍika Buys Jetavana]] — covers the grove with gold coins laid edge to edge, Prince Jeta keeps the gate-trees |
| **The Great Monastery** | Builds cells, meeting halls, bath-houses, kitchens, walkways — the model monastic compound |
| **The Gift** | Offers the Buddha everything: the Buddha accepts "for the Saṅgha, past, present and future" |

## His Generosity

Anāthapiṇḍika's house was famous for its daily alms — not just to monastics but to anyone who came. He fed hundreds daily, and his fortune was said to be inexhaustible — until it wasn't. A story in the Saṃyutta Nikāya (SN 2.20) tells how his wealth was destroyed three times: by fire, by flood, and by debtors. Each time he quietly rebuilt.

> "It is better to light a lamp than to curse the darkness." — Attributed to Anāthapiṇḍika

## His Death

The *Anāthapiṇḍikovāda Sutta* (MN 143) records his last days. Bedridden and in pain, he asked Sāriputta to visit. Sāriputta taught him about detachment from the senses. Anāthapiṇḍika wept — not from fear of death, but because he had served the Buddha so long yet had never heard such a profound teaching. He was reborn in Tusita heaven.
