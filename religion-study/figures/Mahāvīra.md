# Mahāvīra (Vardhamāna)

> Birth: c. 599 BCE, Kṣatriya Kuṇḍagrāma (near modern Vaishali, Bihar)
> Death: c. 527 BCE, Pāvāpurī (Bihar)
> Title: 24th Tīrthaṅkara of the current avasarpiṇī (descending half of the time cycle)

Mahāvīra, meaning "Great Hero," was the 24th and most recent Tīrthaṅkara (ford-maker) of Jainism, a contemporary of the Buddha who systematised and revitalised the Jain tradition. Born Vardhamāna into a kṣatriya clan in the Licchavi republic, he renounced his princely life at age 30 and undertook twelve years of extreme asceticism before attaining kevala jñāna (perfect knowledge) at age 42. For the next 30 years he wandered the Gangetic plain, organising a community of monks, nuns, and lay followers around the five great vows (mahāvratas). His teachings, preserved in the Jain Āgamas, emphasise non-violence (ahiṃsā), non-attachment (aparigraha), and the eternal nature of the soul (jīva). Jain tradition reveres him as the last Tīrthaṅkara of this descending half of the cosmic time cycle, after whom the Dhamma will gradually decline until the next ascending half (utsarpiṇī) brings a new Tīrthaṅkara.

## Biography (in a table)

| Event | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Birth | c. 599 BCE in Kṣatriya Kuṇḍagrāma, a suburb of Vaiśālī (modern Basarh, Bihar). The Śvetāmbara tradition holds that he was born to King Siddhārtha and Queen Triśalā (also called Priyakāriṇī). The Digambara tradition maintains he never married and was born to a different set of parents, King Siddhārtha and Queen Yaśodā |
| Early Life | Raised as a prince of the Jñātṛka (Jñāta) clan, a branch of the Licchavis. Given the name Vardhamāna ("the Increasing One") because of the prosperity that accompanied his birth. Married to Yaśodā (Śvetāmbara tradition) and fathered a daughter, Priyadarśanā (also named Anojjā or Anavadya). Lived a luxurious life until age 30 |
| Renunciation | At age 30 (Śvetāmbara tradition) or 28 (Digambara tradition), he renounced the world. He distributed his wealth, tore out his hair in five handfuls (a key Jain iconographic motif), donned a simple cloth, and began his life as a homeless ascetic |
| Ascetic Period | 12 years of extreme austerity — fasting for long periods, meditating in cemeteries, enduring the seasons naked (Digambara depiction) or with a single robe (Śvetāmbara). He bore attacks from villagers, animals, and the demon Saṅgamaka without anger or fear. This period culminated in utter detachment from the body and world |
| Kevala Jñāna (Omniscience) | At age 42, under a Sāla tree on the banks of the Rijupālikā River near Jṛmbhikagrāma (modern Jethi), he attained kevala jñāna — perfect, infinite knowledge encompassing all substances, times, and modes simultaneously. His body became free of perspiration, he remained motionless for long periods, and he spoke the *divyadhvani* (divine sound) that the ganadharas would translate into human language |
| Ministry | 30 years of teaching across Āṅga, Magadha, and Vajji territories. He attracted 44,000 monks, 36,000 nuns, 159,000 laymen, and 318,000 laywomen (Śvetāmbara count). His 11 chief disciples (ganadharas), led by [[Indrabhūti Gautama]], systematised his teachings. He engaged in debates with rival sramana teachers including Gosāla Maskarīputra (Ājīvika) and Śreṇika Bimbisāra (the Magadhan king) |
| Nirvāṇa (Final Liberation) | At age 72 (Śvetāmbara) or 70 (Digambara), in 527 BCE, at Pāvāpurī (modern Bihar). He achieved mokṣa — complete liberation from all karma, ending the cycle of birth and death. His soul became a siddha (perfected being), residing eternally at the summit of the universe. The date is commemorated as Dīpāvalī (adapted in Jain tradition as the original Dīvālī — "lamp festival" marking the light of his liberation) |

## Teachings & Doctrinal Contributions

Mahāvīra's teachings did not originate ex nihilo — they built upon the earlier Jain tradition established by [[Pārśva]] (23rd Tīrthaṅkara, c. 9th–8th century BCE). But Mahāvīra's contribution was decisive: he systematised, expanded, and transmitted the doctrine in the form that survives today.

### The Five Great Vows (Mahāvratas)

| Vow | Meaning | Implication |
|-----|---------|-------------|
| **Ahiṃsā** | Non-violence | Absolute non-harm in thought, word, and deed toward all living beings — including earth-bodies, water-bodies, fire-bodies, wind-bodies, and plant-bodies (not just animals and humans) |
| **Satya** | Truthfulness | Speaking only that which is true, beneficial, and pleasant |
| **Asteya** | Non-stealing | Taking nothing that is not freely given |
| **Brahmacarya** | Celibacy | Complete abstinence from all sensual pleasures (Mahāvīra expanded this from Pārśva's simpler vow of chastity within marriage) |
| **Aparigraha** | Non-attachment | Complete renunciation of all possessions, including emotional attachments to people and ideas |

These five vows for monks and nuns became the *aṇuvratas* (limited vows) for laypeople.

### Doctrinal Innovations

- **Anekāntavāda (Non-one-sidedness)** — Reality is complex and can be described from multiple viewpoints; no single statement captures the whole truth. This manifests as *syādvāda* (the doctrine of conditional predication) and *saptabhaṅgī* (seven-fold predication).
- **Classification of Knowledge** — Five types: *mati* (sensory), *śruta* (scriptural), *avadhi* (clairvoyance), *manaḥparyāya* (telepathy), and *kevala* (omniscience).
- **Karma as Matter (Karma-Paṇḍa)** — Unlike most Indian traditions that see karma as a metaphysical principle, Mahāvīra taught that karma is a literal, physical substance — fine particles of matter (*karma-varganā*) that attach to the soul (*jīva*) based on actions, passions, and intentions.
- **Nine Tattvas (Fundamental Principles)** — The framework for understanding bondage and liberation: jīva (soul), ajīva (non-soul), puṇya (merit), pāpa (sin), āśrava (influx of karmic matter), bandha (bondage), saṃvara (stoppage of influx), nirjarā (shedding of karma), and mokṣa (liberation).
- **Soul's Co-extensiveness with the Body** — The soul expands and contracts to fill the exact dimensions of the body it inhabits (*pradeśa* theory), a unique Jain position.
- **Rejection of a Creator God** — The universe is eternal and uncreated; no supreme being governs it. Each soul must achieve liberation through its own effort — no grace, no intercession.

### Mahāvīra's Fourfold Saṅgha

Mahāvīra established the *caturvidha saṅgha* — a fourfold community of:
1. **Monks** (*sādhus* / *munis*)
2. **Nuns** (*sādhvīs*)
3. **Laymen** (*śrāvakas*)
4. **Laywomen** (*śrāvikās*)

This structure — identical in principle to the Buddhist Saṅgha — ensured the teachings reached every level of society.

## In Jain Tradition

### Iconography

Mahāvīra is invariably depicted seated in lotus posture (padmāsana) or standing in meditation (kāyotsarga — the posture he held during his asceticism). His identifying emblem is the **lion** (sometimes a swastika), and his colour is **golden/yellow**. Key iconographic features:

- **Downward gaze** — absorbed in inner contemplation
- **Dhyāna mudrā** — hands resting in meditation
- **Clothing** — Śvetāmbara images show him with a small cloth below the navel; Digambara images show him completely naked, symbolising total non-attachment
- **Hair** — Śvetāmbara tradition shows him with short curly hair; Digambara shows him with a topknot (because his hair was pulled out at renunciation and grew back)

### In Śvetāmbara vs Digambara Traditions

The two major Jain sects diverge significantly in their Mahāvīra narratives:

| Aspect | Śvetāmbara | Digambara |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Clothing after renunciation** | Wore a single robe for 13 months, then went naked; celestial beings provided him with robes later | Always remained naked — absolutely non-attached from the moment of renunciation |
| **Marriage** | Married Yaśodā, fathered a daughter | Never married; remained wholly celibate throughout life |
| **Birth** | Born of Queen Triśalā after 15 auspicious dreams (shared with the Buddha's birth narrative) | Born of Queen Yaśodā (different mother); the 15 dreams appear differently |
| **Kevala Jñāna** | Attained kevala at age 42 while seated under a Sāla tree | Attained kevala while standing in kāyotsarga posture |
| **Ritual image** | Adorned with a crown and ornaments (as a spiritual king) | Unadorned, signifying total renunciation |

### Key Texts

- **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** — The earliest Jain canonical text; describes Mahāvīra's ascetic practices in vivid detail
- **Sūtrakṛtāṅga** — Contains teachings and debates between Mahāvīra and rival sramana teachers
- **Kalpa Sūtra** by Bhadrabāhu — The biographies of the Tīrthaṅkaras, including Mahāvīra, with the story of his birth and renunciation
- **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** by Hemacandra — The "Lives of the 63 Illustrious Men," a vast hagiographic compendium that includes Mahāvīra's full story

### Festivals

- **Mahāvīra Jayantī** (March–April) — Celebrates his birth; Jains fast, visit temples, and reenact his life
- **Dīpāvalī / Dīvālī** (October–November) — Marks his nirvāṇa (śvetāmbara tradition); lights are lit to symbolise the inner light of liberation, and the new Jain year begins the day after

### In Jain Philosophy

Mahāvīra's teachings are not seen as "revelation" from a divine source but as the rediscovery and teaching of eternal truths that are always present in the universe. Every Tīrthaṅkara teaches the same Dhamma; Mahāvīra's role was to revive it for the current age. His status as a historical figure is undisputed across all Jain sects — the only Tīrthaṅkara for whom we have corroborating non-Jain sources (including Buddhist texts that mention the "Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta" — the "Jina's follower from the Jñātṛka clan").

## Comparative Cross-Tradition

| Aspect | Mahāvīra (Jain) | The Buddha (Buddhist) | Kṛṣṇa (Hindu) |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|---------------|
| **Nature** | Human who attained omniscience through ascetic effort and became a Tīrthaṅkara (ford-maker) | Human who awakened to the truth of reality and became a Buddha (Awakened One) | Avatāra (divine descent) of Viṣṇu; fully divine while appearing human |
| **Path taught** | Self-effort through the five great vows, asceticism, meditation, and karmic purification | The Middle Way — avoiding extremes of asceticism and indulgence; Eightfold Path | Bhakti (devotion), karma (action), jñāna (knowledge), and surrender to divine will |
| **Ultimate goal** | Mokṣa — the soul's complete liberation from all karma, ascending to the siddha-śilā (abode of perfected beings) | Nibbāna — the blowing out of craving, ending rebirth; no abiding self | Mokṣa — liberation from saṃsāra, union with the divine (depending on school) |
| **View of the self** | The soul (jīva) is eternal, real, and individual — it exists, knows, and acts; liberation is its natural state freed from karma | Anattā (not-self) — no permanent, unchanging self exists; clinging to "self" is the root of suffering | Ātman is eternal, identical with Brahman (Ultimate Reality); liberation is realising this identity |
| **View of God** | No creator God; universe is eternal and self-regulating; Tīrthaṅkaras are teachers, not deities | No creator God; devas exist but are subject to rebirth, not salvific; the Dhamma is discovered, not revealed | Kṛṣṇa is Bhagavān (the Supreme Lord), the ultimate reality and source of all; through devotion to him one attains liberation |
| **Key practice** | Ahiṃsā (non-violence), fasting, meditation (especially kāyotsarga and dhyāna), confession and repentance (pratikramaṇa) | Mindfulness, concentration, virtue, the brahmavihārās (loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, equanimity) | Bhakti (devotion, loving worship), pūjā, chanting the name, and surrender (śaraṇāgati) |
| **Metaphysics** | Dualistic — jīva (soul) and ajīva (non-soul) are eternally separate; karma is physical matter that binds the soul | Non-dualistic epistemology — all conditioned things are impermanent; no permanent substance; the Abhidhamma analyses reality into ultimate dhammas | Non-dualistic (Advaita) or qualified non-dualistic (Viśiṣṭādvaita) or dualistic (Dvaita) — ātman seeks union with Brahman |
| **Sectarian variations** | Digambara vs Śvetāmbara — disagreement on Mahāvīra's nudity, marriage, female liberation | Theravāda vs Mahāyāna vs Vajrayāna — disagreement on the Bodhisattva ideal, emptiness, and Buddha-nature | Various Vaiṣṇava traditions — disagreement on Kṛṣṇa's relationship to the avatāra concept |
| **Historical period** | c. 599–527 BCE (traditional dates); contemporary of the Buddha | c. 563–483 BCE or c. 480–400 BCE (scholarly); contemporary of Mahāvīra | No historical date accepted — mytho-historical figure; appears in texts dating from c. 5th century BCE to 3rd century CE |
| **Afterlife** | The soul of a perfected being (siddha) goes to the siddha-śilā at the top of the universe, where it remains eternally conscious and blissful | No "soul" goes anywhere — Nibbāna is the end of the fire; what continues cannot be described in terms of existence or non-existence | The liberated ātman merges with Brahman (or attains Kṛṣṇa's abode, Goloka, or Vaikuṇṭha depending on the school) |
