# Vilna Gaon (הגאון מווילנה — Rabbi Elijah ben Solomon Zalman)

**Rabbi Elijah ben Solomon Zalman** (1720–1797) — known as the **Vilna Gaon** (the Genius of Vilna) or by the acronym **Gra** (הגר״א) — was the supreme Torah authority of the 18th century and the spiritual leader of the *Mitnagdim* (opponents of Hasidism). His encyclopedic commentaries span Tanakh, Mishnah, Talmud, Zohar, and Kabbalah, and his method of study reshaped Lithuanian Jewish learning for generations.

## Key Contributions

- **Master of All Torah:** The Vilna Gaon's commentaries cover virtually every corpus of Jewish sacred literature — Tanakh (with a precise, peshat-oriented approach), the Mishnah and Talmud (clarifying the plain meaning with extraordinary concision), the Zohar (demystifying its cryptic Aramaic), and the broader Kabbalistic tradition (delving into Lurianic cosmology).
- **Opposition to Hasidism:** As the leading voice of the Mitnagdim, the Gaon issued bans and polemics against the early Hasidic movement (particularly its doctrine of the *tzaddik* and its perceived departures from traditional study). His authority gave the anti-Hasidic position intellectual and moral weight across Lithuania and beyond.
- **The Gaon's Method:** He insisted on a return to rigorous, source-based study — stripping away pilpul (casuistic logic-chopping) and focusing on grammar, lexicography, and straightforward textual interpretation. His approach is the foundation of the *Lithuanian Yeshiva* tradition.
- **Scientific and Secular Learning:** The Gaon held that secular knowledge — mathematics, astronomy, geography, medicine — is essential for understanding the Torah correctly (he famously said that "any deficiency in secular knowledge leads to a corresponding deficiency in Torah knowledge"). He wrote on geometry and astronomy and encouraged the translation of scientific works into Hebrew.
- **Kabbalistic Commentary:** Unlike the Hasidic mystics, the Gaon approached Kabbalah through the lens of intense analytic study. His commentary on the Zohar and his *Sifra de-Tzniuta* (Book of Concealment) commentary remain foundational works in scholarly Kabbalah.

## Life & Legacy

- Born in Sialiec (near Vilna, now Vilnius, Lithuania), he demonstrated prodigious memory and understanding as a child — he was said to have memorised the entire Talmud by age 7
- He refused all official rabbinic positions, preferring to study in seclusion; he supported himself modestly while his fame spread
- His disciples included Rabbi Chaim Volozhin, who founded the Volozhin Yeshiva (the mother of all Lithuanian yeshivas), and Rabbi Shneur Zalman of Liadi (who eventually broke with the Gaon's opposition to become the founder of Chabad — though he remained personally devoted to the Gaon)
- The Gaon seriously considered moving to Eretz Yisrael; a small group of his disciples (the *Perushim*) immigrated in the early 19th century, laying the groundwork for the later Lithuanian community in Jerusalem
- His opposition to Hasidism did not prevent Hasidic leaders from revering his scholarship; the Lubavitcher Rebbe and other Hasidic masters quote his Kabbalistic works
- Died in Vilna in 1797; his tomb in the Vilna cemetery was a pilgrimage site until the cemetery was destroyed by the Soviets
