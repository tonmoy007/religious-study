# Solomon & Sulayman — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

David's son Solomon is the wisdom-king of both scriptures, and both tell of his
encounter with the **Queen of Sheba**. But the *kind* of wisdom differs: the
Bible's Solomon is a shrewd human judge (and, later, a cautionary tale); the
Qur'an's Sulayman commands **jinn, animals, and the wind** as a sinless prophet.
A **genealogical** connection (see [[David and Dawud - A Figure Traced]]).

## Wisdom: the courtroom vs. the cosmos

> [!quote] 1 Kings 3 — wisdom as legal insight
> **3:25** — "And the king said, **Divide the living child in two**, and give half to the one, and half to the other."
> **3:26** — "Then spake the woman whose the living child was… for **her bowels yearned upon her son**… give her the living child, and in no wise slay it."

Solomon's wisdom in the Bible is **psychological and judicial** — the famous
ruse that reveals the true mother by her compassion.

> [!quote] Qur'an, An-Naml 27 — wisdom as command over creation
> **27:16** — "O mankind! Lo! we have been taught **the language of birds**…"
> **27:18** — "when they reached the Valley of the Ants, an **ant exclaimed**: O ants! Enter your dwellings lest Solomon and his armies crush you…"

Sulayman's wisdom is **supernatural dominion**: he understands ants and birds,
musters armies of *jinn*, and rides the wind. The Bible's Solomon is the wisest
of *men*; the Qur'an's is master of the unseen creation.

## The Queen of Sheba: the shared scene, reimagined

> [!quote] 1 Kings 10 — a state visit
> **10:1–2** — "the **queen of Sheba** heard of the fame of Solomon… she came to prove him with **hard questions**… with camels that bare spices, and very much gold."

The Bible's queen is a foreign monarch dazzled by Solomon's wealth and wisdom;
she praises his God and goes home. The Qur'an turns the visit into a **drama of
conversion**:

> [!quote] Qur'an, An-Naml 27 — the throne and the glass floor
> **27:23** — "I found a woman ruling over them… and hers is a **mighty throne**."
> **27:44** — "she saw it she deemed it a pool and **bared her legs**. (Solomon) said: Lo! it is a hall, made smooth, of **glass**. She said: My Lord!… I **surrender with Solomon unto Allah**, the Lord of the Worlds."

In the Qur'an the queen worships the sun (27:24), Solomon tests her with a
glass floor she mistakes for water, and she **converts to monotheism** — the
whole point of the episode. The Bible has no glass floor and no conversion.

## The divergence: the falling king vs. the sinless prophet
As with [[David and Dawud - A Figure Traced|his father David]], the Bible ends
Solomon's story in **failure**: his foreign wives turn his heart to other gods,
and God tears the kingdom from his son (1 Kings 11). The Qur'an's Sulayman has
**no such fall** — he is a righteous prophet throughout, again reflecting the
Islamic doctrine of prophetic sinlessness (*ʿiṣma*).

| | Hebrew Bible | Qur'an |
|---|---|---|
| Wisdom | judicial insight (the divided baby) | command of birds, ants, jinn, wind |
| Queen of Sheba | royal visit; she admires & departs | she **converts** (the glass-floor test) |
| Powers | exceptional human wisdom & wealth | dominion over the unseen creation |
| His end | **idolatry**; the kingdom divided | a **sinless prophet** to the end |

Same king, two visions of greatness: human wisdom that can still corrupt vs.
divinely granted mastery that never does.
