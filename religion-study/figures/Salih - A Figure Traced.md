# Ṣāliḥ — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · an **Arabian-prophet deep dive**

Ṣāliḥ is one of the three "Arabian prophets" — figures the Qur'an treats as major messengers but who have **no biblical counterpart**. He was sent to the ancient people of **Thamūd** in north-west Arabia (modern-day Madāʾin Ṣāliḥ / Hegra), a tribe famed for carving houses into rock cliffs.

See [[The Arabian Prophets - Figures the Bible Lacks]] for the comparative framework linking all three Arabian prophets.

## The message and the she-camel

Ṣāliḥ's story is dominated by the miraculous **she-camel** (*nāqat Allāh*, "the camel of God") — a test from God that his people either let graze freely or kill, bringing destruction upon themselves.

> [!quote] Hud 11:61, 64–65
> **11:61** — "And unto (the tribe of) **Thamud** (We sent) their brother **Salih**… Serve Allah, ye have no other Allah save Him."
> **11:64** — "This is the **camel of Allah**, a token unto you, so suffer her to feed… and **touch her not with harm**."
> **11:65** — "But they **hamstrung her**… Enjoy life in your dwelling-place three days! This is a threat that will not be belied."

The she-camel appears as a divine sign with a share of the water supply (54:28): one day for the camel, the next for the people. When the most wretched among them hamstrings her (91:12), Thamūd is destroyed by a **single Shout** (or earthquake/blast).

## Archaeological connection

The Thamūd are linked to the Nabataean site of **Madāʾin Ṣāliḥ** (Hegra) in modern Saudi Arabia — a city of monumental rock-cut tombs. The Qur'an's description of a people who "carved houses in the rock" (7:74, 15:82) matches this archaeological landscape. Hegra is now a UNESCO World Heritage site.

## Why Ṣāliḥ matters for cross-scripture study

Ṣāliḥ demonstrates that the Qur'an's prophetic landscape is not confined to biblical geography. It asserts that God sent warners to **Arabian peoples** whose history is independently preserved in Arabian memory, and the punishment story template (warn-reject-destroy) ties these Arabian narratives to the same divine pattern found in [[Noah and Nuh - A Figure Traced|Nūḥ]] and [[Lot and Lut - A Figure Traced|Lūṭ]].
