# Gurū Har Rāi (Har Rāi)

> Born: 1630 CE (16 January 1630), Kiratpur Sāhib, Punjab
> Died: 6 October 1661 CE, Kiratpur Sāhib, Punjab (aged ~31)
> Title: Seventh Sikh Guru (1644–1661); the Healer; the Herbalist of Kiratpur
> Birth Name: Har Rāi (of the Sodhi sub-caste of Khatrīs)
> Era: Mughal Empire under Shāh Jahān and Aurangzeb

**Gurū Har Rāi** (1630–1661 CE) was the seventh of the ten Sikh Gurus, serving from 1644 until his premature death in 1661. Born the son of Gurū Har Gobind (the sixth Guru, who introduced the concept of Mīrī-Pīrī — the doctrine of temporal and spiritual authority), Har Rāi inherited a Guruship that was already deeply entangled with the Mughal imperial politics that would define the Sikhs' 17th-century struggle. Installed at the age of fourteen, he is remembered in Sikh tradition primarily for his **vast herb garden and dispensary at Kiratpur**, where he cultivated medicinal plants from across India and beyond, treating the sick regardless of caste, creed, or religion — earning him the enduring popular epithet "the Healer Guru." Theologically, his Guruship was a period of cautious consolidation: he maintained the Sikh army his father had raised (keeping some 2,200 cavalry) but adopted a lower political profile than the militarily assertive Har Gobind, focusing instead on the spiritual and literary life of the community. His most consequential political act was his **sheltering of Dārā Shikoh**, the Mughal crown prince and Sufi scholar, when Dārā was pursued by Aurangzeb during the Mughal war of succession (1658–1659) — a gesture of interfaith humanity that brought Aurangzeb's hostility upon the Sikhs and nearly resulted in the Guru's destruction. Har Rāi died at Kiratpur in 1661, appointing his youngest son, **Gurū Har Kriśan** (then only five years old), as his successor — passing over his elder son Rām Rāi, who had defied the Guru by currying favour with Aurangzeb.

## Biography

| Event | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Birth** | 16 January 1630 (Samvat 1687, according to Sikh tradition) in **Kiratpur Sāhib**, a town on the Sutlej River in the foothills of the Shivalik range, in what is now Rupnagar district, Punjab, India. The region was then under the control of the hill chieftains of the Shivaliks, nominally subordinate to the Mughal Empire |
| **Family Background** | Son of **Gurū Har Gobind** (the sixth Sikh Guru) and **Mātā Damodarī** (also known as Mātā Nānakī or Mātā Sulakkhṇī — sources vary). He was the second of Har Gobind's sons; his elder brother **Bābā Gurdittā** (who had been given charge of the Sikh mission in the Malwa region) died in 1638, leaving Har Rāi as the eldest surviving son. The family were Sodhī Khatrīs — the hereditary lineage established by Gurū Rām Dās. He had two younger brothers: **Bābā Tegh Bahādur** (who would later become the ninth Guru) and **Bābā Suraj Mal** (who died young) |
| **Childhood & Education** | Raised in the martial-spiritual environment of Kiratpur, where his father Gurū Har Gobind maintained a court that blended military discipline with devotional practice. Har Rāi was trained in horsemanship, swordsmanship, and archery — the martial arts of the Sikh *sainā* (army) — alongside the study of Sikh scripture, Persian, and medicine. From an early age he showed a keen interest in **botany and pharmacology**, spending long hours in the gardens surrounding Kiratpur learning the properties of herbs from the travelling ascetics, Sūfī pīrs, and vaidyas (traditional Ayurvedic physicians) who passed through the Guru's darbār |
| **Installation as Guru** | 26 February 1644 (Phālgun Sūdī 10, according to Sikh tradition), at Kiratpur Sāhib. Gurū Har Gobind, feeling his end approaching, tested his sons: he asked who would **serve the sangat** (the congregation) selflessly. Har Rāi was the first to step forward, saying, "Service to the Guru's people is my life's purpose." Har Gobind placed five copper coins and a coconut before him — the traditional symbols of Guruship transfer — and declared him the seventh Guru. Har Rāi was fourteen years old. His younger brother **Tegh Bahādur** (aged twenty-three) was present; the two brothers had an affectionate relationship, and Tegh Bahādur would go on to serve as a devoted Sikh under Har Rāi's Guruship before his own appointment as the ninth Guru decades later |
| **Marriage & Children** | Har Rāi married **Mātā Kishan Devī** (also known as Mātā Sulakkhṇī). The couple had two sons: **Rām Rāi** (born 1646) and **Har Kriśan** (born 1656). Both would play fateful roles in the succession: Rām Rāi, the elder, fell into disgrace for siding with Aurangzeb; Har Kriśan, the younger, succeeded as the eighth Guru at age five. The tension between the two brothers — one compromised by imperial politics, the other an innocent child-Guru — would tear the Sikh community during the brief Guruship of Har Kriśan |
| **The Herb Garden of Kiratpur** | The most distinctive feature of Har Rāi's Guruship was his vast **medicinal herb garden and free dispensary** at Kiratpur. The garden — known as **Guru kī Bāg** or **Har Rāi kī Bāg** — covered several acres on the banks of the Sutlej and contained hundreds of species of medicinal plants collected from the Himalayan foothills, the plains of Hindustan, and even from as far as Persia and Central Asia via travelling merchants and Sūfī missionaries. Har Rāi himself tended the garden, studied the properties of each plant, compounded medicines, and personally treated the sick who came to him. He established a *dawā-khānā* (dispensary) where free medicine and dietary advice were given to all visitors, regardless of religion or caste. This garden is the origin of the Guru's popular epithet as "the Healer" and is unique in Sikh history — no other Guru is remembered primarily for medical work |
| **The Sikh Army Under Har Rāi** | Gurū Har Gobind had raised a Sikh army of several thousand and fought four pitched battles against the Mughals. Har Rāi inherited this army but chose a **defensive posture** rather than offensive campaigning. He maintained a force of some **2,200 cavalry** (the traditional Sikh *sainā*) and kept the Guru's armoury stocked, but instructed his followers not to provoke Mughal forces or the hill rajas. His policy was one of deterrence without aggression — maintaining the capacity for self-defence while avoiding the open warfare that had marked his father's later years. This cautious approach has sometimes been criticised as a retreat from Har Gobind's militancy, but it likely reflected both Har Rāi's temperament (a healer rather than a warrior) and his strategic reading of the political situation: the Mughal Empire under Shāh Jahān was at its territorial and military peak, and an open revolt would have been suicidal |
| **Sheltering of Dārā Shikoh (1658)** | The defining political event of Har Rāi's Guruship. During the Mughal war of succession (1658–1659), the crown prince **Dārā Shikoh** — the eldest son of Emperor Shāh Jahān, a noted scholar of comparative religion and Sūfī mysticism — was defeated by his younger brother Aurangzeb at the battles of Samugarh (May 1658) and Deorai (April 1659). Dārā fled eastward, seeking refuge. He arrived at **Kiratpur** with a small retinue, and Har Rāi, moved by Dārā's plight and by the prince's known sympathy for interfaith dialogue, **sheltered him and gave him safe passage** through Sikh territory. The Guru provided Dārā with an escort and provisions to help him reach Sindh and then Afghanistan, where he hoped to raise a new army. Aurangzeb, upon learning of the Guru's assistance to his rival, was furious. He demanded that Har Rāi appear before him in Delhi to explain himself — a summons the Guru deflected through a series of diplomatic exchanges. The episode permanently soured relations between the Sikh Guruship and the Mughal court under Aurangzeb, setting the stage for the persecution of the ninth Guru, Tegh Bahādur (1675), and ultimately for the Khalsa's armed struggle |
| **The Encounter with Aurangzeb's Messenger** | Sikh tradition preserves a famous episode that captures the Guru's diplomatic skill. Aurangzeb sent a messenger — **Khawājā Ambar** — to summon Har Rāi to Delhi. The Guru refused to travel to the imperial court but sent his son **Rām Rāi** to represent him, along with a gift. When Rām Rāi arrived, Aurangzeb tested him by asking him to interpret a verse from the Guru Granth Sāhib (a verse of Gurū Nānak's that states: "The Muslim's grave turns to clay, the Hindu's corpse turns to ashes —" implying the ultimate equality of bodily end). Aurangzeb asked whether the Guru claimed that Muslim and Hindu dead were equal. Rām Rāi, to ingratiate himself with the emperor, altered the interpretation, saying the verse referred not to the body but to the soul — thereby compromising the Guru's teaching. When Har Rāi heard of this he was deeply distressed, and **he refused to see Rām Rāi again**, disowning him for his dishonesty and cowardice. He appointed his younger son **Har Kriśan** as his successor instead |
| **Composition of Hymns** | Unlike most of the other Gurus — Nānak (974 hymns), Aṅgad (62), Amar Dās (907), Rām Dās (679), Arjan (2,218), and Gobind Siṅgh (extensive compositions in the Dasam Granth) — **Gurū Har Rāi did not compose any hymns** that are preserved in the Guru Granth Sāhib. The canon of Sikh scripture had been closed by Gurū Arjan in 1604, and later Gurus — beginning with Har Gobind — did not add their own compositions to the Ādi Granth. Har Rāi's contribution was not literary but institutional: he preserved and transmitted the scripture as received, maintained the community's organisation, and added the distinctive dimension of healing service |
| **Death** | 6 October 1661 CE (or 20 October 1661, depending on calendar) at **Kiratpur Sāhib**, aged approximately thirty-one. He had been suffering from a severe illness (some traditions say dysentery or an infection contracted during his botanical work; others suggest he was poisoned by Mughal agents, though this is unconfirmed). As he lay dying, he summoned his younger son **Har Kriśan** (only five years old) and placed the five coins and coconut before him, declaring him the eighth Guru. He instructed his elder son Rām Rāi not to be admitted to his presence. His last words to the congregation were a charge to obey the child-Guru as they had obeyed the Gurus before him. His body was cremated at Kiratpur, and a memorial platform (*chābar*) marks the site to this day |

## The Herb Garden and Healing Tradition

Gurū Har Rāi's herb garden — the **Guru kī Bāg** at Kiratpur — is the most distinctive and personal legacy of his Guruship, and it is the foundation of his being remembered as "the Healer" in Sikh popular piety.

### The Garden

According to Sikh tradition, the garden was established on several acres of land near the Sutlej River, where the soil was rich and the water table high. Har Rāi organised the garden into sections:

| Section | Purpose | Plants (Traditional) |
|---------|---------|----------------------|
| **Medicinal herb beds** | Cultivation of healing plants for the free dispensary | Tulsi (Ocimum sanctum), neem (Azadirachta indica), ashwagandhā (Withania somnifera), brahmī (Bacopa monnieri), śatāvarī (Asparagus racemosus), giloy (Tinospora cordifolia), and dozens of Himalayan herbs brought by travelling ascetics |
| **Fruit and nut groves** | Nutritional medicine and the langar kitchen | Almonds, walnuts, mangoes, pomegranates, and the medicinal fruit of the bel (Aegle marmelos) |
| **Exotic plants** | Specimens from distant lands | Herbs from Persia (including varieties of rose used in syrup — *gulāb*), Central Asian fruits, and plants brought by Sikh traders returning from Kabul, Kandahar, and Samarkand |
| **The dispensory (dawā-khānā)** | A permanent building where medicines were compounded, stored, and distributed free of charge | Spices, powders, decoctions, syrups, and ointments prepared according to both Ayurvedic and Unani (Greco-Islamic) medical traditions |

### The Healing Ethos

Har Rāi's medical work was not merely philanthropic — it was an expression of Sikh theology. Key principles:

- **Sebā (Selfless Service) as Medicine** — The Guru treated patients himself, washing wounds and preparing medicines with his own hands. This embodied the Sikh ideal that *sebā* is the highest form of devotion.
- **Treatment Without Discrimination** — The dispensary was open to everyone: Sikhs, Hindus, Muslims, and followers of other traditions. No one was turned away for inability to pay.
- **Integration of Medical Traditions** — Har Rāi was trained in both **Āyurveda** (the traditional Indian system based on the three *doṣas*: vāta, pitta, kapha) and **Yunānī** (the Greco-Islamic system based on the four humours: blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile). His garden included plants used in both traditions, and his dispensary prescribed treatments from both frameworks — a synthesis that reflects the Sikh Guru tradition's characteristic openness to multiple streams of knowledge.
- **Holistic Care** — The Guru did not dispense herbs alone: he offered dietary advice, spiritual counselling (the recitation of Gurbānī for the sick), and, where appropriate, referral to the langar for nutritional support. Healing was understood as a restoration of balance — physical, mental, and spiritual.

### The Healing Tradition in Sikh Memory

Though no other Sikh Guru is known primarily as a healer, Har Rāi's herb garden established a lasting tradition of Sikh medical philanthropy. The **Dawā-khānā** (free dispensary) is a standard institution in most historic Sikh gurdwārās; the **Shiromaṇī Gurdwārā Parbandhak Committee** runs a network of hospitals and medical colleges across Punjab; and the **Khalsa Aid** and **Sikh Relief** organisations continue Har Rāi's legacy by providing free medical care in disaster zones worldwide. In this sense, the healer Guru's work institutionalised something that outlasted his short thirty-one-year life: the Sikh commitment to health as a spiritual practice.

## Relations with the Mughal Empire

Har Rāi's Guruship (1644–1661) spanned the end of Shāh Jahān's reign and the beginning of Aurangzeb's. The period was one of transition from a relatively tolerant Mughal religious policy to the overt Islamisation under Aurangzeb.

### Under Shāh Jahān (1644–1658)

For the first fourteen years of Har Rāi's Guruship, the Mughal Emperor was **Shāh Jahān** — a ruler known more for his architectural patronage (the Taj Mahal) and military campaigns than for religious bigotry. The Sikh community during this period enjoyed relative peace. Shāh Jahān's eldest son and designated heir, **Dārā Shikoh**, was a close intellectual ally of the Sikhs: he had studied the Upaniṣads and the Guru Granth Sāhib, written a Persian work (the *Majmaʻ al-Baḥrayn* — "The Mingling of Two Oceans") comparing Sūfī and Vedāntic thought, and had visited the Sikh Gurus. The Guru's relations with the empire were therefore cautious but not hostile.

### Under Aurangzeb (1658–1661)

Everything changed with Aurangzeb's usurpation. The three years from Aurangzeb's accession (1658) to Har Rāi's death (1661) were marked by direct imperial hostility:

- **Dārā Shikoh incident (1658–1659)** — The Guru's sheltering of Aurangzeb's rival was interpreted as an act of war.
- **Aurangzeb's summons** — The Emperor demanded the Guru's presence in Delhi; Har Rāi sent his son Rām Rāi instead, a diplomatic deflection that bought time but did not resolve the enmity.
- **Rām Rāi's submission** — The Guru's own son compromised the Sikh position by altering the interpretation of Gurbānī to please Aurangzeb. This set a dangerous precedent: the imperial court could now pressure Guru-family members to act against the Guru himself.
- **Aurangzeb's suspicion** — Aurangzeb's court chronicles suggest he viewed the Sikh Gurus as a potential fifth column — a well-armed, well-organised, loyal-to-their-own leader community that could not be controlled through the normal mechanisms of imperial administration. The Sikhs' military capacity (the 2,200 cavalry) was a standing affront to Mughal sovereignty.

The crisis that Har Rāi managed through diplomacy was deferred to his successors. Under the child-Guru Har Kriśan (1661–1664), the pressure intensified; under Gurū Tegh Bahādur (1664–1675), it culminated in the Guru's public execution in Delhi.

## The Succession Crisis

Har Rāi's death at thirty-one triggered the first succession crisis within the **Sodhi hereditary line** — a crisis that exposed the tensions between spiritual authority, family ambition, and imperial manipulation.

### The Two Sons

| Son | Birth Year | Character | Role in the Crisis |
|-----|-----------|-----------|--------------------|
| **Rām Rāi** | 1646 | Ambitious, politically savvy, willing to compromise with the Mughals to secure power | Defied Har Rāi by altering Gurbānī at Aurangzeb's court; was disowned by the Guru; set up a rival Guruship at Dehradun (where his descendants continue as a separate sect — the Rām Rāiyā or Rāvāls) |
| **Har Kriśan** | 1656 | Still a child (age 5) at the time of succession; innocent, unsullied by politics | Designated by Har Rāi as the eighth Guru; had to flee Kiratpur to escape Mughal pressure; died of smallpox at age 8 in Delhi |

### Har Rāi's Decision

By passing over his elder son in favour of his younger, Har Rāi followed the precedent of his predecessors (Nānak passing over Srī Chand and Lakhmī Dās; Aṅgad passing over Piṇḍ and Dātu; Rām Dās passing over Prithi Chand; Har Gobind passing over the sons of the late Gurdittā). The consistent pattern is clear: **the Guru's authority is not determined by primogeniture but by spiritual fitness, as discerned by the living Guru**. In Har Rāi's case, the fitness was negative — Rām Rāi had demonstrated unfitness by his dishonesty — but the principle was the same.

### The Aftermath

The succession of a five-year-old child was unprecedented and deeply problematic. Rām Rāi, supported by the Mughal authorities, claimed the Guruship and established a rival seat at **Dehradun** (where he was granted land by Aurangzeb). The Sikh community was divided for the first time in its history between an authentic Guru (Har Kriśan, backed by the congregation at Kiratpur) and a pretender (Rām Rāi, backed by the Mughals). The crisis was only resolved when Gurū Tegh Bahādur (Har Gobind's son, Har Rāi's younger half-brother) was recognised as the ninth Guru after Har Kriśan's death — an intervention that restored the unity of the community and closed the Rām Rāi schism.

## Legacy

### The Healing Heritage

Gurū Har Rāi's herb garden at Kiratpur is the origin of the Sikh tradition of free medical philanthropy — a tradition that continues today in the network of Sikh-run charitable hospitals (such as the Sri Guru Har Rāi Sāhib Charitable Hospital in Amritsar, and the Guru Har Rāi Sāhib Medical Mission). The principle that **healing is a form of worship** — that tending the body of the sick is tending the presence of God — is directly traceable to his Guruship. In a tradition where the langar addresses hunger, the dispensary addresses illness: the body and the soul are cared for as one.

### The Dārā Shikoh Episode and Sikh-Mughal Relations

The sheltering of Dārā Shikoh is the single most consequential political act of Har Rāi's Guruship, though it is often underemphasised in standard Sikh historiography. Dārā Shikoh represents the **alternative Mughal tradition** — the inclusive, syncretistic, Sūfī-Vedāntic cosmopolitanism that could have shaped Indo-Islamic civilisation had he, not Aurangzeb, won the war of succession. By sheltering Dārā, Har Rāi aligned the Sikh Guruship with that alternative tradition — and paid the price when Aurangzeb triumphed. The episode foreshadowed the Sikhs' 18th-century struggle: they were fighting not merely against Mughal rule but for a pluralistic vision of India that Aurangzeb's policies were systematically dismantling.

### The Rām Rāi Schism

Though often treated as a footnote, the Rām Rāi schism is historically significant. It created the first permanent **sectarian division** within the Sikh tradition (the Rām Rāiyā, also called Rāvāls or Rām Rāi Sikhs), who survive to this day in Dehradun and surrounding areas. The schism demonstrated that Mughal patronage could be used to divide the Sikh community — a tactic that Aurangzeb's successors would use again. It also established a precedent that the legitimate Guru might be a **child** — a radical idea that challenged the notion that only a mature, battle-tested leader could guide the community. The child-Guru Har Kriśan proved that spiritual authority does not depend on age or physical strength.

### In Sikh Memory

| Aspect | Memory in Sikh Tradition |
|--------|-------------------------|
| **Popular epithet** | "Dārdī dā Dārū" (The Medicine for the Afflicted) — a phrase still used in Punjabi for anyone who brings healing to the suffering |
| **Iconography** | Usually depicted seated in his garden, holding a medicinal herb or a bowl of medicine, with the Kiratpur hills visible in the background. Unlike the martial imagery of Har Gobind (with swords) or the intellectual imagery of Arjan (with the Granth), Har Rāi's iconography is **pastoral and healing** — the gentle Guru, the herbalist-physician |

| **Assessment** | Har Rāi is sometimes overshadowed by the dramatic figures around him — the warrior Har Gobind, the martyr Arjan, the martyr Tegh Bahādur, and the Khalsa-founder Gobind Siṅgh. But within the Sikh community, he is deeply loved as "the healer" — the Guru who did not fight, did not compose scripture, did not build monuments, but simply **healed the sick**. In an era of increasing persecution, his gentle presence offered a different model of what the Guru could be: not a warrior or a poet, but a servant who bound wounds and planted gardens |

### Comparison with Other Healing Figures in Religious Traditions

| Dimension | Gurū Har Rāi (Sikh) | The Buddha's Āyurvedic monks (Buddhist) | Jesus' healing ministry (Christian) | Asclepius (Greco-Roman) |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Model** | Guru as herbalist-physician who personally compounds and dispenses medicine | Monastic community as provider of medical care; monks trained in Āyurveda | Jesus as charismatic healer; healing as a sign of the Kingdom of God | Healing as a divine art; Asclepius as the god of medicine |
| **Institution** | The Guru's dispensary and herb garden at Kiratpur — a free, open-to-all clinic | Monasteries established hospitals and *ārāma* (rest houses) for the sick | No permanent institution; healing was itinerant and charismatic | The Asclepieia — temple complexes where patients slept and received dream therapies |
| **Relation to theology** | Healing is *sebā* (selfless service) — an expression of devotion to God through care for the afflicted | Healing reduces suffering, a prerequisite for the spiritual path; the gift of medicine is a *pārāmitā* (perfection) | Healing is a sign of the Messiah; physical restoration mirrors spiritual restoration | Healing is a divine gift; Asclepius's art is a form of revealed knowledge |
| **Type of medicine** | Both Āyurvedic and Unanī — integrated, empirical, plant-based | Āyurvedic — based on the three *doṣas*, with extensive herbal pharmacopoeia | Primarily exorcistic and touch-based (laying on of hands); also clay and spittle cures | Dream incubation, herbal remedies, surgery (the Hippocratic-Oath tradition) |
| **Posthumous legacy** | The gurdwārā-based free dispensary tradition; Sikh medical missions | The Buddhist hospital tradition that spread to China and Sri Lanka; monastic medicine in Tibet | Christian missionary hospitals and the deaconess nursing movement | The Hippocratic corpus; the symbol of the serpent-entwined staff |
| **Core innovation** | A Guru who personally cultivates medicinal plants — the intersection of **spiritual authority and botanical knowledge** as a unified calling | The institutionalisation of healing as a **monastic duty** — every monastery should serve as a clinic for the poor | Healing as a **sign of restored relationship with God** — not merely physical recovery but cosmic reconciliation | The **temple-hospital** — a sacred space dedicated to healing as a form of worship |
