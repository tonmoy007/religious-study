# Gershom Scholem (גרשם שלום — Gershom Gerhard Scholem)

The founder of the modern academic study of Jewish mysticism (Kabbalah) and one of the most important Jewish intellectuals of the twentieth century. Scholem single-handedly transformed the study of Kabbalah from a neglected, often apologetic field into a rigorous historical discipline, reshaping the understanding of Jewish intellectual history in the process.

## Key Contributions

- ***Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism* (1941):** Based on his lectures at the Jewish Institute of Religion in New York, this landmark work surveys the development of Jewish mysticism from the Merkabah tradition of late antiquity through medieval Kabbalah, Lurianic mysticism, and Hasidism. It established the basic periodisation and conceptual framework for the study of Kabbalah and remains the most widely read introduction to the field.
- **Historical-Critical Study of Kabbalah:** Scholem insisted that Kabbalah must be studied with the same rigorous philological and historical methods applied to any other field of Jewish literature. His bibliographic detective work — identifying forgotten manuscripts, reconstructing the textual history of the Zohar, dating the Sefer ha-Bahir — laid the foundations for all subsequent scholarship.
- ***The Zohar and Its Historical Context*:** Scholem's studies of the Zohar argued that it was composed primarily by Rabbi Moses de León (c. 1280) rather than being an ancient text from the Tannaitic period, a thesis that sparked decades of scholarly debate. He identified the *Idra Rabba* and *Idra Zuta* as distinct literary strata within the Zoharic corpus.
- ***Sabbatai Sevi: The Mystical Messiah* (1957):** A monumental biography of the seventeenth-century false messiah Sabbatai Sevi, analysing Sabbatianism as a major historical force in Jewish history. Scholem controversially argued that the Sabbatian crisis was a pivotal event that paved the way for Jewish modernity, Haskalah, and Reform.
- **Historiography of Jewish Mysticism:** Scholem developed a powerful historical narrative in which mysticism, far from being a marginal or heterodox phenomenon, was a central creative force in Judaism — periodically erupting from the esoteric margins to reshape the normative tradition.

## Life & Legacy

- Born in Berlin to an assimilated Jewish family; his brother Werner was a Communist member of the Reichstag later murdered by the Nazis
- Rejected both assimilation and political Zionism in his youth, joining the Zionist student movement and eventually emigrating to Palestine in 1923
- Became the first professor of Jewish mysticism at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem (1933), a position he held until his retirement
- Developed a famously critical relationship with [[Martin Buber]], whom he accused of romanticising and Christianising Hasidism
- Corresponded extensively with Walter Benjamin, Hannah Arendt, and Jorge Luis Borges; his letters are themselves a significant literary and intellectual document
