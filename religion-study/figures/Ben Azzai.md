# Ben Azzai (בן עזאי)

Shimon ben Azzai — known simply as **Ben Azzai** — was a Tanna of the second generation known for his extraordinary devotion to Torah study, his teaching on the enthusiastic performance of mitzvot, and his role in the famous story of the four sages who entered the *Pardes* (פַּרְדֵּס). Like Ben Zoma, he never received formal ordination, but he was regarded with such respect that his opinions are recorded as authoritative in the Mishnah. He is described as having been a student of Rabbi Akiva and a colleague of the leading sages of Yavneh.

Ben Azzai remained unmarried his entire life — a highly unusual choice in rabbinic Judaism, which considers marriage a divine commandment. When questioned about this, he replied that his soul was "in love with the Torah" and that the community could fulfil the commandment of procreation through him. This total dedication to study became both celebrated and somewhat controversial in rabbinic tradition, representing the ideal of single-minded devotion to the word of God.

## Key Contributions

- **The Pardes:** Ben Azzai was among the four sages (alongside Ben Zoma, Elisha ben Avuyah, and Rabbi Akiva) who entered the mystical *Pardes* (Chagigah 14b). He "looked and was harmed" — the Talmud describes that he gazed upon the divine glory and lost his sanity.
- **"Run to perform a light mitzvah":** His ethical teaching (Avot 4:2) exhorts enthusiasm for all commandments: "Run to perform a light mitzvah and flee from transgression; for one mitzvah leads to another, and one transgression leads to another."
- **The Great Principle of the Torah:** Ben Azzai taught that the verse "This is the book of the generations of Adam" (Genesis 5:1) is an even greater principle than "Love your neighbour as yourself" — because it grounds human dignity in our creation in the divine image (Sifra, Kedoshim 4:12).
- **Unmarried Sage:** His decision to remain unmarried to dedicate himself entirely to Torah became a paradigmatic story in rabbinic literature. The Sages debated whether his exceptional devotion justified departing from the commandment to "be fruitful and multiply."

## In Jewish Tradition

- The Mishnah (Yevamot 4:13) cites his debate with the Sages about whether an unmarried man may serve as a judge on the Sanhedrin — a debate that presupposed his own unmarried status
- He was known for his exceptional piety, such that a Heavenly Voice (בַּת קוֹל) would occasionally announce: "Ben Azzai is in the company of the righteous" (Berakhot 57b)
- Like Ben Zoma, he died young, and the two are often remembered together as "ben Zoma and ben Azzai" — a pair representing the heights and perils of total intellectual and spiritual devotion
- His interpretations of the Song of Songs are preserved in midrashic literature, reflecting his contemplative and mystical approach to Torah
