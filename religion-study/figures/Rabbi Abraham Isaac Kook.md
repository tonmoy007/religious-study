# Rabbi Abraham Isaac Kook (הראי"ה — HaRav Kook)

The first Ashkenazi Chief Rabbi of Palestine and one of the most original religious thinkers of the 20th century. Rav Kook developed a mystical, teleological philosophy of Jewish history in which secular Zionism — even its most anti-religious pioneers — was an unconscious instrument of divine redemption. He taught that the return to Zion was the beginning of the messianic era (*atchalta di-geulah*).

## Key Contributions

- ***Orot* (Lights) Series:** His major works — *Orot*, *Orot ha-Teshuvah*, *Orot ha-Emunah*, and *Orot ha-Kodesh* — form a mystical theology of Jewish history, nationhood, and redemption, blending Kabbalistic categories with modern philosophical sensibilities.
- ***Orot ha-Teshuvah* (Lights of Repentance, 1925):** A revolutionary work on *teshuvah* (repentance) presenting it not as guilt-ridden asceticism but as a cosmic return to one's essential self and to God — encompassing the entire Jewish people and all of creation.
- **Religious Zionism:** Rav Kook was the first major religious thinker to give theological legitimacy to secular Zionism. He argued that the souls of the secular pioneers acted out of an unconscious divine imperative; their apparent atheism was a stage in a larger dialectical process leading to full religious consciousness.
- **Chief Rabbinate and National Leadership:** As first Ashkenazi Chief Rabbi of Palestine (1921–1935), he built institutions of religious authority for the Yishuv and worked to bridge the gap between religious and secular Jews.
- **Inclusivity and Dialogue:** Rav Kook affirmed the spiritual value of all Jewish movements — from the most stringent Orthodox to the most radical secularists — seeing each as a partial truth within the larger unfolding of redemption.

## Life & Legacy

- Born in Griva (Latvia) to a family of *mitnagdic* (non-Hasidic) scholarly lineage, with side connections to Hasidic dynasties
- Studied at the Volozhin Yeshiva under the Netziv, receiving a classical Lithuanian yeshiva education
- Served as rabbi in Zaumel (Lithuania) and later in Boisk (Latvia) before immigrating to Jaffa in 1904
- Appointed Chief Rabbi of the New Yishuv in 1921 after the British Mandate was established
- Founded the Mercaz HaRav yeshiva in Jerusalem (1924) to train a new generation of religious Zionist leaders
- His son, Rabbi Zvi Yehuda Kook, carried his teachings forward and became the spiritual father of the religious settler movement after 1967
