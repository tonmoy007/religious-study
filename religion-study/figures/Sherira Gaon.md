# Sherira Gaon (שרירא גאון)

The Gaon of Pumbedita Academy and the father of Jewish historiography. Sherira's *Iggeret* (Epistle) is the single most important source for understanding the transmission of the Oral Law from Moses through the Tannaim, Amoraim, Savoraim, and Geonim — the chain that produced the Talmud.

## Key Contributions

- **The *Iggeret* (Epistle) of Sherira Gaon (987 CE):** Written in response to an inquiry from the Jewish community of Kairouan (modern Tunisia), this letter traces the history of the Mishnah, Talmud, and post-Talmudic rabbinic literature. It provides the chronology of the Sages, explains how the Talmud was compiled, and describes the transition from the Amoraic to the Geonic period. It is the foundation of all subsequent Jewish historiography.
- **Gaon of Pumbedita (968–998 CE):** He led the Pumbedita Academy for thirty years, one of the longest tenures of any Gaon. Under his leadership, Pumbedita regained its stature as the leading centre of Torah learning in Babylon.
- **Responsa Literature:** Sherira's halakhic responsa — many preserved in the Cairo Genizah — demonstrate his mastery of both Talmudic law and his role as a decisor for communities across the Jewish world.
- **Father of Rabbi Hai Gaon:** His son and successor Hai Gaon (939–1038) became the last and one of the greatest of the Geonim. Together they bridged the classical Geonic period and the emerging era of the Rishonim.

## Life & Legacy

- Served as Gaon during the twilight of the Babylonian Geonate, a period of decline under Buyid rule
- His *Iggeret* is the source for the traditional dating of the Sages and the Mishnah's redaction (c. 189 CE, contradicting modern scholarship which dates it c. 220 CE)
- The Epistle has been preserved in two recensions: the Spanish (longer, more detailed) and the French (shorter) — reflecting different manuscript traditions
- His scholarship was transmitted to Europe through the works of his son Hai and the arrival of Geonic texts in the nascent Ashkenazi and Sephardi centres
- The classic critical edition of the *Iggeret* was published by B. M. Lewin (1921)
