# Prophet Muhammad — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · the **Qur'an's central figure**

Muhammad ibn Abdullah (c. 570–632 CE) is the **Seal of the Prophets** (*Khātam al-Nabiyyīn*, 33:40) — the final messenger in the Abrahamic line. Unlike every other major figure traced in this collection, Muhammad has **no biblical counterpart**: he is not mentioned in the Old or New Testament, and the Qur'an is the only scripture that presents him as a prophet. His life, character, and mission form the narrative backbone of the Qur'an — not as a continuous biography but as a mosaic of scenes, commands, consolations, and divine validations scattered across the Meccan and Medinan revelations.

This note traces **Muhammad as a Qur'anic figure**: how the Book itself portrays him, what events of his life it records, and what roles it assigns him.

---

## Muhammad in the Qur'an: An Overview

Muhammad is mentioned by name **four times** in the Qur'an (3:144, 33:40, 47:2, 48:29), but he is referred to throughout by titles and epithets: *al-Rasūl* (the Messenger), *al-Nabī* (the Prophet), *al-Muzammil* (the Enshrouded), *al-Muddaththir* (the Cloaked), *al-Bashīr* (the Bringer of Good News), *al-Nadhīr* (the Warner), and more. The Qur'an speaks **to** him, **about** him, and **through** him — the Meccan surahs console him against persecution, the Medinan surahs instruct him in community leadership, and his very life-events become the occasions of revelation (*asbāb al-nuzūl*).

| Aspect | How the Qur'an Portrays Him |
|--------|----------------------------|
| His role | "We have sent you as a mercy to the worlds" ([[021 Al-Anbiyaa\|21:107]]) |
| His character | "And thou art of a high and noble character" ([[068 Al-Qalam\|68:4]]) |
| His mission | "O Prophet! Lo! We have sent you as a witness, a bearer of good tidings, and a warner" ([[033 Al-Ahzaab\|33:45]]) |
| His finality | "Muhammad is not the father of any one of your men, but he is the Messenger of Allah and the **Seal of the Prophets**" ([[033 Al-Ahzaab\|33:40]]) |
| His humanity | "Say: I am only a mortal like you — it is revealed to me that your God is One God" ([[018 Al-Kahf\|18:110]]) |

## The Prophet's Biography in the Qur'an

The Qur'an is **not a biography** — it does not narrate Muhammad's life in chronological order. But it preserves key moments, and these, together with the *sīra* (prophetic biography) tradition, allow us to reconstruct the arc the Qur'an itself witnesses.

### 1. The Orphan

> [!quote] Ad-Dhuhaa 93:6–8
> "Did He not find you an orphan and give you shelter? Did He not find you wandering and guide you? Did He not find you destitute and enrich you?"

The Qur'an alludes to Muhammad's early hardship with a tenderness that frames his entire mission: the God who sheltered the orphan now sends him as a prophet. The same surah consoles him during a pause in revelation ([[093 Ad-Dhuhaa]]).

### 2. The First Revelation

> [!quote] Al-Alaq 96:1–5
> "**Read!** In the name of your Lord who created — created man from a clot. **Read!** And your Lord is the Most Generous — who taught by the pen, taught man what he knew not."

The revelation began in the Cave of Hirāʾ near Mecca when the angel Gabriel commanded "Iqraʾ!" (Read/Recite). This event is commemorated in [[096 Al-Alaq]], traditionally considered the first surah revealed.

Immediately after came the command to **arise and warn**:

> [!quote] Al-Muddaththir 74:1–5
> "O thou **cloaked**! Arise and warn! Your Lord magnify! Your garments purify! And shun all filth!"

See [[074 Al-Muddaththir]].

### 3. The Meccan Period: Opposition and Consolation

The early Meccan revelations are marked by the Quraysh's rejection. The Qur'an responds with a series of intensely personal **consolation surahs** that reassure the Prophet:

| Surah | The Consolation |
|-------|-----------------|
| [[093 Ad-Dhuhaa]] | "Your Lord has not forsaken you, nor is He displeased" |
| [[094 Ash-Sharh]] | "Did We not expand your chest?… with hardship comes ease" |
| [[073 Al-Muzzammil]] | "O thou enshrouded! Stand the night in prayer" — spiritual armour |
| [[015 Al-Hijr]] | "We know that your breast is straitened by what they say. So glorify the praise of your Lord" (15:97–98) |
| [[068 Al-Qalam]] | "They would almost trip you up with their eyes… but it is a reminder for the worlds" (68:51–52) |

The polytheists demand miracles; the Qur'an insists Muhammad's **only miracle is the Qur'an itself**:

> [!quote] Al-Ankaboot 29:50–51
> "They say: Why are not signs sent down upon him from his Lord? Say: The signs are with Allah only, and I am only a plain warner. Is it not enough for them that We have sent down to you the Book which is recited to them?"

### 4. The Night Journey (Isrāʾ)

> [!quote] Al-Israa 17:1
> "Glorified be He who carried His servant by night from the **Inviolable Place of Worship** (Mecca) to the **Far Distant Place of Worship** (Jerusalem), the neighbourhood whereof We have blessed, that We might show him some of Our signs."

The Night Journey (*Isrāʾ*) and Ascension (*Miʿrāj*) — a single night in which Muhammad travelled from Mecca to Jerusalem and through the heavens — is referenced in the opening of [[017 Al-Israa]]. This event established the five daily prayers and cemented Jerusalem as Islam's first qibla (direction of prayer). See also [[053 An-Najm]] (53:13–18) for a parallel account of the heavenly ascent.

### 5. The Hijra: The Prophet as Statesman

The Qur'an does not narrate the emigration to Medina as a story, but it alludes to it:

> [!quote] At-Tawba 9:40
> "If you do not help him, Allah has already helped him when the disbelievers drove him forth — he being the second of two, when they two were in the cave, when he said to his companion: Grieve not, for Allah is with us."

The Medinan period transforms Muhammad's role: he is no longer only a warner but a **lawgiver, judge, commander, and head of state**. The great Medinan surahs ([[002 Al-Baqara]], [[003 Aal-i-Imraan]], [[004 An-Nisaa]], [[005 Al-Maaida]], [[008 Al-Anfaal]], [[009 At-Tawba]], [[024 An-Noor]], [[033 Al-Ahzaab]], [[047 Muhammad]], [[048 Al-Fath]], [[049 Al-Hujuraat]], [[058 Al-Mujaadila]], [[060 Al-Mumtahana]], [[065 At-Talaaq]], [[066 At-Tahrim]]) all address Muhammad as the leader of a community, legislating for its social, political, and spiritual life.

### 6. The Battles

The Qur'an records three major military engagements by name:

| Battle | Surah | Significance |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| **Badr** (624 CE) | [[008 Al-Anfaal]] | Divine intervention: angels fight alongside the believers; the spoils of war regulated |
| **Uḥud** (625 CE) | [[003 Aal-i-Imraan]] | A setback that tests true faith; the Prophet wounded; lessons in obedience |
| **Al-Aḥzāb** (The Trench, 627 CE) | [[033 Al-Ahzaab]] | The confederates besiege Medina; the hypocrites exposed; the tide turns |

The Qur'an's battle narratives are **theological** rather than strategic — they teach reliance on God, the testing of faith, and the ethical conduct of war.

### 7. The Treaty of Ḥudaybiyyah and the Conquest

> [!quote] Al-Fath 48:1–3
> "Lo! We have given you (O Muhammad) a **clear victory** — that Allah may forgive you of your sin, that He may complete His favour to you and guide you on a straight path, and that Allah may help you with a mighty help."

The Treaty of Ḥudaybiyyah (628 CE) — a diplomatic settlement that initially seemed a setback — is declared a "clear victory" in [[048 Al-Fath]] because it opened the door for the peaceful conquest of Mecca two years later.

> [!quote] An-Nasr 110:1–3
> "When **Allah's victory and the conquest** come, and you see men entering the religion of Allah in throngs — then glorify the praise of your Lord and seek His forgiveness. Lo! He is ever ready to accept repentance."

[[110 An-Nasr]] — the farewell surah — signals that the Prophet's mission is complete. Traditional exegesis holds that its revelation foretold Muhammad's impending death.

### 8. The Prophet's Marriages

Several Medinan surahs address the Prophet's household directly:

| Surah | Content |
|-------|---------|
| [[033 Al-Ahzaab]] | The hijab commandment for the Prophet's wives; their status as "Mothers of the Believers"; the marriage to Zaynab and the abolition of adoption as a legal fiction |
| [[066 At-Tahrim]] | A domestic incident in which the Prophet's wives seek his favour; the surah rebukes them and reaffirms the Prophet's authority |

### 9. The Farewell Sermon and Finality

The later Medinan surahs insist on Muhammad's **finality**:

> [!quote] Al-Maaida 5:3
> "This day I have perfected your religion for you, completed My favour upon you, and have chosen for you **Islam** as your religion."

This verse, revealed during the Farewell Pilgrimage (632 CE), is regarded as the culmination of the prophetic mission.

## The Prophet's Character in the Qur'an

The Qur'an paints Muhammad's character through divine commendation:

**1. The Noble Character** — "And thou art of a high and noble character" (*khuluq ʿaẓīm*, [[068 Al-Qalam|68:4]]). This single verse is the Qur'an's most direct character endorsement.

**2. The Compassionate** — "There has come to you a Messenger from among yourselves; **grievous to him is your suffering, anxious is he over you, gentle and merciful to the believers**" ([[009 At-Tawba|9:128]]).

**3. The Lenient Leader** — "It was by the mercy of Allah that you were lenient with them. If you had been harsh and hard-hearted, they would have dispersed from around you" ([[003 Aal-i-Imraan|3:159]]).

**4. The Human Prophet** — The Qur'an insists on Muhammad's humanity, rejecting any divine attributes:

> [!quote] Al-Kahf 18:110; Al-A'raaf 7:188
> "Say: I am only a mortal like you — it is revealed to me that your God is One God."
> "Say: I have no power to benefit or harm myself, except as Allah wills."

This contrasts sharply with Christian claims about Jesus — the Qur'an takes pains to keep Muhammad fully human, the messenger and never the message.

**5. The Rebuked Prophet** — In two instances the Qur'an directly reproaches Muhammad, with implications for his perfection (*ʿiṣma*):

| Incident | Surah | The Rebuke |
|----------|-------|------------|
| The blind man (Ibn Umm Maktūm) | [[080 Abasa]] | "He frowned and turned away because the blind man came to him" — Muhammad is gently corrected for prioritising powerful pagans over a poor blind believer |
| The prohibition of honey (or the Mariyah incident) | [[066 At-Tahrim]] | The Prophet forbids himself what God has permitted to please his wives; the surah instructs him to free himself from such oaths |

These passages, far from diminishing Muhammad, are understood as divine refinement — the Qur'an modelling prophetic humility.

## Muhammad in the Prophetic Chain

The Qur'an places Muhammad at the **culmination** of the Abrahamic prophetic line. He is not one prophet among many but the one who confirms and supersedes all previous revelations:

> [!quote] Aal-i-Imraan 3:84
> "Say: We believe in Allah and what has been revealed to us, and what was revealed to Abraham, Ishmael, Isaac, Jacob, and the tribes, and what was given to Moses and Jesus and the prophets from their Lord. We make no distinction between any of them."

> [!quote] Al-Ahqaaf 46:9
> "Say: I am not a novelty among the messengers — I do not know what will be done with me or with you. I follow only what is revealed to me, and I am only a plain warner."

Despite this claim of continuity, the Qur'an also asserts **supersession**: the Qur'an *confirms* the earlier scriptures but also *corrects* their corruption and *abrogates* their laws ([[005 Al-Maaida|5:48]], [[016 An-Nahl|16:89]]). Muhammad is both the heir of the prophetic tradition and its final arbiter.

## The Qur'an as Muhammad's Miracle

A distinctive claim the Qur'an makes about Muhammad is that **his only miracle is the Book itself**. Unlike Moses (the staff, the plagues) or Jesus (raising the dead, healing the blind), Muhammad is not portrayed performing supernatural wonders in the Qur'an. The polytheists demand signs:

> [!quote] Al-Israa 17:90–93
> "They say: We will not believe you until you cause a spring to gush from the earth for us… or you cause the sky to fall upon us in pieces… Say: Glorified be my Lord! Am I anything but a human messenger?"

The Qur'an's response is an **aesthetic and theological challenge** (*taḥaddī*): produce a single surah like it ([[002 Al-Baqara|2:23]], [[010 Yunus|10:38]]). In this, the Qur'an makes **itself** the enduring proof of Muhammad's prophethood — a miracle of language, preserved unchanged, accessible to every generation.

## Muhammad in the Hadith

While the Qur'an presents Muhammad as a prophet chosen by God, the **Hadith** — the vast corpus of his recorded sayings, actions, and approvals — reveals Muhammad as a **living human being**: a husband, father, friend, teacher, commander, judge, and ordinary man whose every word and gesture was preserved by his companions. The Hadith collections in this vault (see [[Hadith Library]]) contain over 40,000 narrations spanning nine books, all documenting the life of the man behind the revelation.

This section traces **Muhammad through the Hadith** — his character, his teachings, his daily life, and the image that emerges from the words he spoke and the example he set.

### The Prophet's Character: The Hadith Witness

The Hadith collections open a window onto Muhammad's character that the Qur'an only sketches. Key themes recur across thousands of narrations:

> [!quote] The Best of Character
> *Anas bin Malik said:* "I served the Prophet (ﷺ) for ten years, and he never said to me, 'Uff' (a minor word of complaint). He never said 'Why did you do that?' about something I did, nor 'Why didn't you do that?' about something I didn't do."
> — [Bukhari 6038](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Asking%20Permission)

> [!quote] Mercy to All Creation
> *Abu Huraira reported:* The Messenger of Allah (ﷺ) said: "I have been sent to perfect good character."
> — [Muwatta Malik 1614](scriptures/islam-hadith/07%20Muwatta%20Malik.md#The%20Mudabbar)

> [!quote] The Prophet's Humility
> *Anas bin Malik reported:* "The Prophet (ﷺ) used to visit the sick, attend funerals, ride a donkey, and accept the invitation of a slave. On the day of the battle of Banu Qurayza, he rode a donkey with a rope of date-palm fibres."
> — [Tirmidhi 2701](scriptures/islam-hadith/04%20Jami%60%20at-Tirmidhi.md#The%20Book%20on%20Faith)

> [!quote] The Prophet's Gentleness
> *Aisha reported:* "Whenever the Prophet (ﷺ) was given a choice between two matters, he would always choose the easier one, so long as it was not sin. If it was sin, he would be the farthest from it. He never took revenge for himself — but if Allah's limits were violated, he would take action for Allah's sake."
> — [Bukhari 3566](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Companions%20of%20the%20Prophet)

### The Five Pillars: The Prophet as Teacher

The Hadith collections record how Muhammad **taught the foundational practices of Islam** — the Five Pillars — often with memorable sayings that embed the practice in faith:

**The Testimony of Faith (Shahada):**
> The Prophet (ﷺ) said: "Whoever testifies that there is no god but Allah alone, with no partner, and that Muhammad is His servant and messenger, and that Jesus is the servant of Allah… and that Paradise is true and Hell is true — Allah will admit him into Paradise through whichever of its eight gates he wishes."
> — [Bukhari 3435](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Virtues%20and%20Merits%20of%20the%20Prophet%20%28pbuh%29%20and%20his%20Companions)

**Prayer (Salat):**
> *Narrated Abu Huraira:* I heard Allah's Messenger (ﷺ) saying, "If there was a river at the door of anyone of you, and he bathed in it five times a day, would you notice any dirt on him?" They said, "Not a trace of dirt would be left." The Prophet added, "That is the example of the five prayers with which Allah wipes out evil deeds."
> — [Bukhari 528](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Times%20of%20the%20Prayers)

**Charity (Zakat):**
> The Prophet (ﷺ) said: "Give charity without delay, for it stands in the way of calamity."
> — [Tirmidhi 589](scriptures/islam-hadith/04%20Jami%60%20at-Tirmidhi.md#The%20Book%20on%20Traveling)

**Fasting (Sawm):**
> The Prophet (ﷺ) said: "Whoever fasts Ramadan with faith and hope of reward, his past sins will be forgiven."
> — [Bukhari 38](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Belief)

**Pilgrimage (Hajj):**
> The Prophet (ﷺ) said: "Whoever performs Hajj for Allah's pleasure and does not have sexual relations with his wife, and does not do evil or sins, then he will return (after Hajj free from all sins) as if he were born anew."
> — [Bukhari 1521](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Hajj%20%28Pilgrimage%29)

### The Prophet as Husband and Father

Some of the most intimate portrayals of Muhammad come from his wife **Aisha**, who narrated hundreds of hadiths about their life together:

> [!quote] Aisha on the Prophet at Home
> *Aisha was asked:* "What did the Prophet (ﷺ) do in his house?" She replied: "He used to serve his family, and when the time for prayer came, he would go out for prayer."
> — [Bukhari 676](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Call%20to%20Prayers%20%28Adhaan%29)

> [!quote] Aisha on the Prophet's Love
> *Aisha reported:* "The Prophet (ﷺ) would drink from the same vessel as I did while I was menstruating, and he would eat meat from the bone I had bitten."
> — [Muslim 300](scriptures/islam-hadith/02%20Sahih%20Muslim.md#The%20Book%20of%20Faith)

> [!quote] The Prophet and His Grandchildren
> *Narrated Abu Huraira:* "The Prophet (ﷺ) kissed his grandson Al-Hasan bin 'Ali while Aqra' bin Habis was sitting nearby. Aqra' said, 'I have ten children and I have never kissed any of them.' The Prophet looked at him and said, 'Whoever does not show mercy will not be shown mercy.'"
> — [Bukhari 5997](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Asking%20Permission)

### The Prophet's Mercy and Forgiveness

The Hadith consistently portray Muhammad as a man who forgave even his worst enemies:

> [!quote] The Conquest of Mecca
> *Narrated Ibn 'Abbas:* "When the Prophet (ﷺ) conquered Mecca, he said to the Quraysh: 'What do you think I will do to you?' They said: 'We expect only good from you — you are a noble brother and the son of a noble brother.' He said: 'Go, for you are free.'"
> — [Musnad Ahmad](scriptures/islam-hadith/08%20Musnad%20Ahmad.md)

> [!quote] Forgiving the Bedouin
> *Anas bin Malik reported:* "A Bedouin urinated in the mosque, and the people rushed to stop him. The Prophet said: 'Leave him alone and do not interrupt him.' When he finished, the Prophet called for a bucket of water and poured it over the urine. Then he said to the Bedouin gently: 'These mosques are not places for urine and filth — they are for the remembrance of Allah, prayer, and recitation of the Qur'an.'"
> — [Bukhari 219](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Ablutions%20%28Wudu%27%29)

### The Prophet's Manner of Worship

The Hadith preserve the Prophet's prayer in exquisite detail — his standing, bowing, prostrating, supplicating — which Muslims have replicated for fourteen centuries:

> [!quote] The Prophet's Night Prayer
> *Al-Mughira bin Shu'ba reported:* "The Prophet (ﷺ) stood in prayer until his feet swelled. It was said to him: 'Allah has forgiven your past and future sins — why do you exert yourself?' He replied: 'Should I not be a grateful servant?'"
> — [Bukhari 4836](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Virtues%20of%20the%20Qur%27an)

> [!quote] The Prophet's Supplication
> *Ibn Mas'ud reported:* "The Prophet (ﷺ) used to say: 'O Allah, I ask You for guidance, piety, chastity, and self-sufficiency.'"
> — [Muslim 2721](scriptures/islam-hadith/02%20Sahih%20Muslim.md#The%20Book%20of%20Pilgrimage)

> [!quote] The Prophet's Tears
> *Anas reported:* "The Messenger of Allah (ﷺ) delivered a sermon the likes of which I had never heard. He said: 'If you knew what I know, you would laugh little and weep much.' Thereupon the companions covered their faces and sobbed."
> — [Bukhari 4621](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Prophetic%20Commentary%20on%20the%20Qur%27an%20%28Tafseer%20of%20the%20Prophet%20%28pbuh%29%29)

### The Prophet's Physical Description (Shamā'il)

The Hadith even preserve Muhammad's physical appearance — a genre known as *Shamā'il* that became a devotional tradition in its own right:

> [!quote] The Prophet's Appearance
> *Al-Bara' bin 'Azib reported:* "The Messenger of Allah (ﷺ) was of medium height, broad-shouldered, with hair reaching his earlobes. I saw him wearing a red garment — I never saw anything more beautiful."
> — [Bukhari 3551](scriptures/islam-hadith/01%20Sahih%20al-Bukhari.md#Companions%20of%20the%20Prophet)

> [!quote] The Prophet's Face
> *Jabir bin Samura reported:* "The Prophet (ﷺ) had a face that shone like the full moon."

> [!quote] The Prophet's Seal of Prophethood

> [!quote] The Prophet's Character on His Face

### The Prophet's Sayings on Character and Ethics

A vast number of hadiths are **ethical instructions** — the Prophet shaping the moral conscience of his community:

| Theme | Hadith | Source |

### How the Hadith Complements the Qur'an

| Dimension | In the Qur'an | In the Hadith |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| Muhammad's character | "And thou art of a high and noble character" (68:4) — a single, magnificent verse | Hundreds of narrations detailing his gentleness, humility, mercy, courage, and humour |
| The Prophet's inner life | Commands to pray, fast, give charity | His tears at night prayer, his joy at his grandchildren, his anger at injustice — the lived texture of piety |
| The Prophet as teacher | "He recites to them His verses, purifies them, and teaches them the Book and wisdom" (62:2) | Recorded classroom scenes: he taught with parables, repetition, gestures, and patience |
| The Prophet as human | "Say: I am only a mortal like you" (18:110) | We see him eating, sleeping, walking through the market, mending his own shoes, milking his goat |
| The Prophet's mercy | "A mercy to the worlds" (21:107) | He frees war captives for teaching Muslims to read; he forgives the woman who threw garbage on him; he visits the Jewish boy who served him when the boy falls ill |
