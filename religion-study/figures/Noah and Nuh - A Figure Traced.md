# Noah & Nuh — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

The [[Flood Narratives Compared]] note compares the **event** of the deluge;
this one traces **Noah the man** — and finds the same divergence that runs
through every prophet-king: the Bible's Noah is a patriarch in a covenant saga,
the Qur'an's Nuh is a long-suffering **warning-prophet** whose own son is lost.
A **genealogical** connection (see [[Shared Abrahamic Figures]]).

## Noah the preacher
The Qur'an gives Noah something the Bible barely does — a **preaching ministry**.
A whole surah ([[071 Nooh|Nuh, 71]]) is his sermon, pleading with a people who
will not listen across centuries (the Qur'an says he stayed among them "a
thousand years save fifty," 29:14):

> [!quote] Surah Nuh 71:1, 26
> **71:1** — "We sent Noah unto his people (saying): **Warn thy people** ere the painful doom come unto them."
> **71:26** — "And Noah said: My Lord! **Leave not one of the disbelievers** in the land."

Genesis, by contrast, gives Noah **almost no recorded speech at all** — he is the
silent man who "walked with God" (6:9) and simply *does* what he is told. The
Qur'an makes him an eloquent, rejected prophet; the Bible makes him an obedient
builder.

## The lost son — the Qur'an's signature divergence
The sharpest difference is *who is saved*. In Genesis, **all three of Noah's
sons** (Shem, Ham, Japheth) board the ark and survive — the flood reboots
humanity through Noah's whole family, and the genealogies of the nations descend
from them (Genesis 10).

The Qur'an tells a wrenching addition: **one of Noah's sons refuses to believe
and drowns**, despite his father's plea:

> [!quote] Hud 11:42–45
> **11:42** — "Noah cried unto his son — and he was standing aloof — **O my son! Come ride with us**, and be not with the disbelievers."
> **11:45** — "And Noah cried unto his Lord: My Lord! Lo! **my son is of my household**! Surely Thy promise is the truth…"

God's reply (11:46): "**He is not of thy household**; lo! he is of evil conduct."
Faith, not blood, defines who is saved — the same principle the Qur'an applies to
[[Abraham and Ibrahim - A Figure Traced|Abraham's "covenant includeth not
wrong-doers"]]. This recasts the flood from a story about preserving a *family*
into a story about the dividing line of *belief*.

## The covenant and the rainbow — the Bible's signature
What the Bible adds is the **covenant** afterward — the rainbow as an everlasting
sign that God will never again flood the earth:

> [!quote] Genesis 9 (the bow in the cloud)
> Genesis 9:13–16 — "I do set **my bow in the cloud**… for a token of a covenant… the **everlasting covenant** between God and every living creature."

This fits the Bible's larger pattern of **covenant-making** (Noah → Abraham →
Moses), a thread the Qur'an does not develop in the same way.

| | Genesis | Qur'an |
|---|---|---|
| Noah's voice | nearly silent; "walked with God" | a **preaching prophet** (whole Surah 71) |
| His sons | **all three saved** | one son **disbelieves and drowns** |
| Who is saved | Noah's **family** | the **believers** ("not of thy household") |
| Aftermath | the **rainbow covenant** (Gen 9) | a warning to later peoples |
| Resting place | Ararat | Al-Judi (see [[Flood Narratives Compared]]) |

## Same man, two roles
The Bible's Noah anchors a **covenant of renewed humanity**; the Qur'an's Nuh is
the first in its long line of **rejected warner-prophets** (Nuh → Hud → Salih →
Lut → Shu'aib), and the lost son makes faith, not lineage, the criterion of
salvation. The flood is the same; the *meaning* of the man who survived it is
not.
