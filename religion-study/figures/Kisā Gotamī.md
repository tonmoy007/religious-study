# Kisā Gotamī

> The grieving mother who became an arahant — a story of death's universality, the futility of clinging, and the peace that lies beyond sorrow.

> "I've gotten past the killing of [my] sons, have made that the end to [my search for] men. I don't grieve, I don't weep." — Kisā Gotamī, SN 5.3

---

## Biography

Kisā Gotamī (Sanskrit: *Kṛśā Gautamī*, Pali: *Kisā Gotamī*) was a young woman of Śrāvastī who married into a wealthy family. She is one of the most beloved figures in Buddhism, remembered for her profound grief at losing her only child and the powerful teaching she received from the Buddha that led to her awakening.

---

## The Story of the Mustard Seed

The commentarial tradition (Dhammapada Aṭṭhakathā) tells the famous tale:

When Kisā Gotamī's infant son died, she refused to accept it. Carrying the child's body, she went from door to door begging for medicine that could revive him. People thought she had lost her mind. An old man directed her to the Buddha.

The Buddha, seeing her desperate state, said he could revive the child if she could procure a handful of white mustard seeds — but only from a house where no one had ever died. She went from house to house, and at every door, people gladly gave her mustard seeds. But when she asked, "Has anyone died in this family?" the answer was always the same: "The dead are many, the living are few." She could not find a single house untouched by death.

Gradually, the realization dawned: **death is universal**. No home is free from mortality. She returned to the Buddha without the mustard seeds, having understood the lesson. She renounced the child's body, took ordination, and in time became an arahant.

> *Yo ca vassasataṁ jīve, apassaṁ amataṁ padaṁ; Ekāhaṁ jīvitaṁ seyyo, passato amataṁ padaṁ.*
>
> "Though one should live a hundred years without seeing the Deathless State, yet better indeed is a single day's life of one who sees the Deathless State."
>
> — Dhammapada, verse 114

## Her Enlightenment

The canonical verses (Therīgāthā 10.1) tell a different, complementary story:

Kisā Gotamī was pregnant with her second child and travelling to her parents' home with her husband and firstborn. A storm struck; her husband went to gather shelter, was bitten by a snake, and died. She gave birth alone in the rain, sheltering both children with her body. At dawn she discovered her husband dead. Crossing a swollen river, a hawk carried off her newborn, and her firstborn drowned trying to follow her. Half-mad with grief, she reached her parents' home — only to find it had burned down; her parents and brother were being cremated on a single pyre.

Stricken to utter ruin, she wandered naked and insane until she came into the Buddha's presence. He taught her the Dhamma, and she recovered. She ordained and became an arahant. In her own verses:

> "With family killed, despised by all, my husband dead — I reached the Deathless.
> I've developed this path, noble, eightfold, going to the Deathless.
> Having realized Unbinding, I've gazed in the mirror of Dhamma.
> I've cut out the arrow, put down the burden, done the task."

The Buddha declared her **foremost among the bhikkhunīs in discipline** (*etadaggaṁ bhikkhunīnaṁ vinītānaṁ*).

## Teaching — The Embers Verse

Kisā Gotamī's teaching emphasizes impermanence and the universality of suffering. In the Gotamī Sutta (SN 5.3), she declares victory over Māra:

> "Having defeated the army of death, free of fermentations I dwell. It's everywhere destroyed — delight. The mass of darkness is shattered."

The tradition also preserves an "embers" teaching attributed to her — a reflection on the fragility of human life:

> "Truly, like a small fire smouldering under ashes, so is this human life — brief, uncertain, fragile. Therefore, be diligent and practise without delay."

This teaching compares human life to a dying ember — glowing for a moment, then gone — urging mindfulness of death (*maraṇasati*) and the urgency of practice.

## Comparison with Other Traditions

| Tradition | Parallel |
|-----------|----------|
| **Buddhism** | The mustard seed parable itself — death as the universal condition |
| **Hasidic Judaism** | The story of **Bāhilā** — a rabbi sends a grieving parent to find someone who has not suffered loss; none exists |
| **Greek philosophy** | The story of **Demonax** — a philosopher promises to summon a dead son's shadow if given three names of those who never grieved; none can be found |
| **Christianity** | The parable of the **burning house** (Matthew 7:24–27) — building on the rock vs. sand as a metaphor for preparing for inevitable loss |
| **Zen Buddhism** | The parable of the **tea cup** — a teaching on accepting impermanence through direct realization |

The story of Kisā Gotamī belongs to a widespread folk-motif (ATU type 2075 — "Search for a Person Who Has Never Lost Anyone") that appears across cultures, but only in Buddhism does it culminate in full awakening rather than mere consolation.
