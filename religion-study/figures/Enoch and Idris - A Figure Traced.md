# Enoch & Idris — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

Enoch — *Idrīs* in Arabic — is the mysterious **seventh patriarch from Adam** who "walked with God" and was **translated to heaven without dying**. In the Bible he receives just two verses in Genesis, yet becomes a towering figure in Jewish apocalyptic literature (1 Enoch). In the Qur'an he is named twice as a prophet of the highest rank — "upright" and "exalted." A **genealogical** connection; a companion to [[Adam - A Figure Traced]], [[Noah and Nuh - A Figure Traced]] (Enoch is Noah's great-grandfather), and [[Elijah and Ilyas - A Figure Traced]] (the other figure taken to heaven without death).

## The shared tradition: a man taken by God

Both scriptures mark Enoch/Idris as **unusual** — he escapes death and is raised to a high station.

> [!quote] Genesis 5:21–24 — he was not, for God took him
> "And **Enoch** lived sixty and five years, and begat Methuselah: And Enoch **walked with God** after he begat Methuselah three hundred years… And all the days of Enoch were three hundred sixty and five years: And Enoch **walked with God**: and **he was not; for God took him.** "

> [!quote] Qur'an — raised to a high place
> **Maryam 19:56–57** — "And make mention of **Idris** in the Scripture. Lo! he was a **man of truth, a prophet**, And We **raised him to a high station**."
> **Al-Anbiya 21:85–86** — "And (mention) **Ishmael, and Idris, and Dhul-Kifl**. All were of the patient. And We brought them in to Our mercy. Lo! they are among the righteous."

The Hebrew phrase "he was not; for God took him" (וְאֵינֶנּוּ כִּי־לָקַח אֹתוֹ אֱלֹהִים) is the Bible's most enigmatic description of a death that is **not a death**. The Qur'an's "raised him to a high station" (وَرَفَعْنَاهُ مَكَانًا عَلِيًّا) echoes the same idea — Idris was elevated by God.

## The identification: why Idris = Enoch?

Islamic tradition consistently identifies Idrīs with the biblical Enoch, though the Qur'an never explicitly states this. The evidence:

1. **Genealogical position** — Idris is placed in the prophetic chain before Noah, matching Enoch (the seventh from Adam, great-grandfather of Noah)
2. **Name** — Idrīs may derive from the Arabic *dars* ("study"), suggesting he was a scholar, which aligns with the Jewish tradition that Enoch was a revealer of hidden knowledge
3. **Elevation** — "raised to a high station" matches "God took him" — both suggest **translation to heaven without death**

## The Bible's extra-biblical tradition: 1 Enoch

While the canonical Bible says almost nothing about Enoch, a vast **apocryphal literature** grew around him:

> [!quote] The Book of Enoch (1 Enoch) — extra-canonical
> Although not part of the biblical canon for most traditions (Ethiopian Orthodox excepted), 1 Enoch was known to the New Testament — the Epistle of **Jude quotes it directly** (Jude 14–15):
>
> "And Enoch also, the seventh from Adam, **prophesied** of these, saying, Behold, the Lord cometh with ten thousands of his saints, to execute judgment…"
>
> This quotation gives Enoch a prophetic role the Genesis text never describes, and suggests his voice carried authority in Second Temple Judaism.

The Qur'an's depiction of Idris as a **prophet** ("a man of truth, a prophet") aligns more closely with this expanded Enochic tradition than with the two-verse mention in Genesis.

## What it means

Enoch/Idris is the **shadow figure** of the patriarchs — barely mentioned in the canonical texts yet enormously influential in the traditions that grew around them. Both scriptures agree he was **taken by God** (translated to heaven), and both place him **before Noah** in the patriarchal line. The Bible leaves his story tantalizingly brief, while the Qur'an gives him the honorific of **prophet** — a station Genesis never explicitly grants him, though the New Testament's Jude assumes it. He is, along with [[Elijah and Ilyas - A Figure Traced]], one of only two figures in the Abrahamic traditions believed to have **escaped death** and been taken bodily to heaven.
