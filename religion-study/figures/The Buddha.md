# The Buddha (Siddhartha Gautama)

> The Awakened One — born a prince in the 5th century BCE in Lumbinī (now Nepal), he renounced his palace, attained enlightenment under the Bodhi tree, and spent 45 years teaching the path to liberation. His influence spans Asia and the modern world, touching more human lives than any other teacher in history.

## Core Teachings

### The Four Noble Truths (*Cattāri Ariyasaccāni*)

| Truth | Pāli | Meaning |
|-------|------|---------|
| **1. Dukkha** | *Dukkha ariyasacca* | Life is marked by suffering, dissatisfaction, impermanence |
| **2. Samudaya** | *Dukkha samudaya ariyasacca* | The origin of suffering is craving (*taṇhā*) |
| **3. Nirodha** | *Dukkha nirodha ariyasacca* | The cessation of suffering is possible |
| **4. Magga** | *Dukkha nirodha gāminī paṭipadā* | The Noble Eightfold Path leads to cessation |

### The Noble Eightfold Path (*Ariyo Aṭṭhaṅgiko Maggo*)

| Division | Factors |
|----------|---------|
| **Wisdom (Paññā)** | Right View, Right Intention |
| **Ethics (Sīla)** | Right Speech, Right Action, Right Livelihood |
| **Concentration (Samādhi)** | Right Effort, Right Mindfulness, Right Concentration |

### Other Landmark Teachings

- **Anattā** (Not-self) — no permanent, unchanging self exists
- **Anicca** (Impermanence) — all conditioned things pass away
- **Paṭiccasamuppāda** (Dependent Origination) — the chain of causes that binds beings to saṃsāra
- **Mettā** (Loving-kindness) — universal goodwill toward all beings

## The Buddha's Character

The Pāli Canon preserves a vivid, human portrait: a teacher who laughed, who scolded, who grieved at the death of his disciples, who praised his cook, who walked miles to comfort a grieving mother, who washed a sick monk, who stopped a war by standing in the path of an army. He was not a distant deity but a human being who had, in his own words, "woken up."

> "Now I am old, Ānanda. My body is like an old cart held together with straps. It is only when I enter into deep samādhi that my body is comfortable." — DN 16

## The Historical Buddha

Scholars generally agree that Siddhartha Gautama was a historical figure — a 5th-century BCE teacher in the Gangetic plain. The exact dates are debated: the Buddhist tradition places his parinirvāṇa in 543–544 BCE; modern scholars prefer c. 400 BCE. He taught in Magadhi (a Prakrit), wandering between towns, and his teachings were preserved orally for four centuries before being written down in Pāli in Sri Lanka (c. 1st century BCE).
