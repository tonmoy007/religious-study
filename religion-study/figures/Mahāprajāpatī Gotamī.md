# Mahāprajāpatī Gotamī

> The first bhikkhunī — stepmother who raised the Buddha, aunt who walked 150 miles on swollen feet to demand that women, too, could awaken. The woman who made the Sangha whole.

---

## Biography

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| **Role** | First bhikkhunī (Buddhist nun), founder of the order of nuns |
| **Relation** | Step-mother and maternal aunt of the Buddha |
| **Mother** | Raised Siddhartha after Queen Māyā died seven days after his birth |
| **Known for** | Establishing the Bhikkhunī Sangha; foremost in seniority among nuns |
| **Died** | Before the Buddha, at the age of 120 |

---

## The Ordination Story

After the Buddha's awakening, Mahāprajāpatī repeatedly asked him to allow women to renounce and join the Sangha. He refused each time. Undeterred, she took matters into her own hands:

- **The Walk**: Accompanied by 500 Sakyan women, she **shaved her head**, donned saffron robes, and **walked 150 miles** from Kapilavastu to Vesālī (the modern-day Bihar-Nepal border).
- **The Arrival**: She arrived with **swollen, bleeding feet**, weeping at the gate of the Mahāvana monastery.
- **Ānanda Intercedes**: The Buddha's attendant **Ānanda** found her and asked the Buddha on her behalf. When the Buddha again refused, Ānanda asked a different question: *"Are women capable of attaining enlightenment?"* The Buddha admitted they were. Ānanda pressed: *"Then let her ordain."*
- **The Eight Chief Rules (Garudhammas)**: The Buddha laid down eight strict rules that nuns must follow — accepting seniority of all monks, even those ordained later — as conditions for ordination. Mahāprajāpatī accepted them wholeheartedly.
- **Her Response**: She caught her reflection in water and said, *"So this head is shaved… these robes are ochre… now I go for alms."*

---

## Her Attainment

Mahāprajāpatī **became an arahant** — fully enlightened — along with her 500 companions. She is recorded as **foremost among nuns in seniority** (etadagga). Her awakening is described in the Aṅguttara Nikāya, where the Buddha himself declares her foremost.

---

## Her Verses (Therīgāthā)

Mahāprajāpatī's verses are preserved in the **Therīgāthā** (Songs of the Elder Nuns), one of the oldest collections of women's spiritual poetry. Her stanzas (Therīgāthā 157–162) celebrate:

> *"I have done what the Buddhas teach — free from desire, free from birth, free from sorrow."*

Her verses mark a radical assertion: that women's voices belong in the canonical record of enlightenment.

---

## Her Death

Mahāprajāpatī **passed away before the Buddha**, at the reported age of 120. Her death is described in the **Parinibbāna Sutta**-adjacent texts. The Buddha himself presided over her funeral rites — an unprecedented honour. Her relics were enshrined in a stupa.

---

## Comparison

Mahāprajāpatī is the **first female religious founder figure** in a world religion still alive today. Her story echoes across traditions:

| Figure | Tradition | Parallel |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Mary Magdalene** | Christianity | First witness of the risen Christ; apostle to the apostles; a woman at the centre of a male-dominated movement |
| **Hājar (Hagar)** | Islam / Judaism | The cast-out woman who walks through the desert with her son; her struggle becomes the centre of the Hajj |
| **Miriam** | Judaism | Prophetess who leads women in song after the Red Sea crossing; challenges Moses' authority (echoing Mahāprajāpatī challenging the Buddha) |
| **Sita** | Hinduism | Embodiment of wifely virtue who endures exile and trial; counterpart to Mahāprajāpatī's renunciant path |

What unites them: each is a **woman who steps outside the expected role** — exile, desert, or monastery gate — and in doing so, reshapes the tradition. Mahāprajāpatī's singular achievement was making the path of full renunciation available to women, creating the first institution where women could pursue enlightenment on equal spiritual footing with men.
