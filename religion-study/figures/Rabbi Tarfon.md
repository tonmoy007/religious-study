# Rabbi Tarfon (רבי טרפון)

A Tanna of the second generation, a priest (Kohen), and a wealthy contemporary of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Tarfon is known for his stringent legal rulings, his generous financial support of Torah scholarship, and his distinctive Aramaic sayings. He appears frequently in the Mishnah, Tosefta, and midrashic collections, often in vigorous debate with Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael. His name may derive from the Greek *Tryphon* (Τρύφων), reflecting the Hellenistic influence on Judea in this period.

Rabbi Tarfon served in the Temple as a priest before its destruction in 70 CE, and his rulings often reflect a deep attachment to Temple procedures and the laws of purity. Unlike many sages who lived in poverty, Tarfon was wealthy and used his resources to support the Sages, though the Talmud criticises him for not being as generous as he could have been. He is described as having avoided authority and responsibility, once hiding from the community to avoid being appointed a communal leader (Nedarim 62a).

## Key Contributions

- **Stringent Legal Rulings:** Rabbi Tarfon was known for a stringent approach to the law (מחמיר), particularly in matters of divorce, purity, and the laws of vows. His rulings are often contrasted with the more lenient positions of Rabbi Akiva.
- **The Threefold Reply:** At the end of Yom Kippur, Rabbi Tarfon would recite a blessing of gratitude for having been spared the fate of the wicked, and he would say: "May it be Your will that we not be put to shame nor brought to disgrace by our ancestors" (Yoma 5:3).
- **Wealth and Scholarship:** The Talmud records that Rabbi Tarfon owned valuable Temple artifacts, including the veil of the Holy of Holies, and that he once rescued his mother by covering her honour with lavish spending (Kiddushin 31a–b).
- **Debates with Rabbi Akiva:** The Mishnah records numerous disputes between Tarfon and Akiva. In one famous exchange, Tarfon swore that Akiva's interpretation was a "great rule of the Torah" — the same respect he normally reserved for his own stringent positions.

## In Jewish Tradition

- Known for the saying in Pirkei Avot: "The day is short, the task is great, the labourers are lazy, the reward is great, and the Master of the house is insistent" (Avot 2:15)
- He ruled that one must save Jewish captives at any cost, to the point of selling a Torah scroll if necessary (Bava Batra 8b)
- The Mishnah in Nedarim (62a) records that he avoided positions of authority, saying: "If you are a judge, do not become a ruler"
- He is associated with the chain of tradition in Pirkei Avot (2:15), where he quotes his teacher — traditionally Rabbi Yohanan ben Nuri or possibly Rabban Gamaliel
