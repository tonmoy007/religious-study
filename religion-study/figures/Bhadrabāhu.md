# Bhadrabāhu

> Born: c. 433 BCE (traditional), Pratiṣṭhānapura (modern Paithan, Maharashtra)
> Died: c. 367 BCE (traditional), Katavapra (near Shravanabelagola, Karnataka)
> Title: The 8th Śruta Kevalin; author of the Kalpa Sūtra; the last human to know the complete Jain canon by heart

Bhadrabāhu is the most pivotal figure in Jain history after the Tīrthaṅkaras themselves — the last Śruta Kevalin, meaning "one who possesses the complete scripture" in its entirety through perfect memory. He was the final human to know the whole of the Jain Āgamas by heart before the canon began to fragment and be partially lost. A śramaṇa (ascetic) of extraordinary foresight, he prophesied a 12-year Great Famine in Magadha (c. 350 BCE) and led a mass migration of his monastic community southward to Karnataka, laying the foundation for the Digambara tradition in South India. His foremost disciple, Sthūlabhadra, remained in Magadha and presided over the Pāṭaliputra Council that attempted to codify the surviving canon — an act that would anchor the Śvetāmbara tradition. Bhadrabāhu is also revered as the author of the **Kalpa Sūtra**, the most widely read Jain scripture, which records the lives of the 24 Tīrthaṅkaras and is recited annually during the Paryuṣaṇā festival. Revered by both Digambara and Śvetāmbara traditions, he stands at the great fork in Jain history — the last witness to the complete Dhamma and the figure after whom the unified Jain Saṅgha divided.

## Biography

The exact dates of Bhadrabāhu's life are debated. Traditional Jain sources place him in the 4th–3rd century BCE; modern scholars generally date him to the 4th–3rd century BCE as well, though some push later. The following table synthesises the Jain traditional account.

| Event | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Birth | c. 433 BCE in Pratiṣṭhānapura (modern Paithan, Maharashtra), on the banks of the Godāvarī River. Born into a Brāhmaṇa family that later converted to Jainism. His father was Yajñadatta (a Vedic scholar) and his mother was Dhāriṇī (also called Yaśomatī in some sources). Alternate traditions place his birthplace in Magadha itself |
| Early Life | Educated in the Vedas and śāstras, he was drawn to the Jain monastic order early in life. He became a disciple of Ācārya Yaśobhadra (or, in some lineages, of Ācārya Sambhūtivijaya), the 7th Śruta Kevalin — inheriting a tradition of oral transmission that stretched back through Jambūsvāmin (Mahāvīra's successor) to Mahāvīra himself |
| Attainment of Śruta Kevalin | Through intense study, meditation, and the grace of his teacher, Bhadrabāhu mastered the fourteen *Pūrvas* (the earliest, most ancient Jain scriptures, now lost) and all eleven *Aṅgas*. He became the 8th and final Śruta Kevalin — the last human being to possess the entire Jain canon in memory |
| The Great Famine Prophecy | While residing in Magadha (the heartland of Jainism, in present-day Bihar), Bhadrabāhu perceived — through his *avadhijñāna* (clairvoyant knowledge) — that a 12-year famine was coming. He gathered his disciples and announced that the Saṅgha could not survive in Magadha. He prophesied that those who followed him south would preserve the pure tradition, while those who remained would have to adapt — an event that would lead to the eventual schism between Digambara and Śvetāmbara traditions |
| Migration to Karnataka | c. 350–340 BCE. Bhadrabāhu led a mass migration of approximately 12,000 monks southward, travelling through Madhya Pradesh and the Deccan plateau until they reached the region of modern Karnataka. The journey was arduous — many monks perished from hunger, thirst, and disease. The survivors settled in the hills of Katavapra (near present-day Shravanabelagola), a site that became the centre of the Digambara Jain community for centuries |
| Sthūlabhadra's Decision | Bhadrabāhu's chief disciple, **Sthūlabhadra**, chose to remain in Magadha. Bhadrabāhu knew that Sthūlabhadra had the capacity to become the next Śruta Kevalin but also foresaw that he would instead lead the council and preserve what could be saved. When Sthūlabhadra asked his teacher's permission to stay, Bhadrabāhu replied: "The Dhamma will be preserved in two streams — one in the south that keeps the ancient ways, one in the north that adapts to survive. Neither is wrong." This moment is regarded as the symbolic origin of the two major Jain sectarian traditions |
| The Pāṭaliputra Council | After the famine, the remaining Śvetāmbara monks convened a council at Pāṭaliputra (modern Patna) under Sthūlabhadra's leadership. They attempted to compile the Jain canon from memory. However, by this time, the fourteen *Pūrvas* had been largely forgotten — only Bhadrabāhu still knew them in full. The council codified eleven *Aṅgas* (canonical texts) but acknowledged that the complete canon of the Śruta Kevalins was lost |
| Death (Nirvāṇa) | c. 367 BCE at Katavapra, near Shravanabelagola. He performed the *sallekhanā* (the Jain death-rite of fasting unto death), surrounded by his disciples. His final hillside tomb became a pilgrimage site; the Chandragiri Hill at Shravanabelagola is named after his disciple Chandragupta Maurya (who, according to Digambara tradition, became a Jain monk under Bhadrabāhu and followed him south). Many believe the footprints on Chandragiri mark Bhadrabāhu's final resting place |

## The Last Śruta Kevalin

### What is a Śruta Kevalin?

In Jain tradition, a **Śruta Kevalin** ("one whose scripture is perfect"; Sanskrit: *śruta* = scripture/heard, *kevalin* = possessed of perfect knowledge) is a human being who has attained complete mastery of the entire Jain canon, perfectly remembered and transmitted in its original form. This is distinct from a *Kevalin* in the full sense — one who has attained *kevala jñāna* (omniscience). A Śruta Kevalin knows the *words* of the teaching completely, while a Tīrthaṅkara knows the *reality* directly.

The lineage of the Śruta Kevalins, according to Jain tradition:

| # | Śruta Kevalin | Relation to Previous | Notes |
|---|---------------|---------------------|-------|
| 1 | **Jambūsvāmin** | Mahāvīra's successor | The first Ganadhara to assume leadership after Mahāvīra's nirvāṇa |
| 2 | **Prabhava** | Disciple of Jambūsvāmin | Carried the tradition forward |
| 3 | **Śayyambhava** | Disciple of Prabhava | Also known as Śyāma; some sources place him as a key early Ācārya |
| 4 | **Yaśobhadra** | Disciple of Śayyambhava | The 4th Śruta Kevalin |
| 5 | **Sambhūtivijaya** | Disciple of Yaśobhadra | The 5th Śruta Kevalin |
| 6 | **Bhadrabāhu** | Disciple of Sambhūtivijaya | The 6th Śruta Kevalin (by some counts); the 8th by others |

> **Note on numbering**: Different Jain sources give different numbers for the Śruta Kevalins. The Śvetāmbara tradition generally lists 5 Śruta Kevalins starting from Jambūsvāmin; the Digambara tradition lists up to 8 or 9. All agree that Bhadrabāhu was the **last**.

### Why He Was the Last

Bhadrabāhu is the final Śruta Kevalin because after his death, no one remained who could perfectly recite the entire Jain canon, especially the fourteen *Pūrvas*. Three factors contributed:

1. **The Famine**: The 12-year Famine scattered the monastic community. Many monks died. Oral transmission depends on continuous, active rehearsal; the disruption broke the chain.
2. **The Migration**: Those who followed Bhadrabāhu south preserved parts of the tradition but were isolated from the main centres of textual recitation.
3. **The Council's Limitations**: The Pāṭaliputra Council (c. 350 BCE), led by Sthūlabhadra, codified what could be remembered — but the fourteen *Pūrvas* were already largely lost. Only Bhadrabāhu still knew them. After his death, the complete canon was gone.

The Śvetāmbara tradition holds that the surviving canon consists of 11 *Aṅgas* (out of 12) and various subsidiary texts — but the original 14 *Pūrvas* are lost forever. The Digambara tradition holds that the entire original canon was lost and the present Āgamas are reconstructions from a later period.

## The Kalpa Sūtra

Bhadrabāhu's most enduring literary legacy is the **Kalpa Sūtra** (the "Book of Ritual" or "Book of Righteous Practices"), a sacred text in the Śvetāmbara Jain canon that is among the most widely read and ritually important of all Jain scriptures.

### Contents

The Kalpa Sūtra is structured in three main sections:

| Section | Content | Significance |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **The Jina-caritra** (Lives of the Jinas) | Biographies of the 24 Tīrthaṅkaras, with special focus on Mahāvīra, Pārśva, Ṛṣabha, and Neminātha | This is the most well-known portion; it recounts the nativity, renunciation, enlightenment, and liberation of each Tīrthaṅkara. The birth narratives include the 14 (or 16) auspicious dreams of the Tīrthaṅkara's mother |
| **The Sthavirāvalī** (Lineage of the Elders) | A list of the patriarchal succession from Mahāvīra through the Ganadharas and the early Ācāryas, down to Bhadrabāhu's own time | This is the single most important source for Jain ecclesiastical history — a record of who succeeded whom, in what order, and for how long |
| **The Sāmācārī** (Rules of Conduct) | Detailed regulations for the monastic life during the *cāturmāsa* (the four-month rainy-season retreat) and the *Paryuṣaṇā* festival | These rules govern where monks may stay, how they may interact with laity, what they may eat, and how they should perform the annual confession (pratikramaṇa) |

### Authorship and Dating

Tradition ascribes the Kalpa Sūtra to Bhadrabāhu, but its composition likely has a layered history:

- The core **Sthavirāvalī** (the lineage list) was probably composed by Bhadrabāhu himself, as it ends with him and his immediate successors — it is essentially his autobiographical record of the transmission.
- The **Jina-caritra** and **Sāmācārī** sections may have been composed or compiled by Bhadrabāhu but were expanded in subsequent centuries.
- The oldest extant manuscript of the Kalpa Sūtra dates to the 13th–14th century CE (a palm-leaf manuscript from Gujarat), but the text is certainly far older — it was already being commented upon by the 6th century CE.

### Ritual Importance

The Kalpa Sūtra holds a unique place in Jain liturgy:

- It is **recited in full** during the *Paryuṣaṇā* festival (the 8-day annual period of fasting and confession), the most important festival in the Śvetāmbara calendar.
- The recitation is a public event — monks read the text to lay audiences, often with dramatic elaboration and commentary.
- Manuscripts of the Kalpa Sūtra are among the most beautifully illuminated of all Indian texts, particularly in the western Indian painting tradition (c. 15th–16th century). These illustrated manuscripts are masterpieces of Jain art, with vivid depictions of the Tīrthaṅkaras, the auspicious dreams, and the Kalpavṛkṣa (wish-fulfilling trees).
- It is considered so sacred that in some traditions, the text is worshipped as a physical object — placed on a pedestal and honoured with offerings before being read.

### Other Writings Attributed to Bhadrabāhu

In addition to the Kalpa Sūtra, Bhadrabāhu is credited with several other works in Jain tradition:

- **Uvāsagadasāo** (The Ten Chapters on Lay Followers) — a Śvetāmbara Āgama text on the lives of famous Jain laymen, sometimes attributed to him
- **Nirayāvaliyā** — a text on the hellish realms of Jain cosmology
- **Vipāka Sūtra** — a text on the fruition of karma
- **Bṛhat-kalpa-Sūtra** — a longer version of the Kalpa Sūtra with more detailed monastic rules (some scholars consider this a later expansion)

However, modern scholarship is cautious about these attributions. The Kalpa Sūtra itself is the only work universally accepted as Bhadrabāhu's composition.

## The Great Famine and the Split of the Saṅgha

### The Famine (c. 350 BCE)

The 12-year Great Famine (also called the *Durbhikṣa* or *Mahā-durbhikṣa*) was a catastrophic drought that struck the Magadha region (present-day Bihar) in the 4th century BCE. Jain sources describe it as a time of severe suffering — crops failed, rivers dried, people abandoned their villages, and the monastic community could not obtain alms. Bhadrabāhu's clairvoyant perception of this famine before it struck is treated as proof of his extraordinary spiritual attainment.

### The Two Decisions

| Aspect | Bhadrabāhu (Migration South) | Sthūlabhadra (Stay Behind) |
|--------|------------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Leader** | Bhadrabāhu (the 8th Śruta Kevalin) | Sthūlabhadra (Bhadrabāhu's chief disciple) |
| **Decision** | Lead 12,000 monks south to Karnataka | Remain in Magadha with those who could not travel or chose to stay |
| **Rationale** | The Saṅgha cannot survive 12 years of famine in Magadha; the pure tradition must be preserved elsewhere | The Dhamma must be maintained where it is established; the laity need guidance even — especially — in times of crisis |
| **Outcome** | Established the Digambara tradition in South India | Presided over the Pāṭaliputra Council and preserved the Śvetāmbara canon |
| **Subsequent history** | The Digambara community in Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, and Maharashtra flourished under the patronage of the Gaṅga, Hoysaḷa, and Vijayanagara empires | The Śvetāmbara community in Gujarat and Rajasthan continued the textual tradition, preserving the Āgamas in written form |
| **Attitude to nudity** | Maintained the strict practice of nudity (*digambara* = "sky-clad") as the original ascetic ideal | Accepted the use of white robes (*śvetāmbara* = "white-clad") as a practical adaptation for monks living in settled monastic communities |

### The Emergence of the Two Sects

The Great Famine and the split between Bhadrabāhu and Sthūlabhadra did not instantly create the Digambara and Śvetāmbara sects — the division crystallised over several centuries. However, this event is remembered as the **point of origin** for the divergence:

- **Digambara Tradition** ("Sky-clad"): Traces its lineage through Bhadrabāhu and the monks who migrated south. They maintain that the original Jain canon was lost entirely after Bhadrabāhu's death; the existing texts are later compositions. They hold that nudity is essential for monks seeking liberation, and that women cannot achieve mokṣa without first being reborn as men.
- **Śvetāmbara Tradition** ("White-clad"): Traces its lineage through Sthūlabhadra and the monks who remained in Magadha. They maintain that 11 of the 12 *Aṅgas* have been preserved. They accept white robes for monks (monks may wear robes without compromising their renunciation) and hold that women can achieve mokṣa as women.

Despite this divergence, both traditions revere Bhadrabāhu as a saint and a teacher. His image is worshipped in both Digambara and Śvetāmbara temples, and the Kalpa Sūtra is accepted (though with different interpretative emphases) by both.

## The Chandragupta Connection

A significant and controversial element of Bhadrabāhu's story is the tradition that **Chandragupta Maurya** (founder of the Mauryan Empire, c. 321–297 BCE) became a Jain monk under Bhadrabāhu and followed him south.

### The Digambara Tradition

According to Digambara sources, after Chandragupta conquered the Nanda Empire and unified most of India, he became disillusioned with worldly power, renounced his throne, and became a disciple of Bhadrabāhu. He accompanied the Ācārya on the migration to Karnataka, living as a simple ascetic, performing severe penances, and finally ending his life by *sallekhanā* at Shravanabelagola. The Chandragiri Hill (lit. "Chandragupta's Hill") at Shravanabelagola is named after him, and a small shrine marks the traditional site of his death.

### The Śvetāmbara Position

Śvetāmbara sources generally do not mention Chandragupta Maurya in connection with Bhadrabāhu. Some acknowledge the tradition as a later Digambara legend; others accept it but place it at a different time (after Chandragupta's grandson Aśoka's reign).

### Scholarly Assessment

| Position | View | Evidence / Argument |
|----------|------|---------------------|
| **Traditional Digambara** | Chandragupta was Bhadrabāhu's disciple and ended his life at Shravanabelagola | The site name (Chandragiri), the local tradition, and the *Bhadrabāhu-carita* (a medieval biography) |
| **Critical scholars** | The story is plausible but unverifiable; the dates may not align perfectly | Chandragupta's dates (c. 321–297 BCE) overlap with the traditional date of the famine (c. 350 BCE), but the ten-year discrepancy is notable; the name "Chandragupta" could refer to a different person (the Jain monk's name) |
| **Skeptical historians** | The connection is a later invention to enhance Bhadrabāhu's prestige by associating him with India's first emperor | No contemporary inscription or text from the Mauryan period mentions the connection; the earliest sources linking them date from the 1st millennium CE |

Regardless of historical accuracy, the Chandragupta–Bhadrabāhu connection has enormous symbolic power — it anchors the Digambara tradition to the imperial Mauryan dynasty and gives Jainism a foundational role in the political history of South India.

## In Jain Tradition

### Iconography

Bhadrabāhu is depicted in Jain art with the following distinguishing features:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Posture** | Seated in meditation (*padmāsana* or *ardha-padmāsana*), often with a manuscript (the Kalpa Sūtra) in his hands or resting beside him |
| **Beard** | Shown with a long, flowing white beard — the sign of his age and wisdom as a elder teacher |
| **Facial expression** | Serene, with penetrating eyes that suggest his prophetic vision (*avadhijñāna*) |
| **Clothing** | Śvetāmbara images show him in a white robe; Digambara images show him naked, consistent with each sect's iconographic conventions |
| **Attributes** | A water pot (kamaṇḍalu) and a manuscript — symbolising his ascetic piety and his role as the canon's preserver |
| **Colour** | Golden or white (prabhā-maṇḍala — a radiant halo around his head) |
| **Distinguishing symbol** | The Kalpa Sūtra text, sometimes shown open, emphasising his literary legacy |

### Temples & Pilgrimage

- **Chandragiri Hill, Shravanabelagola, Karnataka** — The most sacred site associated with Bhadrabāhu. The hill bears his footprints (*pādukās*), a small shrine, and the place of his *sallekhanā*. The nearby Bhadrabāhu Cave (also called the Bhadrabāhu Guhā) is a meditation site.
- **Shravanabelagola** — The entire complex is one of the most important Digambara pilgrimage destinations in India, centred on the colossal Bāhubali statue but intimately connected to Bhadrabāhu's migration.
- **Paithan (Pratiṣṭhānapura), Maharashtra** — Bhadrabāhu's birthplace; a temple commemorates him, and the site is venerated by both sects.
- **Mount Śatruñjaya, Palitana, Gujarat** — The most sacred Śvetāmbara pilgrimage site; includes shrines to Bhadrabāhu alongside the Tīrthaṅkaras.

### Key Texts

| Text | Author / Attribution | Significance |
|------|---------------------|--------------|
| **Kalpa Sūtra** | Bhadrabāhu (traditional) | The most important Jain scripture after the Āgamas; recited during Paryuṣaṇā |
| **Bhadrabāhu-carita** | Ratnanandi (c. 15th century CE) | A medieval Digambara hagiography of Bhadrabāhu's life, including the Chandragupta story |
| **Āvaśyaka Niryukti** | Attributed to Bhadrabāhu (disputed) | A commentary on the essential duties of Jain monks; sometimes linked to him |
| **Sthavirāvalī** (within Kalpa Sūtra) | Bhadrabāhu | The patriarchal lineage from Mahāvīra to Bhadrabāhu |
| **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** | Hemacandra (12th c.) | The "Lives of the 63 Illustrious Men" — includes Bhadrabāhu as one of the great Ācāryas |

### Festivals & Commemorations

- **Paryuṣaṇā** (Śvetāmbara, August–September) — The annual 8-day festival during which the Kalpa Sūtra is recited; this is Bhadrabāhu's most important liturgical legacy.
- **Daśa-lakṣaṇa-parvan** (Digambara, August–September) — The Digambara equivalent of Paryuṣaṇā; while the Kalpa Sūtra is not central here, Bhadrabāhu is honoured as the patriarch of the Digambara lineage.
- **Bhadrabāhu Nirvāṇa Divasa** — The anniversary of his death; observed by both sects, especially at Shravanabelagola.

### In Jain Philosophy

Bhadrabāhu's philosophical significance lies not in original doctrinal contributions (he transmitted rather than innovated) but in his role as the **guarantor of the canon's authenticity**. For Jains, the fact that Bhadrabāhu knew the entire Āgama by heart — that there was a living human being who possessed the complete teaching — establishes a direct link of transmission from Mahāvīra to the present. After him, the canon becomes a matter of reconstruction, commentary, and interpretation. Every Jain scholar who writes a commentary on the Āgamas is, in a sense, trying to recover what Bhadrabāhu once knew perfectly.

Bhadrabāhu also illustrates the Jain ideal of *aparityāga* (non-abandonment of dharma even in crisis). His decision to migrate rather than stay and die was not a failure of renunciation but a strategic preservation of the teaching. This is a distinctively Jain approach: the ascetic must protect the Dhamma even by physically moving, because the Dhamma is more important than any single location or community.

## Comparative: Canon-Preserving Figures Across Traditions

Bhadrabāhu belongs to a type found in several religious traditions: the figure who preserves the complete scripture at a moment of crisis, whose memory bridges the living revelation and the written canon. The following table compares him with analogous figures.

| Aspect | Bhadrabāhu (Jain) | Ānanda (Buddhist) | Ezra (Jewish) | ʿUthmān ibn ʿAffān (Islamic) | Mahākassapa (Buddhist) | Pāṇini (Hindu grammatical) |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|---------------|------------------------------|------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Tradition** | Jainism (both sects) | Buddhism (Theravāda) | Judaism (Second Temple) | Islam (Sunni) | Buddhism (Theravāda) | Hinduism (grammatical/ritual) |
| **Role** | Last Śruta Kevalin — final human to know the full Jain canon by heart | The Buddha's personal attendant who memorised every sermon; recited the Sutta Piṭaka at the First Council | Scribe-priest who re-promulgated the Torah after the Babylonian Exile; credited with restoring the lost Law | Third Caliph who standardised the Quranic text, suppressing variant readings | Presided over the First Buddhist Council; authenticated the Buddha's teachings | Codified Sanskrit grammar in the Aṣṭādhyāyī, preserving Vedic and classical speech |
| **Crisis** | 12-year Great Famine (c. 350 BCE) — monastic community scattered; canon fragmenting | The Buddha's death (c. 483 BCE) — no single authority left; risk of doctrinal confusion | Babylonian Exile (c. 586–539 BCE) — loss of Temple, scrolls, and national identity | Rise of variant Quranic readings in the expanding Islamic empire (c. 650 CE) | The Buddha's death — Subhadda's disrespectful remark suggesting the Dhamma could now be ignored | Decline of correct Vedic pronunciation and grammatical understanding in the post-Vedic period |
| **Action** | Migrated south with 12,000 monks to preserve the pure tradition; authored the Kalpa Sūtra | Recited the entire Sutta Piṭaka from memory at the First Council (Rājagṛha) | Read the rediscovered Torah to the assembled people; led a religious covenant renewal | Commissioned the standard Uthmanic codex; burned all non-standard copies | Convened and presided over the First Council of Rājagṛha; questioned Ānanda and Upāli to verify each teaching | Composed the Aṣṭādhyāyī — 3,959 sūtras that systematically describe the Sanskrit language |
| **What was preserved** | Complete Jain Āgama (14 Pūrvas + 11 Aṅgas) — now lost after his death | The Sutta Piṭaka (discourses) and parts of the Vinaya (monastic rules) | The Torah (Pentateuch) in a definitive written form | The Quran in a single, authoritative written edition | The Dhamma and Vinaya as spoken by the Buddha | The rules of Sanskrit morphology, syntax, phonology, and semantics |
| **Method** | Oral memory (śruta = "heard") — exact recitation through perfect retention | Oral recitation (bhāṇaka tradition) — phrase-by-phrase verification | Reading from a rediscovered scroll; priestly teaching | Written collection and recension — dictation from multiple memorisers | Oral cross-examination — questioning witnesses to establish authenticity | Written grammatical rules — a concise, algebraic notation |
| **Post-crisis canon status** | Lost in its complete form; fragmentary Āgamas remain; both sects accept the loss | The Sutta Piṭaka survives in Pāli (and in Chinese/ Tibetan translations) | The Torah survives as the central scripture of Judaism; the Prophets and Writings were later canonised | The Quran in its Uthmanic recension is the universal text of Islam | The Pāli Tipiṭaka is the definitive Theravāda canon | The Aṣṭādhyāyī remains the foundational text of Sanskrit grammar |
| **Veneration** | Revered as a saint and Ācārya by both Jain sects | Venerated as a patriarch; included in the list of the Buddha's chief disciples | Venerated as a restorer of the Law; a major figure in Jewish and Christian tradition | Respected as a Rightly Guided Caliph; the third of the Rāshidūn | Venerated as president of the First Council and foremost in ascetic practices | Revered as the greatest grammarian in Indian tradition; a semi-divine figure in myth |
| **Sectarian significance** | Both Digambara and Śvetāmbara claim him; his split with Sthūlabhadra is the origin-point of the two sects | All Buddhist schools revere Ānanda; the Mahāyāna also credits him with preserving Mahāyāna sūtras | Both Jewish and Christian traditions honour him; Samaritan tradition has a different version | All Muslims revere the Uthmanic codex; Shīʿa regard ʿUthmān's caliphate as illegitimate, but accept the text | All Buddhist schools honour Mahākassapa; Mahāyāna developed a different lineage tradition | All Hindu grammatical traditions depend on Pāṇini; the Pāṇinīya school is the dominant one |
| **Key difference** | Bhadrabāhu could not transmit the canon intact before his death; the loss is permanent and shapes Jain self-understanding | Ānanda successfully transmitted the Suttas; the canon survived substantially intact | Ezra successfully restored the Torah; the canon continued to grow | ʿUthmān created a closed, definitive text; no significant variation remains | Mahākassapa's verification process succeeded; the canon was fixed at the First Council | Pāṇini created an independent system that could be learned without a living teacher |

### Why This Comparison Matters

Bhadrabāhu is unique among these figures in one crucial respect: **he failed to preserve the canon**. Unlike Ānanda (whose recitation survived), Ezra (whose Torah was restored and canonised), or ʿUthmān (who produced the standardised Quran), Bhadrabāhu was the *last* person to know the complete text — and after his death, the complete canon was lost forever. Jainism is the only major world religion that acknowledges, as a central historical fact, that its original scripture has been permanently lost and that all surviving texts are incomplete reconstructions.

This shapes Jain epistemology and practice in distinctive ways:
- It creates an openness to multiple versions of the same text (Śvetāmbara and Digambara canons differ significantly)
- It places greater emphasis on the living teacher (guru) than on fixed scripture
- It encourages a philosophical rather than literalist approach to textual authority — the truth of the Dhamma is eternal, even if its verbal expression is lost
- It explains why Jain ritual (such as Paryuṣaṇā) centres on the *recitation* of texts by a living monk, not on silent reading of a fixed canon
