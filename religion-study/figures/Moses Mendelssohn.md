# Moses Mendelssohn (משה מנדלסון — The Socrates of Berlin)

The father of the *Haskalah* (Jewish Enlightenment) and the first Jewish philosopher to fully integrate into European intellectual life while remaining a committed Jew. Moses Mendelssohn's translation of the Torah into German, his philosophical defence of Judaism, and his advocacy for Jewish civil rights laid the foundations for Jewish modernity.

## Key Contributions

- ***Jerusalem, or on Religious Power and Judaism* (1783):** A landmark essay arguing for the separation of church and state, religious pluralism, and the freedom of conscience. Mendelssohn contends that the state has no authority over matters of belief — only over actions — and that Judaism does not coerce belief but invites rational conviction.
- **Torah Translation into German (1780–1783):** Mendelssohn translated the Pentateuch (and later Psalms) into High German, written in Hebrew letters. The *Biur* (commentary) that accompanied the translation, produced with a team of collaborators, helped Germanspeaking Jews access Scripture in literary German and became a gateway to secular education.
- ***Phädon, or On the Immortality of the Soul* (1767):** A dialogue modelled on Plato's *Phaedo* arguing for the immortality of the soul on rational grounds. It was a bestseller in Enlightenment Germany, translated into many languages, and earned Mendelssohn the title "The Socrates of Berlin."
- ***Morning Hours* (1785):** A defence of rational metaphysics and of the existence of God, written in response to Spinoza's pantheism and the *Pantheismusstreit* (pantheism controversy) with Friedrich Heinrich Jacobi.
- **Jewish Emancipation Advocacy:** Mendelssohn corresponded with Christian Wilhelm Dohm on the civil improvement of the Jews and influenced the Edict of Toleration (1782) in Austria and later Jewish emancipation in France and Prussia.

## Life & Legacy

- Born in Dessau to a poor Torah scribe; a childhood illness left him with a curved spine
- Followed his teacher Rabbi David Fränkel to Berlin, where he taught himself German, Latin, Greek, French, English, and philosophy
- Became a partner in a silk-factory while establishing himself as a public intellectual and correspondent of the leading figures of the German Enlightenment (Lessing, Kant, Lavater)
- His friendship with Gotthold Ephraim Lessing inspired Lessing's play *Nathan the Wise* (1779), a plea for religious tolerance
- The controversy with Johann Caspar Lavater (1769–1770), who publicly challenged Mendelssohn to convert to Christianity, established Mendelssohn as the public face of Judaism in Europe
- His children converted to Christianity, including his granddaughter Felix Mendelssohn — a painful legacy that reflected the Haskalah's unintended outcome: assimilation
- His life symbolises the promise and the peril of the Jewish encounter with modernity
