# Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah (רבי אלעזר בן עזריה)

A Tanna of the second generation who was appointed Nasi (Patriarch) of the Sanhedrin at the extraordinarily young age of 18. Descended from Ezra the Scribe and remarkably wealthy, Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah is remembered for his humility, his wisdom, and his famous teaching in Pirkei Avot that links Torah study to right conduct. His appointment as Nasi followed the deposition of Rabban Gamaliel II, whom the sages removed for repeatedly humiliating Rabbi Yehoshua.

Although young, Rabbi Elazar possessed the wisdom and lineage befitting the Nasi — he was a tenth-generation descendant of Ezra, and his wealth allowed him to conduct the office with dignity. When Rabban Gamaliel was eventually reinstated, Rabbi Elazar graciously stepped down, and a compromise was reached whereby certain honours rotated between the two. The Talmud describes how his face shone with youthful radiance but also with divine wisdom, and his debates with his elders commanded immediate respect.

## Key Contributions

- **Appointment as Nasi:** After the sages deposed Rabban Gamaliel for his autocratic conduct, Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah — still a young man — was appointed Nasi of the Sanhedrin (Berakhot 27b–28a). His appointment marked a significant democratisation of rabbinic authority.
- **"Where there is no Torah, there is no right conduct":** His most famous saying (Avot 3:17) draws a reciprocal relationship between Torah study and ethical behaviour: "Where there is no Torah there is no right conduct (דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ); where there is no right conduct there is no Torah. Where there is no wisdom there is no fear of God; where there is no fear of God there is no wisdom."
- **The Passover Haggadah:** Rabbi Elazar is quoted in the Mishnah (Pesachim 10:4) regarding the requirement to tell the story of the Exodus on Passover night — his teaching establishes that the telling must begin with disgrace (גְּנוּת) and conclude with praise (שֶׁבַח), a principle that shaped the structure of the Haggadah.
- **Opening of the Yavneh Academy:** During his tenure as Nasi, the academy at Yavneh was opened to all students — tradition says that hundreds of additional benches were added to accommodate those previously excluded (Berakhot 28a).

## In Jewish Tradition

- The Mishnah (Berakhot 7:3) notes that during his tenure as Nasi, the requirement to invite a priest to lead the blessing after meals was relaxed
- His wealth is described in the Talmud as including flocks of sheep, vineyards, and orchards that sustained his ability to support scholars
- He is one of the few sages quoted in both the Mishnah and the Passover Haggadah
- His conciliatory response after losing the Nasi-ship — "He has come to the harbour and not only to the sea" — is cited as a model of gracious conduct
