# Ba'al Shem Tov (בעש"ט — Rabbi Israel ben Eliezer)

The **Besht** (acronym of *Ba'al Shem Tov* — "Master of the Good Name") was the founder of [[Hasidism|Hasidic Judaism]] (חסידות — Chasidut). He taught that God is present in all things, that joy is a form of holiness, and that the simple, heartfelt service of ordinary Jews is as precious as the scholarship of the learned. His movement revolutionised Jewish spirituality.

## Key Contributions

- **Founder of Hasidism:** The Besht transformed Jewish religious life by democratising mysticism. He taught that *devekut* (cleaving to God) is accessible to every Jew, and that serving God with joy (*simchah shel mitzvah*) is the highest spiritual path.
- **God in All Things:** His central theological insight — *leit atar panuy minei* (there is no place empty of God) — meant that even mundane activities can be acts of divine service if performed with intention. He saw sparks of holiness trapped in all material existence.
- **Prayer and Hitbodedut:** He emphasised intense, spontaneous prayer over fixed scholastic study and taught *hitbodedut* (personal communion with God) as the core of spiritual life.
- **Healing and Wonders:** As a *ba'al shem* (practitioner of practical Kabbalah), he was renowned as a healer and wonder-worker. He travelled among the simple folk of Podolia and Volhynia, bringing hope and spiritual renewal.
- **Oral Teachings:** The Besht wrote no books. His teachings were transmitted orally and later recorded by disciples — especially *Shivchei ha-Besht* and *Keter Shem Tov*.

## Life & Legacy

- Orphaned as a child; grew up in the Jewish community of Okopy (then Poland, now Ukraine)
- Served as a *melamed* (teacher's assistant) and later as a *shochet* (ritual slaughterer) before revealing himself as a mystical leader around 1740
- Relocated to Medzhybizh (Podolia), which became the centre of his movement
- His followers included both great scholars and unlettered folk — a deliberate community of equals
- His successor, the [[Maggid of Mezritch]], systematised his teachings and trained the next generation of Hasidic leaders
- The opposition of the [[Vilna Gaon]] and the Mitnagdim defined the major religious conflict of 18th-century Judaism
