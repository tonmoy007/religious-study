# Hind bint Utbah

One of the most complex figures in early Islamic history — the Quraysh matriarch who lost her father and brother at Badr, mutilated the Prophet's uncle at Uhud, and later converted at the Conquest of Mecca. Her story is a study in vengeance, honour, and transformation.

---

## Biography

- **Father:** Utbah ibn Rabi'ah (killed by Hamzah at Badr)
- **Brother:** al-Walid ibn Utbah (killed by Ali at Badr)
- **Husband:** [[Abu Sufyan]]
- **Son:** **Muawiyah** — founder of the Umayyad Caliphate
- **Died:** c. 640 CE, Medina

---

## Key Moments

### The Vengeance (Uhud, 625 CE)

After Badr, Hind was consumed with grief and rage — her father, brother, and uncle had all been killed in the battle. At Uhud, she rallied the Quraysh women to fight alongside the men, beating drums and singing war songs.

When Hamzah was killed by Wahshi, Hind:
- Cut open his chest and tore out his liver
- Attempted to eat it (some traditions say she couldn't swallow it)
- Mutilated his body in a brutal act of vengeance

### The Aftermath

When the Prophet saw Hamzah's body, he swore he would mutilate 30 Quraysh in return. But revelation descended:
> *"If you punish, punish as you were punished — but if you are patient, that is better for the patient."* — **16:126**

The Prophet chose forgiveness over vengeance.

### The Conversion (Conquest of Mecca, 630 CE)

Five years later, when the Muslim army entered Mecca, Hind came before the Prophet trembling. She covered her face and said: "Muhammad, I was a woman wronged."

The Prophet recognized her but forgave her — one of the most dramatic pardons of the Conquest.

She emerged as a sincere Muslim. She personally killed **Abu Jandal**'s father in battle later, and her son **Muawiyah** would go on to found the Umayyad empire.

---

## Legacy

- Her story is the most dramatic personal transformation among the Quraysh converts
- The Prophet's forgiveness of her is a key example of his capacity to pardon even the worst offences
- Often depicted as the Arab "Lady Macbeth" — complex, fierce, and ultimately reformed
