# Kundakunda

> Born: c. 2nd century CE, South India (traditional site: Koṅkaṇa region, modern Maharashtra/Karnataka border)
> Died: c. 3rd century CE
> Title: The greatest Digambara philosopher; author of the four "Essence" texts; the most influential Jain thinker after the Āgamic period

Kundakunda is the most profound and original philosopher in the Digambara Jain tradition — a figure whose influence on Jain thought is comparable to Śaṅkara's influence on Advaita Vedānta. He is the author of four seminal Sanskrit and Prakrit works — **Samayasāra** (The Essence of the Self), **Pravacanasāra** (The Essence of the Teaching), **Pañcāstikāya** (The Five Existents), and **Niyamasāra** (The Essence of Discipline) — which together form the bedrock of Digambara philosophy. His central contribution is the systematic distinction between *vyavahāra naya* (conventional truth) and *niścaya naya* (ultimate truth), a hermeneutic that resolves apparent contradictions in Jain scripture and provides a radical spiritual path centred on the pure self rather than external ritual. After the Jain Āgamas, his works are the most important philosophical texts in the Digambara tradition, commented upon by every major Digambara thinker for nearly two millennia. He transformed Jainism from a religion of ascetic practice into a profound metaphysical system grounded in the absolute reality of the self.

## Biography

The historical Kundakunda is elusive — no contemporary biography survives, and traditional accounts are mixed with hagiography. The following table synthesises what is known and traditionally accepted.

| Event | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Name | His given name is uncertain; "Kundakunda" may refer to his lineage (the Kundakunda *gaccha* or monastic order) or to his birthplace. Some traditions call him **Padmanandi** or **Vakragrīva**. He is also referred to as **Kundakundācārya** ("Teacher Kundakunda") |
| Birthplace | Traditional accounts place his birth in the Koṅkaṇa region (the coastal strip of modern Maharashtra and Karnataka), either in a village named Kundakunda or in the town of Koṅkaṇapura. The region was a thriving centre of Digambara monasticism |
| Era | Scholarly consensus places him c. 2nd–3rd century CE, though traditional Digambara dating sometimes pushes him earlier (c. 1st century BCE). The latest plausible date is the 4th century CE, as his works were already being commented upon by the 5th–6th centuries |
| Lineage | He belonged to the **Mūla Saṅgha** (the original monastic order) and is considered the founder or eponymous head of the **Kundakunda Anvaya** (Kundakunda lineage), the most influential Digambara monastic lineage in South India. He is traditionally regarded as a successor in the lineage descending from Bhadrabāhu's southern migration |
| Initiation | Initiated into the Digambara monastic order at a young age. He studied the Jain Āgamas (the 11 Aṅgas and the lost Pūrvas) under his teacher — though by his time the complete canon had been lost, and his works show familiarity with the surviving Āgamic traditions |
| Ascetic Life | Lived as a Digambara monk (*muni*) — naked, possessionless, observing the five mahāvratas. His works emphasise that the true monk is one who knows the self, not merely one who wears the external signs of renunciation |
| Composition of Works | He wrote four major texts in **Prakrit** (Jain Māhārāṣṭrī), the language of the Jain Āgamas, along with several shorter works. The four are universally accepted as authentic; numerous other attributions are traditional but disputed |
| Death | Attained *sallekhanā* (the Jain death-rite of fasting unto death) at an advanced age. The traditional site is on a hill in the Koṅkaṇa region, now a Digambara pilgrimage site |
| Traditional veneration | Honoured as an *Ācārya* of the highest rank. His image is found in Digambara temples; his *nirvāṇa divasa* (death anniversary) is observed by the Kundakunda Anvaya monasteries |

## The Four "Essence" Texts

Kundakunda's four major works are collectively known as the **Caturbhāṇī** or the four Essence texts. They are written in Prakrit verses (*gāthās*) and are studied as foundational philosophical treatises in the Digambara tradition — equivalent in importance to the Upaniṣads in Vedānta or the Mādhyamika Kārikās in Buddhism.

### Samayasāra (The Essence of the Self)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Title meaning** | *Samaya* = "the self" or "the essential nature"; *sāra* = "essence". Thus, "The Essence of the Self" or "The Nature of Reality" |
| **Structure** | 415 Prakrit verses in 12 chapters (or, in some recensions, 439 verses in 10 chapters) |
| **Core thesis** | The self (*jīva*) is the only absolute reality. All external phenomena — karma, body, possessions, rituals, even the scriptural teachings themselves — are ultimately unreal from the standpoint of the self's pure nature. The self is eternally conscious, blissful, and pure; it only *appears* to be bound by karma due to ignorance (*mithyātva*) |
| **Central verse** | Verse 156 (varies by recension): *"Jaha savvaṁ jaṁ nāma savvaṁ, savvaṁ jaṁ ca savvado; savvaṁ jaṁ ca na savvaṁ ca, savvaṁ savvāṇa savvado."* — a paradoxical verse pointing to the ineffable nature of the self |
| **Significance** | This is Kundakunda's **magnum opus** — the most studied, commented upon, and revered Jain philosophical text after the Tattvārtha Sūtra. It is considered the key to all his other works |
| **Key commentaries** | *Samayasāra Kalaśa* by Amṛtacandra (10th c.), *Ātma-Khyāti* by Jayasena (11th c.), *Samayasāra-Ṭīkā* by Pūjyapāda (5th–6th c.) — the cumulative commentarial tradition rivals that of any Indian philosophical text |

### Pravacanasāra (The Essence of the Teaching)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Title meaning** | *Pravacana* = "teaching" (especially the teaching of the Jina); *sāra* = "essence" |
| **Structure** | 295 Prakrit verses in 10 chapters (3 chapters in some recensions) |
| **Core thesis** | A systematic exposition of the Jain doctrine of **dravya** (substance) and **paryāya** (modification). All real things have three aspects: substance (the permanent substratum), quality (the inherent characteristics), and mode (the transient state). Kundakunda applies this framework to the self, karma, and the path to liberation |
| **Central teaching** | The Jina's teaching has two levels: the *pramāṇa* (valid knowledge) that reveals reality as it is, and the *naya* (standpoint) that speaks in conventional terms. Understanding this distinction is the key to understanding the Āgamas |
| **Significance** | It is the most systematic of Kundakunda's works — essentially a textbook of Jain ontology and epistemology from the niścaya naya standpoint |

### Pañcāstikāya (The Five Existents)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Title meaning** | *Pañca* = "five"; *astikāya* = "that which occupies space" or "extensive existent" |
| **Structure** | 153 Prakrit verses |
| **Core thesis** | An exposition of the five fundamental substances of Jain metaphysics: (1) **Jīva** (soul/substance), (2) **Pudgala** (matter), (3) **Dharma** (motion-medium), (4) **Adharma** (rest-medium), and (5) **Ākāśa** (space). The sixth substance, **Kāla** (time), is not *astikāya* because it does not occupy space |
| **Significance** | This is the most accessible of Kundakunda's four major works — a concise, verse-by-verse description of the six substances (*dravyas*) that constitute reality, drawing directly on the Āgamic classification. It serves as a primer for his more advanced philosophical works |

### Niyamasāra (The Essence of Discipline)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Title meaning** | *Niyama* = "discipline" or "rule" (especially the rules of conduct for monks); *sāra* = "essence" |
| **Structure** | 187 Prakrit verses |
| **Core thesis** | A radical redefinition of what constitutes true monastic discipline. External rules of conduct (*veśa-dharma*) are subordinate to internal realisation of the self. A monk who knows the self is truly observing discipline even if he does not perform external rituals; one who merely follows external rules without self-knowledge is not truly disciplined |
| **Central argument** | There are two types of discipline: *yathā-prāvṛttikara* (discipline that works through external action — the conventional path) and *a-prāvṛttikara* (discipline that works through internal cessation — the ultimate path). The highest discipline is to abide in the self's pure nature, unaffected by karmic influx |
| **Significance** | This is Kundakunda's most controversial work — it challenges the external ritualism of some Jain monastic traditions and insists that true asceticism is an internal state of realisation |

## The Two-Truths Doctrine (Vyavahāra Naya and Niścaya Naya)

Kundakunda's most original and influential contribution is his systematic distinction between two standpoints or levels of truth — a hermeneutic that he applies throughout all four of his major works.

### The Two Nayas

| Standpoint | Sanskrit | Meaning | Status | Content |
|-----------|----------|---------|--------|---------|
| **Conventional truth** | *Vyavahāra naya* | "Practical standpoint" or "worldly usage" | Relative, pedagogical, provisional | The self appears to be bound by karma; rebirth is real; external rituals purify the soul; the Āgamas describe the path literally; merit and demerit (puṇya and pāpa) are real distinctions |
| **Ultimate truth** | *Niścaya naya* | "Certain standpoint" or "absolute viewpoint" | Absolute, final, real | The self is eternally pure, conscious, and free; karma has never touched it; bondage is an illusion caused by ignorance; liberation is not attained — it is realised as already present |

### How the Two Truths Relate

| Traditional analogy | Explanation |
|--------------------|-------------|
| **The Lotus in Mud** | The lotus root is in the mud (vyavahāra — the self is "fallen" into karma), but the flower is untouched by the mud (niścaya — the self is eternally pure). Both statements are true from their respective standpoints |
| **The Rope and the Snake** | From the conventional standpoint, a rope appears to be a snake — the appearance is real enough to cause fear and action. From the ultimate standpoint, there is nothing but rope — the snake never existed. Similarly, the self *appears* bound (vyavahāra) but is *never actually* bound (niścaya) |
| **The Dreamer and the Awakened** | In a dream, one experiences bondage, suffering, and action (vyavahāra). Upon waking, one realises none of it was real — the self was always safe in bed (niścaya). Liberation is waking up, not escaping |
| **The Mirror and Dust** | The mirror is covered with dust (vyavahāra — the self is "dirty" with karma). When the dust is wiped away, the mirror's natural clarity is revealed — it was never *in* the dust; the dust was only ever on the surface (niścaya — the self was never truly bound) |

### Implications for Jain Practice

Kundakunda's two-truths framework has profound consequences for how Jainism is understood and practised:

| Aspect | Vyavahāra Naya (Conventional) | Niścaya Naya (Ultimate) |
|--------|-------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Bondage** | The soul is bound by karma and must work to eliminate it | The soul is eternally free; bondage is an illusion born of ignorance |
| **Liberation** | Mokṣa is attained after a long process of karmic shedding, culminating in absolute purity | Mokṣa is the realisation of what the self has always been — pure, conscious, and free |
| **Ethics** | External conduct — fasting, celibacy, non-violence, confession — purifies the soul and stops karmic influx | Internal state — abiding in the self's nature — is the true meaning of every ethical vow; external acts are mere signs |
| **Scripture** | The Āgamas are authoritative descriptions of reality and the path | The Āgamas are *upāya* (means) that lead to realisation; their literal statements are not final truth |
| **The Monk** | One who renounces possessions, wears no clothes, follows the monastic code, and performs rituals | One who knows the self and abides in it; external renunciation is secondary to internal realisation |
| **God / Jina** | The Jina is a historical teacher who attained perfection and is worthy of worship | The Jina is the realised self that every being already is; worship is recognising one's own true nature |
| **Karma** | Karma is a real substance that binds the soul, causing rebirth and suffering | Karma is *mithyā* (false) — it cannot touch the self, which is beyond all substance interactions |

### Why This Matters

Kundakunda's two-truths doctrine is not a form of illusionism (māyāvāda) like Advaita Vedānta. He does not say the world is unreal — the world of matter and karma is real at its own level. What he says is that the **self's relationship to karma** is the key: the self *seems* bound but *is not actually* bound. This is a distinctively Jain form of two-truths theory, different from:

- **Nāgārjuna's two truths** (Buddhist Mādhyamika): Both saṃvṛti (conventional) and paramārtha (ultimate) are empty; there is no substantial self at either level.
- **Śaṅkara's two truths** (Advaita Vedānta): The world (vyāvahārika) is illusory; only Brahman (pāramārthika) is real; the individual self is ultimately identical with Brahman.
- **Kundakunda's two truths**: The self is *real* and *individual* at both levels; what changes is our understanding of its relationship with karma. The world of substance interactions is real — it just does not touch the self's true nature.

This is sometimes called **Jain Spiritual Monism** or **Ātma-Advaita** — a monism of the self within an ontologically dualistic framework. The self alone is ultimately real, but the non-self (ajīva) is also real at its own level — it is just irrelevant to the self's true nature.

## Influence: Kundakunda as the "Śaṅkara of Jainism"

Kundakunda's influence on Digambara Jainism is so profound that he is often called the **Śaṅkara of Jainism** — a comparison that reveals both his historical role and his philosophical method.

| Aspect | Kundakunda (Jain) | Śaṅkara (Hindu, Advaita Vedānta) |
|--------|------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Era** | c. 2nd–3rd century CE | c. 8th century CE (Šaṅkara is later by ~500 years) |
| **Tradition** | Digambara Jainism | Advaita Vedānta (Hinduism) |
| **Core move** | Distinguished vyavahāra naya (conventional) from niścaya naya (ultimate) to resolve scriptural contradictions | Distinguished vyāvahārika (empirical) from pāramārthika (transcendental) reality to resolve Upaniṣadic contradictions |
| **Ultimate reality** | The self (jīva) in its pure, eternal nature — distinct from matter, karma, and all external conditions | Brahman — the single, non-dual reality; the self (ātman) is identical with Brahman |
| **Status of the world** | Real at its own level (dualistic ontology); the world of matter and karma genuinely exists, but is irrelevant to the self's ultimate nature | Illusory (māyā) from the ultimate standpoint; only Brahman is real |
| **Nature of bondage** | The self *appears* bound by karma due to ignorance; bondage is real at the conventional level but never touches the self's essence | The self *appears* bound by avidyā (ignorance); bondage has no ultimate reality |
| **Path to liberation** | Self-knowledge (*samyag-darśana* + *samyag-jñāna*) combined with ethical purification; the internal realisation of the self's true nature | Knowledge (*jñāna*) alone; karma and bhakti are preparatory but cannot directly liberate |
| **Relationship to scripture** | Āgamas are authoritative at the conventional level but must be interpreted through the niścaya naya to reveal their true meaning | The Upaniṣads are authoritative, but their apparent contradictions are resolved by recognising the distinction between higher and lower knowledge |
| **Commentarial tradition** | Amṛtacandra, Jayasena, Pūjyapāda — each building on Kundakunda's framework | Sureśvara, Padmapāda, Vācaspati Miśra — each building on Śaṅkara's framework |
| **Institutional legacy** | Founded the Kundakunda Anvaya (the dominant Digambara monastic lineage in South India) | Established the Daśanāmī order of monks and the four cardinal maṭhas (monasteries) |
| **Role in tradition** | The philosopher who systematised Jain spirituality around the primacy of self-knowledge, making Jainism a genuinely philosophical path rather than merely an ascetic practice | The philosopher who systematised Vedānta around the identity of ātman and Brahman, re-establishing Hinduism as a sophisticated philosophical tradition in the face of Buddhist and Jain critiques |
| **Key difference** | Maintains the reality of the individual self (pluralism of jīvas) within a dualistic framework; the self is not identical with any universal reality | Maintains the non-duality of all existence; the individual self is ultimately identical with the single Brahman |

### Why the Comparison Holds

1. **Systematiser**: Both took a diverse, multi-layered scriptural tradition and showed that its apparent contradictions resolve into a coherent system when the distinction between two levels of truth is understood.
2. **Hermeneutics**: Both introduced a two-level interpretive framework that became the standard method for reading their respective scriptures.
3. **Centrality of Self-Knowledge**: Both made *ātma-jñāna* (self-knowledge) the direct path to liberation, subordinating ritual, ethics, and devotion to the realisation of the self's true nature.
4. **Commentarial Tradition**: Both generated a massive commentarial tradition that continues to this day — no Digambara philosopher after Kundakunda can avoid engaging with his system.
5. **Sectarian Identity**: Both became the defining philosopher of their respective traditions — to be a Digambara intellectual is, in part, to be a follower of Kundakunda, just as to be an Advaita Vedāntin is to be a follower of Śaṅkara.

## Kundakunda's Place in the Jain Philosophical Canon

The relationship between Kundakunda and the other great Jain philosophers can be understood as follows:

| Philosopher | Text | Sect | Role | Kundakunda's Relationship |
|-------------|------|------|------|---------------------------|
| **[[Umaswāmi]]** (Umasvāti) | Tattvārtha Sūtra (c. 2nd–5th c. CE) | Both sects | The systematic theologian — composed the only Jain text accepted by all sects, presenting Jain metaphysics in a terse sūtra format | Kundakunda presupposes and deepens the Tattvārtha framework; he provides the hermeneutic key (the two nayas) for reading the sūtras |
| **Kundakunda** | Samayasāra, Pravacanasāra, etc. (c. 2nd–3rd c. CE) | Digambara | The radical philosopher — centres everything on self-knowledge and the ultimate standpoint | — |
| **Pūjyapāda** | Sarvārthasiddhi (c. 5th–6th c. CE) | Digambara | The commentator — wrote the first surviving commentary on Umaswāmi's Tattvārtha Sūtra | Wrote commentaries on Kundakunda's works; synthesised Kundakunda's two-truths with Umaswāmi's system |
| **Amṛtacandra** | Puruṣārthasiddhyupāya, Samayasāra Kalaśa (10th c. CE) | Digambara | The practical philosopher — showed how Kundakunda's philosophy translates into daily Jain practice | His *Samayasāra Kalaśa* is the most famous commentary on Kundakunda's magnum opus |
| **Jayasena** | Ātma-Khyāti (11th c. CE) | Digambara | The dialectician — defended Kundakunda against Buddhist and Hindu critiques | His commentary on Samayasāra is essential for understanding Kundakunda's logical structure |
| **Haribhadra** | Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya (8th c. CE) | Śvetāmbara | The comparativist — studied and summarised all six Indian philosophical systems | Engages with Kundakunda's ideas critically; shows that Śvetāmbara philosophy also values the ultimate standpoint |
| **Hemacandra** | Yogaśāstra, Anyayogavyavacchedikā (12th c. CE) | Śvetāmbara | The polymath — integrated Jain philosophy, grammar, and logic | His Yogaśāstra continues the internalisation of Jain practice that Kundakunda pioneered |

## In Jain Tradition

### Iconography

Kundakunda is depicted in Digambara art with the following features:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Posture** | Seated in meditation (*padmāsana*), holding a manuscript or with a palm-leaf book representing his four Essence texts |
| **Clothing** | Naked, consistent with the Digambara (sky-clad) ideal of complete non-attachment |
| **Facial expression** | Contemplative, with eyes half-closed — absorbed in the realisation of the self |
| **Attributes** | A manuscript (the Samayasāra) in hand; sometimes a *jñāna-mudrā* (gesture of knowledge) with thumb and forefinger touching |
| **Body colour** | Golden or radiant white, signifying his purity and wisdom |
| **Distinguishing mark** | Often shown with a long beard, marking his age and spiritual maturity |

### Key Commentaries on His Works

| Commentary | Author | Century | On Which Text | Significance |
|------------|--------|---------|---------------|-------------|
| **Samayasāra Kalaśa** ("The Jar of the Essence of the Self") | Amṛtacandra | 10th c. | Samayasāra | The most widely read commentary; written in Sanskrit verses with prose explanation; clarifies Kundakunda's paradoxes through the analogy of a pot (kalaśa) — the self is like the space inside a pot, which is not truly inside anything |
| **Ātma-Khyāti** ("The Illumination of the Self") | Jayasena | 11th c. | Samayasāra | A massive Sanskrit prose commentary that defends Kundakunda against the charge of crypto-Buddhism or illusionism |
| **Pravacanasāra-Ṭīkā** | Amṛtacandra | 10th c. | Pravacanasāra | Systematic exposition of Kundakunda's ontology of substances |
| **Pañcāstikāya-Ṭīkā** | Amṛtacandra | 10th c. | Pañcāstikāya | Clarifies the six substances and their modes |
| **Niyamasāra-Ṭīkā** | Padmaprabha (or Māṇikyanandi) | 11th–12th c. | Niyamasāra | Explains the internalisation of monastic discipline |

### The Kundakunda Anvaya (Lineage)

Kundakunda is the eponymous founder of the **Kundakunda Anvaya**, the most influential Digambara monastic lineage in South India. This lineage:

- Traces itself back through Bhadrabāhu's southern migration to Mahāvīra himself
- Produced the greatest Digambara philosophers and commentators: Pūjyapāda, Amṛtacandra, Jayasena, Vīrasena, and many others
- Controls the major Digambara centres in Karnataka and Maharashtra: Shravanabelagola, Mudabidri, Kārañjā, and others
- Maintains the strictest interpretation of Digambara monastic rules (nudity, non-possession, begging for food)
- Continues to this day as the dominant monastic order among the Digambara community

### Festivals & Commemorations

- **Kundakunda Jayantī** — Celebrated by the Digambara community, especially in Karnataka and Maharashtra, marking his birth. Includes recitations of the Samayasāra, lectures on his philosophy, and special pūjās
- **Samayasāra Pārāyaṇa** — A ritual reading of the entire Samayasāra text, often performed over 7 or 14 days in Digambara monasteries
- **Kundakunda Nirvāṇa Divasa** — The anniversary of his death; observed by the Kundakunda Anvaya monasteries with fasting, meditation, and recitation of his works

## Comparative: The "Philosopher of the Self" Across Traditions

Kundakunda belongs to a specific type of religious philosopher: the thinker who makes **self-knowledge** (*ātma-jñāna*) the centre of the spiritual path and subordinates all external religion — ritual, scripture, ethics — to the direct realisation of the self's true nature. The following table compares him with analogous figures.

| Aspect | Kundakunda (Jain) | Śaṅkara (Advaita Vedānta) | Nāgārjuna (Mādhyamika) | Plotinus (Neoplatonism) | Meister Eckhart (Christian Mysticism) |
|--------|------------------|--------------------------|------------------------|------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Tradition** | Digambara Jainism | Hinduism (Vedānta) | Buddhism (Mahāyāna) | Hellenistic philosophy | Christian mysticism (Rhineland) |
| **Core question** | What is the self? | What is Brahman? | What is emptiness? | What is the One? | What is the Godhead? |
| **Ultimate reality** | The pure self (*jīva*) in its intrinsic nature — eternal, conscious, blissful, but individual | Brahman — non-dual, qualityless, identical with the individual self once ignorance is removed | Śūnyatā (Emptiness) — all dharmas are empty of intrinsic nature (svabhāva) | The One (to Hen) — beyond being, thought, and all determination | The Godhead (Gottheit) — beyond God, beyond all names and attributes |
| **Ultimate vs conventional** | Vyavahāra naya (conventional) vs niścaya naya (ultimate) — the self appears bound but is actually free | Vyāvahārika (empirical) vs pāramārthika (transcendental) — the world is illusory, Brahman alone is real | Saṃvṛti (conventional) vs paramārtha (ultimate) — emptiness is the ultimate nature of all phenomena; no self exists at either level | Two levels of reality: the sensible world (appearance) and the intelligible world (reality culminating in the One) | The distinction between God (as creator, personal being) and the Godhead (as the abyssal, non-relational ground) |
| **Self** | The jīva is real, eternal, individual — a plurality of selves exists | The ātman is identical with Brahman — ultimately one, not many | No self (anātman) — the notion of self is the root delusion to be abandoned | The soul's higher self is in constant contact with the Intellect (Nous) and can ascend to the One | The soul's *scintilla animae* (spark of the soul) is uncreated and identical with the Godhead |
| **World / Matter** | Real at its own level — karma and matter genuinely exist and condition experience, but do not touch the self's nature | Illusory (māyā) — the world has no ultimate reality; it is a superimposition on Brahman | Empty (śūnya) — phenomena are dependently arisen and lack intrinsic existence | The material world is the lowest emanation; matter is a falling-away from the light of the One | The world is created and good, but is not-God; one must go beyond all created things to reach the Godhead |
| **Path** | Self-knowledge (samyag-darśana) + ethical purification — the self is realised through direct insight combined with karmic attenuation | Knowledge (jñāna) alone — through śravaṇa (hearing), manana (reflection), nididhyāsana (contemplation) of the mahāvākyas | Wisdom (prajñā) — understanding that all phenomena are empty; no path exists apart from seeing things as they are | Ascesis + theurgic ascent — purification from the body, dialectical ascent to Intellect, and union with the One | *Abegescheidenheit* (detachment) — letting go of all created things and all images of God to experience the Godhead directly |
| **Scripture** | Āgamas are authoritative at the conventional level; must be interpreted through niścaya naya | The Upaniṣads are the highest authority; their contradictory statements are resolved by the two-level hermeneutic | Sūtras are *upāya* (skillful means); they point to emptiness but are themselves empty | Not text-based; reason and spiritual experience are the guide | The Bible is authoritative but must be interpreted allegorically and mystically |
| **Role of ethics** | Essential at the conventional level (vyavahāra); the internalisation of ethics (niścaya) is the ultimate path | Preliminary (for purifying the mind) but not the direct cause of liberation | Ethics (śīla) is the foundation of the path; without it wisdom is impossible | Virtue purifies the soul and enables ascent, but is not the final goal | Works flow from the contemplative state; detachment is the core virtue |
| **Method** | Hermeneutical — interpreting scripture through the two-naya framework | Dialectical — using śravaṇa, manana, nididhyāsana to remove ignorance | Dialectical — the *prasaṅga* method of reductio ad absurdum to demonstrate emptiness | Dialectical — the ascent from multiplicity to unity through negation (apophasis) | Apophatic — the via negativa; stripping away all attributes to reach the Godhead |
| **Key text** | Samayasāra | Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya + Upadeśasāhasrī | Mūla-Mādhyamika-Kārikā | The Enneads | Sermons and treatises (esp. Sermon 52, "Beati pauperes spiritu") |

### The Distinctive Jain Contribution

What makes Kundakunda unique among these "philosophers of the self" is the way he combines:

1. **Pluralism of selves**: Each self is eternally distinct and individual, even in liberation — there is no merging into a universal reality.
2. **Dualistic ontology**: Matter, space, motion, rest, and time are all ultimately real — they just do not touch the self's nature.
3. **Ethical continuity**: The path to self-knowledge includes the ethical life; unlike Śaṅkara (for whom ethics is merely preparatory) or Nāgārjuna (for whom emptiness deconstructs ethics along with everything else), Kundakunda preserves the full Jain ethical framework within the conventional standpoint while transcending it from the ultimate standpoint.
4. **Non-illusionism**: The world is not a dream or an illusion; it is genuinely real. The mistake is not that the world does not exist but that the self's relationship to it is misunderstood.

This position is sometimes called **Ātma-Advaita** (Self-Non-Dualism) — a non-dualism of the self within a dualism of substances.
