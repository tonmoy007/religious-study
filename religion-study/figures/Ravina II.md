# Ravina II (רבינא — Ravina the Later)

**Ravina II** was a leading Babylonian Amora (sage of the Talmudic period) who, together with Rav Ashi's successors, completed the redaction and final editing of the Babylonian Talmud. He is one of the last of the Amoraim — the *Savoraim* (explainers) who succeeded him added only minor clarifications to the text. His work brought the approximately three-century-long process of Talmudic compilation to its definitive conclusion.

## Key Contributions

- **Completion of the Babylonian Talmud:** Ravina II oversaw the final redaction of the Babylonian Talmud alongside Rav Ashi's successors at the Sura and Pumbedita academies. Where Rav Ashi had begun the systematic editing of the Gemara, Ravina II brought it to completion around the turn of the 6th century.
- **Last of the Amoraim:** Ravina II is traditionally regarded as the last generation of the Amoraim (c. 500 CE). The period that follows — the Savoraic era — involved only modest editorial work and the addition of explanatory glosses rather than substantive debate or redaction.
- **Preservation of the Oral Law:** By fixing the text of the Talmud, Ravina II ensured that the Oral Torah — the debates, rulings, and traditions of the Tannaim and Amoraim — would be transmitted faithfully to the Jewish people for all subsequent generations.
- **Bridge to the Geonic Period:** The completed Talmud became the foundation text for the Geonim (the heads of the Babylonian academies), who relied on the redacted text to issue responsa to Jewish communities across the diaspora.

## Life & Legacy

- Little is known of his personal biography, as the Talmud itself is the primary record of his era
- He is distinguished from the earlier *Ravina I* (a contemporary of Rav Ashi) — the honorific "Ravina II" or "Ravina the Later" marks this distinction
- His death around 500 CE is conventionally taken as the endpoint of the Amoraic period
- The Babylonian Talmud as we know it today — the *Bavli* — is essentially the text that Ravina II and his colleagues finalised; later scribes and printers introduced minor variations, but the framework is his
