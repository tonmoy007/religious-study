# Ṛṣabha (Rishabhanatha / Ādinātha)

> The first Tīrthaṅkara of the present half of the time-cycle (*avasarpini*) — a prehistoric sage-king who re-established the eternal Jain path after it had been forgotten, and who taught humanity the foundational arts of civilization: agriculture, writing, law, politics, and the crafts. He is the "ford-maker" who first opened the crossing.

---

## The First Tīrthaṅkara

Ṛṣabha is the first of the 24 Tīrthaṅkaras — beings who have conquered the cycle of birth and death (*saṃsāra*) and who "make a ford" (*tīrtha*) across the ocean of suffering for others to follow. He appeared at the beginning of the present descending half-cycle (*avasarpini*), at a time when human life, happiness, and knowledge were at their peak — yet spiritual knowledge had been lost.

### Jain Cosmological Context

| Age (Ara) | Description | Duration |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **1st Ara** (*Suṣama-suṣamā*) | Perfect happiness — no suffering, no need for teachings | 4 × 10¹⁴ sāgaropama years |
| **2nd Ara** (*Suṣamā*) | Decline begins — Ṛṣabha appears here | 3 × 10¹⁴ sāgaropama years |
| **3rd Ara** (*Suṣama-duḥṣamā*) | More suffering — the 20 other Tīrthaṅkaras (2nd–22nd) appear | 2 × 10¹⁴ sāgaropama years |
| **4th Ara** (*Duḥṣama-suṣamā*) | Much suffering — Pārśva (23rd) appears | 1 × 10¹⁴ sāgaropama years |
| **5th Ara** (*Duḥṣamā*) | Maximum suffering — Mahāvīra (24th) appears; we are here now | 21,000 years |
| **6th Ara** (*Duḥṣama-duḥṣamā*) | Worst — no religion possible | 21,000 years |

Ṛṣabha appears in the **second ara** (*suṣamā*), when the world has begun to decline from its peak. At this time, humans were still largely content but had lost the knowledge of the Jain path. Ṛṣabha's role was to re-establish the fourfold order (*saṅgha*) of monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen — the *tīrtha* itself.

---

## The Civilising Hero

Ṛṣabha is far more than a spiritual teacher. Jain tradition portrays him as the **founder of human civilisation** — the one who taught humanity the arts of settled life. The story is preserved in the *Ādīpurāṇa* of Jinasena (9th century CE) and in Hemacandra's *Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra* (Lives of the 63 Illustrious Men).

### The Earth's Wish-Fulfilling Trees Cease to Bear

In the first ara, the earth was a paradise: trees called *kalpavṛkṣas* granted all desires — food, clothing, shelter. But as the second ara began, these trees stopped bearing. Humans were terrified. They went to Ṛṣabha, their king, for help.

Ṛṣabha stepped forward to teach them what they needed to survive:

| Civilisation Gift | What Ṛṣabha Taught | Significance |
|-------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Agriculture** | Ploughing, sowing, harvesting, storing grain, irrigation | The settled life that made cities possible |
| **Animal Husbandry** | Domesticating cows, goats, buffalo; milking, butter-making | From hunting-gathering to pastoral life |
| **Writing / Script** | The *Brahmi* script (named after his daughter Brahmī), and accounting | Literacy, record-keeping, law |
| **Numbers & Mathematics** | Counting, measuring, weighing — the *Saṃkhyāna* system | Trade, taxation, construction |
| **Law & Polity** | Kingship, punishment, boundaries, property rights, contracts | The state, social order |
| **Arts & Crafts** | Pottery, weaving, carpentry, metalwork, architecture | Material culture, technology |
| **The Vedas & Ritual** | The three Vedas (Ṛg, Yajur, Sāma) and the science of sacrifice | Though Jainism later rejected Vedic ritual, Ṛṣabha is credited with originating it for those who could not grasp the higher path |
| **Marriage & Family** | Monogamy, lineage, inheritance | Social structure |
| **Medicine** | Herbal remedies, surgery | Healing and health |
| **Warfare** | The arts of weaponry, forts, armies | Defence and conquest |

> "He, the First Jina, gave the science of numbers, writing, and all other arts — in this world that was sinking in a flood of ignorance." — *Ādīpurāṇa*, 1.42

After establishing all these arts, Ṛṣabha **renounced his kingdom** and became a wandering ascetic — showing that even the founder of civilisation must leave it all behind.

## Renunciation and Enlightenment

### The Gift of the Kingdom

Ṛṣabha divided his kingdom among his 100 sons. He gave half the kingdom to **Bharata**, his eldest, and the other half to his remaining 99 sons. But Bāhubali, the second son, was not content with his share — a conflict that would later define the Jain story of renunciation and rivalry.

### The Renunciation

During a festival, a celestial dancer named Nīlāñjanā was performing in Ṛṣabha's court. She dropped her garland, and a **divine being** appeared. Ṛṣabha saw a vision of his own past lives and realised the vanity of all worldly power. He cut his hair (a Tīrthaṅkara's symbolic act of renunciation), gave away his ornaments, and left the palace — followed by thousands of men who became his first monks.

### The Ascetic Practice

For **1,000 years** Ṛṣabha wandered and meditated in the wilderness, eating very little. His body was covered in dust, and **climbing plants grew over him** — a famous motif in Jain iconography. Finally, while standing in *kāyotsarga* (a standing posture of meditation, arms at the sides), he attained *kevala jñāna* (omniscience) beneath a banyan tree.

### The First Sermon

After enlightenment, Ṛṣabha's first sermon was given at a great gathering in Ayodhyā. It was heard and understood by all beings — humans, animals, gods. His chief disciples (*gaṇadharas*) — 84 of them — understood his teaching fully and became the first lineage of Jain monastic leaders.

### Nirvāṇa

Ṛṣabha attained liberation (*mokṣa* / *nirvāṇa*) on Mount Aṣṭāpada (identified with Mount Kailāsa in Tibet, or with a hill near Hastināpura according to some traditions). He is worshipped as the *Siddha* — a perfected being who will never be reborn.

## Sons: Bharata and Bāhubali

### Bharata

Bharata, the eldest son, inherited the core of Ṛṣabha's kingdom. A **cakravartin** (universal emperor), he conquered the six divisions of India and ruled righteously. He is the eponymous ancestor of **Bhārata-varṣa** — the ancient name for India. Though a lay follower, Bharata is revered in Jain tradition as an ideal king.

> See the full note: [[Bharata (Jain)]]

### Bāhubali

Bāhubali, the second son, defeated Bharata in a duel but renounced the world out of disgust with violence and ego. He stood in **standing meditation** (*kāyotsarga*) for 12 years, unmoving, until vines grew over his body — a motif parallel to his father's. He attained *kevala jñāna* as a non-Tīrthaṅkara Kevalin (not a ford-maker, but fully enlightened).

His 57-foot monolithic statue at **Shravanabelagola** (Karnataka, commissioned by Cāmuṇḍarāya in 981 CE) is the tallest monolithic statue in India and a pilgrimage site for Digambara Jains.

## Association with the Indus Valley Civilisation

Several scholars have proposed connections between Ṛṣabha and the archaeological remains of the Indus Valley Civilisation (c. 2600–1900 BCE):

### The Pashupati Seal (Mohenjo-daro)

The famous **Pashupati seal** (M-304, found at Mohenjo-daro) depicts a figure seated in a yogic posture, surrounded by animals, wearing a horned headdress. Sir John Marshall (1920s) identified it as "Proto-Śiva" (Pashupati, "Lord of Beasts").

However, a competing interpretation connects the seal to **Ṛṣabha**:

| Feature | Possible Ṛṣabha Connection |
|---------|----------------------------|
| **Yogic posture** | Tīrthaṅkaras are always depicted in meditation (*dhyāna-mudrā* or *kāyotsarga*) |
| **The Bull symbol** | Ṛṣabha's lāñchana (emblem) is the bull — bull motifs are ubiquitous in Indus seals |
| **Horned headdress** | Horns may represent the bull — Ṛṣabha's identity is "the Bull" |
| **Surrounded by animals** | Reflects the Tīrthaṅkara's dominion over all beings (non-violence extended to all creatures) |

### Other Evidence

- **Seals with bulls alone** — hundreds of Indus seals feature the bull (Ṛṣabha's symbol). The unicorn motif may also be related to the bull or represent an ancient Jain emblem.
- **Figurines in meditation posture** — Several terracotta figurines from Indus sites (especially Harappa) show figures seated in what appear to be yogic postures.
- **The term "Keśin"** — Jain texts refer to Ṛṣabha as having long matted hair (*jaṭā*); Indus "yogi" figurines likewise show matted or bound hair.
- **Linguistic links** — Some etymologists trace the name Rishabha (the Bull) to Dravidian roots, suggesting pre-Vedic antiquity.

### Scholarly Caution

These connections remain **speculative**. No direct textual evidence links the Indus script to Jainism, and the Pashupati seal is equally plausibly a Śaiva or general fertility motif. Nevertheless, the possibility that proto-Jain yogic traditions predate the Vedic period is a significant (if unproven) hypothesis in the study of Indian religions.

| Perspective | View on Ṛṣabha | Source |
|-------------|----------------|--------|
| **Jain orthodoxy** | Ṛṣabha is a real, superhuman being who lived millions of years ago | Śvetāmbara and Digambara canon |
| **Comparative mythologists** | Ṛṣabha is an archetypal "culture hero" (like Prometheus or Osiris) who embodies the invention of civilisation | Campbell, Eliade |
| **Historians of religion** | Ṛṣabha may reflect a real prehistoric sage-king whose memory was preserved in oral tradition, later mythologised | Heinrich Zimmer, Mircea Eliade, Coomaraswamy |
| **Archaeologists** | No direct archaeological evidence for Ṛṣabha; Indus parallels are suggestive but inconclusive | Gregory Possehl, Jonathan Mark Kenoyer |
| **Indigenous Jain scholarship** | Ṛṣabha is historical and dates to the third or fourth millennium BCE; the Indus Valley was a Jain civilisation | Champat Rai Jain, K.V. Sharma, L.P. Tessitori |

## Iconography

Ṛṣabha is depicted in Jain art and sculpture with distinctive features:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Standing or Seated** | Usually in *kāyotsarga* (standing, arms at sides, motionless) — the posture in which he attained enlightenment. Sometimes seated in *padmāsana* (lotus) |
| **Body Colour** | Golden (*svarṇa*) |
| **Symbol** | The bull (*vṛṣabha*) carved beneath the idol on the pedestal |
| **Hair** | Long matted locks (*jaṭā-jūṭa*) — he is one of the few Tīrthaṅkaras depicted with hair (most are shown with shaven heads) |
| **Climbing Vines** | Some depictions show vines and plants growing up his legs — a reference to his long standing meditation |
| **Attributes** | No weapons or ornaments — the Tīrthaṅkara ideal of complete non-attachment |
| **Face** | Serene, with a half-smile (*ardha-hāsya*) — the peace of one who has conquered all passion |

## Significance

Ṛṣabha is foundational to Jain self-understanding in three ways:

1. **Continuity of the Teaching** — He demonstrates that Jainism is not a "founded" religion. It is *anādi* (beginningless) and eternal. Ṛṣabha "re-discovered" the path and re-established the *tīrtha* for this cycle, but the same path was known to previous Tīrthaṅkaras in past cosmic cycles.

2. **The Unity of Life and Renunciation** — Ṛṣabha lived a full worldly life as a king, father, and civiliser *before* renouncing. This models the Jain ideal of *gṛhastha* (householder) dharma culminating in monastic renunciation — a path available to all.

3. **Ahiṃsā at the Origins of Culture** — Every civilisational gift Ṛṣabha gave was permeated by non-violence. Agriculture was taught with respect for all creatures; law was taught as just restraint; politics as righteous rule. The later Jain emphasis on *ahiṃsā-paramo-dharmaḥ* (non-violence is the supreme dharma) has its roots in Ṛṣabha's civilising mission.

> "That first Jina, lord of all beings, taught the ways of life and release — and the world, which had been darkness, was filled with light." — *Ādīpurāṇa*
