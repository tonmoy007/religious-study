# Rabbi Yehudah Halevi (רבי יהודה הלוי)

The greatest Hebrew poet since the biblical period and one of the most original Jewish philosophers of the Middle Ages. Yehudah Halevi's poetry — both liturgical and secular — is unmatched in its lyrical power and devotional intensity, while his philosophical masterwork, the *Kuzari*, remains a classic defence of Judaism.

## Key Contributions

- ***The Kuzari* (Kitāb al-Ḥujjah, c. 1140):** Written in Arabic as a dialogue between the king of the Khazars and a Jewish sage, this philosophical work defends Judaism against the claims of Aristotelian philosophy, Karaism, Christianity, and Islam. Its central thesis — that Jewish truth rests on historical revelation (mass revelation at Sinai) rather than abstract reasoning — makes it one of the most influential and original works of Jewish thought.
- **Poetry:** Yehudah Halevi's corpus includes hundreds of poems: *zemirot* (Sabbath songs), *selihot* (penitential prayers), wedding odes, elegies, and secular love poems. His poems of longing for Zion are among the most emotionally powerful in Hebrew literature — most famously "Libi ba-Mizrach" (My Heart is in the East) and "Tzion ha-Lo Tish'ali" (Zion, Will You Not Ask?).
- **Zionide Poetry:** He virtually invented the genre of the Zionist poem — odes of aching love for the land of Israel, which he insisted was not merely a spiritual concept but the actual geographic locus of prophecy and divine presence. He died on a pilgrimage to Jerusalem.
- **Physician and Philosopher:** Trained in medicine and Arabic philosophy, Halevi was thoroughly immersed in the Andalusian intellectual tradition. He engaged critically with Aristotelianism, Neoplatonism, and Islamic Kalām, arguing that religious truth is grounded in history, not metaphysics.

## Life & Legacy

- Born in Toledo (Castile) and educated in the Andalusian tradition of Hebrew poetry under Moses ibn Ezra and others
- Practiced medicine in Toledo and later in Christian Spain, moving between Jewish communities
- In 1140, he embarked on his famous journey to the Land of Israel — documented in his letters and poems
- According to tradition, he reached the gates of Jerusalem and was killed by an Arab horseman as he recited his elegy "Tzion ha-Lo Tish'ali"
- His *Kuzari* profoundly influenced later Jewish thought including Maimonides (who quotes it), Judah Abarbanel, and the Maharal of Prague
