# Osiris — Egyptian God

## Summary
Osiris is the god of the underworld, death, resurrection, and regeneration in ancient Egyptian religion. He is the central divine figure of the [[The Egyptian Book of the Dead — Stories, Figures and Connections|Book of the Dead]] — every deceased person seeks to be "justified" (*ma'a kheru*) by becoming a *Osiris* ([[Ma'at — Goddess of Truth]]). His story of murder, dismemberment, and restoration forms the core narrative that underwrites Egyptian funerary theology.

## Role in the Book of the Dead

Osiris permeates the entire text:

| Aspect | Occurrence |
|--------|-----------|
| **Opening Rubric** | The deceased is declared "Osiris N." throughout — the dead person *becomes* Osiris |
| **Chapter 1–16** | Hymns to Osiris: "Hail, Osiris, Lord of Eternity" |
| **Chapter 125** | The deceased stands before Osiris in the Hall of Righteousness, claiming purity |
| **Chapter 175** | Osiris speaks with Thoth about the fate of the soul |
| **Passim** | "Osiris N., triumphant" (*ma'a kheru*) — the standard epithet of the blessed dead |

## Iconography
- Mummified form (white wrappings), green or black skin (fertility of the Nile / rot of death)
- White crown of Upper Egypt (the *atef* crown, flanked by ostrich feathers)
- Crook and flail crossed on the chest — kingship and shepherding
- Often enthroned in the judgment hall, with [[Ma'at — Goddess of Truth]] on a pedestal before him

## The Osiris Myth

See full treatment at [[The Osiris Myth — Egyptian Story]].

In brief:
1. **Osiris rules Egypt** as a wise, just king; [[Set — Egyptian God|Set]] is jealous.
2. **Set murders Osiris**, traps him in a chest, throws it into the Nile.
3. [[Isis — Egyptian Goddess]] searches, finds the chest, but Set discovers the body and **dismembers it into 14 pieces**.
4. Isis and [[Nephthys — Egyptian Goddess]] recover the pieces (one eaten by a fish/crab).
5. **Isis conceives [[Horus — Egyptian God|Horus]]** with the reassembled Osiris.
6. Osiris descends to the **Duat** (underworld) to become Lord of the Dead.
7. Horus grows to **avenge his father** and claims the throne after a contest with Set before the Ennead.

## Theological Significance

### Osiris as the Dying-and-Rising God
Osiris is the archetypal dying-and-rising deity, though his "resurrection" is to the underworld, not to earthly life. This makes him a key figure in comparative religion:
- **Parallel**: [[Jesus Christ — Biblical Figure]] — death, lamentation, victory over death (difference: Osiris rules the dead; Christ conquers death entirely)
- **Parallel**: Tammuz/Dumuzid (Mesopotamia), Ba'al (Canaan), Adonis (Greek) — season-based dying-and-rising vegetation gods
- **Influence on Christianity?**: Some scholars (e.g., Frazer, *The Golden Bough*) suggest Osiris motifs influenced early Christian resurrection theology; most modern scholars see typological parallels rather than direct borrowing

### Osiris as Judge
Unlike many underworld gods, Osiris is a *just* judge — he presides over the [[The Weighing of the Heart — Egyptian Story]] not as punisher but as sovereign. The true judgment comes from the scales; Osiris ratifies the result. He does not condemn; the soul condemns itself.

### Osiris as Every Deceased Person
The formula "Osiris N." means that through proper rites, ethical living, and the knowledge of the [[The Egyptian Book of the Dead — Stories, Figures and Connections|Book of the Dead]], any person can *become* Osiris — achieving justification and eternal life. This democratization of the afterlife is one of the most profound theological moves in ancient religion.

## Key Epithets from the Texts
- "Lord of Eternity" (*Neb-djet*)
- "Foremost of the Westerners" (*Khenti-Amentiu*)
- "Risen in the Morning" — identified with the rising sun (syncretized with [[Ra — Egyptian God|Ra]] as *Ra-Osiris*)
- "The Great God who lives in the Tuat"
- "He who is ever in peace"
