# Abraham & Ibrahim — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

Abraham is the **shared root** of all three faiths — the one figure Judaism,
Christianity, and Islam each claim as founding father (which is why they are
called the *Abrahamic* religions; see [[Abrahamic Traditions]]). Yet the same
man anchors **three different things**: a *covenant of lineage*, a *model of
faith*, and the *first pure monotheist*. Tracing him exposes the deepest fork in
the family — over **which son** lay on the altar. A clear **genealogical**
connection.

## The two great scenes

### 1. The covenant (Genesis) vs. the leadership (Qur'an)
[[scriptures/bible-kjv/01 Genesis|Genesis]] makes Abraham the head of a *bloodline* bound to God:

> [!quote] Genesis — an everlasting covenant of seed
> **12:2** — "I will make of thee a **great nation**…"
> **17:5** — "…a **father of many nations** have I made thee."
> **17:7** — "I will establish my covenant between me and thee and **thy seed after thee**… for an everlasting covenant."
> **17:19** — "Sarah thy wife shall bear thee a son indeed; and thou shalt call his name **Isaac**: and I will establish my covenant with him."

The Qur'an reframes the promise from *bloodline* to *moral leadership* — and
pointedly excludes the unrighteous from inheriting it:

> [!quote] Qur'an — a leader for mankind
> **2:124** — "I have appointed thee a **leader (imam) for mankind**. (Abraham) said: And of my offspring? He said: **My covenant includeth not wrong-doers**."

Where Genesis ties the covenant to *descent*, the Qur'an ties it to *conduct*.

### 2. The near-sacrifice — and the great divergence
This is the most famous Abraham story, and where the traditions split hardest.

> [!quote] Genesis 22 — Isaac, the bound son
> **22:2** — "Take now thy son, **thine only son Isaac**, whom thou lovest… and offer him there for a burnt offering."
> **22:12** — "Lay not thine hand upon the lad…"
> **22:13** — "behold behind him **a ram** caught in a thicket… and offered him up… in the stead of his son."

> [!quote] As-Saaffat 37 — the son who consents (unnamed)
> **37:102** — "O my dear son, I have seen in a dream that I must sacrifice thee. So look, what thinkest thou? He said: **O my father! Do that which thou art commanded.** Allah willing, thou shalt find me of the steadfast."
> **37:103–105** — "…when they had both surrendered… We called unto him: O Abraham! Thou hast already fulfilled the vision."
> **37:107** — "Then We ransomed him with a **tremendous victim**."

| | Genesis | Qur'an (As-Saaffat) |
|---|---|---|
| Which son? | **Isaac**, named explicitly | **unnamed** — Muslim tradition identifies **Ishmael** |
| Does the son know? | No — Isaac asks "where is the lamb?" | **Yes** — the son is told and *consents* |
| The command comes via | God's direct speech | a **dream** Abraham must interpret and obey |
| The substitute | a ram in a thicket | "a tremendous victim" |
| The point | Abraham's obedience / God provides | mutual **surrender** (*islam*) of father *and* son |

The **son's identity** is the theological hinge: Jewish/Christian scripture
names **Isaac** (ancestor of Israel); Islamic tradition reads the unnamed son as
**Ishmael** (ancestor of the Arabs, through whom the line runs to Muhammad). One
altar, two genealogies — and two nations trace their election back to it.

## What only the Qur'an tells: the monotheist's reasoning
The Qur'an gives Abraham an *intellectual conversion* absent from Genesis — he
reasons his way to one God by rejecting the heavenly bodies:

> [!quote] Al-An'am 6:76–79
> "…he beheld a star. He said: This is my Lord. But when it set, he said: **I love not things that set**… the moon… the sun… **This is greater!** And when it set… O my people! Lo! I am free from all that ye associate (with Him). I have turned my face toward Him Who created the heavens and the earth."

And it makes Abraham the **builder of the Kaaba** with Ishmael — founding the
sanctuary at Mecca, with no biblical parallel:

> [!quote] Al-Baqara 2:125–127
> "…Take as your place of worship the place where Abraham stood… when **Abraham and Ishmael were raising the foundations of the House** (at Makka)…"

This relocates Abraham's legacy *geographically* — from Canaan/Israel (Genesis)
to Mecca (Qur'an), grounding Islamic pilgrimage in him.

## Same man, three claims
| Tradition | Abraham is primarily… | Key text |
|---|---|---|
| Judaism | the **covenant-father** of Israel through Isaac | Genesis 17 |
| Christianity | the **exemplar of faith** justified before the Law | Romans 4 (cf. Genesis 15:6) |
| Islam | the **first pure monotheist (*ḥanīf*)** and a *muslim*, builder of the Kaaba | Qur'an 2:124–127; 6:79 |

Trace Abraham and you trace the *whole* relationship of the three faiths — they
share an ancestor but draw the line of inheritance through different sons toward
different peoples, lands, and theologies (see [[Shared Abrahamic Figures]]).
