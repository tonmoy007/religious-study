# Mary Magdalene

> The first witness to the Resurrection — the "apostle to the apostles" — who has been misremembered, maligned, and reclaimed throughout Christian history.

---

## Biography

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| **Origin** | **Magdala** (a town on the Sea of Galilee) |
| **Healing** | Jesus cast out **seven demons** from her (Luke 8:2) |
| **Role** | Faithful disciple — supported Jesus' ministry financially |
| **Presence at the cross** | One of the few who stayed (Matthew 27:55–56) |
| **At the tomb** | First to arrive on Easter morning (John 20:1) |
| **Resurrection witness** | First person to see the risen Christ (John 20:11–18) |
| **Title** | *Apostola Apostolorum* — "Apostle to the Apostles" |

---

## What the Bible Actually Says

Contrary to popular tradition:
- **She is NOT a prostitute** — that identification (with the sinful woman of Luke 7) was a Western tradition dating to Pope Gregory I (591 CE) that has been rejected by modern scholarship
- **She is NOT the woman caught in adultery** (John 8)
- **She is NOT Mary of Bethany** (sister of Martha and Lazarus)

Her biblical portrait: a woman healed by Jesus, who followed him faithfully, supported his ministry, stood at the cross when others fled, and was the **first to proclaim the Resurrection**.

> [!quote] John 20:18
> "Mary Magdalene went and announced to the disciples: 'I have seen the Lord!'"

---

## Connection with Islam

Mary Magdalene is **not named in the Qur'an**. The Qur'an mentions **Maryam** (Mary the mother of Jesus) extensively but does not name any of Jesus' other female followers. In Islamic tradition:
- The disciples (*Ḥawāriyyūn*) are male — Mary Magdalene's role is unknown
- Some Christian apocryphal traditions about Mary Magdalene have parallels in Islamic gnostic literature, but these are speculative
