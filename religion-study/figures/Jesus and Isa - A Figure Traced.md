# Jesus & ʿĪsā — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

No shared figure shows **more agreement on the surface and more disagreement at
the core** than Jesus. The Gospels and the Qur'an both affirm his virgin birth,
his miracles, his title "Messiah," and his special standing before God — yet
they divide absolutely on the two questions Christianity is built on: **is he
divine?** and **was he crucified?** This builds on
[[Birth of Jesus Across Scriptures]]; a clear **genealogical** connection.

## Where they agree
| | Gospels | Qur'an |
|---|---|---|
| Virgin birth | Luke 1:35; Matthew 1:23 | [[019 Maryam\|Maryam]] 19; "Be! and it is" (3:47) |
| Title "Messiah" | "Jesus, who is called Christ" (Matt 1:16) | "the **Messiah**, Jesus, son of Mary" ([[003 Aal-i-Imraan\|3:45]]) |
| Miracles | heals, raises the dead (John 11) | heals the blind & leper, raises the dead ([[003 Aal-i-Imraan\|3:49]]) |
| Word / Spirit from God | "the **Word** was made flesh" (John 1:14) | "a **word** from Him… a spirit from Him" (3:45; 4:171) |

Both even use the loaded vocabulary of "Word" and "Spirit" for Jesus — but the
Qur'an means *a* word God *spoke*, not the eternal *Logos* who "was God" (see
[[Creation by Divine Word and Light]]).

## Miracles: shared, but credited differently

> [!quote] Gospels — Jesus acts by his own authority
> **John 11:25, 43** — "**I am the resurrection, and the life**… he cried with a loud voice, **Lazarus, come forth.**"

> [!quote] Qur'an — Jesus acts "by Allah's leave"
> **Al-Imran 3:49** — "I fashion for you out of clay the likeness of a bird, and I breathe into it and it is a bird, **by Allah's leave**. I heal him who was born blind, and the leper, and I raise the dead, **by Allah's leave**."

Same category of miracle (even **more** in the Qur'an — the clay birds and
infant speech have no Gospel parallel) — but the Gospels present them as
Jesus' *own* divine power, while the Qur'an repeatedly stamps each one **"by
Allah's leave"** to guard strict monotheism.

The Qur'an alone has the **infant Jesus speak from the cradle** — and his first
words deny exactly what Christianity affirms:

> [!quote] Maryam 19:30, 33
> "Lo! I am **the slave of Allah**. He hath given me the Scripture and hath appointed me a Prophet… Peace on me the day I was born, and the day I die, and the day I shall be raised alive!"

## Divergence 1 — Son of God, or slave of God?

> [!quote] Gospels — divine sonship
> **John 1:14** — "the Word was made flesh… the glory as of the **only begotten of the Father**."
> **Matthew 27:54** — "Truly this was the **Son of God**."

> [!quote] Qur'an — a prophet, never divine
> **Maryam 19:30** — "I am the **slave of Allah**… a Prophet."
> **Maryam 19:35** — "It befitteth not (the Majesty of) Allah that He should take unto Himself a son."

This is the single sharpest theological fork in the entire Abrahamic family:
the very phrase the Gospels climax on ("Son of God") is the one the Qur'an most
emphatically rejects.

## Divergence 2 — the crucifixion

> [!quote] Gospels — he was crucified and died
> **Matthew 27:35** — "And they **crucified him**, and parted his garments…"
> **27:46** — "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"
> **27:50** — "Jesus… **yielded up the ghost**."

> [!quote] Qur'an — he was not killed; God raised him
> **An-Nisa 4:157–158** — "…**they slew him not nor crucified him, but it appeared so unto them**… they slew him not for certain. But Allah **took him up unto Himself**."

Here the texts don't merely *interpret* differently — they report a **different
event**. The crucifixion is the foundation of Christian theology (the
atoning death and resurrection); the Qur'an denies the death outright, holding
that God rescued His prophet and "it only appeared so."

## Same figure, opposite conclusions
| Question | Christianity | Islam |
|---|---|---|
| Born of a virgin? | Yes | **Yes** (agreement) |
| Worked miracles? | Yes, by his own power | Yes, **by God's leave** |
| Divine / Son of God? | **Yes — God incarnate** | **No — a prophet and slave of God** |
| Crucified and died? | **Yes — central to salvation** | **No — God took him up** |
| Will return? | Yes (Second Coming) | Yes (in Islamic eschatology) |

Jesus is the **hinge** of the whole comparison: the more the two scriptures
share (and they share a great deal), the more starkly their irreducible
difference stands out — exactly the pattern this whole project keeps finding
across the [[Shared Abrahamic Figures|Abrahamic family]].
