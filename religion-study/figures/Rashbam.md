# Rashbam (רשב"ם — Rabbi Shmuel ben Meir)

Rashi's grandson and a pioneering commentator of the plain sense (*peshat*) of the Tanakh. Rashbam pushed biblical exegesis in a more radical, grammatically and contextually driven direction than his grandfather, sometimes arriving at interpretations that contradicted traditional rabbinic midrash.

## Key Contributions

- ***Peshat* Commentary on the Torah:** Rashbam's Torah commentary consistently pursues the plain meaning of the text, understood in its historical and linguistic context. He famously wrote: "The primary meaning of Scripture is its plain sense — even though the ultimate truth is in the midrashic exposition" — a principle he applied more consistently than Rashi.
- **Commentary on the Talmud:** He authored Tosafot (glosses) on several Talmudic tractates, clarifying and extending Rashi's explanations. His Talmudic methodology influenced the entire Tosafist school.
- **Grammatical and Literary Sensitivity:** Rashbam paid careful attention to biblical parallelism, repetition, and structure — often noting that a repeated verse or phrase serves a specific literary function rather than merely conveying additional halakhah.
- **Radical Exegesis:** In several places (e.g., Genesis 1:5, the creation account; Exodus 21:6, the ear-piercing ceremony), Rashbam offered interpretations that departed sharply from rabbinic tradition, insisting on the historical-grammatical meaning even when it conflicted with received halakhah.

## Life & Legacy

- Studied under his grandfather Rashi in Troyes and later under his uncle Rabbi Yitzchak ben Eliezer of Worms
- Younger brother of Rabbi Yitzchak (the Ri) and older brother of Rabbi Yaakov (Rabbeinu Tam) — together the three grandsons of Rashi formed the core of the Tosafist movement
- His Torah commentary was lost for centuries and rediscovered in the 19th century; it was first published in 1882
- Known for his boldness: "I do not fear the scorn of those who mock me" — he wrote in defence of his peshat approach
