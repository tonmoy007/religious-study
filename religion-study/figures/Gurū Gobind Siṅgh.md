# Gurū Gobind Siṅgh (Gobind Rāi)

> **1666–1708 CE** — The Tenth and final human Sikh Guru. He created the **Khalsa** (the order of the pure), finalised the **Gurū Granth Sāhib** as the eternal Guru, fought the Mughal Empire across a decade of battles, composed the **Dasam Granth** and the **Zafarnāmā**, and saw all four of his young sons martyred. More than any other Guru, he shaped Sikhism into a sovereign, martial, and literary tradition.

## Early Life and Installation

Gobind Rāi was born in Patna to Gurū Tegh Bahādur and Māta Gujri. His father was travelling widely during his early years, and the young boy was raised under the care of his mother and grandmother. A famous childhood episode: at age 5, he declared that his father would return from a journey on a specific day — and did.

In 1675, Gurū Tegh Bahādur was summoned to Delhi by Emperor Aurangzeb and asked to convert to Islam or demonstrate a miracle. He refused both and was beheaded on 24 November 1675 in Chandnī Chowk — a martyrdom undertaken to defend the right of Kashmiri Pandits to practise their faith freely. The young Gobind Rāi, only nine, was installed as the Tenth Guru at Anandpur Sāhib on Bhadon 8, 1732 Vikram Samvat (24 November 1675).

His famous declaration at his installation:

> "I shall make my Sikhs into such warriors that a single Sikh shall face an army. I shall make my Sikhs into saints, warriors, and scholars — *sant-sipāhī*."

He spent his adolescence studying martial arts, archery, horsemanship, and swordsmanship at Paonta Sāhib, while also mastering Persian, Sanskrit, Braj, and Punjabi literature. This dual education — the sword and the pen — would define his entire Guruship.

## Creation of the Khalsa (1699)

The single most transformative event in Sikh history after the founding of the faith itself.

### Background

By the late 1690s, the Sikh community was caught between Mughal hostility and the vulnerability of a pacifist identity. Gurū Tegh Bahādur's martyrdom had demonstrated that the Panth needed both spiritual resolve and physical capacity for self-defence. Gurū Gobind Siṅgh had already fought several defensive battles, but he saw the need for a permanent, institutional transformation — not merely a militia but a **spiritual brotherhood bound by a shared identity, oath, and discipline**.

### The Vaisākhī of 1699

On 30 March 1699 (Vaisākhī day), thousands of Sikhs gathered at Anandpur Sāhib. The Guru emerged from a tent carrying a naked sword and called out:

> "Is there any Sikh here who will offer his head to the Guru?"

The crowd fell silent. He called a second time, then a third. On the fourth call, **Dayā Rām** (a Lahori Khatri) stood up. The Guru took him into the tent. Moments later, the audience heard a sword strike. The Guru emerged alone, his sword dripping with blood, and called for another head.

Four more Sikhs came forward one by one: **Dharam Dās**, **Mohkam Chand**, **Sāhib Chand**, and **Himmat Rāi**. Each was taken into the tent — each time the same blow, the same blood.

Finally, the Guru emerged with all five men alive and intact. He had slaughtered goats, not men. The five — later immortalised as the **Pañj Piāre** (the Five Beloved) — had passed the ultimate test of loyalty.

### The Baptism Ceremony (*Khaṇḍē dī Pāhul*)

The Guru then prepared water in an iron bowl (*baṭā*), stirring it with a two-edged sword (*khaṇḍā*) while reciting the five Banīs (sacred compositions): **Japjī Sāhib, Jāp Sāhib, Tīā Sāhib, Baintī Chaupāī, and Anand Sāhib**. His wife, Māta Jito, added sugar crystals (*patāse*) to the water, giving the *amrit* (nectar of immortality) both the sharpness of steel and the sweetness of devotion.

He baptised the five men, and they in turn baptised him — the Guru became a disciple of his own disciples. This was the revolutionary egalitarian principle of the Khalsa: **the Guru and the disciple were now one in the same brotherhood**.

He renamed himself from **Gobind Rāi** to **Gobind Siṅgh** (Siṅgh = lion), and gave the five men the surname **Siṅgh** (for men) and **Kaur** (for women — princess).

### The Five K's (*Kakār*)

Every Khalsa Sikh was henceforth to wear five articles of faith, each beginning with the letter K (Gurmukhi: ਕ):

| Article | Gurmukhī | Meaning | Purpose |
|---------|----------|---------|---------|
| **Keś** | ਕੇਸ | Uncut hair | Acceptance of God's will; natural form; identity |
| **Kaṅghā** | ਕੰਘਾ | Wooden comb | Cleanliness, discipline, order |
| **Kirpān** | ਕਿਰਪਾਨ | Ceremonial sword | Defence of the weak; justice; sovereignty |
| **Kachh** | ਕਛ | Short breeches | Modesty, readiness, physical activity |
| **Kaṛā** | ਕੜਾ | Steel bracelet | Restraint; bound to God; no beginning/end |

The Khalsa also committed to a rigorous ethical code: no tobacco, no adultery, no halal meat, no cutting of hair, and daily recitation of the Banīs.

### The *Rehit* (Code of Conduct)

The Guru laid down the Khalsa's discipline: worship the One God, regard all humans as equal regardless of caste or creed, defend the poor and oppressed, share earnings with the needy (*vand chaknā*), practise charity (*dān*), and remain ever ready to fight tyranny. The Khalsa was a **saint-soldier** (*sant-sipāhī*) — a warrior who meditates and a mystic who fights.

### Theological Meaning of the Khalsa

The word *Khālsā* derives from Persian *khālis* (pure, exclusive), meaning both "the pure ones" in a spiritual sense and "the crown lands" (direct subjects of the Guru, free of intermediaries). In Sikh theology, the Khalsa represents:

- **Direct relationship with God** — no priests, no intermediaries, no caste hierarchy
- **Collective sovereignty** — the Guru vested his authority in the Khalsa itself (*Gurū Panth*)
- **Eschatological army** — the Khalsa is the army of the Lord (*Akal Purakh kī Fauj*)
- **Corporate body of the Guru** — where five baptised Sikhs gather, the Guru is present

## The Pañj Piāre (Five Beloved)

The first five initiates of the Khalsa, whose names live in Sikh liturgy and memory:

| Name | Original Background | Birthplace | Role |
|------|-------------------|------------|------|
| **Bhāī Dayā Siṅgh** (Dayā Rām) | Khatri (merchant caste) | Lahore | First to offer his head; became the Guru's most trusted messenger; carried the Zafarnāmā to Aurangzeb |
| **Bhāī Dharam Siṅgh** (Dharam Dās) | Jat (farmer caste) | Hastināpur, Delhi region | Second to offer; known for his steadfastness |
| **Bhāī Mohkam Siṅgh** (Mohkam Chand) | Tailor (low-caste Chhīmba) | Dwarka, Gujarat | Third to offer; died in the battle of Chamkaur (1705) |
| **Bhāī Sāhib Siṅgh** (Sāhib Chand) | Barber (Nāī) | Bidar, Karnataka | Fourth to offer; died in the battle of Chamkaur |
| **Bhāī Himmat Siṅgh** (Himmat Rāi) | Water-carrier (Jhīvar) | Jagannath Puri, Odisha | Fifth to offer; died in the battle of Chamkaur |

The revolutionary significance cannot be overstated: the five came from five different castes and five different regions of India — a Khatri, a Jat, a tailor, a barber, and a water-carrier. The Guru abolished caste distinctions within the Khalsa at a stroke, declaring all equal in *amrit*. The Pañj Piāre embodied the principle that **the Guru's sovereignty resides in the collective body of the Khalsa** — they baptised him, they became the Guru's visible form, and they represent every decision-making body (*gurmatā*) in the Sikh tradition today.

## The Sāhibzāde (The Four Martyred Sons)

Perhaps the most painful chapter in Sikh history and the greatest testament to the Guru's sacrifice.

### Ajit Siṅgh (1687–1705) — The Eldest

Born to Māta Sundarī in Paonta. Trained from childhood in martial arts and scripture. Fought alongside his father at Anandpur, Sārsā, and Chamkaur. At Chamkaur (22 December 1705, the Guru's 39th birthday), the Sikhs were besieged in a mud fort by a Mughal army of perhaps 10,000. Ajit Siṅgh led a sortie under cover of darkness and fell fighting heroically. He was 18.

### Jujhār Siṅgh (1691–1705) — The Second

Born to Māta Jito in Anandpur. A brilliant swordsman. Alongside Ajit, he fought in the same battles and fell in the same sortie at Chamkaur. He was 14.

### Zorāwar Siṅgh (1696–1705) — The Third

Born to Māta Jito. After the fall of Anandpur, the Guru's family became separated during the chaotic retreat across the flooded Sarsā River. Zorāwar and his younger brother Fateh were captured with their grandmother Māta Gujri by the Mughal governor of Sirhind, Wazīr Khān.

On 26 December 1705, the two boys — ages 9 and 6 — were brought before Wazīr Khān and asked to convert to Islam. They refused. He threatened them with death. They refused again. He ordered them to be **walled alive** — a brick enclosure was built around them, and the walls closed in, crushing them to death as they recited the Mūl Mantar.

Māta Gujri, who had witnessed their martyrdom from her prison, died of shock the same day.

### Fateh Siṅgh (1699–1705) — The Youngest

Born to Māta Jito. Died beside his brother Zorāwar, walled alive in Sirhind, refusing conversion. He was 6.

The Guru learned of his sons' deaths months later. In the Zafarnāmā — the letter of victory he wrote to Aurangzeb — he did not mourn them once. Instead, he wrote with fierce tenderness:

> "What is the use of putting out a small fire with a spark? What does it matter if a few small candles are extinguished when the whole world is illuminated?"
>
> "The Lord's purpose shall prevail. The Khalsa shall inherit the earth."

The two younger Sāhibzāde are remembered in Sikh liturgy as **the ones who smiled at death** (*muskarāndiāṅ mautāṅ nūṅ*, they who smiled at death).

Today, a magnificent **Gurdwārā Fatehgarh Sāhib** (the Gurdwara of Victory) stands at Sirhind on the site of their martyrdom, and 26 December is observed as **Shaheedī Diwas** (Martyrdom Day) across the Sikh world.

## Battles and Military Campaigns

Gurū Gobind Siṅgh fought thirteen major battles against the Mughal Empire and the hill chieftains who allied with it. These were consistently **defensive wars** — the Guru never attacked first, and he never took territory for himself.

| Battle | Year | Location | Opponent | Outcome |
|--------|------|----------|----------|---------|
| **Bhangāṇī** | 1688 | Paonta Sāhib area | Hill rajas + Mughal contingents | Sikh victory; the Guru's first battle |
| **Nadaun** | 1691 | Nadaun (Himachal) | Mughal army under Alif Khan | Sikh victory; Guru came to aid of Raja Bhim Chand |
| **Guler** | 1696 | Guler (Himachal) | Hill rajas | Sikh victory |
| **Anandpur (First Siege)** | 1700 | Anandpur | Hill rajas led by Raja Ajmer Chand | Sikh victory; the siege lifted |
| **Anandpur (Second Siege)** | 1701 | Anandpur | Combined hill rajas + Mughal forces | Sikh victory; the coalition retreated |
| **Anandpur (Third Siege)** | 1704 | Anandpur | Mughal army led by Wazīr Khan | Sikhs forced to evacuate after prolonged siege and a false oath on the Qur'an |
| **Sārsā** | 1704 | Near Sirsa (flooded Sarsā River) | Mughal forces | Sikhs crossed the river under heavy fire; many died in the flood |
| **Chamkaur** | 22 Dec 1705 | Chamkaur Sāhib (Punjab) | 10,000-strong Mughal army | 40 Sikhs held the fort against thousands; Ajit and Jujhār martyred; Guru escaped |
| **Kīrtan Sāhib** | 1705 | Kīrtan Sāhib (near Ropar) | Mughal forces | Sikh victory; Guru regrouped his followers |
| **Muktsar (Khidrāṇā)** | 1705 | Muktsar (Punjab) | Mughal army | Sikh victory; the Forty Saved Ones (*Chālī Mukte*) martyred |

### The Forty Saved Ones (*Chālī Mukte*)

At Muktsar, forty Sikhs who had deserted the Guru at Anandpur (signing a deed of disclaimer) returned to fight under him. Led by **Māī Bhāgo**, they fell in battle, and the Guru tore up their disclaimer, declaring them liberated.

## The Zafarnāmā (The Epistle of Victory)

After the battles of Chamkaur and Muktsar, the Guru wrote a long Persian poem — the **Zafarnāmā** (Letter of Victory) — and sent it with Bhāī Dayā Siṅgh to Emperor Aurangzeb in Ahmednagar (Deccan). Dated 1705–1706, it runs to 111 Persian verses (*band*) and is a masterpiece of political theology, moral rebuke, and spiritual defiance.

### Key Arguments of the Zafarnāmā

The letter does not ask for peace or forgiveness. It **indicts the Emperor**:

- **On the oath**: Aurangzeb had sworn on the Qur'an that the Sikhs could leave Anandpur safely if they surrendered the fort. The Mughals attacked them the moment they left, violating the oath. The Guru writes: "You swore by God and then broke your oath — what kind of Muslim are you?" (verse 74)
- **On martyrdom**: "When a tyrant exceeds all limits of cruelty, it is righteous to take up the sword" (verse 22)
- **On divine justice**: "I shall not trust your oath again. The Lord is the only refuge."
- **On sovereignty**: "My religion is to protect the helpless. This is my life's mission."

The most famous verse:

> **"Chīṅ chīṅ terā sūrat, namāz nahīṅ terī sūrat; sachch kahūṅ sun merīāṅ, jhūṭh nahīṅ bolṅā."**
> ("Your appearance is pure, your prayer is not pure; listen, I speak the truth — I do not lie.")

And the theological climax:

> "What is the use of putting out a small fire with a spark?
> He who creates and destroys is the One Lord alone."

The Zafarnāmā is considered the Sikh tradition's most eloquent statement on **the ethics of righteous war** and the hypocrisy of religious authority that violates its own scriptures.

Legend holds that Aurangzeb, upon reading the letter, was deeply moved. He invited the Guru to meet him and even offered to punish Wazīr Khān. The Guru set out for the Deccan, but Aurangzeb died in 1707 before they could meet. The Guru then supported Bahādur Shāh (Aurangzeb's more tolerant successor) in the war of succession.

## Literary Works

Gurū Gobind Siṅgh was among the most prolific and learned of the Gurus — fluent in Persian, Sanskrit, Braj, Punjabi, and Arabic. He composed thousands of lines of poetry, much of it martial, mystical, and mythological.

### The Dasam Granth (The Book of the Tenth Master)

A massive scripture of 1,428 pages, traditionally attributed to the Guru, compiled after his death by Bhāī Maṇī Siṅgh. It contains:

| Composition | Language | Content |
|-------------|----------|---------|
| **Jāp Sāhib** | Persianized Braj/Punjabi | A 199-verse hymn of divine praise; recited daily by Khalsa Sikhs |
| **Akāl Ustat** | Braj/Punjabi | Praise of the Timeless One (Akāl) who is beyond all religious categories |
| **Vāṅgā (Bachitra Nāṭak)** | Braj | The Guru's autobiography — his birth, lineage, battles, and spiritual mission |
| **Chauḍī Chaupāī** | Braj | Hymn on divine protection; part of the daily *Nitnem* |
| **Chauḍī Avtār** | Braj | The martial exploits of the goddess Chandi (Durgā) — a Puranic theme adapted for Sikh warrior spirituality |
| **Krishnāvār** | Braj | The life of Krishna, reinterpreted through a heroic lens |
| **Gobind Gītā** | Braj | A dialogue between the Guru and the soul on spiritual liberation |
| **Hikāyat/Chitrakathā** | Persian | 12 Persian tales with Sufi ethical themes |
| **Zafarnāmā** | Persian | The letter to Aurangzeb (see above) |
| **Śabad Hazāre** | Punjabi | 10 short hymns in the Guru Granth Sāhib style |

The Dasam Granth's theology is distinctive: a fierce, monotheistic **Bhakti fused with Śakti** — God is not only the loving Beloved but also the **warrior-destroyer of evil**, the one who wields the sword of cosmic justice. The Divine Mother (Chandi) is celebrated as the power of God in action, and the Guru urges the Khalsa to see themselves as her soldiers.

### The Guru Granth Sāhib — Finalisation

Before his death, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh completed the work of his predecessors. At **Damdamā Sāhib** (Talwandi Sabo, Bathinda), he dictated and compiled the final, authoritative version of the Gurū Granth Sāhib, adding the hymns of his father, Gurū Tegh Bahādur. The Damdamā version — called the *Damdamī Bīṛ* — is the standard text used in all Gurdwārās today.

### The 52 *Ḍikhāwe* (Couplets of Wisdom)

A collection of 52 aphorisms attributed to the Guru on the ethics of the Khalsa:

- "The Khalsa is my body, my soul, my very life"
- "Recognise the whole human race as one"
- "Do not trust a river, a treacherous person, or a horned animal"
- "The sword I wield is for the protection of the poor"
- "He who does not take care of his family cannot take care of the Panth"

## Final Days (1708)

In 1708, the Guru travelled to **Nanded** (in present-day Maharashtra) on the banks of the Godāvarī. Here he met **Banda Siṅgh Bahādur** — a Bairagi ascetic who would become the Khalsa's greatest general, leading the first armed Sikh uprising against the Mughals.

In October 1708, a Pathan named **Jamshed Khan** — sent by Wazīr Khān of Sirhind — stabbed the Guru in the chest while he was resting. The Guru killed the assassin with his sword, but the wound festered. He called his disciples together and declared:

> "Do not weep for me. I have lived for the Khalsa, and I am content."

On 7 October 1708, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh died from his wounds. Before his death, he formally declared the **Gurū Granth Sāhib** as the eternal, living Guru for the Sikhs:

> "Sabat Siṅgh — the complete lion."

> "I entrust you to the **Gurū Granth Sāhib**. It shall be your Guru after me. Consider it your living Guru. Wherever five Sikhs are gathered in my name, there I shall be present."

This act — the installation of scripture as Guru — was unprecedented in world religious history. No other founder had declared a book to be their living successor. The Sikh tradition thus became the **religion of the Book** in a uniquely literal sense: the Book is the Guru, and the Guru is the Book.

## Legacy

### The Khalsa Today

More than 25 million Sikhs worldwide trace their spiritual lineage directly to the Vaisākhī of 1699. The Khalsa remains the visible body of Sikhism — every baptised Sikh wears the Five K's, recites the daily prayers, and belongs to a tradition that the Guru declared "shall never be defeated."

### Gurdwārās and Takhts

Five of Sikhism's five **Takhts** (seats of temporal authority) are directly associated with the Guru:

| Takht | Location | Association |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Sri Akāl Takht Sāhib** | Amritsar | Built by Gurū Har Gobind; authority of the Khalsa |
| **Takht Sri Kesgarh Sāhib** | Anandpur | Site of the creation of the Khalsa (1699) |
| **Takht Sri Damdamā Sāhib** | Talwandi Sabo | Where the Guru finalised the Guru Granth Sāhib |
| **Takht Sri Patna Sāhib** | Patna | The Guru's birthplace |
| **Takht Sri Hazūr Sāhib** | Nanded | The Guru's death site |

### In Sikh Liturgy

The Guru's compositions form part of the daily *Nitnem* prayers recited by Khalsa Sikhs:

- **Jāp Sāhib** (morning)
- **Tīā Sāhib** (morning)
- **Čauḍī Chaupāī** (evening)
- **Anand Sāhib** (any time)
- **Zafarnāmā** (recited on special occasions)

### The Guru's Vision of Scripture

By installing the Guru Granth Sāhib as the living Guru, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh enacted one of the most radical moves in religious history: **a founder who abdicates power to a book**. The implication was profound — the Sikh community would be led not by a person but by the Word, not by a dynasty but by the text itself. This made Sikhism uniquely resistant to both dynastic corruption and cults of personality.

### Comparative Religious Significance

Gurū Gobind Siṅgh is one of the few religious founders to have:

1. **Created a martial order within a faith** — the Khalsa as a standing body of committed initiates
2. **Declared Scripture as successor** — the only major religious tradition to replace the founder with a book as living Guru
3. **Seen all four of his sons die before him** — a sacrifice of family to principle rarely paralleled (comparable to Abraham's willingness to sacrifice Isaac, but fulfilled in reality)
4. **Defeated the largest empire of his age in battle** — the Mughals never successfully conquered the Sikhs after him
5. **Composed scripture while leading armies** — a unique combination of the poet-saint and warrior-king in one figure
