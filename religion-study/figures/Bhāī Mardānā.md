# Bhāī Mardānā (Mardānā)

**Bhāī Mardānā** (c. 1459 – 1534), born **Mīr Mardānā** into a Muslim family of Dabgar (tambourine-maker) caste, was the lifelong companion, friend, and *rabāb* player of **Gurū Nānak Dev**, the founder of Sikhism. He accompanied Gurū Nānak on all four major journeys (Udāsīs) and his musical accompaniment of the *sabad* (divine hymn) on the *rabāb* became the template for Sikh *kīrtan*. Dialogues between Mardānā and Gurū Nānak preserved in the *Janam Sākhīs* (hagiographical narratives) and in the **Gurū Granth Sāhib** itself (notably the *Ṭukhārī* Chauntissā) reveal a relationship of profound spiritual intimacy and mutual affection. Mardānā is the archetypal *sewāk* (selfless servant-companion) in the Sikh tradition.

## Biography

Mardānā was born around 1459 in Talvaṇḍī (the same village as Gurū Nānak, though Nānak was about ten years younger). His family were hereditary musicians of the Dabgar community, traditionally makers of *ḍaphlī* (tambourine) and practitioners of folk music. As a young man Mardānā served as a *mirāsi* — a village bard and genealogist — skilled in singing, drumming, and string instruments.

The traditional account of his meeting with Gurū Nānak is preserved in several *Janam Sākhī* recensions. While Nānak was bathing at a well (or, in some versions, sitting in meditation), Mardānā approached him. Nānak immediately recognised him as "Mardānā" — the one who would "mercilessly consume" (*mardanā*) the ego of the world through divine music — and accepted him as his constant companion. From that moment until Mardānā's death some thirty-five years later, the two were inseparable.

Mardānā was present at the pivotal event of Nānak's disappearance into the Veyī (Bein) river, his three-day sojourn in the Divine Presence, and his subsequent declaration: *Nā kō hindu nā musalmān* ("There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim"). During the Udāsīs, Mardānā carried the *rabāb* and provided musical accompaniment for Nānak's hymns, often at moments of interfaith encounter where the universality of the Divine Name (*Nām*) was being proclaimed.

After the Udāsīs, Mardānā settled with Gurū Nānak in Kartārpur, where regular *kīrtan* assemblies were held. He died in 1534, shortly after Gurū Nānak himself (who died in 1539). Some *Janam Sākhīs* record that Nānak composed the *Ṭukhārī* Chauntissā — a 34-pair dialogue set to the rāga Ṭukhārī — as a direct response to Mardānā's questions about the condition of the soul after death. The hymn is included in the Gurū Granth Sāhib (GGS, pp. 1107–1112) and is the only extended exchange between the two preserved in scripture.

## Role in Gurū Nānak's Journeys

### The Udāsīs (c. 1500–1521)

Mardānā accompanied Gurū Nānak on all four great Udāsīs:

| Udāsī | Direction | Key Regions Visited | Mardānā's Role |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|----------------|
| **First** (c. 1500–1506) | East & South | Bengal, Assam, Jaipur, Ujjain, Varanasi, Gaya, Puri, Rameswaram | Accompanied Nānak in dialogues with Hindu ascetics (Siddhs) and temple priests; played *rabāb* during *kīrtan* at Jagannath Puri |
| **Second** (c. 1507–1510) | South | Sri Lanka (Ceylon), through Tamil country | Provided music during encounters with Buddhist monks and Tamil Shaivites; sustained Nānak during harsh jungle travel |
| **Third** (c. 1511–1514) | North | Kashmir, Mount Sumeru (mythical), Ladakh, Tibet | Played *rabāb* in high-altitude conditions; present for Nānak's debates with Buddhist pandits and Nath yogis |
| **Fourth** (c. 1515–1521) | West | Mecca, Medina, Baghdad, Persia | Accompanied Nānak into the Grand Mosque of Mecca; present at the famous "turn the feet toward God" exchange; his Muslim identity facilitated entry into Muslim-majority areas |

### Companion in Dialogue

Throughout the Udāsīs, Mardānā functioned as more than a musician. He was Nānak's foil — asking the questions that a sincere but unenlightened seeker would ask, allowing Nānak to unfold his teachings. In the *Janam Sākhī* of the *Siddh Goṣṭ* (Discourse with the Siddhs), Mardānā is depicted as the anxious questioner who fears the forest ascetics, prompting Nānak to declare that the true yogi lives in the world yet remains untouched by it.

## The Rabāb Tradition

### Instrument and Performance

The *rabāb* (also *rabābā* or *ravāj* in some sources) used by Mardānā was a bowed or plucked string instrument, typically with a wooden body covered in animal skin and three or four gut strings — the ancestor of the modern Punjabi *tumbī* and *sāraṅgī* tradition. Mardānā was celebrated not merely as an instrumentalist but as someone who could attune his playing to the spiritual mood (*rasa*) of Nānak's hymns, a skill that later became the hallmark of Sikh *kīrtanīs*.

### The *Rabābī* Order

After Mardānā's death, his son Sajādā (also called **Bhai Sajādā**) and subsequently his descendant **Bhai Abdullāh** were appointed by Gurū Arjan Dev to sing at the Harmandir Sāhib (Golden Temple). This established the **Guru ke Rabābī** — a hereditary lineage of Muslim musicians who served as the official hymn-singers at Sikh shrines for over 300 years. Notable later *rabābīs* include:

- **Bhai Nihāla** (18th c.) — served under Gurū Gobind Singh
- **Bhai Attar Singh** (19th c.) — served at the Golden Temple
- **Bhai Ghulām Muhammad Chand** (19th c.) — last major *rabābī* before the 1947 Partition disrupted the tradition

The *rabābī* tradition declined in the 20th century with the rise of purist Sikh reform movements (Tat Khalsa) that sought to minimise Muslim participation in Sikh institutions, but it has seen a modest revival in the 21st century through scholars and performers committed to preserving this interfaith musical heritage.

## Dialogues Recorded in Janam Sākhīs

The *Janam Sākhī* literature (especially the *Bālā* and *Miharbān* recensions) records many exchanges between Mardānā and Gurū Nānak. Notable themes include:

### 1. The Nature of God

> **Mardānā**: "Master, is God a Hindu or a Muslim?"
> **Nānak**: "Neither, Mardānā. God is the One Truth without form, without colour, without caste — yet present in all forms, all colours, all castes. The Hindu prays at the temple, the Muslim at the mosque, but God hears only the heart."

### 2. The Condition of the Soul After Death

The *Ṭukhārī* Chauntissā (GGS, pp. 1107–1112) is structured as Mardānā's query about what happens to the soul once it leaves the body, followed by Nānak's response in 34 poetic stanzas. The refrain captures the dialogue's core:

> *Mardānā asks: "How does the soul depart? To whose house does it go?"*
> *Nānak answers: "As you sow, so shall you reap. The soul departs in the company of its deeds — good or bad — and comes before the True Judge."*

This composition is unique in the Gurū Granth Sāhib for being explicitly dialogic. Mardānā's name is invoked in the text itself, making him the only companion of a Gurū directly named and addressed in the scripture outside of the Gurūs themselves.

### 3. The Equality of All Humanity

Recurring across *Janam Sākhī* accounts are exchanges where Mardānā, born a Muslim and conscious of social hierarchy, questions Nānak about his teachings of equality:

> **Mardānā**: "Lord, you say caste is nothing. But the Brahmins say they are born pure and we are born low."
> **Nānak**: "Look at the clay of the potter, Mardānā. From the same clay come vessels of all shapes. In the end, they all crumble back to the same dust. So too with humanity — the same flesh and blood, the same breath, the same death. Heaven does not ask your caste."

## Legacy as the Archetypal Companion

### The First *Sewāk* (Selfless Servant)

Mardānā embodies the ideal of *sewā* (selfless service) in the Sikh tradition — not as a disciple following a master, but as a *companion* walking alongside. His relationship with Nānak is often described in Punjabi as *mitr* (friendship) rather than *guru-chelā* (master-disciple). This friendship model became paradigmatic for later Sikh relationships with the Gurū: intimate, questioning, utterly devoted, yet suffused with humour and affection.

### Interfaith Pioneer

As a Muslim who became the closest companion of Sikhism's founder, Mardānā is the original symbol of the Sikh tradition's interfaith openness. His story is frequently invoked by contemporary Sikh and interfaith writers to counter exclusivist or communalist interpretations of Sikh identity.

### Representation in Sikh Art and Memory

- In **Sikh painting** (particularly the *Janam Sākhī* illustrated manuscripts of the 18th–19th centuries), Mardānā is invariably depicted seated at Nānak's side, his *rabāb* in hand, his posture one of attentive listening.
- The **Ṭukhārī Chauntissā** remains part of the Sikh liturgical repertoire, sung in rāga Ṭukhārī at the Golden Temple and elsewhere.
- Gurdwārās named **Gurdwārā Bhāī Mardānā** exist in Nankana Sāhib (Pakistan) and other locations associated with his life.
- A **mausoleum** traditionally identified as Mardānā's tomb exists in Kartārpur, Pakistan.

### In Comparative Perspective

| Role | Bhāī Mardānā | Comparable Figures in Other Traditions |
|------|--------------|-----------------------------------------|
| **Musician-companion** | Played *rabāb* for Gurū Nānak, establishing *kīrtan* tradition | Tansen (poet-musician at Akbar's court); David playing harp for King Saul |
