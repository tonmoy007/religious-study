# Isaiah

> The greatest of the writing prophets — whose 66-chapter book spans judgment, history, messianic prophecy, and visions of a new heaven and a new earth.

---

## Biography

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| **Name** | Yesha'yahu ("Yahweh saves") |
| **Ministry** | c. 740–701 BCE — under Kings Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, Hezekiah |
| **Location** | Jerusalem — a court prophet |
| **Family** | Married to "the prophetess" — two sons (Shear-Jashub, Maher-shalal-hash-baz) |
| **Death** | Tradition: **sawn in two** during Manasseh's reign (referenced in Hebrews 11:37) |

---

## Key Themes

| Theme | Passage | Significance |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| **The holiness of God** | "Holy, holy, holy — the whole earth is full of his glory" (6:3) | The seraphim's cry — the foundation of Isaiah's theology |
| **The virgin birth** | "A virgin shall conceive and bear a son — Immanuel" (7:14) | Interpreted by Christians as prophecy of Jesus |
| **The suffering servant** | "He was pierced for our transgressions — crushed for our iniquities" (53:5) | The fourth Servant Song — central to Christian atonement theology |
| **Universal peace** | "They shall beat their swords into plowshares — nation shall not lift up sword against nation" (2:4) | The vision of messianic peace |
| **New creation** | "New heavens and a new earth" (65:17, 66:22) | Eschatological hope |

---

## The Vision (Chapter 6)

In the year King Uzziah dies, Isaiah sees the Lord — high and lifted up — seated on a throne. The train of his robe fills the Temple. Seraphim cry: *"Holy, holy, holy!"* The doorposts shake. Isaiah cries: *"Woe is me — I am a man of unclean lips!"* A seraph touches his lips with a burning coal: *"Your guilt is taken away."* The Lord asks: *"Whom shall I send?"* Isaiah: *"Here I am — send me!"*

---

## Connection with Islam

Isaiah (*Sha'ya* or *Ishe'ya*) is **not named in the Qur'an** but is recognized in Islamic tradition as a prophet of Israel. The Qur'an references the stories of Israel's prophets without naming many of the writing prophets. Some Islamic scholars see Isaiah's prophecies as foretelling the coming of Muhammad (Isaiah 42 — "the servant" as Ishmael's descendant; Isaiah 21:13–17 — prophecy about Arabia).
