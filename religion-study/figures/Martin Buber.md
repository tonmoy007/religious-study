# Martin Buber (מרטין בובר — Martin Mordechai Buber)

One of the most influential Jewish philosophers and religious existentialists of the twentieth century. Buber's philosophy of dialogue — centred on the distinction between I-Thou and I-It relationships — transformed modern Jewish theology and profoundly influenced Christian thought, education theory, and social philosophy.

## Key Contributions

- ***I and Thou* (Ich und Du, 1923):** Buber's masterwork argues that human existence is fundamentally relational. In an I-Thou encounter, each party meets the other in full presence and mutuality — including the ultimate Thou encountered through finite others. I-It relations, by contrast, are instrumental and objectifying. This distinction became a cornerstone of modern religious existentialism.
- **Translation of the Tanakh into German:** Together with [[Franz Rosenzweig]], Buber undertook a landmark translation of the Hebrew Bible into German (1925–1961), aiming to preserve the original's oral rhythm, semantic range, and Hebraic cadence. The *Buber-Rosenzweig Bibel* is considered a literary and theological masterpiece.
- ***Tales of the Hasidim* (Die Erzählungen der Chassidim, 1949):** Buber collected and retold stories of the Hasidic masters, presenting Hasidism as a model of lived, joyous spirituality and community. Through these volumes, he introduced Hasidic thought to a wide Western audience.
- **Dialogical Education and Social Philosophy:** Buber's model of genuine dialogue shaped his theories of education (the educator as a trusted other in I-Thou relation), community, and politics. He advocated a binational state in Palestine and was a leading figure of the Ihud movement.
- **Two Types of Faith (1950):** A comparative study of Jewish (*emunah* — trust) and Christian (*pistis* — belief) concepts of faith, exploring the inner logic of each religious tradition.

## Life & Legacy

- Born in Vienna, raised in Lemberg (Lviv) by his grandparents; his grandfather Solomon Buber was a noted Midrash scholar
- Studied philosophy and art in Vienna, Leipzig, Zurich, and Berlin, where he was influenced by Dilthey, Simmel, and the Neo-Kantians
- Deeply engaged with Zionism from his youth, representing the Cultural Zionist faction; he was a friend and critic of Theodor Herzl
- Fled Nazi Germany in 1938 and settled in Jerusalem, where he became a professor of social philosophy at the Hebrew University
- His controversial binationalist views put him at odds with mainstream Zionism; he remained a moral critic of Israeli policy throughout his life
