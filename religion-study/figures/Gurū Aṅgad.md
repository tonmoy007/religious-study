# Gurū Aṅgad (Lehnā)

> Born: 1504 CE, Matte di Sarai (Ferozepur district, Punjab, now in India)
> Died: 29 March 1552 CE, Khadūr Sāhib, Punjab (aged ~48)
> Title: Second Sikh Guru (1539–1552); the Developer of the Gurmukhī Script; the Collector of Nānak's Word
> Era: The Consolidation of the Sikh Tradition under the Sūr and Early Mughal Empires

**Gurū Aṅgad** (1504–1552 CE), born **Lehnā** (also Lahiṇā), was the second of the ten Sikh Gurus, serving from 1539 until his death in 1552. His thirteen-year Guruship is the bridge between the founding revelation of Gurū Nānak and the institutional expansion of the later Gurus. Born a Hindu Khatri in the village of Matte di Sarai, Lehnā was originally a devotee of the goddess Durgā (whom he called *Bhagautī*), annually leading pilgrimages to her temple. His life changed irrevocably in 1532 CE when he heard a hymn of Gurū Nānak being sung by a Sikh named Jodhā at the village of Khadūr Sāhib. Deeply moved, he abandoned his previous path, journeyed to Kartarpur to meet Nānak, and became the most devoted of his disciples. Nānak renamed him **Aṅgad** — "of my limb" or "my own body" — signifying that the disciple had become inseparable from the Guru. In 1539, Nānak formally installed Aṅgad as his successor, passing over his own sons, Srī Chand and Lakhmī Dās, and bowing to him before the assembled congregation — a revolutionary act that established the principle of meritocratic spiritual succession. As Guru, Aṅgad established his seat at **Khadūr Sāhib** (the village where he first encountered the Sikh faith) and undertook two foundational tasks: the development of the **Gurmukhī script** for writing the Punjabi language and the systematic **collection and preservation of Gurū Nānak's hymns**. He also composed 62 of his own hymns (preserved in the Guru Granth Sāhib), expanded the institution of the **langar** (communal kitchen), and promoted the **Vār** tradition of heroic balladry that would later flower in the Sikh martial tradition. His disciple and successor, **Gurū Amar Dās** (then over sixty years old), served him with extraordinary humility — a model of *sevā* (selfless service) that remains central to Sikh spiritual practice. Gurū Aṅgad died in 1552 at Khadūr Sāhib, appointing Amar Dās over his own sons, Piṇḍ and Dātu, thereby reinforcing the meritocratic model of succession that Nānak had inaugurated.

The Lord is ever with you."* He asked Jodhā who had composed it, and Jodhā told him of the Guru at Kartarpur. Lehnā immediately abandoned his pilgrimage, journeyed to Kartarpur, and presented himself before Nānak. The Guru, seeing his spiritual intensity, accepted him as a disciple. He performed the most menial tasks — fetching water, washing clothes, cleaning the Guru's dwelling, and serving in the langar — with ceaseless joy. The Janam Sākhīs record that Nānak tested Lehnā repeatedly: on one occasion, the Guru threw a bowl of dirty water at him, and Lehnā caught it saying "Gurū kā parsād" (a gift from the Guru). When Nānak's sons Srī Chand (a celibate ascetic) and Lakhmī Dās (a worldly householder) proved unfit for the succession, Nānak tested his disciples by asking each to carry the Guru's possessions across the flooded Ravi River — only Lehnā, unhesitating, waded through the currents, holding the Guru's bundle above the water. Nānak embraced him, saying, "You are Aṅgad — part of my own body" |

He placed five copper coins and a coconut before Lehnā (the traditional symbols of authority transfer), then bowed to him — a gesture that shocked the assembled Sikhs, as no Guru had ever bowed to a disciple. Nānak declared: "The same light that was in me is now in Aṅgad. The Word (Śabad) is the Guru; the Guru is the Word." He then moved to Khadūr Sāhib at Aṅgad's request, and Aṅgad became the public face of the community. After Nānak's death (22 September 1539 CE), Aṅgad formally took up the Guruship, shifting the centre of the Sikh community to **Khadūr Sāhib** |

The Punjabi language had been written in the **Lahndā** (Landā) script — a commercial script used by traders and merchants that lacked the phonetic precision needed for recording sacred verse. Aṅgad reformed the Lahndā alphabet, adding new characters for Punjabi's distinctive sounds (especially the tonal and retroflex consonants), creating a script capable of representing the exact pronunciation of Gurū Nānak's hymns. The name "Gurmukhī" itself signifies that the script comes from the Guru's mouth — it is the vehicle of the Guru's *Śabad* (Word). This innovation was foundational not only for Sikh scripture but for the Punjabi language itself: Gurmukhī gave Punjabi a distinct literary identity separate from the Perso-Arabic script favoured by Mughal officialdom and the Devanāgarī script of Hindi |
| **Collection of Nānak's Hymns (c. 1539–1552)** | During his Guruship, Aṅgad undertook a systematic effort to collect, authenticate, and preserve the compositions of Gurū Nānak. Many of Nānak's hymns had been transmitted orally or existed in scattered manuscripts. Aṅgad gathered the disciples who had been with Nānak, asked them to recite what they remembered, and compared versions for authenticity. He established the principle — later formalised in the Ādi Granth — that the Guru's word was to be preserved in writing, in its original form, and transmitted without alteration. This collection formed the nucleus of the Sikh scriptural canon, later expanded by Gurū Amar Dās (Goindval Pothīs) and finalised by Gurū Arjan (Ādi Granth, 1604 CE) |
| **Composition of His Own Hymns** | Gurū Aṅgad composed **62 hymns**, all preserved in the Guru Granth Sāhib. His hymns are notable for their emphasis on the *Śabad* (the divine Word) as the living presence of the Guru, the importance of *sevā* (selfless service), and the rejection of caste and ritual. Though fewer in number than Nānak's 974 or Amar Dās's 907, Aṅgad's hymns carry the authority of the second Guru who directly received the tradition from Nānak himself. His compositions appear in 8 different rāgas in the Guru Granth Sāhib |
| **Expansion of the Langar** | Following Nānak's institution of the langar at Kartarpur, Gurū Aṅgad expanded the communal kitchen at Khadūr Sāhib. Under the management of **Mātā Khīvī** (his wife), the langar became a permanent, well-organised institution serving all visitors regardless of caste, religion, or social status. Aṅgad enforced the principle that the langar was not a charity but a *right* — all were equally worthy to eat at the Guru's table. This practice, inherited from Nānak and passed to Amar Dās (who would later make it compulsory for an audience with the Guru), is one of the most distinctive Sikh institutions |
| **Death and Succession (1552 CE)** | 29 March 1552 CE at **Khadūr Sāhib**, aged approximately 48. Sensing his end, Gurū Aṅgad tested his two sons (Piṇḍ and Dātu) but found them proud and unsuitable. He then summoned his most devoted disciple — the seventy-three-year-old **Amar Dās** — and, following the precedent of Nānak, placed five copper coins and a coconut before him, bowing in acknowledgment of the new Guru. He died shortly after, instructing the congregation to obey the third Guru as they had obeyed him. His sons, particularly Dātu, protested the succession — Dātu even kicked the aged Amar Dās off his seat — but the congregation's loyalty to the meritocratic principle held, and Amar Dās's Guruship was confirmed |

## Development of the Gurmukhī Script

Gurū Aṅgad's invention of the **Gurmukhī script** is arguably his most consequential and enduring contribution to Sikh tradition and to the Punjabi language as a whole. It is a development that combines the practical necessity of accurate textual transmission with profound theological implications.

### The Pre-Gurmukhī Situation

Before the Gurmukhī script, the Punjabi language had no standardised writing system. The following scripts were in use for different purposes:

| Script | Used By | Limitations |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **Lahndā (Landā)** | Merchants, traders, and shopkeepers for account-keeping and commercial correspondence | A cursive, abbreviated script with no standardised alphabet; incapable of representing Punjabi's full phonetic range (especially tonal consonants, retroflex sounds, and the distinctive Punjabi nasals); varied significantly by region (there were dozens of local variants) |
| **Perso-Arabic (Shahmukhī)** | Mughal administration, Muslim scholars, and the Persian-speaking elite | An imported script designed for Persian and Arabic; poorly suited to Punjabi's vowel system (short vowels are omitted in Arabic script) and its retroflex consonants (for which Persian has no equivalents) |
| **Devanāgarī** | Brahmin scholars, Sanskrit texts, and traders in eastern Punjab | A script designed for Sanskrit and its derivative languages (Hindi, Marathi); lacked letters for several Punjabi consonants (the implosives and certain tonal distinctions); associated with Brahminical Hindu orthodoxy |
| **Takrī** | The Hill States of the Punjab (Himachal region); used for administrative records in the Shivalik hill kingdoms | A script of limited geographical range; lacked the prestige and standardisation needed for a trans-regional religious movement |

None of these scripts was adequate for the exact preservation of sacred verse — where pronunciation, metre, and rhythm carry theological meaning. Gurū Nānak's hymns, composed in the vernacular Punjabi of the people, required a script that could capture every nuance of the spoken word.

### Aṅgad's Reform

Gurū Aṅgad undertook a systematic reformation of the existing Lahndā script:

1. **Standardisation of the Alphabet** — He selected the clearest, most phonetically useful characters from the various Lahndā regional variants and fixed their forms, eliminating cursive variations and creating a standardised set of 35 characters (the *Pañchākharī* — "the five-group alphabet," so called because its 35 characters can be grouped into 5 sets of 7 consonants each, plus 3 vowel diacritics later expanded to 10).

2. **Addition of New Characters** — The Lahndā script had no symbols for the **retroflex sounds** (e.g., *ṭ*, *ḍ*, *ṛ*) that are characteristic of Punjabi and distinct from Hindi's more dental articulation. Aṅgad (or his scribes under his direction) added new characters or modified existing ones to represent these sounds precisely.

3. **The Vowel System** — He developed a system of **vowel diacritics** (dependent vowel signs) and independent vowel characters that could represent all ten Punjabi vowels (three short, seven long) with a precision that neither Perso-Arabic nor Devanāgarī could match. This was crucial for the *rāga*-based singing of hymns, where vowel length and quality affect the melodic line.

4. **The *Halant* (Subscript Hook) — Aṅgad's script introduced the *halant* (a subscript diacritic) to indicate the suppression of the inherent vowel — a feature shared with Devanāgarī but refined for Punjabi's distinctive consonant clusters.

5. **Numerals** — The Gurmukhī numeral set (0–9) was also standardised during this period, replacing the various regional numeral systems.

### The Name "Gurmukhī"

The name **Gurmukhī** (Punjabi: *gur* = guru, *mukh* = mouth, *ī* = adjectival suffix) means "from the mouth of the Guru." This is simultaneously a literal description (the script emerged from the Guru's circle and was used to record the Guru's words) and a theological statement: the script is not a neutral tool but a sanctified vehicle for the *Śabad* (divine Word). To write in Gurmukhī is to participate in the Guru's presence. The script's very name asserts that:

- The Guru's teaching (*Gurmukh*) is the source of the script
- The script exists for the sake of the Word, not the Word for the sake of the script
- Literacy in Gurmukhī is not merely a practical skill but a spiritual discipline — learning to read the Guru's words in the Guru's script is itself a form of *Nām Simran*

### Impact

| Dimension | Impact of Gurmukhī |
|-----------|--------------------|
| **Textual preservation** | Enabled the exact transmission of Nānak's hymns without the corruption that oral transmission or transcription in inadequate scripts would have caused; made the Ādi Granth (1604 CE) possible |
| **Linguistic standardisation** | Gave the Punjabi language a fixed orthography for the first time, creating a unified literary standard across regional dialects |
| **Community identity** | Created a visual marker of Sikh identity — the Gurmukhī script is instantly recognisable and distinct from the Perso-Arabic (Shahmukhī) associated with Punjabi Muslims and the Devanāgarī associated with Punjabi Hindus |
| **Liturgical use** | The script's phonetic precision allows for the correct pronunciation of hymns in *kīrtan* (congregational singing), where vowel length and consonant quality affect both meaning and melody |
| **Printing and modernisation** | Gurmukhī was one of the first Indian scripts to be adapted for printing (early 19th century) and later for typewriters and computer fonts, ensuring Punjabi's survival in the digital age |
| **Theological continuity** | The script is itself a carrier of the Guru's authority: reading Gurmukhī is understood as a direct encounter with the Guru's *Śabad* |

## Langar Institutionalisation

While the langar (communal free kitchen) was instituted by Gurū Nānak at Kartarpur, it was Gurū Aṅgad who gave it permanent institutional form at Khadūr Sāhib. Under the management of **Mātā Khīvī** — his wife, whom Sikh tradition remembers as the first *sewādār* (volunteer) of the Guru's langar — the institution was expanded, regularised, and given a set of practices that foreshadowed the reforms of Gurū Amar Dās:

- **Daily operation** — The langar served meals to all visitors every day, without exception. It was not an occasional feast but a permanent institution
- **Volunteer service** — All work in the langar — from fetching water and grinding grain to cooking and serving — was done as *sevā* (selfless service) by the Sikhs themselves. No paid staff were employed
- **Equality in practice** — All visitors ate together, seated in rows (*peṅgh*), regardless of caste, religion, or social status. High-caste Brahmins who refused to eat with lower-caste people were turned away from the Guru's presence
- **Simple, identical food** — The meal was always the same simple fare for everyone: *roṭī* (whole-wheat bread), *dāl* (lentils), and *sabzī* (seasonal vegetables). No distinction was made between rich and poor
- **Women's leadership** — Mātā Khīvī's role as the langar's manager established the principle that women could hold positions of responsibility in the Sikh community, a principle that Gurū Amar Dās would later expand by appointing women preachers

The langar at Khadūr Sāhib became the model that Gurū Amar Dās would later make compulsory (no one could see the Guru without first eating in the langar) and that every Sikh gurdwārā maintains to this day. It is one of the oldest continuously operating volunteer-run free food institutions in the world.

## The Vār Tradition

Gurū Aṅgad is credited with encouraging and developing the **Vār** — a genre of heroic ballad poetry that became one of the distinctive literary forms of the Sikh tradition. The word *Vār* (Punjabi: *vār*) derives from Persian *bār* — "a turn, a time, a blow" — and in the Punjabi poetic tradition came to mean a narrative ballad that describes heroic deeds, battles, or the praises of a patron or a divine figure.

### Aṅgad's Contribution

Though Aṅgad himself did not compose Vārs (his 62 hymns are primarily *Śabads* — lyrical devotional poems), he actively promoted the composition and performance of Vārs by the Sikh bards at his court. The most famous of these was **Bhāī Nattā** (also known as **Nattā Vāri**), a bard whose Vārs on the life and teachings of Gurū Nānak became part of the oral tradition that later fed into the Janam Sākhī literature. Aṅgad also encouraged the development of the *dhādī* tradition — wandering minstrels who sang heroic Vārs accompanied by the *dhadd* (a small drum) and *sarangi* (a bowed string instrument).

### The Place of Vār in Sikh Scripture

The Vār as a genre reached its fullest expression in the Ādi Granth, where the most famous examples include:

| Vār | Author | Location in Guru Granth Sāhib |
|-----|--------|------------------------------|
| **Āsā dī Vār** | Gurū Nānak (with later stanzas by Gurū Arjan) | pp. 462–475 (Rāga Āsā) |
| **Rāmkalī dī Vār** | Gurū Rām Dās | pp. 946–957 (Rāga Rāmkalī) |
| **Vār Mājh kī** | Gurū Arjan | pp. 137–150 (Rāga Mājh) |
| **Vār Malār kī** | Bhāī Gurdās | (Vaars of Bhai Gurdas — 40 Vārs, not in the Guru Granth Sāhib but canonical in Sikh tradition) |

The Vār tradition that Aṅgad nurtured was not merely literary — it had a social and spiritual function. Vārs were sung in the *saṅgat* (holy congregation) to inspire courage, devotion, and remembrance of the Guru's deeds. In the later, more militarised phase of Sikh history (the 18th-century Khalsa), the Vār tradition would become the poetic backbone of the Sikh martial ethos — the *sarbat dā ballā* (the strong arm of all) sung in Vārs of valour. But its roots lie in Gurū Aṅgad's patronage at Khadūr Sāhib, where the sung word was honoured alongside the written word as a vehicle of the Guru's presence.

## Legacy

### The Gurmukhī Script as the Carrier of Sikh Scripture

Gurū Aṅgad's development of the Gurmukhī script is the single most important material contribution to the preservation and transmission of Sikh tradition. Without a standardised, phonetically precise script capable of recording Nānak's hymns exactly as they were composed, the Sikh scriptural canon could not have been compiled by Gurū Arjan in 1604 CE. The script's name — "from the mouth of the Guru" — is a theological claim that continues to shape Sikh identity: the Gurmukhī script is not merely a writing system but a *sacrament of the Word*. To read the Guru Granth Sāhib in Gurmukhī is to hear the Guru's voice directly, without mediation. Every Sikh child who learns Gurmukhī in *paṭṭī* (alphabet) classes is participating in a tradition that Gurū Aṅgad initiated: the literacy of devotion.

### The Collection of Nānak's Hymns

Without Aṅgad's systematic collection of Nānak's hymns in the years following the first Guru's death, a significant portion of the Sikh scriptural corpus might have been lost. The oral tradition alone, however powerful, is vulnerable to corruption, abbreviation, and regional variation. Aṅgad's insistence on written preservation — and his method of cross-checking versions among the original hearers of Nānak's hymns — set a standard of textual accuracy that the subsequent Gurus maintained. The Goindval Pothīs of Gurū Amar Dās and the Ādi Granth of Gurū Arjan both rest on the textual foundation that Aṅgad established.

### The Meritocratic Succession Principle

By appointing Amar Dās over his own sons, Aṅgad reinforced — under conditions far more testing than Nānak had faced — the principle that the Guruship is a spiritual office, not a hereditary property. Nānak had passed over Srī Chand and Lakhmī Dās; Aṅgad passed over Piṇḍ and Dātu. This double precedent decisively established that the Guru's *jot* (divine light) could not be inherited by blood. The Sikh tradition's rejection of dynastic succession — unique among Indian religious movements of its time — was locked in by Aṅgad's decision. When his son Dātu physically assaulted the aged Amar Dās in protest, the congregation's loyalty to the Guru's choice confirmed that the community, not the family, was the bearer of the succession.

### The Langar at Khadūr Sāhib

Under Mātā Khīvī's management, the langar at Khadūr Sāhib became the model for every later Sikh langar. Its daily operation, its volunteer staffing, its insistence on caste-free dining, and its simple, identical food for all — these practices were transmitted to Goindval (under Gurū Amar Dās), to Rāmdāspur/Amritsar (under Gurū Rām Dās), and ultimately to every gurdwārā in the world. The langar is the most visible and most imitated Sikh institution globally, and its form was decisively shaped during Aṅgad's Guruship.

### The Vār Tradition as a Source of Sikh Identity

The Vār tradition that Aṅgad encouraged gave the Sikh community a literary genre that bridged the devotional (kīrtan) and the heroic (martial balladry). This genre would prove crucial in the later periods of Sikh history — especially during the 18th-century persecution under the Mughal Empire — when heroic Vārs of the Gurus and martyrs sustained the community's courage. Bhāī Gurdās's 40 Vārs (composed in the late 16th century) and the later *Śāhīd Bilās* literature are direct descendants of the tradition Aṅgad fostered.

### Comparative Significance

| Dimension | Gurū Aṅgad's Contribution |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| **Script** | Created the Gurmukhī alphabet — the most significant act of linguistic standardisation in Punjabi history; gave Sikhism its own script, distinct from both Devanāgarī and Perso-Arabic |
| **Scripture preservation** | Collected and authenticated Nānak's hymns; laid the foundation for the Ādi Granth (1604 CE) and the Goindval Pothīs |
| **Institution-building** | Expanded the langar; established Khadūr Sāhib as the second Sikh centre; promoted the Vār tradition |
| **Succession precedent** | Confirmed the meritocratic principle by appointing Amar Dās over his own sons — a decision as consequential as Nānak's original choice of Aṅgad himself |
| **Women's role** | Through Mātā Khīvī's leadership of the langar, established that women could hold institutional responsibility in the Sikh community |
| **Textual contribution** | Composed 62 hymns preserved in the Guru Granth Sāhib, in 8 rāgas; his hymns emphasise the centrality of the Śabad and sevā |

### Aṅgad's Character in Sikh Memory

Sikh tradition remembers Gurū Aṅgad as the faithful servant who became the Guru — the model of discipleship who was himself the ideal disciple before he was the ideal teacher. The image of Lehnā carrying Nānak's possessions across the flooded Ravi River, holding the bundle above the water, is one of the most beloved images in Sikh piety: the disciple who trusts the Guru so completely that he will wade through any current, bear any burden, risk any danger, for the sake of the Guru's word. That same Lehnā — renamed Aṅgad, "part of my body" — would go on to give the Sikh tradition its script, its textual foundation, and the institutional continuity that allowed Nānak's vision to survive and flourish.

## In Sikh Tradition

### Iconography

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Posture** | Seated in meditation (*sukhāsan*), cross-legged on a low platform, with a calm, dignified expression |
| **Appearance** | Usually depicted as a man in early middle age (he became Guru at ~35 and died at ~48), with a black or greying beard — noticeably younger than the very aged Gurū Amar Dās. Wears simple white robes and a white turban (*dastār*) |
| **Attributes** | Often shown holding a **manuscript** (the collection of Nānak's hymns) or a **writing implement** (a reed pen) — emphasising his role as the preserver of the Word and the developer of Gurmukhī. Sometimes shown with a rosary (*mālā*) in his other hand |
| **Distinguishing symbol** | A radiant halo or *jot* around his head. The Gurmukhī alphabet (the *Pañchākharī*) is sometimes depicted in the background or on a scroll — a unique iconographic marker distinct from the other Gurus |
| **Setting** | Most commonly depicted at **Khadūr Sāhib**, seated near the Beās River, with the langar hall visible in the background — connecting him to the institutional centre he established |
| **Companions** | Often shown with **Mātā Khīvī** (his wife, managing the langar) and **Gurū Amar Dās** (his devoted disciple and successor) grinding grain or fetching water — a visual representation of the ideal of *sevā* (selfless service) |

### Key Sources in the Sikh Canon

| Source | Type | Significance |
|--------|------|--------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib** | Primary scripture | Contains 62 hymns by Gurū Aṅgad, in 8 rāgas (including Rāga Gūjrī, Āsā, Dhanāsarī, Sūhī, Bilāval, Gauṛī, Rāmkālī, and Malār). His hymns are a relatively small but theologically significant portion of the canon |
| **Goindval Pothīs** | Manuscript canon | The first collection of Sikh scripture, compiled by Gurū Amar Dās, which built upon Aṅgad's earlier collection of Nānak's hymns; preserves the early textual tradition that Aṅgad initiated |
| **Bhāī Gurdās Vārān** | Commentary | The Vārs of Bhāī Gurdās (late 16th century) contain the most authoritative accounts of Aṅgad's life, his service to Nānak, his installation, and his institutional reforms |
| **Gurbilās Chhevīn Pātshāhī** | Hagiography | An 18th-century narrative of the sixth Guru that also preserves detailed accounts of the early Gurus, including Aṅgad's testing and succession |
| **Purātan Janam Sākhī** | Hagiography | Contains the episode of Nānak's selection of Aṅgad and the famous Ravi River crossing test |
| **Sikh Rahit Maryādā** | Code of conduct | The modern Sikh code (1945) prescribes the daily recitation of hymns from the Guru Granth Sāhib, including those of Gurū Aṅgad, in the *Nitnem* (daily prayer regimen) |
