# Job & Ayyub — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

Job is the Bible's great meditation on **innocent suffering** — and the Qur'an's
model of **patient endurance** (*ṣabr*). Both tell of a righteous man stripped of
everything who keeps faith and is restored twofold. But the Bible turns his
story into a vast, anguished *argument* with God, while the Qur'an distills it to
a few verses of steadfast trust. A **genealogical** connection (see
[[Shared Abrahamic Figures]]).

## The shared story
Both: a man of exemplary righteousness loses his wealth, children, and health;
his faith is tested; he is vindicated and restored *double*.

> [!quote] Job 1–2 — the wager and the integrity
> **1:8** — "Hast thou considered **my servant Job**… a perfect and an upright man…?"
> **1:21** — "Naked came I out of my mother's womb… the **LORD gave, and the LORD hath taken away**; blessed be the name of the LORD."
> **2:9** — (his wife) "Dost thou still retain thine integrity? **curse God, and die.**"

> [!quote] Qur'an — the cry and the mercy
> **Al-Anbiya 21:83** — "And Job, when he cried unto his Lord, (saying): Lo! **adversity afflicteth me**, and Thou art Most Merciful…"
> **Sad 38:41** — "Our bondman Job, when he cried unto his Lord: Lo! the **devil doth afflict me** with distress and torment."

> [!quote] The restoration — shared and doubled
> Job 42:10 — "the LORD gave Job **twice as much as he had before**."
> Sad 38:43 — "We bestowed on him (again) his household and therewith **the like thereof**, a mercy from Us."

## Where they differ: argument vs. patience
This is the heart of it.

- **The Bible** devotes ~40 chapters to Job's **protest** — he demands an answer,
  his three friends insist his suffering must be deserved, and finally **God
  answers out of the whirlwind** (Job 38–41) not with reasons but with the
  overwhelming mystery of creation. Job is a *theodicy* — a wrestling with *why
  the innocent suffer* (see [[The Problem of Evil]]).
- **The Qur'an** gives no debate. Ayyub is simply the **emblem of patience**: he
  cries to God, trusts, and is healed. The lesson is endurance, not inquiry:
  "Lo! We found him **steadfast**, how excellent a slave!" (Sad 38:44).

The Qur'an even adds a gentle miracle absent from the Bible — God tells him,
"**Strike the ground with thy foot**. This (spring) is a cool bath and a
refreshing drink" (Sad 38:42) — a healing spring.

| | Book of Job | Qur'an |
|---|---|---|
| Length & form | ~40 chapters of poetic **debate** | a handful of verses |
| The question | *why* do the innocent suffer? | (not raised) |
| God's reply | the whirlwind speeches (Job 38–41) | a healing spring; restoration |
| Core virtue | honest **lament** that God accepts | **patience** (*ṣabr*) & trust |
| Restoration | "twice as much as before" | "the like thereof" again |

Same sufferer, two spiritual lessons: the Bible legitimizes **questioning God
from the depths**; the Qur'an models **serene submission**. Job's wife's bitter
"curse God and die" survives in Islamic tradition too — but the canonical
Qur'an keeps the focus squarely on Ayyub's steadfastness.
