# Rav (Abba Arikha) (רב — אבא אריכא)

Rav, whose full name was Abba bar Aivu but who is known universally by the honorific "Rav" (the master), was the first great Amora and the founder of the Sura Academy in Babylonia. He studied under Rabbi Yehudah haNasi (Judah the Prince), the redactor of the Mishnah, and carried the Palestinian tradition eastward, where it took root and flourished. Together with his contemporary Samuel of Nehardea, Rav shaped the intellectual foundations of the Babylonian Talmud.

Born in Kafri, Babylonia, Rav travelled to the Land of Israel to study in the academy of Rabbi Yehudah haNasi, where he absorbed both the Mishnaic traditions and the hermeneutical methods of the Tannaim. Upon returning to Babylonia, he found that the religious leadership had not yet fully integrated the Mishnah into Babylonian practice. He established an academy at Sura on the Euphrates River, which became one of the two great centres of Babylonian Jewish learning (alongside Nehardea). His teachings and rulings are cited hundreds of times in the Talmud, and his debates with Shmuel define the first generation of the Amoraic period.

## Key Contributions

- **Founding of Sura Academy:** Rav established the Sura Yeshiva, which became the dominant centre of Torah learning in Babylonia and the birthplace of the Babylonian Talmud. The academy remained a major institution for nearly 800 years.
- **Transmission of the Mishnah:** He systematically taught the Mishnah of Rabbi Yehudah haNasi in Babylonia, ensuring that the Oral Law was preserved and interpreted according to Palestinian tradition while being adapted to Babylonian contexts.
- **Halakhic Rulings:** Rav's legal decisions cover the full spectrum of Jewish law — Shabbat, kashrut, marriage, torts, and prayer. His approach combined strict adherence to received tradition with practical reasoning.
- **Aggadic Teachings:** Rav was also a master of aggadah (non-legal tradition). His teachings on the nature of God, creation, prayer, and the afterlife are among the most quoted in the Talmud.

## In Jewish Tradition

- His name "Abba Arikha" (אבא אריכא — the Tall One) refers to his exceptional height; he is said to have been taller than the rest of the academy
- The Talmud states that "Rav and Shmuel are the two great pillars of the Babylonian Talmud" — their disagreements are the starting point for countless Talmudic discussions
- His student Rav Yehudah bar Yehezkel would go on to found the Pumbedita Academy
- His funeral is described in moving detail in the Talmud (Moed Katan 25a)
