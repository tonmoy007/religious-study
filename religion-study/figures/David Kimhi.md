# David Kimhi (רד"ק — Rabbi David Kimhi)

The great Provençal grammarian and exegete whose works became the standard reference for Hebrew linguistics and biblical interpretation for centuries. The Radak's commentaries synthesise the methods of Rashi and Ibn Ezra into a balanced, accessible whole, and his grammatical treatises effectively fixed the rules of Hebrew grammar.

## Key Contributions

- ***Sefer Mikhlol* (Book of Completeness):** A comprehensive grammar of Biblical Hebrew divided into two parts — *Mikhlol* proper (syntax and morphology) and *Makhberet* (a dictionary). It became the authoritative reference for Hebrew grammar in both Jewish and Christian Hebraist circles for over five hundred years.
- ***Sefer ha-Shorashim* (Book of Roots):** A lexicon of Biblical Hebrew roots arranged alphabetically, with each root's derivatives and meanings illustrated by biblical verses. This work standardised the root-based approach to Hebrew and remains a model of pre-modern lexicography.
- **Biblical Commentaries:** The Radak wrote commentaries on the Former and Latter Prophets (Nevi'im), Psalms, Chronicles, and Genesis. His approach is moderate: he follows the *peshat* (plain sense) method of Ibn Ezra but integrates rabbinic midrash where it illuminates the text, much as Rashi did.
- **Defender of Maimonides:** During the Maimonidean Controversy (1230s), Kimhi became one of the most vocal defenders of the Rambam, travelling through Spain to rally support for Maimonidean philosophy against the anti-rationalist camp led by Rabbi Solomon of Montpellier.
- **Christian Hebraism:** Kimhi's grammatical works were studied by Christian humanists (Pico della Mirandola, Reuchlin) during the Renaissance. His *Mikhlol* was one of the first Hebrew texts taught to non-Jews studying the Bible in the original.

## Life & Legacy

- Born into a family of grammarians: his father Rabbi Joseph Kimhi and his brother Rabbi Moses Kimhi were both noted grammarians and exegetes
- Lived in Narbonne, Provence — a vibrant centre of Jewish learning under Christian rule, near the border with Sepharad
- His commentaries became the standard Jewish interpretation of the Prophets, printed in nearly every edition of Mikra'ot Gedolot
- The *Mikhlol* was translated into Latin and used by Christian Hebraists; his concept of Hebrew roots influenced all subsequent Semitic linguistics
