# Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananyah (רבי יהושע בן חנניה)

A leading Tanna of the second generation and one of the foremost students of Yohanan ben Zakkai. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananyah was known for his sharp wit, his humility, and his ability to defend Judaism against sectarians (מִינִים) and Roman authorities. He served as a key figure in the Yavnean academy and later as a member of the reconstituted Sanhedrin, and he played a central role in several of the most dramatic episodes of the post-Temple period.

Rabbi Yehoshua was a Levite who worked as a blacksmith (or needle-maker) to support himself — the Talmud records his poverty with admiration, noting that his workshop produced the very tools used in Temple service (Berakhot 28a). Despite his modest means, he earned the profound respect of his colleagues for his wisdom, and he frequently clashed with Rabban Gamaliel over matters of law and administration.

## Key Contributions

- **Defense Against Minim (Heretics):** Rabbi Yehoshua was known for his skill in debating Jewish sectarians (including early Christians) and pagan philosophers. The Talmud records several encounters in which his sharp responses silenced opponents (Bekhorot 8b, Sanhedrin 38b).
- **The Confrontation with Rabban Gamaliel:** Rabbi Yehoshua was repeatedly humiliated by the autocratic Nasi Rabban Gamaliel, leading to a crisis in which Rabban Gamaliel was temporarily deposed as Nasi (Berakhot 27b–28a). Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah was appointed in his place. This episode established that even the Nasi is accountable to the sages.
- **Witness to the Temple's Destruction:** Rabbi Yehoshua, who had served in the Temple as a Levite, was present during its destruction. When he lamented the loss of atonement through sacrifices, Yohanan ben Zakkai comforted him by teaching that acts of loving-kindness are now the means of atonement (Avot de-Rabbi Natan 4).
- **Role in the Yavneh Academy:** He was among the leading sages of Yavneh, participating in the key decisions that shaped post-Temple Judaism — the calendar, the order of blessings, and the canonisation of biblical books.

## In Jewish Tradition

- Known for the saying: "The evil eye, the evil inclination, and hatred of humanity remove a person from the world" (Avot 2:11)
- The Talmud describes his mother as having brought his cradle into the study hall so his ears would absorb Torah from infancy (Yerushalmi Yevamot 1:6)
- He was the chief disputant of Rabbi Eliezer ben Hyrcanus in the Oven of Akhnai story — it was his ruling "It is not in heaven" that overruled the Heavenly Voice
- His debates with the Roman Emperor (often identified as Hadrian) are celebrated in aggadic literature
