# Maharal of Prague (מהר"ל מפראג — Rabbi Judah Loew)

The **Maharal** (Moreinu ha-Gadol Rabbi Loew) was one of the most original Jewish thinkers of the early modern period — a philosopher, mystic, educator, and community leader. He wrote revolutionary super-commentaries on Torah and aggadah, taught that the universe is structured by divine order and opposites, and is legendary as the creator of the Golem of Prague.

## Key Contributions

- ***Gur Aryeh* (The Young Lion):** A super-commentary on Rashi's Torah commentary that penetrates beneath Rashi's concise language to reveal the philosophical and structural principles Rashi implied. It remains a widely studied work.
- ***Netivot Olam* (Paths of the World) and *Derekh Chaim* (Way of Life):** Works on ethics, character development, and the meaning of the commandments, drawing on aggadic sources and offering a systematic philosophy of Jewish existence.
- ***Tiferet Yisrael* (The Glory of Israel)** and other volumes (*Gevurot Hashem*, *Nezach Yisrael*, etc.): A series of works on the Jewish festivals, each exploring the metaphysical significance of the holiday cycle.
- ***Be'er ha-Golah* (The Well of the Exile):** A defence of the Aggadah (rabbinic non-legal tradition) against rationalist critics, arguing the Sages spoke in metaphors encoding deep cosmic truths.
- **Philosophy of Education:** The Maharal was a pioneering educator who insisted education must match the child's developmental stage, criticising *pilpul* (casuistic dialectics).

## Life & Legacy

- Born in Poznań (or possibly Prague) into a distinguished family claiming descent from the Davidic line
- Served as Chief Rabbi of Nikolsburg (Moravia) for 20 years before returning to Prague
- Established a yeshiva in Prague that became a centre of Jewish learning; his educational reforms influenced later Jewish pedagogy
- Associated with the Golem legend: according to 19th-century tradition, he created a living being (*golem*) from clay to protect the Prague Jewish community, then deactivated it by erasing the *emmet* on its forehead
- His thought profoundly influenced Jewish existentialism — Martin Buber and Abraham Joshua Heschel both drew on his ideas; his emphasis on Israel's spiritual destiny also shaped later Zionist thought
