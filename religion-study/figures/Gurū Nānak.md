# Gurū Nānak (Nānak Dev)

> Born: 15 April 1469 CE, Talwaṇḍī (Nānkaṇā Sāhib), Punjab (now Pakistan)
> Died: 22 September 1539 CE, Kartarpur, Punjab (now Pakistan)
> Title: Founder of Sikhism; the First Gurū; the Satgurū; Bābā Nānak
> Era: The Sant Tradition of 15th–16th Century North India

**Gurū Nānak** (1469–1539 CE) is the founder of Sikhism and the first of the ten Sikh Gurus — a lineage of teachers whose authority culminated in the Guru Granth Sāhib, the living scripture of the Sikh tradition. Born into a Hindu Khatri family in the village of Talwaṇḍī (now Nānkaṇā Sāhib in Punjab, Pakistan), Nānak was from childhood marked by a contemplative nature that set him apart. His father, Kalu Mehta (also known as Kalu Bedī or Kalu Chand), was a village accountant (pātvarī) under the Muslim chieftain Daulat Khan Lodi; his mother, Triptā Devī, was a devout woman who nurtured his spiritual disposition. His elder sister, Bebe Nānakī, recognised his extraordinary nature early and remained his devoted companion throughout his life. The foundational event of Nānak's life — and the axis around which all Sikh history turns — occurred in 1499 CE when he was working in the town of Sultānpur Lodhī. At age thirty, Nānak entered the Vein River for his morning bath and disappeared for three days. When he emerged, he was transformed — silent for a full day, then proclaiming: *"There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim"* (*Nā kōī hindū, nā kōī musalmān*). This moment of divine commission — the receiving of the *Nām* (the divine Name) directly from the court of God — inaugurated a twenty-year ministry during which Nānak, accompanied by the Muslim rebāb-player **Bhāī Mardānā**, undertook four great journeys (Udāsīs) that spanned the Indian subcontinent, Tibet, Sri Lanka, Mecca, Medina, and Baghdad. In old age, he settled at Kartarpur ("the City of the Creator") on the banks of the Ravi River, where he founded the first Sikh commune — institutionalising the three pillars of Sikh practice: *Nām Japo* (meditating on the Divine Name), *Kirat Karo* (earning an honest living), and *Vaṇḍ Chakko* (sharing with others). He appointed **Gurū Aṅgad** (Lahiṇā) as his successor, passing over his own sons — a precedent of meritocratic succession that would define the Sikh Guru tradition. Nānak's 974 hymns, preserved in the Guru Granth Sāhib, constitute the core of Sikh scripture and theology, and his life — as remembered through the Janam Sākhīs (birth-stories) — provides the narrative foundation of the Sikh faith.

The Janam Sākhīs record his famous encounter with the Qazis of Mecca: when told to remove his shoes because he was in a holy place, he replied that the entire earth is God's mosque. At Mecca, he slept with his feet pointing toward the Kaaba — when a Qazi angrily moved his feet, the Kaaba miraculously turned to face whichever direction Nānak's feet pointed. Here he established the first Sikh commune, instituting the daily practice of: (1) **Kīrtan** (congregational singing of hymns to the accompaniment of the rebāb and the rabāb), (2) **Laiṇ (Langar)** — the free communal kitchen where all ate together regardless of caste or religion, (3) **Saṅgat** — the holy congregation meeting for worship and instruction. Kartarpur became the model for all later Sikh communities — a self-sufficient religious settlement that rejected asceticism in favour of householder life combined with disciplined devotion |
| **Selection of Successor (1539 CE)** | In his final years, Nānak tested his two sons, Srī Chand and Lakhmī Dās — both refused the role. He then tested several disciples, eventually fixing on **Lahiṇā**, a devoted follower from the village of Khadūr Sāhib. Nānak renamed him **Aṅgad** ("of my limb," i.e., "my own body") and formally installed him as Gurū — bowing to him before the assembled saṅgat as a sign of complete transference of spiritual authority. This established the principle — unique among Indian religious traditions — that the Gurūship was a spiritual office, not a hereditary one |
| **Death (1539 CE)** | 22 September 1539 CE at Kartarpur, aged 70. The Janam Sākhīs record a famous deathbed scene: the village Hindus wanted to cremate his body; the Muslims wanted to bury it. Nānak told them to place flowers on either side — whichever side remained fresh would determine the disposal. When the sheet was removed after his death, the flowers had been replaced by the *flower of the Gurū's teaching itself* — a resolution that transcends the ritual dispute. The Hindus took the flowers and cremated them; the Muslims took the remaining sheet and buried it. Both shrines — the Samādh (Hindu cremation marker) and the Kabar (Muslim grave) — were venerated. The site is now known as Gurudwārā Kartarpur Sāhib |

## The Four Udāsīs (c. 1499–1521 CE)

Nānak's four great journeys — the *Udāsīs* — are the central narrative of his ministry and one of the great pilgrimage cycles in world religious history. They cover a span of approximately twenty-two years, during which Nānak is said to have walked tens of thousands of kilometres, visiting every major religious centre in the Indian subcontinent and reaching into Central Asia and the Middle East. The journeys are preserved in the Janam Sākhī tradition, and though their historicity is debated by scholars (some argue that a single journey is more likely, with the four-journey framework being a later hagiographic construction), they function as the symbolic geography of Sikh universalism — the idea that the Gurū's message is for all people, of all faiths, in all lands.

| Udāsī | Direction | Destinations | Duration | Key Encounters |
|-------|-----------|--------------|----------|----------------|
| **First** | East | Sayyidpur (Eminabad), Delhi, Banaras, Patna, Assam, Bengal | c. 1499–1506 (7 years) | Pandits of Banaras; Tantric yogis of Assam; the robber-saint Sajjan Thag |
| **Second** | South | Rajasthan, Somnath, Maharashtra, Deccan, Rāmeśvaram | c. 1506–1513 (7 years) | Jains in Gujarat; Śaiva priests at Somnath; Brahmins at Rāmeśvaram |
| **Third** | North | Kashmir valley, Ladakh, possibly Tibet, Mount Sumeru (Kailash?) | c. 1513–1518 (5 years) | The Siddha-Nath yogis (famous dialogue recorded in the *Siddha Goṣṭi*); Kashmiri Pandits; Tibetan Buddhist lamas |
| **Fourth** | West | Sindh, Balochistan, Afghanistan, Mecca, Medina, Baghdad | c. 1518–1521 (3 years) | The Qazis of Mecca; Sufi pīrs in Baghdad; possibly the Mongol court at Herat |

The purpose of the Udāsīs was not pilgrimage in the conventional sense but *mission* — Nānak travelled not to gain merit but to teach. At every stop he sang hymns, debated scholars, challenged hypocrisy, and offered a simple, universal message: remember the One Name, live truthfully, and share what you have. The *Siddha Goṣṭi* (The Discourse with the Siddhas) — a dialogue preserved in the Guru Granth Sāhib (pages 938–946) — records his encounter with a group of Siddha-Nath yogis in the Himalayas. They ask why he, a householder, dares to teach renunciates. Nānak's response is the theological core of his mission: the true renunciation is not leaving the world but living in it without attachment — the *rājā-yogī* (king-yogi) who rules his mind while engaged in worldly life.

## Key Teachings

### Ik Oaṅkār — The One God

The foundational doctrine of Sikhism is expressed in the opening phrase of the Guru Granth Sāhib (the *Mūl Mantra*):

> **Ik Oaṅkār**  
> *Sat Nām*  
> *Kartā Purakh*  
> *Nirbha'u*  
> *Nirvair*  
> *Akāl Mūrat*  
> *Ajūnī*  
> *Saibhaṅg*  
> *Gur Parsād*  

**Translation:** "There is One God, the Eternal Reality, the Creator, without fear, without enmity, timeless, unborn, self-existent — realised through the Gurū's grace."

This is simultaneously a confessional statement and a polemical one. Against polytheistic Hinduism, Nānak asserts a radical monotheism — God is one, not many. Against the Islamic conception of God as a remote, transcendent judge (Allāh as *Qādir*, the All-Powerful), Nānak emphasises God as immanent, loving, and accessible through the *Nām* (the divine Name). God is *Nirbha'u* (without fear — not to be feared but loved) and *Nirvair* (without enmity — not partial to any religion or people). The *Mūl Mantra* opens every major section of Sikh scripture and is the first thing a Sikh child learns — it is the creed of the faith, though Sikhism prefers the term *mūl* (root) to *creed* (dogma), implying a living foundation rather than a fixed formula.

### Nām Simran — The Practice of the Divine Name

The central spiritual practice in Nānak's teaching is *Nām Simran* — the loving remembrance and repetition of the divine Name. The *Nām* is not a mere word (like "Rām" or "Allāh") but the manifest presence of God in creation. By meditating on the *Nām* — through silent repetition, aloud singing (*kīrtan*), and living in awareness of God's presence — the soul purifies itself of ego (*haumai*), the fundamental disease that separates humans from God. Nānak's theology is thus a **theology of the Name**: the *Nām* is the bridge between the unmanifest God (*Nirguṇ*) and the manifest world (*Sarguṇ*); it is God's self-communication to creation.

### The Three Pillars of Sikh Practice

At Kartarpur, Nānak institutionalised three practices that remain the ethical foundation of Sikhism:

| Pillar | Punjabi | Meaning | Practice |
|--------|---------|---------|----------|
| **Meditation** | *Nām Japo* | Meditate on the Name | Daily recitation of the Mūl Mantra and Japjī Sāhib; congregational kīrtan; constant awareness of God |
| **Honest Livelihood** | *Kirat Karo* | Work with your own hands | Earn a living through honest, productive labour — no begging, no exploitation, no idleness |
| **Sharing** | *Vaṇḍ Chakko* | Share what you have | Give a portion of your earnings to the needy; serve in the langar; treat all food as God's gift to be shared |

These three pillars together constitute Nānak's revolutionary rejection of the two dominant models of religious life in his time: the renunciate ascetic (who abandons work and family for spiritual pursuits) and the ritual-bound householder (who performs religious actions without inner transformation). Nānak's ideal — the *gurmukh* (one who faces the Guru) — is a householder engaged fully in the world but inwardly detached, earning honestly, sharing generously, and meditating constantly.

### Haumai — The Ego as the Root of Bondage

Nānak's diagnosis of the human condition centres on *haumai* (ego, self-centredness, "I-am-ness" — from *hau* = "I," *mai* = "me"). It is not the body that binds the soul but the false sense of a separate, self-existing "I" that forgets its dependence on God. The path of liberation is the progressive dissolution of *haumai* through *Nām Simran*, *sevā* (selfless service), and *satsaṅgat* (the company of holy people). Nānak uses vivid metaphors: *haumai* is a wall, a disease, a darkness; the *Nām* is the ladder, the medicine, the light.

### The Rejection of Ritual Asceticism and Caste

| Rejected Practice | Nānak's Critique |
|-------------------|------------------|
| **Caste hierarchy** | "Recognise the divine light in all, and ask no question of caste — there is no caste in the next world" |
| **Ascetic renunciation** | The true yogi is not one who leaves the world but one who lives in it without attachment; the *rājā-yogī* surpasses the *jogī* |
| **Idol worship** | God cannot be confined to stone, wood, or metal; the true temple is the heart purified by the Nām |
| **Pilgrimage to external sites** | The true pilgrimage is bathing in the Name — the *Nām* is the *tīrtha* (sacred ford) that washes away sin |
| **Empty ritual (Hindu and Muslim)** | Neither the sacred thread nor the circumcision makes one holy; what matters is the heart's orientation toward God |
| **Fasting and bodily mortification** | The body is God's temple, not an obstacle; fasting from the *Nām* is the only fast that matters |

### The Equality of Women

Nānak was radical for his time in affirming the spiritual and social equality of women:

> "From woman, man is born; within woman, man is conceived; to woman he is engaged and married. Woman is his life partner. When one woman dies, he seeks another. Through woman he establishes his place in society. Why call her evil, when from her are born kings? Woman is born from woman; there is none without woman — only the One True Lord, O Nānak."  
> — *Guru Granth Sāhib, Āsā dī Vār, p. 473*

This affirmation — embedded in the most authoritative Sikh scripture — placed Sikhism far ahead of both Hindu and Muslim norms of its era, though the practical realisation of this ideal has been uneven in Sikh history.

## The Janam Sākhīs

The **Janam Sākhīs** (Punjabi: *janam* = birth, *sākhī* = testimony/evidence; literally "birth testimonies") are the narrative collections of Gurū Nānak's life stories, composed in Punjabi prose between the early 17th and early 19th centuries. They are not biographies in the modern historical sense — they are hagiographic narratives that function as theological documents, moral instruction, and community-identity literature. They are, however, the primary source for all knowledge of Nānak's life and remain the most beloved body of Sikh narrative literature.

| Janam Sākhī Tradition | Date | Language | Key Features |
|-----------------------|------|----------|--------------|
| **Purātan Janam Sākhī** | c. 1580–1650 (oldest recension c. 1635) | Punjabi (Gurmukhī script) | The most historically reliable; older recension discovered at the Hafizabad Gurudwara (1880s); includes 57 sākhīs (episodes); presents Nānak as a reformer within a monotheistic framework |
| **Bālā Janam Sākhī** | c. 1600–1650 (attributed to Bhāī Bālā — but probably mid-17th c. composition) | Punjabi (Gurmukhī) | The most popular and widely read tradition; attributed to Bhāī Bālā, a companion who allegedly travelled with Nānak and Mardānā; includes more miraculous elements; presents Nānak in a more syncretic Hindu-style hagiography; contains the famous story of Nānak's feet at the Kaaba |
| **Miharbān Janam Sākhī** | c. 1650 (by Sodhī Miharbān, a descendant of Gurū Rām Dās) | Punjabi with Braj Bhāṣā influences | A theological Janam Sākhī that emphasises Nānak as the divine *Śabad* incarnate; includes detailed commentary on Nānak's hymns within the narrative framework |
| **Ādi Janam Sākhī** | c. 1690–1710 (by Sodhī Mīharbān's son, Bābā Gur Dās) | Punjabi | A shorter version of the Miharbān tradition; often included in manuscripts of the Ādi Granth |
| **Gurū Nānak Prakāś** | 1835 (by Bhāī Santokh Siṅgh) | Braj Bhāṣā (with some Persian and Punjabi) | A later poetic retelling in the *kāvya* (Sanskrit-style epic poetry) tradition — not a Janam Sākhī strictu sensu but the most comprehensive 19th-century compilation of the Nānak narrative |

### The Sākhī of Sajjan Thag (The Robber-Saint)

One of the most famous Janam Sākhī episodes, illustrative of Nānak's method of conversion through truth rather than confrontation. **Sajjan** (literally "noble friend") was the chief of a band of robbers who posed as a pious man, building a rest-house (*dharmśālā*) for travellers — then robbing and murdering them after they fell asleep. Nānak and Mardānā arrived at his lodge. Sajjan expected to plunder them. But Nānak began to sing a hymn — about the true Lord who sees all secrets — and Sajjan, struck by the Gurū's words, broke down, confessed, and converted. Nānak's response is the heart of the story: "If you wish to serve, serve others. If you wish to give, give to the needy. Your past is washed away — but do not return to it." The story encapsulates Nānak's method: not denunciation but presence — the power of the *Śabad* (divine Word) to awaken the soul to its own condition.

### The Sākhī of the Kaaba

During the fourth Udāsī, Nānak arrived in Mecca. As he slept, he placed his feet in the direction of the Kaaba (the Muslim direction of prayer). A Qazi (Muslim judge) saw this — an act of supreme disrespect — and kicked Nānak, angrily ordering him to turn his feet away from God's house. Nānak replied, "Turn my feet toward the place where God is not." The Qazi, enraged, grabbed Nānak's feet and dragged them away from the Kaaba — at which point the Kaaba itself moved to face the direction of Nānak's feet. The Qazi fell at Nānak's feet, recognising the Gurū. This sākhī is not anti-Muslim — it does not claim that the Kaaba is false or that Islam is wrong. It subverts the claim that any direction or place has a monopoly on God's presence. The whole earth — every direction — is God's mosque.

| **Death account** | Ambiguous: Hindus wanted cremation, Muslims wanted burial; the body disappeared, leaving flowers; both cremation and burial venerated | Body cremated; relics distributed to eight kingdoms, enshrined in stūpas | Buried in the Mosque of the Prophet in Medina; grave (rawḍa) is the second holiest site in Islam |
| **Primary narrative source** | Janam Sākhīs (c. 1600–1835 CE) — hagiographic birth-stories in Punjabi | Jātaka tales (previous lives); Buddhacarita (Aśvaghoṣa, 1st c. CE); later hagiographies | Sīra (biographical literature, especially Ibn Isḥāq, 8th c. CE); Ḥadīth (sayings and actions) |
| **Foundational community** | The **Sikh Saṅgat** (holy congregation) at Kartarpur — a householder community rejecting monasticism | The **Bhikkhu Saṅgha** (monastic order) — the community of monks and nuns; lay supporters (upāsaka/upāsikā) as secondary | The **Ummah** of Medina — a religio-political community (both believers and citizens) governed by Islamic law |
| **Later tradition** | Ten Gurus (1469–1708); the Guru Granth Sāhib as eternal Guru; the Khalsa (1699); Sikh Empire (1801–1849) | Divided into Theravāda, Mahāyāna, and Vajrayāna; spread across Asia; significantly diversified in doctrine and practice | Divided into Sunnī and Shī'a; spread globally; developed legal schools (madhāhib) and theological schools (kalām) |
| **Core slogan** | **Ik Oaṅkār Sat Nām** — "There is One God whose Name is Truth" | **Buddhaṃ Śaraṇaṃ Gacchāmi** — "I go for refuge to the Buddha" | **Lā ilāha illā Allāh** — "There is no god but God" |
| **Encounter with other traditions** | Nānak debated Siddhas, Pandits, Qazis, and Sūfīs; did not claim to supersede them but to purify them | The Buddha debated Brahmins, Jains (Nirgranthas), and Ājīvikas; rejected the authority of the Vedas and the sacrifice system | Muhammad fought the Quraysh polytheists, negotiated with Jews and Christians; claimed to restore the original monotheism of Abraham |

## Legacy

### The Guru Tradition

Nānak's most consequential institutional legacy is the system of Guru succession he established. By appointing Gurū Aṅgad over his own sons, Nānak created a spiritual office that was:

1. **Meritocratic** — authority based on spiritual attainment, not birth
2. **Progressive** — each Guru built upon the previous one's work, and the later Gurus (especially Arjan and Gobind Siṅgh) significantly developed Nānak's original vision
3. **Transformative** — the office evolved from teacher to scripture: when the tenth Guru, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh (1666–1708), ended the line of human Gurus, he declared the scripture itself — the Guru Granth Sāhib — as the eternal Guru (*Śabad Gurū*)

The ten Gurus are understood in Sikh theology as a single spiritual light (*jot*) that passed from body to body — Nānak's light was present in Aṅgad, Amar Dās, and all the way to Gobind Siṅgh. In this sense, Nānak is not just *a* Guru but *the* Guru — all later Gurus are manifestations of Nānak's authority.

### Textual Legacy — The 974 Hymns

Nānak composed 974 hymns, which constitute a distinct and theologically foundational portion of the Guru Granth Sāhib. His compositions are arranged in 15 musical measures (*rāgas*) — a choice that reflects his conviction that truth is best received through beauty, that theology must be sung to be truly heard. The most important of his works is the **Japjī Sāhib**, a 38-stanza composition that opens the Guru Granth Sāhib and is recited daily by Sikhs as their morning prayer. The Japjī is at once the most condensed statement of Nānak's theology and the most accessible entry-point into Sikh spirituality — a meditation on God, creation, the human condition, and the path of liberation.

| Major Composition | Location in Guru Granth Sāhib | Theme |
|-------------------|-------------------------------|-------|
| **Japjī Sāhib** | Opening (pp. 1–8) | The Mūl Mantra; the nature of God; the stages of spiritual progress; the grace of the Guru |
| **Āsā dī Vār** | pp. 462–475 (in Rāga Āsā) | The praise of the true Lord; critiques of empty ritual and caste; the equality of women; the importance of honest labour |
| **Siddha Goṣṭi** | pp. 938–946 (in Rāga Rāmkalī) | Dialogue with the Siddha-Nath yogis; the superiority of the householder path over ascetic renunciation |
| **Sohilā** | Evening prayer (pp. 12–13) | Meditation on death and the soul's journey to God |
| **Rahirās** | Evening prayer (composite) | Part of the evening liturgy; hymns on grace and surrender |
| **Bārāmāhā** | pp. 1107–1110 (in Rāga Tukhārī) | The twelve months as a metaphor for the soul's longing for God |

### The Langar — The Revolution of the Common Table

The **Langar** (communal kitchen) that Nānak instituted at Kartarpur is one of the most radical social innovations in Indian religious history. All visitors to the Gurū's darbār — whether Hindu or Muslim, rich or poor, Brahmin or Dalit, king or beggar — must sit together on the floor in a row (*peṅgh*) and eat the same simple meal (*roṭī* — bread, *dāl* — lentils, *sabzī* — vegetables) prepared and served by volunteers. The Langar is simultaneously a theological statement (God's grace is for all, without discrimination), a social practice (caste is annulled at the door of the Gurū's kitchen), and a spiritual discipline (selfless service, *sevā*, purifies the ego). Every Sikh gurudwara maintains a Langar to this day, serving free meals to thousands of people daily — the largest volunteer-run free food network in any religious tradition.

### The Panth — The Sikh Community as the Guru's Body

Nānak's founding of the Sikh community (*Panth*) at Kartarpur created a new social entity in the Punjab: a religious community that was neither Hindu nor Muslim, neither caste-based nor monastic, neither priestly nor kingly, but something unprecedented — a community of householders united by a common spiritual discipline, a common scripture, and a common commitment to the Guru. This community, initially small (perhaps a few hundred families at Nānak's death), grew over the next 250 years to become the Sikh Empire (1801–1849 CE), and today Sikhism is the fifth-largest organised religion in the world, with approximately 25–30 million adherents.

### Comparative Significance

Nānak stands alongside three other great founders of dharmic traditions — **Mahāvīra** (Jainism), **the Buddha**, and **Kṛṣṇa Caitanya** (Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavism) — as a 15th-century exponent of the *bhakti* (devotional) and *sant* (saintly) reformation that swept across India. But Nānak is unique among them in:

1. **Rejecting both asceticism and ritualism** simultaneously — the middle path of the householder-yogī
2. **Institutionalising his reforms in a standing community** — Kartarpur as an ongoing model
3. **Creating a scriptural tradition that became the Guru itself** — the Guru Granth Sāhib's authority as the living word
4. **Bridging Hindu and Muslim worlds** — without syncretism, without compromise, but with a radical universalism that transcended both

The Sikh historian **Trilochan Singh** wrote: *"Nānak was the first religious founder in history who not only preached the unity of God but demonstrated the unity of humanity. He not only said 'God is one' but 'Humanity is one.'"*

### World-Historical Significance

The tradition Nānak founded went through extraordinary transformations: from the pacific householder community at Kartarpur to the militarised Khalsa of Gurū Gobind Siṅgh; from a marginal sect in Mughal Punjab to the ruling power of the Sikh Empire; from the trauma of Partition (1947) — which split the Sikh heartland between India and Pakistan — to a global diaspora community of millions. Through all these transformations, Nānak's original vision — the vision of the *Satgurū* who awakens the soul to the One Name — has remained the animating centre of Sikh identity. The **Gurūdwarā Kartarpur Sāhib**, located on the Pakistani side of the India-Pakistan border, has since 2019 been accessible to Indian Sikhs through a visa-free corridor — a concrete symbol in the 21st century of Nānak's message that divides of nation, religion, and politics cannot contain the truth of the One.

## In Sikh Tradition

### Iconography

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Posture** | Seated in meditation (*sukhāsan*), typically cross-legged on a low platform (*manjī sāhib*), with a calm, compassionate expression |
| **Appearance** | Depicted as a mature man with a grey or white beard, wearing simple white robes (a *chola* or *kurtā*) and a white turban (*dastār*) — never a crown or elaborate ornament |
| **Attributes** | A rosary (mālā) in one hand, sometimes a book (the Guru Granth Sāhib); a *chaurī* (fly whisk) held as a mark of respect; the *Ek Oaṅkār* symbol (ੴ) often appears above or beside him |
| **Distinguishing symbol** | A halo of light or a luminous *jot* (divine light) emanating from his face or turban — representing the *Nūr* (divine light) that Sikh tradition holds was present in all ten Gurus |
| **Companions** | Almost always shown with **Bhāī Mardānā**, the Muslim rebāb-player who accompanies his singing; occasionally with **Bhāī Bālā**, a Hindu companion (though Bālā's historicity is disputed) |
| **Background** | Often depicted at Kartarpur, or during an Udāsī journey, or in the Vein River at the moment of his disappearance; the village of Talwaṇḍī and the *berī* (jujube) tree under which he meditated as a child are common motifs |

### Festivals

| Festival | Date | Significance |
|----------|------|-------------|
| **Gurpurab (Gurū Nānak's Birthday)** | Full moon of Kattak (October–November) | The most widely celebrated Sikh festival; includes *akhaṇḍ pāṭh* (continuous 48-hour reading of the Guru Granth Sāhib), *nagar kīrtan* (processional hymn-singing), *langar* (communal feasts), and fireworks |
| **Gurū Nānak's Gurugaddī (Installation Day)** | 7th day of the bright half of Bhādon (August–September) | Commemorates Nānak's installation as Gurū after the 3-day Vein River commission |

### Key Sources in the Sikh Canon

| Source | Type | Significance |
|--------|------|--------------|
| **Guru Granth Sāhib** | Primary scripture | Contains Nānak's 974 hymns in 15 rāgas, including the Japjī Sāhib, Āsā dī Vār, Siddha Goṣṭi, and Sohilā |
| **Purātan Janam Sākhī** | Hagiography | The oldest Janam Sākhī recension (c. 1635); 57 sākhīs covering Nānak's life from birth to death |
| **Bālā Janam Sākhī** | Hagiography | The most popular tradition; includes expanded miraculous details; attributed to Bhāī Bālā |
| **Miharbān Janam Sākhī** | Hagiography / Commentary | Theological Janam Sākhī with embedded exegesis of Nānak's hymns |
| **Bhāī Gurdās Vārān** | Commentary | The Vārs of Bhāī Gurdās (16th century) — the most authoritative commentary on Nānak's life and teachings; composed in 40 cantos |
| **Gurū Nānak Prakāś** (1835) | Epic poem | Bhāī Santokh Siṅgh's comprehensive poetic biography in Braj Bhāṣā; the most complete 19th-century compilation |
