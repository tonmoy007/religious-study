# Sāriputta

> The General of the Dhamma (Dhammasenāpati) — foremost in wisdom among the Buddha's disciples, the silent architect of the Abhidhamma, whose penetrating mind earned him the title "the Buddha's right-hand disciple."

## Relationship with Moggallāna

Sāriputta and Moggallāna were **childhood friends** from the neighbouring villages of Upatissa and Kolita. They made a pact: whoever attained liberation first would teach the other. Both became disciples of the wandering teacher **Sañjaya Belaṭṭhaputta**, but grew dissatisfied with his agnostic teaching.

When Sāriputta encountered the monk **Assaji** (one of the first five disciples), he heard a single verse that changed everything:

> *"Ye dhammā hetuppabhavā, tesaṃ hetuṃ tathāgato āha; tesañca yo nirodho, evaṃ vādī mahā samaṇo."*
>
> "Of things that arise from a cause, the Tathāgata has told the cause, and also their cessation."

Sāriputta attained stream-entry on the spot. He relayed these words to Moggallāna, who also attained stream-entry. Together they left Sañjaya's following and ordained under the Buddha, becoming **arahats** within two weeks.

Throughout their ministry the pair was inseparable — the Buddha's "two chief disciples," complementing each other perfectly: Sāriputta as the master of analytical wisdom (paññā), Moggallāna as the master of psychic powers (iddhi).

---

## His Wisdom — The Abhidhamma

Sāriputta is indelibly linked to the **Abhidhamma Piṭaka**, the third basket of the Pāli Canon. According to tradition, the Buddha spent a rains-retreat in the Tāvatiṃsa heaven teaching the Abhidhamma to his mother (reborn there as a deva). Each day he returned to the human realm and summarised the day's teaching to **Sāriputta**, who then elaborated it in exhaustive detail to the monks. The resulting systematic exposition of ultimate realities (dhammas) — mental factors (cetasikas), matter (rūpa), aggregates (khandhas), sense bases (āyatanāni), and dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda) — forms the core of the Abhidhamma.

Sāriputta's method was analytical and pedagogical: he classified, defined, and interrelated phenomena with a precision that anticipates Western analytic philosophy. Works such as the *Dhammasaṅgaṇī* (enumeration of dhammas) and *Vibhaṅga* (analysis) bear the unmistakable stamp of his systematic mind.

## His Death

Sāriputta died **before the Buddha**, on the full-moon day of the month Kattika (October–November), in the village of **Nālaka**, the very room where he was born. He had foreseen his death through meditative insight and chose to pass away there, attended by his novice **Cunda** (Cundasāmaṇera).

His passing was a profound loss for the Saṅgha. Yet the Buddha declared that Sāriputta's death was no cause for grief — he who is born must die — and praised his disciple's unparalleled wisdom and selfless service. The earth trembled when the General of the Dhamma breathed his last. Moggallāna, unable to bear the separation, died shortly after by assassination at the hands of ascetics jealous of the Buddha's growing influence.

---

## Comparison

Sāriputta's role as the "General of the Dhamma" parallels the **chief apostle** or **vicar** figure found in many traditions — a trusted disciple who systematises and transmits the teacher's message (e.g. Peter in Christianity, Ananda in the broader Saṅgha). Unlike the Buddha's cosmic, transcendent role, Sāriputta remains fully human — an exemplar of what penetrative wisdom can achieve through sustained practice.

His pairing with Moggallāna recalls the archetype of complementary heroes (thought and action, wisdom and power), analogous to Krishna and Arjuna in the Bhagavad Gītā, though Sāriputta's "power" is siddhi (psychic attainment) rather than martial strength. In the Zen tradition, Sāriputta is honoured as a paragon of prajñā (wisdom), mirroring Mañjuśrī's role in Mahāyāna iconography.
