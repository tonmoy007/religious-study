# Gurū Har Gobind (Har Gobind)

> Birth: 19 June 1595, Guru kī Wadālī (near Gurdaspur, Punjab)
> Death: 3 March 1644, Kīratpur Sāhib (Punjab)
> Title: Sixth Sikh Guru (1606–1644); creator of the **Akāl Takht**; originator of the **Mīrī-Pīrī** doctrine (two swords of temporal and spiritual authority); first Guru to militarise the Sikh community; warrior-saint who fought four battles against the Mughals

Gurū Har Gobind (Punjabi: ਗੁਰੂ ਹਰਿ ਗੋਬਿੰਦ) was the sixth Sikh Guru, who transformed the Sikh community from a pacifist devotional movement into a sovereign, armed polity capable of defending itself against the Mughal Empire. Installed at the age of 11 following the brutal martyrdom of his father, [[Gurū Arjan]], he embodied the synthesis of spiritual authority (*pīrī*) and temporal power (*mīrī*) — a doctrine he proclaimed by wearing two swords (the *Mīrī* sword of worldly authority and the *Pīrī* sword of spiritual authority). He built the **Akāl Takht** (Throne of the Timeless One) directly opposite the Harimandir Sāhib in Amritsar, establishing the seat of temporal governance for the Sikh community. He fought four defensive battles against Mughal forces — **Amritsar (1628)**, **Hargobindpur (1629)**, **Gurūsar (1631)**, and **Kartarpur (1634)** — and never lost. He also personally rescued 52 Hindu princes from imprisonment in the Gwalior Fort, earning the epithet **Bāndī Chhoṛ** (the Liberator of the Captives). His twenty-eight-year Guruship permanently altered the trajectory of Sikhism, laying the military and institutional foundations upon which [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]] would later build the Khalsa.

## The Mīrī-Pīrī Doctrine

The concept of *Mīrī-Pīrī* is Gurū Har Gobind's most enduring contribution to Sikh theology and the single idea that distinguishes Sikhism from every other Indian religious tradition.

### Meaning and Origin

| Term | Gurmukhī | Meaning | Symbol |
|------|----------|---------|--------|
| **Mīrī** | ਮੀਰੀ | From Persian *mīr* (prince, commander, temporal lord). Temporal authority — the right to rule, wage war, dispense justice, and govern the community as a sovereign entity | The sword worn on the **right** side, representing the power of the ruler |
| **Pīrī** | ਪੀਰੀ | From Persian *pīr* (saint, spiritual elder). Spiritual authority — the right to teach, guide, bless, and lead the community in devotion to God | The sword worn on the **left** side, representing the authority of the saint |

The doctrine holds that **spiritual authority without the power to defend it is hollow**, and **temporal power without spiritual guidance is tyranny**. The Guru must hold both swords in balance — neither a pacifist ascetic nor a secular prince, but a *sant-sipāhī* (saint-soldier) who combines the two roles in a single person.

### Theological Implications

1. **Rejection of Quietism** — The Sikh path is not one of withdrawal from the world but of engagement with it. The Guru has a duty to fight injustice, protect the weak, and establish righteousness (*dharam*) in society. This directly opposes the renunciant ideal of Hinduism (sannyāsa) and the quietist piety of much Sūfī Islam.

2. **Sovereignty of the Panth** — By sitting on the Akāl Takht as a sovereign ruler, Gurū Har Gobind claimed that the Sikh community was not merely a religious congregation but a political entity with its own territory, army, and legal authority. The Guru answered to no emperor — he was the emperor of his own people.

3. **Defensive War as Dharma** — The *Mīrī* sword is not an instrument of aggression. Its purpose is *dharam-yuddh* (righteous war) — the defence of the innocent, the punishment of tyrants, and the protection of the faith. The Guru never attacked the Mughals unprovoked; he only responded to attacks on the Sikh community.

4. **Integration of Bhakti and Śakti** — Sikhism under Gurū Har Gobind fused the devotional path of [[Gurū Nānak]] (bhakti — loving devotion to the One God) with the warrior ethos of śakti (the divine power that destroys evil). The Guru who meditates and the Guru who fights are one and the same.

### In Sikh Practice

The Mīrī-Pīrī doctrine is reflected in every major Sikh institution:

- **The Akāl Takht** — the seat of temporal authority, from which the Guru issued political edicts
- **The Harimandir Sāhib** — the seat of spiritual authority, where the scripture is recited day and night
- **The Two Swords in Sikh Iconography** — every Sikh depiction of Gurū Har Gobind shows him with two swords or flanked by a sword and a volume of scripture
- **The Saint-Soldier Ideal** — the Khalsa created by [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]] is the full institutionalisation of Mīrī-Pīrī: every Khalsa Sikh is both a devotee (wears the Five K's, recites scripture) and a soldier (carries a kirpān, trains for battle)
- **The Gurdwārā Complex** — almost every major gurdwārā today has both a darbār hall (prayer room) and a langar hall (community kitchen), reflecting the inseparability of spiritual and material service

## The Akāl Takht

### Construction and Architecture

Built between 1606 and 1609, the Akāl Takht (ਅਕਾਲ ਤਖ਼ਤ) is a 12-foot-high raised platform made of brick and lime plaster, with a domed canopy supported by pillars. Unlike the Harimandir Sāhib, which is set low in the Sarovara (tank) and entered by descending steps, the Akāl Takht is elevated — the Guru sits above the congregation, as a king on his throne. The building faces west, directly towards the Harimandir Sāhib, establishing a visual and ideological dialogue between the two structures.

### Functions

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Royal Court (Darbār)** | The Guru held daily court here, hearing petitions, dispensing justice, and issuing decrees. Unlike the Harimandir (where the Guru led worship), the Akāl Takht was where the Guru governed |
| **Military Headquarters** | Soldiers drilled in the forecourt; weapons were stored and blessed; war councils were convened |
| **Hukamnāmā Issuance** | Edicts from the Guru (*hukamnāme*) were read aloud from the Takht to the assembled congregation. These carried the authority of both spiritual command and temporal law |
| **Symbolic Centre of Sikh Sovereignty** | The Akāl Takht proclaimed that the Guru acknowledged no earthly overlord. It was — and remains — the seat of the *Gurū Panth* (the authority of the Sikh community as a whole) |

### The Akāl Takht in Later Sikh History

The Akāl Takht became the central institution of Sikh temporal authority. In the 18th century, when the Sikhs faced genocide under the Mughals and Afghans, the Akāl Takht was where the Sikh *dal khālsā* (the army) held its councils (*gurmatā*) and launched military campaigns. It was the site where **Banda Siṅgh Bahādur** was proclaimed leader of the Sikh uprising in 1708, where the **Sarbat Khālsā** (the entire Sikh community) met to decide policy during the 18th century, and where Maharaja **Raṇjīt Siṅgh** was anointed as the ruler of the Sikh Empire in 1801. Today, it remains one of the five Takhts and the supreme centre of Sikh temporal authority, from which edicts on matters of doctrine and discipline are issued to the global Sikh community.

## Battles of Gurū Har Gobind

The Guru fought four major battles, all defensive in nature, against Mughal forces over a seven-year period (1628–1634). These battles established the military reputation of the Sikhs and proved that the community could successfully resist the Mughal Empire.

### Background: The Breakdown of Peace with Jahāngīr

After Gurū Har Gobind's release from Gwalior (c. 1611–1612), relations with Emperor Jahāngīr were surprisingly cordial for nearly two decades. The Guru visited the imperial court, and Jahāngīr — who had personally ordered his father's execution — seems to have had a grudging respect for the young Guru. However, after Jahāngīr's death in 1627 and the accession of his son **Shāh Jahān** (builder of the Taj Mahal, but far less tolerant than his father), relations deteriorated rapidly. Shāh Jahān viewed the Guru's armed fort, his growing army, and his independent court as a direct challenge to Mughal sovereignty. Conflict became inevitable.

### The Four Battles

| Battle | Year | Location | Mughal Commander | Sikh Commander(s) | Outcome | Significance |
|--------|------|----------|-----------------|-------------------|---------|-------------|
| **Amritsar (First Battle)** | 1628 | Amritsar (near Akāl Takht) | General Mukhlis Khān (subadār of Punjab) | Gurū Har Gobind, Bhāī Bhāṇo, Bhāī Pārā | Sikh victory. The Mughal force was routed and Mukhlis Khān killed | First open Sikh military victory against the Mughals. Demonstrated that the Sikh army could defeat imperial troops in the heart of Amritsar |
| **Hargobindpur (Battle of the River Beās)** | 1629 | Hargobindpur (town founded by the Guru on the Beās River) | Painde Khān (Mughal commander sent from Lahore) | Gurū Har Gobind, Bābā Gurdittā | Sikh victory. Painde Khān was killed in single combat by the Guru | The battle took its name from the new Sikh stronghold the Guru had founded on the Beās. The Guru personally dispatched the Mughal commander |
| **Gurūsar (Battle of the Ruins)** | 1631 | Gurūsar (near Jhabal, Amritsar district) | General Lālā Beg (under orders from Shāh Jahān) | Gurū Har Gobind, Bhāī Bhāṇo | Sikh victory. The Mughal army was shattered; Lālā Beg fled the field | The fiercest of the four battles. The Guru's army was outnumbered but used superior tactics and mobility. Bhāī Bhāṇo fell in this battle — the Guru's dearest companion martyred |
| **Kartarpur (Fourth Battle)** | 1634 | Kartarpur (the original town founded by [[Gurū Nānak]], on the Beās River) | General Painde Khān's son and successor, supported by the combined forces of Lahore and Kabul | Gurū Har Gobind, Bābā Gurdittā | Decisive Sikh victory. The Guru killed the Mughal commander with his own hands | The largest and most decisive battle. After this defeat, the Mughals stopped sending major expeditions against the Guru. Shāh Jahān effectively acknowledged Sikh military parity in the Punjab region |

### Military Tactics

Gurū Har Gobind's military approach emphasised:

- **Mobility** — Sikh forces were primarily cavalry, able to outmanoeuvre the heavier Mughal infantry
- **Defensive positioning** — The Guru chose battlefields that favoured his smaller, more agile force
- **Personal leadership** — The Guru always fought at the front, inspiring his men by example
- **Limited objectives** — The goal was never conquest but repulsion: drive the Mughals out of Sikh territory and then stop
- **Single combat** — On multiple occasions, the Guru challenged and killed the opposing commander in man-to-man combat, decapitating the enemy command structure

### The Aftermath of Kartarpur (1634)

After the fourth battle, the Mughals ceased major offensive operations against the Guru. Shāh Jahān turned his attention to the Deccan and Central Asia. The Guru relocated his headquarters from Amritsar to Kīratpur Sāhib (near the foothills of the Siwaliks, in present-day Ropar district), which he had founded earlier. This was partly to avoid further provocations and partly because his beloved disciple Bābā Buddhu Shāh had granted him land there. The remaining decade of his life was relatively peaceful — he continued to teach, train his successors, and consolidate the institutions he had built.

## The Liberation of the 52 Princes (Bāndī Chhoṛ)

### Historical Context

Emperor Jahāngīr had imprisoned Gurū Har Gobind in the **Gwalior Fort** — the Mughal empire's most secure state prison — in 1609 or 1610. The official reason was the Guru's refusal to pay a fine imposed on his father, Gurū Arjan; the real reason was Jahāngīr's fear of the Guru's growing influence, his military preparations, and the "illegal" court he was holding at the Akāl Takht. The Guru was held for approximately two years.

### The Captivity

During his imprisonment, the Guru shared the prison's conditions with 52 Hindu princes (rājās) who had been captured by the Mughals and held for ransom or political leverage. They were from various Rajput kingdoms that had resisted Mughal suzerainty. The Guru became their spiritual guide and moral leader, holding daily prayer sessions, teaching them the Sikh path, and raising their spirits. The princes came to revere him as their liberator.

### The Liberation

According to Sikh tradition, the Guru used his influence with the Mughal court — perhaps aided by the Sufi saint Mīān Mīr (who had laid the foundation stone of the Golden Temple) — to secure his own release. But he refused to leave without the princes. He negotiated a clever condition with Jahāngīr: **"Only those who can hold on to my cloak-tail as I walk out will be freed."**

The Guru had a special cloak made with **52 long tassels** — one for each prince. As he walked out of the fort, each prince held onto a tassel. The guards, bound by the emperor's word, could not stop them. All 52 walked out to freedom behind the Guru.

### Theological Meaning

| Element | Symbolism |
|---------|-----------|
| **The cloak with tassels** | The Guru's authority and protection extends to all who grasp it — spiritual refuge is also physical refuge |
| **The 52 princes** | The Guru is the liberator of the oppressed, not just the spiritual guide of his own followers — his duty extends to all who suffer tyranny |
| **The clever condition** | The Guru outwits the tyrant through wisdom and diplomacy, not brute force — the saintly path that complements the martial path |
| **The walk to freedom** | Liberation is collective — no one is free until all are free. The Guru cannot accept his own release while others remain captive |

### Bandī Chhoṛ Divas

The day of the Guru's release with the 52 princes — traditionally associated with the autumn festival of **Dīvālī** (the festival of lights) — is celebrated in the Sikh tradition as **Bandī Chhoṛ Divas** (the Day of Liberation). The Harimandir Sāhib and the Akāl Takht are illuminated with thousands of lamps and lights. The coincidence with the Hindu Dīvālī has caused some confusion, but for Sikhs the festival commemorates the Guru's act of liberation. The date continues to be one of the most important annual festivals in the Sikh calendar.

## Key Hymns and Teachings

While Gurū Har Gobind is primarily remembered as a warrior-administrator, he also composed hymns that appear in the Gurū Granth Sāhib. His compositions are comparatively few — **two hymns** (a *solahā* and a *chaupadā*) registered under the title **Gurū Har Gobind** in the scripture — but they contain the doctrinal essence of his Guruship.

### The Solahā (Thirty-eight couplets)

This composition, found in the **Rāga Bhairon** section of the Gurū Granth Sāhib (pages 1139–1140), expresses the Guru's synthesis of devotion and militancy:

> *"One who does not remember God even for a moment — what use is that life? Like a tree that bears no fruit, like a cloud that gives no rain."*

> *"The mortal being is entangled in worldly pleasures; he does not realise that his days are numbered. The True Guru has given me this understanding: without the Name, there is no liberation."*

> *"The sword of the Lord cuts down the tyrant; the Name of the Lord saves the saint. The Guru has shown me this path: sing the Lord's praises, and fight for the right."*

### The Chaupadā (Four-stanza hymn)

In **Rāga Basant** (page 1186), the Guru teaches:

> *"Har Gobind says: listen, O mind — the Lord is the only true King. He alone is eternal, all others pass away. Why do you fear death when the Lord is with you? Sing the praises of the One who creates and destroys."*

### Doctrinal Themes in the Hymns

1. **Nām Simaran** — Remembrance of the Divine Name remains the core spiritual practice, unchanged from Gurū Nānak
2. **God as King and Warrior** — God is described as "the slayer of tyrants" (*śatrū-nāś*), a more militant depiction than in earlier Gurus
3. **Fearlessness** — The Sikh who has faith in God should fear nothing, not even the Mughal emperor
4. **Karma and Grace** — The Guru affirms the balance between human effort (martial training, community organisation) and divine grace
5. **The Guru as Protector** — The Guru is the refuge of the helpless and the champion of the oppressed

### Contribution to Sikh Liturgy

Though few in number, Gurū Har Gobind's hymns established a theological precedent: the Guru could teach with the sword and the pen, the battlefield and the hymn-book. This dual literacy — the warrior who composes devotion — would reach its fullest expression in [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]], who wrote the Dasam Granth while leading his army.

## Succession and Legacy

### Appointment of Gurū Har Rāi

Before his death in 1644, Gurū Har Gobind appointed his grandson **Har Rāi** (the son of his eldest son Bābā Gurdittā) as the seventh Guru. This was a notable departure from primogeniture: his own surviving sons, Bābā Sūraj Māl and Bābā Tegh Bahādur (the future ninth Guru), were passed over. Sūraj Māl was deemed too intemperate, and Tegh Bahādur was still immersed in spiritual practice and not yet ready for leadership. The Guru's decision affirmed that the Guruship was not a hereditary right but a divine trust to be placed in the most qualified candidate — a principle that [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]] would reaffirm by ending the line of human Gurus altogether.

### The Gurū Tegh Bahādur Connection

Gurū Har Gobind's younger son, **Tegh Bahādur**, was born in 1621 and raised in the Guru's household — trained in both scripture and martial arts. After the Guru's death, Tegh Bahādur retreated to Bakala (a village near Amritsar) and spent over two decades in meditation and spiritual practice before emerging as the ninth Guru in 1664. Gurū Har Gobind's legacy directly shaped Tegh Bahādur: the ninth Guru inherited his father's courage (he would be martyred in Delhi in 1675 defending the right of Hindus to practise their faith) and his commitment to the inseparability of spirituality and social responsibility.

### The Foundation for the Khalsa

Gurū Har Gobind's institutional and military work was the necessary precondition for the creation of the Khalsa by [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]] in 1699. Without the sixth Guru:

- The Sikh community would not have had a standing army or military tradition
- The Akāl Takht would not have existed as the seat of temporal authority making the *gurmatā* (Guru's resolution) possible
- The doctrine of Mīrī-Pīrī would not have been formulated — the Khalsa's identity as *sant-sipāhī* was built directly on this foundation
- The memory of Sikh military parity with the Mughals (four battles, four victories) would not have inspired later generations

Gurū Gobind Siṅgh explicitly acknowledged this lineage: he completed the work begun by Gurū Har Gobind. The sixth Guru built the fort; the tenth Guru garrisoned it permanently.

| **Theology of authority** | *Mīrī-Pīrī* — the Guru must hold both spiritual and worldly power; the saint sits on a throne with a sword | *Gelasian doctrine* — two swords of spiritual (Church) and temporal (State) authority, but distinct (the priest holds the spiritual sword, the king holds the temporal) | *Imāmate* — the Imām is the divinely appointed successor of Muhammad, holding both spiritual authority (wilāyah) and temporal authority (caliphate). ʻAlī is the "gate of the city of knowledge" |
| **Personal combat** | Killed two Mughal generals in single combat (Painde Khān and his successor at Kartarpur) | — (more a strategist than a front-line warrior) | Renowned as the "Lion of God" (Asad Allāh) — unparalleled in single combat; killed numerous opponents at Badr, Khaybar, and in duels |
| **Age at death** | 48 | 53 | 61 |
| **Manner of death** | Peaceful death at Kīratpur (1644), surrounded by family | Natural death (or possibly poison); buried at Saint-Denis | **Assassinated** — stabbed by a Khārijite (Ibn Muljam) in the mosque of Kufa while leading dawn prayer (661 CE) |
| **Successor** | Grandson Har Rāi (bypassing own sons) | Son Pepin the Short (who became King of the Franks and father of Charlemagne) | His son Ḥasan ibn ʻAlī (second Imām); the Imāmate continued through his lineage (Ḥusayn, then the subsequent Imāms) |
| **Legacy** | Transformed Sikhism from a pacifist bhaktī movement to a sovereign, armed community; created the institutional and theological foundation for the Khalsa | Checked the Umayyad advance into Europe; founded the Carolingian dynasty; defended Latin Christendom | The archetype of the Shia Imām: the suffering, just, divinely-guided leader who is opposed by tyrants. | **Shared pattern** | A spiritual leader who, in response to the persecution and martyrdom of his predecessor, takes up arms to defend the community, creates lasting institutions of temporal authority, and transforms a quietist faith into a militant one — without losing the spiritual core |

## Legacy

### In Sikh Institutions

- **The Akāl Takht** remains one of the five Takhts of Sikhism and the supreme seat of temporal authority for the global Sikh community. Every *hukamnāmā* (edict) affecting the Panth is issued from here.
- **Bandī Chhoṛ Divas** (often coinciding with Dīvālī) is one of the most widely observed Sikh festivals, celebrated with lamp-lighting, processions, and special prayers at the Harimandir Sāhib.

### In Sikh Theology

- The **Mīrī-Pīrī** doctrine is the foundation of Sikh political theology — the insistence that Sikhs must be both saintly and sovereign, both spiritual and temporal.
- The ideal of the **sant-sipāhī** (saint-soldier) that [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]] institutionalised in the Khalsa was first articulated by Gurū Har Gobind.
- The principle that a Sikh must be **fearless in the face of tyranny** — derived from the Guru's refusal to submit to Jahāngīr or Shāh Jahān — remains central to Sikh identity.

### In Sikh History

Gurū Har Gobind occupies a critical transitional position in the chain of ten Gurus:

| Guru | Period | Contribution |
|------|--------|-------------|
| [[Gurū Nānak]] (1st) | 1469–1539 | Founding of the faith; the theology of *ik oaṅkār* and nām-simaran |
| [[Gurū Aṅgad]] (2nd) | 1539–1552 | Gurmukhī script; langar institution |
| [[Gurū Amar Dās]] (3rd) | 1552–1574 | Sikh liturgy; missionary organisation |
| [[Gurū Rām Dās]] (4th) | 1574–1581 | Foundation of Amritsar; excavation of the holy tank |
| [[Gurū Arjan]] (5th) | 1581–1606 | Compilation of the Ādi Granth; construction of the Harimandir Sāhib; first martyrdom |
| **Gurū Har Gobind (6th)** | **1606–1644** | **Mīrī-Pīrī; Akāl Takht; militarisation; liberation of the prisoners; four battles** |
| [[Gurū Har Rāi]] (7th) | 1644–1661 | Preservation of the Sikh army; medical missions |
| [[Gurū Har Krishan]] (8th) | 1661–1664 | Child Guru; ministry in Delhi |
| [[Gurū Tegh Bahādur]] (9th) | 1664–1675 | Martyrdom in Delhi defending religious freedom |
| [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]] (10th) | 1675–1708 | Creation of the Khalsa; finalisation of the Gurū Granth Sāhib |

### In World Religious History

Gurū Har Gobind is one of the earliest examples of a religious leader who **deliberately militarised a pacifist community in response to state persecution** — a pattern that would recur in many traditions (Mormons under Brigham Young, the early Muslim community under Muhammad's migration to Medina, or the Jewish Maccabees). What makes him distinctive is:

1. The **speed of the transformation** — within his own lifetime, from age 11 to 48, he turned a pacifist sect into a viable military power
2. The **balance he maintained** — he never abandoned the spiritual core of Sikhism (nām-simaran, the langar, the Ādi Granth) even as he built an army
3. The **institutional durability** — the Akāl Takht, the two-sword doctrine, and the sant-sipāhī ideal survive intact nearly 400 years later
4. The **theological sophistication** — Mīrī-Pīrī is not a crude warrior ethic but a nuanced integration of spiritual and temporal authority that continues to inform Sikh political thought

In the Sikh memory, Gurū Har Gobind is recalled with the epithet:

> **The Sixth Guru — who wore two swords, freed the captives, built the Throne of the Timeless One, and taught the Khalsa to be both saint and soldier.**
