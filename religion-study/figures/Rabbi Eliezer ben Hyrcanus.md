# Rabbi Eliezer ben Hyrcanus (רבי אליעזר בן הורקנוס)

A leading Tanna of the first and second generations and one of the greatest students of Yohanan ben Zakkai. Rabbi Eliezer ben Hyrcanus is remembered for his prodigious memory, conservative approach to Jewish law, and the dramatic story of the **Oven of Akhnai**, in which he was excommunicated by his colleagues for refusing to yield to the majority opinion. He represents, in Jewish tradition, the tension between individual authority and communal consensus.

Rabbi Eliezer was born into a wealthy family but did not begin studying Torah until his early adulthood — a late start that, like Rabbi Akiva's, became part of his legend. He travelled to Jerusalem to study under Yohanan ben Zakkai, mastering both the written and oral laws with extraordinary diligence. His legal opinions tend to be stricter than those of his contemporaries, and he is frequently recorded in the Mishnah as the dissenting minority voice.

## Key Contributions

- **Oven of Akhnai (תַּנּוּרוֹ שֶׁל עַכְנַאי):** The most famous legal dispute in the Talmud (Bava Metzia 59a–b). Rabbi Eliezer brought every possible proof for his position, including miraculous signs — a carob tree moved, a stream flowed backward, and the walls of the study hall bowed — but the Sages rejected his proofs, ruling that the Torah is not in heaven and the majority decides the law. When a Heavenly Voice declared in his favour, Rabbi Yehoshua replied: "It is not in heaven" (Deuteronomy 30:12).
- **Excommunication:** After refusing to accept the majority ruling on the clean/unclean status of the oven, Rabbi Eliezer was placed under a ban (חֵרֶם). The tragic aftermath, in which many of his possessions were destroyed, is recounted with great poignancy in the Talmud.
- **Conservative Legal Approach:** Rabbi Eliezer generally followed the school of Shammai and was known for strict interpretations, especially in matters of purity, family law, and Sabbath observance.
- **Prodigious Knowledge:** The Talmud says of him that he "never said a thing he had not heard from his teacher" (Sukkah 28a) — he was a transmitter of tradition who rarely innovated.

## In Jewish Tradition

- His students called him "a cemented cistern that loses not a drop" (Avot 2:8)
- Although excommunicated, his legal opinions are preserved in the Mishnah, and the Sages later lamented that "the entire law became forgotten" after his death (Sanhedrin 68a)
- His wife, Ima Shalom — the sister of Rabban Gamaliel — attempted to moderate his stubbornness but could not prevent his excommunication
- The story of the Oven of Akhnai is one of the most discussed passages in the Talmud, often cited in debates about tradition vs. innovation and authority vs. consensus
