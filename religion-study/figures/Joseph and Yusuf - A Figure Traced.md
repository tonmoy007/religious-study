# Joseph & Yusuf — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

The story of Joseph is the longest sustained narrative about one person in
*both* the Hebrew Bible and the Qur'an — [[scriptures/bible-kjv/01 Genesis|Genesis 37–50]]
(14 chapters) and [[012 Yusuf|Surah 12, Yusuf]] (one continuous 111-verse surah
the Qur'an itself calls "the best of narratives," 12:3). Reading them in
parallel is the clearest lesson in *how* the Qur'an retells a biblical story:
**same plot, same order, different emphasis**. This is a textbook
**genealogical** connection (see [[Shared Abrahamic Figures]]).

## The shared plot, beat by beat

| Beat | Genesis (KJV) | Qur'an (Yusuf) |
|---|---|---|
| The dream | sun, moon, **eleven stars** bow to him (37:9) | "eleven planets… prostrating themselves" (12:4) |
| Brothers' plot | "cast him into some pit" (37:20–24) | "fling him into the depth of the pit" (12:10, 15) |
| The cover story | bloodied coat, "evil beast" (37:31–33) | torn shirt, "the **wolf** devoured him" (12:13–17) |
| Sold | "twenty pieces of silver" (37:28) | "a low price, a number of silver coins" (12:20) |
| Egypt | sold to Potiphar (37:36; 39:1) | bought by "he of Egypt" who says *adopt him as a son* (12:21) |
| The seduction | master's wife: "Lie with me" (39:7) | the master's wife desires him; he refuses (12:23) |
| The garment | "left his garment in her hand, and fled" (39:12) | "she **tore his shirt from behind**" (12:25) |
| Prison | jailed; interprets butler's & baker's dreams (40) | jailed; interprets two prisoners' dreams (12:36–41) |
| Pharaoh's dream | seven fat / seven lean kine (41) | "seven fat kine which seven lean were eating" (12:43–49) |
| Rise to power | set over all Egypt (41:41) | given "sovereignty" and authority (12:56, 101) |
| The reunion | "**I am Joseph**; doth my father yet live?" (45:3) | "**I am Joseph** and this is my brother" (12:90) |
| The dream fulfilled | family bows; Joseph weeps (45–46) | parents & brothers "fell down before him prostrate… this is the interpretation of my dream of old" (12:100) |

The skeleton is **identical and in the same sequence** — far closer than the
[[Flood Narratives Compared|flood]] or [[First Murder - Cain and Abel|Cain & Abel]]
retellings.

## Where the Qur'an differs

**1. Details the Qur'an adds**
- **The torn shirt as forensic evidence.** A witness reasons: if the shirt is torn *from behind*, Joseph fled and she lied (12:26–28). Genesis has no such courtroom logic.
- **The women who cut their hands.** The noblewomen mock Potiphar's wife; she invites them to a feast, hands each a knife, and summons Joseph — they are so stunned by his beauty they cut their own hands (12:31). A famous Qur'anic scene with no biblical counterpart.
- **The shirt that heals Jacob's blindness.** Joseph sends his shirt to be laid on his father's face: *"he will become (again) a seer"* (12:93); Jacob smells Joseph on the wind before it arrives (12:94) and his sight returns (12:96). Pure Qur'anic addition.

**2. Details Genesis adds**
- Far more **family and political texture**: Reuben's and Judah's distinct roles, the silver cup planted in Benjamin's sack, Judah's long plea, the migration of all Israel to Goshen — the genealogical machinery that sets up the Exodus. The Qur'an compresses all of this.

**3. The deepest divergence — what the story is *for*.**

> [!quote] Genesis — providence within a family saga
> **45:5** — "…be not grieved… for God did send me before you to preserve life."
> **50:20** — "ye thought evil against me; **but God meant it unto good**, to bring to pass… to save much people alive."

Genesis frames Joseph as the hinge of a **national** story: God preserves the
twelve tribes through famine, seeding the nation that Exodus will deliver.

> [!quote] Qur'an — submission, patience, and signs
> **12:101** — "…Make me to die **muslim** (unto Thee), and join me to the righteous."

The Qur'an frames Joseph as a model of **personal righteousness**: he resists
temptation by God's grace, endures prison with patience (*ṣabr*), credits Allah
at every turn, and the moral is individual submission — not the birth of a
nation. Where Genesis looks *forward* to Israel, Surah Yusuf looks *upward* to
God.

## Why this comparison is worth doing
Because the plots are so close, the **differences are pure signal** — each one
shows a tradition's priorities. Genesis = covenant, lineage, national destiny.
Qur'an = *tawḥīd* (God's oneness), patience, chastity, the unity of the
prophetic message. Same man, two theologies — the pattern of the entire
[[Shared Abrahamic Figures|Abrahamic family]] in a single story.
