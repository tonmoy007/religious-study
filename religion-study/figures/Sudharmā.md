# Sudharmā

> Birth: c. 6th century BCE, Magadha (modern Bihar)
> Death: c. 5th century BCE
> Title: Ganadhara (Chief Disciple) of Mahāvīra; the sole Ganadhara through whom the Śvetāmbara Āgamas have been transmitted

Sudharmā (also spelled Sudharman or Sudharma) was one of the eleven Ganadharas (chief disciples) of Mahāvīra, the 24th Tīrthaṅkara, and the single most important figure for the transmission of the Jain canonical scriptures. Originally a lay follower (śrāvaka) of [[Pārśva]]'s established order, he became Mahāvīra's disciple after the Tīrthaṅkara's enlightenment. He outlived all other Ganadharas, and his student [[Jambūsvāmin]] became the first Śruta Kevalin (knower of all scripture) — the last human to know the complete Jain canon by heart. Every Śvetāmbara monastic lineage traces its spiritual descent through Sudharmā, and the Śvetāmbara Āgamas are believed to have been transmitted through his uninterrupted line of disciples. This makes Sudharmā the single human link between Mahāvīra's oral teachings and the entire Śvetāmbara scriptural tradition.

## The Lineage of the Śvetāmbara Āgamas

Sudharmā's unique place in Jain history rests on a single fact: **he is the sole Ganadhara through whom the Śvetāmbara scriptural tradition has been transmitted**. The transmission chain is as follows:

### The Post-Nirvāṇa Transmission

| Successor | Role | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| **Mahāvīra** (24th Tīrthaṅkara) | Source of the teaching | Spoke the *divyadhvani* (divine sound) which the Ganadharas "translated" into human language |
| **Sudharmā** | Ganadhara | Outlived all other Ganadharas; sole link between Mahāvīra and the next generation |
| **[[Jambūsvāmin]]** | First Śruta Kevalin | The last human to know the entire canon by heart; perfected the oral transmission |
| **Prabhava** (or Prabhāsa) | Śruta Kevalin | Continued the tradition |
| **Śayyambhava** (or Sthavira) | Ācārya | Preserved and taught the canon |
| **Yasobhadra** | Ācārya | Further transmission |
| **Saṃbhūtavijaya** | Ācārya | Continued the lineage |
| **Bhadrabāhu** (I) | Ācārya | The author of the *Kalpa Sūtra*; prophesied the Great Famine (c. 350 BCE) |
| **Sthūlabhadra** | Ācārya | Presided over the Pāṭaliputra Council (c. 310 BCE) which fixed the Śvetāmbara canon |

This chain — from Mahāvīra → Sudharmā → Jambūsvāmin → ... → Bhadrabāhu → Sthūlabhadra — is the basis on which the Śvetāmbara tradition claims that its Āgamas are the authentic, unbroken transmission of Mahāvīra's original teaching.

### Why Sudharmā, Not Gautama?

A natural question arises: why does the entire Śvetāmbara canon come through Sudharmā rather than through [[Indrabhūti Gautama]], who was Mahāvīra's *first* and *chief* Ganadhara?

| Reason | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Longevity** | Gautama predeceased Sudharmā. Though he lived long enough to organise the teachings immediately after Mahāvīra's nirvāṇa, he could not transmit them to the next generation. Sudharmā outlived all ten other Ganadharas |
| **Succession** | As the sole surviving Ganadhara, Sudharmā naturally became the sole link between the first generation (who heard Mahāvīra directly) and the second generation (who received the teaching from a Ganadhara) |
| **Disciple quality** | Jambūsvāmin, Sudharmā's student, was uniquely capable of retaining the entire canon — a feat no other disciple of any Ganadhara could match |
| **The Śvetāmbara commitment** | All Śvetāmbara monastic lineages trace their ordination lineage (patrāvalī) back to Sudharmā. This is a genealogical claim: every Śvetāmbara monk or nun today is, through an unbroken chain of initiations, a spiritual descendant of Sudharmā |

### The Patrāvalī (Lineage Lists)

Śvetāmbara monastic lineages (*patrāvalī* or *gaṇadhara-paramparā*) typically begin:

> Mahāvīra → Sudharmā → Jambūsvāmin → Prabhava → Śayyambhava → ...

The exact names and order vary between sub-traditions, but Sudharmā is always the first link after Mahāvīra. This is the Śvetāmbara equivalent of the Buddhist *paramparā* (lineage) that links the present-day Saṅgha to the Buddha.

## Doctrinal Role

### What Sudharmā Transmitted

As a Ganadhara, Sudharmā was responsible for:

- **Hearing** Mahāvīra's *divyadhvani* — a divine, non-linguistic sound that only the Ganadharas could "translate" into human language
- **Organising** the teachings into the twelve Aṅgas (limbs) of the canon
- **Teaching** the next generation of monastics, especially Jambūsvāmin
- **Presiding** over the monastic community after the other Ganadharas had passed away

### The Twelve Aṅgas

The twelve Aṅgas that Sudharmā transmitted (according to Śvetāmbara tradition) are:

| Aṅga | Name | Content |
|------|------|---------|
| 1 | **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** | Monastic conduct, rules, and Mahāvīra's ascetic biography |
| 2 | **Sūtrakṛtāṅga** | Doctrinal positions and debates with rival teachers |
| 3 | **Sthānāṅga** | Numerical lists of doctrines (1 to 10) |
| 4 | **Samavāyāṅga** | Extended numerical lists |
| 5 | **Vyākhyāprajñapti (Bhagavatī Sūtra)** | Doctrinal questions and answers — the longest and most important Aṅga |
| 6 | **Jñātādharmakathā** | Parables and stories illustrating Dharma |
| 7 | **Upāsakadaśā** | Conduct of the ten principal lay followers |
| 8 | **Antakṛtdaśā** | Lives of those who attained liberation |
| 9 | **Anuttaraupapātikadaśā** | Rebirths in the highest heavens |
| 10 | **Praśnavyākaraṇa** | Questions and explanations on karma |
| 11 | **Vipākaśruta** | The ripening of karma — stories of reward and punishment |
| 12 | **Dṛṣṭivāda** | The lost twelfth Aṅga — contained the most profound doctrines; no complete copy survives |

The first eleven Aṅgas have been preserved in the Śvetāmbara canon; the twelfth (*Dṛṣṭivāda*) is universally regarded as lost.

## In Śvetāmbara vs Digambara Traditions

| Aspect | Śvetāmbara | Digambara |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Role of Sudharmā** | The sole Ganadhara through whom the entire canon was transmitted; every Śvetāmbara monastic lineage descends from him | The Āgamas are also traced through Sudharmā in some Digambara accounts, but Digambara tradition holds that the original canon was entirely lost — what survives are later reconstructions |
| **Lost Aṅga (Dṛṣṭivāda)** | The 12th Aṅga is lost because a monk named Jīvāśoka (or Jīvānandin) drowned while crossing a river with the manuscript, leaving only fragments cited in commentaries | The entire canon was lost due to the Great Famine; the *Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama* and *Kaṣāyapāhuda* are later Digambara reconstructions from what remained |
| **Lineage** | Unbroken: Mahāvīra → Sudharmā → Jambūsvāmin → ... → present-day Śvetāmbara Ācāryas | Broken by the famine; Digambara teachers rely on a separate lineage through Ārya Viṣṇu and Ārya Indradatta (also Sudharmā's line, but the transmission was interrupted) |
| **Veneration** | Sudharmā is a major figure in Śvetāmbara monastic ritual; his name is recited in daily prayers and in the *pratikramaṇa* (confession) liturgy | Honoured but less prominent; Digambara focus falls more on the post-canonical Ācāryas such as Kundakunda and Umāsvāmi |

## Comparative: The Three Jain Scriptures and Their Human Origins

| Aspect | Śvetāmbara Āgamas | Digambara Scripture (Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama) | Non-Sectarian (Tattvārtha Sūtra) |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Human source** | Mahāvīra → Sudharmā → Jambūsvāmin | Traced back to Mahāvīra but transmitted through separate post-canonical redactors (Puṣpadanta, Bhūtabali) | Authored by Umāsvāmi (c. 2nd–5th c. CE); accepted by both sects as the definitive systematic treatise |
| **Language** | Ardhamāgadhī Prākrit | Saurasenī Prākrit | Sanskrit |
| **Date of fixation** | c. 310 BCE (Pāṭaliputra Council); final redaction at Vallabhī (c. 5th c. CE) | c. 2nd century CE (original composition); preserved in manuscript form from c. 10th c. | c. 2nd–5th century CE |
| **Canonical status** | The complete, authoritative canon for Śvetāmbaras | The most authoritative post-canonical work for Digambaras (sometimes called the "Digambara Āgama") | The single text accepted by all Jain sects; often called the "Jain Gītā" |
| **Lost texts** | 12th Aṅga (Dṛṣṭivāda) is lost; 11 Aṅgas survive | The original 14 Pūrvas (pre-canonical texts) were lost; the Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama summarises them | No lost texts — a single complete composition |
| **Role of Sudharmā** | *Essential* — without Sudharmā, no Śvetāmbara canon | *Implied* — the early transmission passes through him, but the chain was broken | *Acknowledged* — Umāsvāmi draws on the Śvetāmbara tradition but does not depend on a single lineage |

## Key Texts

| Text | Tradition | Content |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** | Śvetāmbara | The first Aṅga; describes Mahāvīra's ascetic practice and the monastic code — transmitted through Sudharmā's line |
| **Bhagavatī Sūtra (Vyākhyāprajñapti)** | Śvetāmbara | The longest Aṅga; a vast compendium of doctrine in question-answer format — attributed to Indrabhūti Gautama's teaching but preserved through Sudharmā's lineage |
| **Kalpa Sūtra** (Bhadrabāhu, c. 350 BCE) | Śvetāmbara | Contains the biographies of the Tīrthaṅkaras and the lineages of the Sthaviras (elders), including the full transmission chain from Mahāvīra through Sudharmā to the present |
| **Nandī Sūtra** | Śvetāmbara | A canonological text that lists the Sthavira lineages and explains how the canon was transmitted through Sudharmā's line |
| **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (Hemacandra, 12th c. CE) | Śvetāmbara | The "Lives of the 63 Illustrious Men" — includes a section on Sudharmā and his role in preserving the teaching |
| **Śrīpāla-Bhadracarita** | Śvetāmbara | Medieval text honouring the post-canonical teachers in Sudharmā's lineage |

## Significance

Sudharmā occupies a unique position in Jain history because he is the **sole human link** between Mahāvīra's original oral teaching and the entire scriptural tradition of Śvetāmbara Jainism. His significance can be summarised in three points:

1. **Scriptural Custodian** — He outlived all other Ganadharas, making him the only person who had heard Mahāvīra's complete teaching and could transmit it to the next generation. Without him, the entire Śvetāmbara canon would have been lost.

2. **Founder of the Monastic Lineage** — Every Śvetāmbara monk and nun today traces their ordination lineage (*patrāvalī*) back to Sudharmā. This is not merely symbolic: it means that the Śvetāmbara monastic community understands itself as a direct, uninterrupted continuation of the community that Sudharmā led.

3. **Bridge Between Traditions** — As a former follower of [[Pārśva]] who became a Ganadhara of [[Mahāvīra]], Sudharmā embodies the historical continuity between the two great Jain teachers. His life and transmission show that Jainism is not a single "founding moment" but a flowing tradition that absorbed and adapted earlier streams.

> "When Mahāvīra attained nirvāṇa, the other ten Ganadharas had already passed away. Only Sudharmā remained — the sole vessel of the Tīrthaṅkara's teaching. He poured that teaching into Jambūsvāmin, and Jambūsvāmin poured it into the world. The Śvetāmbara Āgamas are the water that has flowed from that single vessel." — Adapted from Śvetāmbara tradition

## Comparative Cross-Tradition

| Aspect | Sudharmā (Jain) | Jambūsvāmin (Jain) | Ānanda (Buddhist) | Mahākassapa (Buddhist) |
|--------|----------------|--------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Role** | Ganadhara — one of the eleven chief disciples of Mahāvīra; preserver of the canon | First Śruta Kevalin; the last human to know the complete canon by heart | The Buddha's personal attendant; heard and remembered all suttas | Presided over the First Buddhist Council; convened the monks to fix the canon |
| **Relationship to founder** | Disciple of Mahāvīra, from Pārśva's tradition | Disciple of Sudharmā | Cousin and personal attendant of the Buddha | Senior monk who convened the post-nirvāṇa council |
| **Key achievement** | Outlived all other Ganadharas; transmitted the complete teaching to Jambūsvāmin | Became the first Śruta Kevalin; fixed the oral canon in its definitive form | Recited the Sutta Piṭaka at the First Council after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa | Recited the Abhidhamma and presided over the council that fixed the Tipiṭaka |
| **Canon transmitted** | The twelve Aṅgas of the Śvetāmbara Āgamas | The same twelve Aṅgas (now perfected by his memorisation) | The Sutta Piṭaka (discourses of the Buddha) | The Vinaya Piṭaka (monastic rules) and Abhidhamma Piṭaka |
| **Lineage significance** | The sole Ganadhara from whom all Śvetāmbara lineages descend | The final link in the direct oral transmission | All Buddhist reciters trace back to Ānanda for the Suttas | All Vinaya lineages trace back to Mahākassapa's council |
| **Sectarian status** | Venerated by both Śvetāmbara and Digambara (though Digambara emphasis on his line is weaker) | Venerated by both; though Digambara tradition holds the canon was lost after him | Venerated by all Buddhist schools | Venerated by all Buddhist schools, especially Theravāda |
| **Parallel function** | Link between the Tīrthaṅkara's generation and the post-Tīrthaṅkara generation | Link between the age of direct hearing and the age of scriptural study | Link between the Buddha's generation and the post-Buddha generation | Link between the Buddha and the institutional Saṅgha |
