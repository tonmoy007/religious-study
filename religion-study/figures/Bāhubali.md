# Bāhubali (Gommateshvara)

> The son of the first Tīrthaṅkara who defeated his brother in single combat, only to renounce the world in disgust at its violence — then stood motionless in meditation for twelve years while vines and anthills grew over his body. He attained *kevala jñāna* as a non-Tīrthaṅkara Kevalin ("silent enlightened one"), and his 57-foot (17.4 m) monolithic statue at Shravanabelagola is the tallest monolithic statue in India and one of the most iconic monuments in Jain history.

## The Story

### The War of the Brothers

After Ṛṣabha renounced the world, he divided his kingdom among his 100 sons. **Bharata**, the eldest, received the core of the kingdom — the city of Ayodhyā and its dominions. The remaining 99 sons received shares of the outlying territories. But **Bāhubali** — the second son, a giant of strength and a peerless warrior — refused to accept subordination to his elder brother.

> "Why should I bow to Bharata? Am I not his equal in strength, in courage, in wisdom?" — Bāhubali's attitude, as conveyed in the *Ādīpurāṇa*

When Bharata attempted to assert his authority as *cakravartin* (universal emperor), Bāhubali and his 98 other brothers resisted. A great war erupted — the legendary **"War of the Brothers"** that pitted the sons of Ṛṣabha against each other.

### The Three Contests

Rather than a full-scale war that would devastate the earth, the brothers agreed to settle the dispute through three ritual contests between Bharata and Bāhubali alone:

| Contest | Description | Outcome |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Dṛṣṭi-yuddha (Gaze Battle)** | The two brothers stood motionless and stared at each other, attempting to overpower the other with sheer force of will | Bāhubali's gaze did not waver; Bharata was defeated |
| **Jala-yuddha (Water Battle)** | They waded into a river and fought with water-throwing — a contest of stamina and presence | Bāhubali overwhelmed Bharata in the water |
| **Malla-yuddha (Wrestling / Single Combat)** | The final physical contest — the brothers fought hand-to-hand, and Bāhubali lifted Bharata above his head, ready to dash him to the ground | **Bāhubali won decisively** |

### The Moment of Renunciation

As Bāhubali stood holding his brother aloft, about to hurl him to his death, a **flash of realisation** struck him:

> "This is my brother — the same flesh and blood. We fought over... what? Earth? Stones? Power? And I am about to kill him for it. What has this pride done to me?"

In that instant, Bāhubali saw the utter **futility of violence**, the vanity of worldly dominion, and the deep bonds of karma that entangled him. He gently set Bharata down, renounced all claim to the kingdom, and walked away. He tore out his hair in five handfuls (the Jain *pañca-muṣṭi-loca* — the ritual act of renunciation) and became a wandering monk.

> "Having conquered the greatest enemy — his own pride — Bāhubali abandoned the kingdom, his brothers, and all his possessions, and entered the life of a homeless ascetic." — *Ādīpurāṇa*

### Twelve Years of Motionless Meditation

Bāhubali chose the most extreme form of Jain asceticism: **standing meditation** (*kāyotsarga* — the posture of "abandoning the body"). In this posture, the arms hang stiffly at the sides, the legs remain perfectly straight and unmoving, and the entire body becomes as still as a statue.

For **twelve years**, Bāhubali never moved:

- He stood day and night, through blazing sun and monsoon rain, never shifting his weight
- **Creepers and vines** grew up his legs, wrapping around his thighs and torso
- **Ants and termites** built anthills around his feet, rising up to his knees
- **Snakes** coiled around his ankles
- The **sun and elements** weathered his skin; his hair and nails grew wild
- He remained **absolutely motionless**, absorbed in meditation, unaware of his body

| Detail | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Duration** | 12 years (or 12 months in some variant traditions) |
| **Posture** | *Kāyotsarga* — standing perfectly straight, arms at sides, eyes fixed on the tip of the nose |
| **Food** | Complete fasting for extended periods; minimal intake of water and nourishment |
| **Vines** | Creepers (*latā*) grew up his legs (often depicted as flowering vines) |
| **Anthills** | *Valmīka* — termite mounds formed around his feet and lower legs |
| **State** | Complete absorption in the self (*svātma-dhyāna*), unaware of external conditions |

### The Intervention of Brāhmī

Bāhubali's sister **Brāhmī** (or **Brahmanī**) — one of Ṛṣabha's two daughters — saw her brother's condition. He had been standing so long that the anthills and vines had encrusted his body, and he was on the verge of death, yet he had not yet attained enlightenment.

Brāhmī, learned in spiritual matters (her name is traditionally linked to the invention of the *Brāhmī* script), understood the problem: Bāhubali's **ego** — the subtle pride that he was "the one who had renounced" — still clung to him. The very extremity of his asceticism had become a source of subtle self-attachment.

She approached him and spoke with love and clarity. According to the *Ādīpurāṇa*, she said something like:

> "Brother, why do you cling to this body? You have fought and conquered your enemies — but have you conquered the enemy within? The pride that says 'I am the greatest ascetic' — that is the last chain."

Her words struck Bāhubali like a thunderbolt. He realised that even his renunciation had become an object of pride. In that instant of profound insight, the last vestiges of karmic attachment dissolved. He attained **Kevala Jñāna** (perfect knowledge / omniscience) — standing exactly as he was, covered in vines and anthills, without moving.

### After Enlightenment

As a **Kevalin** (one who possesses *kevala jñāna*), Bāhubali possessed:
- Perfect knowledge of all substances, times, and modes
- Complete freedom from the four *ghātiyā karmas* (obscuring karmas)
- The ability to preach (though Jain tradition says he taught only through silence, not through discourse — hence a "silent Kevalin")

Unlike his father Ṛṣabha, who founded a *tīrtha* (a fourfold order of monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen), Bāhubali did not establish a teaching community. He is therefore classed as a **non-Tīrthaṅkara Kevalin** — fully enlightened, but not a "ford-maker." He attained final liberation (*mokṣa*) on Mount Aṣṭāpada (Mount Kailāsa).

## The Monolithic Statue at Shravanabelagola

### Overview

The **57-foot (17.4 m) monolithic statue of Bāhubali (Gommateshvara)** at Shravanabelagola, Karnataka, is the most celebrated Jain monument in the world and the **tallest monolithic statue in India**.

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Location** | Śravaṇa Beḷagoḷa (Kannada: "the white pond of the ascetic"), Hassan district, Karnataka |
| **Height** | 57 ft (17.4 m) — carved from a single block of granite |
| **Sculptor** | Unknown — commissioned by **Cāmuṇḍarāya**, a Digambara Jain general of the Gaṅga dynasty |
| **Year** | 983 CE (consecrated), took approximately 10–15 years to carve in situ |
| **Material** | Single block of fine-grained white granite |
| **Hill** | Vindhyagiri Hill (also called Doddabetta), overlooking the town |
| **Style** | Digambara Jain iconography — nude, standing in *kāyotsarga*, arms at sides |
| **Facial expression** | Serene, inward-gazing, with a faint smile (*ardha-hāsya*) |
| **Hair** | Curling locks falling to the shoulders (not shaven — a distinctive Digambara treatment for non-Tīrthaṅkara Kevalins) |
| **Vines & Anthills** | Carved on the legs — permanent iconographic reference to his 12-year meditation |
| **Inscription** | A Kannada inscription at the base dates the installation to 983 CE and names Cāmuṇḍarāya as the patron |
| **Orientation** | Faces north, towards the reservoir (the "white pond") |

### How It Was Made

The statue was carved from a **single outcrop of granite** on the summit of Vindhyagiri Hill. Rather than being quarried and moved, the rock was carved *in situ*: the hilltop was flattened, the statue shaped from the living rock, and the excess stone removed. The technique is similar to that used for the great rock-cut monuments of the Deccan (Ellora, Māmallapuram).

- **Step 1**: The natural granite outcrop was selected for its size and quality
- **Step 2**: The surrounding rock was excavated and cut away, leaving the statue as a central mass
- **Step 3**: The sculptors worked from top to bottom, carving the figure with chisels and hammers
- **Step 4**: The final polishing was done with abrasives (sand, emery powder)
- **Step 5**: The statue was consecrated in a grand ceremony in 983 CE

The sheer scale — a 57-foot standing figure from a single stone, with perfect proportions — is considered a **miracle of medieval Indian engineering**. No contemporary parallels of this scale exist in India outside of Jain patronage.

### The Mahamastakabhisheka Festival

The **Mahamastakabhisheka** ("great head-anointing") is the ritual consecration of the Bāhubali statue, performed once every **12 years**. It is one of the largest Jain festivals in the world, drawing hundreds of thousands of pilgrims and spectators.

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| **Frequency** | Every 12 years (next occurred in 2018; next scheduled ~2030) |
| **Ritual** | The statue's head and body are bathed in 1,008 pots of sacred substances |
| **Anointing substances** | Water, milk, coconut water, sugarcane juice, sandalwood paste, turmeric, vermilion, flower petals, and precious offerings |
| **The 1,008 Pots** | 1,008 *kalaśas* (auspicious pots) are carried in procession up the hill |
| **Scaffolding** | A massive bamboo scaffolding is erected around the statue — covering the entire 57-foot height |
| **Priests** | Digambara Jain *ācāryas* and *munis* perform the rites from the scaffolding |
| **Procession** | Begins at the Chandragiri hill temple complex, proceeds through the town, and climbs Vindhyagiri |
| **Duration** | Multiple days of rituals, culminating in the *abhisheka* (pouring) itself |
| **Significance** | Symbolises the washing away of karma, the renewal of vows, and the celebration of Bāhubali's enlightenment |
| **First recorded** | The 12-year cycle was established by Cāmuṇḍarāya himself after the statue's consecration in 983 CE |

> *"The pouring of the 1,008 pots over the silent, unblinking figure — standing as he has for a millennium — is a meditation on time itself. The statue remains still; the waters pour; the centuries pass."*

## Bāhubali in Jain Art and Iconography

### Distinguishing Features

Bāhubali's iconography is distinct from that of a Tīrthaṅkara:

| Feature | Bāhubali | Tīrthaṅkara (e.g., Ṛṣabha, Mahāvīra) |
|---------|----------|----------------------------------------|
| **Posture** | Always standing (*kāyotsarga*), never seated | Both standing and seated |
| **Clothing** | Nude (Digambara) — no robes whatsoever | Nude (Digambara) or with minimal cloth (Śvetāmbara) |
| **Hair** | Long, curling locks falling to the shoulders | Usually shaven head or close-cropped (except Ṛṣabha who has matted locks) |
| **Legs** | Vines and anthills carved on the legs | Clean legs (no foliage or earth) |
| **Emblem** | Serpent or coiled cobra at the base | The *lāñchana* (identifying symbol) of that Tīrthaṅkara |
| **Eyes** | Open, gazing forward (focused on the tip of the nose) | Half-closed or downcast in meditation |
| **Facial hair** | None — beardless | Beardless (except certain later Digambara depictions) |
| **Yakṣa / Yakṣī** | Sometimes attended by the yakṣa **Jvālāmālinī** and yakṣī **Kuṣmāṇḍinī** | Each Tīrthaṅkara has fixed yakṣa/yakṣī attendants |

### The Shravanabelagola Depiction

The canonical image at Shravanabelagola is the **definitive Bāhubali icon**:

- **Monolithic granite** — single stone, no joints or additions
- **Nude** — Digambara tradition (sky-clad), representing total non-attachment
- **Kāyotsarga posture** — arms hanging straight down, fingers slightly curved, thumbs touching the thighs
- **Vines** — twining up both legs from the ankles to the mid-thigh
- **Anthills** — at the base of the feet, with snakes emerging from them
- **Face** — broad, serene, with a meditative half-smile; eyes wide but inward
- **Hair** — carved in tight spirals, falling in symmetrical locks
- **Proportions** — The body follows the *daśa-tāla* (ten-face-length) canon of Jain iconometric texts

## Comparison: Bāhubali and Similar Figures of Extreme Asceticism / Renunciation

Bāhubali's narrative — a warrior-prince who renounces at the height of victory, then undergoes extreme motionless asceticism — has parallels across Jain, Buddhist, Hindu, and other traditions.

| Aspect | **Bāhubali** (Jain) | **Ṛṣabha / Ādinātha** (Jain) | **Mahāvīra** (Jain) | **The Buddha** (Buddhist) | **Śiva as Yogī** (Hindu) | **St. Simeon Stylites** (Christian) |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------------|---------------------|--------------------------|--------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Tradition** | Jain (Digambara & Śvetāmbara) | Jain (both sects) | Jain (both sects) | Buddhism | Hinduism (Śaiva) | Syriac Christianity |
| **Background** | Warrior prince — second son of a king-conquering sage | King — first Tīrthaṅkara, founder of civilisation | Prince of the Jñātṛka clan | Prince Siddhārtha of the Śākya clan | Theological — Śiva is the archetypal ascetic deity | Shepherd; later monk in Syria |
| **Catalyst for renunciation** | Defeated his brother in combat, then felt disgust at violence | Saw a dancer and realised the vanity of worldly power | Witnessed old age, sickness, death; renounced at 30 | Witnessed old age, sickness, death, and an ascetic; renounced at 29 | No personal renunciation story — Śiva *is* the eternal ascetic | Reading the Beatitudes; felt called to radical separation from the world |
| **Form of asceticism** | *Kāyotsarga* — standing motionless for 12 years | *Kāyotsarga* — standing under a banyan tree, vines growing over him | 12 years of extreme fasting, exposure, and meditation in cemeteries | 6 years of extreme fasting and self-mortification (before rejecting it as the Middle Way) | Deep meditation in the Himālayas (Gaṅgā on head, moon on crown) | 37 years on a **pillar** (stylite) — first 9 ft, then 60 ft; never descended |
| **Duration** | 12 years | 1,000 years | 12 years | 6 years | Eternity (mythic) | 37 years (c. 422–459 CE) |
| **Physical effects** | Vines grew over legs; anthills formed at feet; snakes coiled around ankles | Vines and plants grew over his body | Endured beatings, insect bites, starvation; body wasted | Near-starved, emaciated — "his spine became like a cord" (Majjhima Nikāya) | Covered in ash — the "blue-throated" deity (from drinking poison) | Rope burns, ulcers, gangrene in his foot — permanently standing, could not lie down |
| **Did he move?** | **No** — completely motionless for 12 years | **No** — motionless in kāyotsarga | Moved as a wandering ascetic (not a stationary penance) | Moved — wandering, sitting, walking | Mythic — stillness in meditation | Could stand, lean on a rail, but never sat or lay down |

| **Attained enlightenment?** | Yes — *kevala jñāna* | Yes — *kevala jñāna* | Yes — *kevala jñāna* | Yes — *Bodhi* (Awakening) | Yes — Śiva is eternally enlightened | Not in the same sense; died in sanctity |
| **Posture in iconography** | Standing (*kāyotsarga*) with vines + anthills | Standing (*kāyotsarga*) or seated | Standing (*kāyotsarga*) or seated (*padmāsana*) | Seated (*padmāsana*) — earth-touching gesture (*bhūmi-sparśa mudrā*) | Seated (*padmāsana*), or standing, crowned, with serpent, trident | Standing on a pillar (rarely depicted in Eastern icons; more in modern Western art) |
| **Statue / monument** | **57-ft monolith** at Shravanabelagola — tallest in India | Temples & bronzes — no single dominant monument | Temples throughout India — no single dominant monument | **Bamiyan Buddhas** (destroyed 2001), **Leshan Giant Buddha**, etc. | **Elephanta**, **Natarāja** at Chidambaram | No pillar itself survives, but stylite columns appear in Syriac iconography |
| **Legacy** | Symbol of renunciation of pride; model for the Digambara ideal of *ekānta* (solitary) asceticism | Founder of the Jain *tīrtha* — the model Tīrthaṅkara | The last Tīrthaṅkara — the face of Jainism to the world | Founder of Buddhism — the Awakened One | The lord of yoga (*Yogēśvara*) — the divine ascetic | The first and most famous stylite saint; established a new genre of Christian asceticism |

## Significance in Jain Tradition

### The Model of Ekānta Sādhana (Solitary Asceticism)

Bāhubali represents a distinct ideal within Jain spirituality: the **solitary practitioner** who attains enlightenment entirely through personal effort, without teaching others, without establishing a community, without any institutional role. He is the archetype of *ekānta-sādhana* — "single-pointed spiritual practice."

This ideal is particularly emphasised in the **Digambara** tradition, which values the *muni* (monk) who stands alone. The Shravanabelagola statue is a Digambara pilgrimage site above all, and the Mahamastakabhisheka is a Digambara festival.

### The Defeat of Pride

Bāhubali's story is fundamentally about **the renunciation of pride** (*māna* / *abhimāna*). He conquered Bharata in every contest — but his real enemy was his own ego. Even after renouncing the kingdom, the subtle pride of "I am the greatest ascetic" clung to him until Brāhmī's words shattered it.

In Jain ethics, *māna* (pride / conceit) is one of the four *kaṣāyas* (passions) that bind the soul to karma:
1. **Krodha** — Anger
2. **Māna** — Pride / Ego
3. **Māyā** — Deceit
4. **Lobha** — Greed

Bāhubali's narrative is a dramatic illustration of the subtlety of *māna* — it can survive even the most extreme asceticism. The last chain is always the chain of "I am the one who has broken the chains."

### The Warrior-Ascetic Archetype

Bāhubali embodies a recurring figure in Indian religious imagination: the **kṣatriya (warrior) who is also a yogī**. The warrior's qualities — fearlessness, endurance, single-mindedness, and the capacity for extreme discipline — are turned inward. The same man who could defeat any enemy in battle uses that same capacity to defeat his own inner enemies (passion, attachment, ego).

This archetype appears in:
- **Mahāvīra** — a kṣatriya prince who became the greatest Jain ascetic
- **The Buddha** — a kṣatriya prince who renounced
- **Bhīma** and **Arjuna** in the Mahābhārata — warriors who also had spiritual dimensions
- **Kṛṣṇa** — the divine charioteer who is also a kṣatriya

### Bāhubali as a Non-Tīrthaṅkara Kevalin

Bāhubali's status as a **non-Tīrthaṅkara Kevalin** is theologically significant:

- He attained the same state of omniscience (*kevala jñāna*) as his father Ṛṣabha
- But he did **not** establish a *tīrtha* — he did not become a spiritual teacher or organise a community
- This distinguishes him from the 24 Tīrthaṅkaras and from other Kevalins (like his father's chief disciples, the *gaṇadharas*)
- He represents the Jain doctrine that **enlightenment is available to all** who practice with sufficient intensity — not only those who are destined to become "ford-makers"
- His silent teaching by example (rather than by discourse) is a reminder that the highest truth is ultimately ineffable
