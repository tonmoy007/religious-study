# Gurū Rām Dās (Rām Dās)

> Born: 24 September 1534 CE, Chuna Mandi, Lahore (Punjab, now Pakistan)
> Died: 1 September 1581 CE, Goindval, Punjab (now India)
> Title: Fourth Sikh Guru (1574–1581); founder of Rāmdāspur (Amritsar); excavator of the Amritsar tank; architect of the Harimandir Sāhib foundation
> Birth Name: Jethā ("first-born"); of the Sodhi sub-caste of Khatrīs
> Era: Mughal Empire under Akbar (1556–1605)

**Gurū Rām Dās** (1534–1581 CE) was the fourth of the ten Sikh Gurus and the first Guru to inherit the Guruship through marriage into the Guru family. Born Jethā in Lahore into a Sodhi Khatrī family — a sub-caste distinct from the Bedī Khatrī lineage of Gurū Nānak — he came to Goindval as a young man, encountered Gurū Amar Dās, and became one of his most devoted disciples. His unflinching service — the famous story of the platform of bricks he built despite repeated demolition by a jealous rival — so impressed the third Guru that he gave Jethā his daughter Bibi Bhānī in marriage in 1554 and, two decades later, appointed him Guru over his own sons. Rām Dās's short seven-year Guruship (1574–1581) was remarkably productive: he founded the city of Rāmdāspur (later Amritsar), excavated the sacred tank (the Amritsar — "Pool of Nectar"), initiated the construction of the Harimandir Sāhib (the Golden Temple), composed 679 hymns of deeply nuptial spirituality, and institutionalised the systems of offerings and regional congregations that gave the Sikh community its first organisational infrastructure. He died in Goindval in 1581, passing the Guruship to his youngest son, Gurū Arjan — the first time the office passed from father to son, establishing the Sodhi hereditary line that would hold the Guruship for its remaining six generations.

## Biography

| Event | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Birth** | 24 September 1534 CE in Chuna Mandi, Lahore (then part of the Lodi Sultanate, soon to become the Mughal Empire). Born into a Sodhi Khatrī family — traditionally merchants and administrators, the Sodhīs were a sub-caste of the Khatrīs distinct from the Bedīs of Gurū Nānak. His father was **Harī Dās** (a respectable but not wealthy shopkeeper) and his mother was **Anūp Devī** (or Dayā Kaur) |
| **Youth & Early Life** | Named **Jethā** ("first-born"). He received a basic education in Punjabi and some Persian. As a teenager, he worked as a vendor selling boiled chickpeas (chole) and later helped his father in the family shop. From childhood he showed a contemplative disposition and was drawn to the company of holy men. At around age 12, he heard stories of Gurū Amar Dās and felt an irresistible pull toward Goindval |
| **Meeting Gurū Amar Dās** | Arrived in Goindval as a young man (c. 1548–1550) and presented himself before Gurū Amar Dās. The Guru asked him what he wanted; Jethā replied, "I want nothing but to serve you." He was accepted as a disciple and given the duty of drawing water from the Beās River and fetching firewood for the Guru's langar — menial tasks he performed with exceptional humility and devotion |
| **The Platform of Bricks (Sākhī)** | The most famous episode from his early service: a jealous rival (identified in some traditions as Mohan, the Guru's son) kept dismantling a brick platform Jethā was building for the Guru's meditation. Each night the bricks were knocked down; each morning Jethā rebuilt them without complaint. When the Guru discovered this, he recognised Jethā's absolute humility and patience — a test that confirmed Jethā as worthy of the highest spiritual office |
| **Marriage to Bibi Bhānī** | 1554 CE (some sources give 1553). Gurū Amar Dās married his daughter **Bibi Bhānī** to Jethā in Goindval. The marriage was extraordinary in Indian social terms: the Guru gave his daughter to a disciple of modest means, rejecting wealthier proposals from established families. The union produced three sons: **Prithi Chand** (1558–1618), **Mahādev** (c. 1560–?), and **Arjan** (1563–1606). Bibi Bhānī is herself revered as a paragon of Sikh womanhood — resourceful, devout, and instrumental in the completion of the Amritsar tank |
| **Service as Disciple** | For over two decades (c. 1550–1574), Jethā lived in Goindval as a householder-disciple, managing the langar, supervising construction, and assisting Gurū Amar Dās in institutionalising the Sikh community. He refused all positions of privilege, insisting on serving alongside the humblest of the Guru's followers. His eldest son Prithi Chand was born in Goindval (1558); Mahādev and Arjan followed. During this period Gurū Amar Dās authorised Jethā to undertake preaching missions, including a journey to Lahore to establish a Sikh congregation there |
| **Installation as Fourth Guru** | 1 September 1574 (or late August 1574, depending on calendar). Gurū Amar Dās, at age 95, called his disciples together at Goindval and declared Jethā his successor — passing over his own sons Mohan, Mohri, and Datu. He renamed him **Rām Dās** ("servant of God," or specifically "servant of Rām — the all-pervading Lord"). The installation was opposed by the Guru's sons, particularly Mohan, who sealed himself in a room with the Guru's manuscripts and refused to hand them over — a conflict that foreshadowed the succession disputes of later decades |
| **Founding of Rāmdāspur (Amritsar)** | Shortly after becoming Guru, Rām Dās acquired a tract of land near the village of Tung (or Sultanpur Lodhi region) from the Mughal Emperor Akbar through the good offices of local zamīndārs. The land included a natural depression fed by a spring. Rām Dās named the new settlement **Rāmdāspur** ("the town of God's servant") and began laying out a planned town with a central tank, residential blocks (galleys), and a marketplace. This was the first deliberately planned Sikh urban centre |
| **Excavation of the Amritsar Tank** | The centrepiece of Rāmdāspur was the excavation of a large sacred tank. Tradition holds that Gurū Rām Dās personally supervised the digging and that his wife Bibi Bhānī and their sons worked alongside the labourers. The tank was called **Amritsar** ("Pool of Nectar") — a name already in local circulation, associated with a mythological pool of immortality. The water was believed to have healing properties. The tank was later enlarged and lined with brick by Gurū Arjan |
| **Construction of the Harimandir Sāhib Begun** | Gurū Rām Dās did not live to build the central shrine itself, but he laid its theological and physical foundation. He planned a temple in the middle of the Amritsar tank, accessible from all four sides — a design his son Gurū Arjan would realise beginning in 1588. Rām Dās also established the site as a pilgrimage destination, attracting merchants and settlers who transformed Rāmdāspur from a village into a town |
| **Composition of Hymns** | Gurū Rām Dās composed **679 hymns** (śabads) in **30 rāgas** (musical measures), the third-largest contribution by a Guru to the Guru Granth Sāhib after Gurū Arjan (2,218) and Gurū Nānak (974). His hymns are distinguished by their marital and nuptial imagery — the soul as bride longing for the divine Bridegroom. His most famous compositions include the **Ghoṛīāṁ** (marriage songs), the **Chhant** (lyrical verses of praise), and the **Āratī** — a hymn of adoration that is sung daily at the Harimandir Sāhib as part of the closing ritual (the *Sukhāsan* ceremony) |
| **Institutional Innovations** | (1) **Masand system** — Rām Dās established a rudimentary network of regional representatives (masands) who collected offerings, maintained discipline, and spread the Guru's message in distant towns. (2) **Bhungā system** — he organised the Langar at an unprecedented scale, feeding thousands daily at Goindval and Rāmdāspur. (3) **Urban planning** — his design for Rāmdāspur (with its tank, temple precinct, bazaar, and residential quarters) became the template for all subsequent Sikh holy cities |
| **Death** | 1 September 1581 CE in Goindval (some sources say Rāmdāspur), aged 46. He was suffering from a prolonged illness. As he lay dying, he summoned his three sons and, despite the protests of Prithi Chand (the eldest), designated his youngest son **Arjan** as his successor — echoing Nānak's precedent of passing over the eldest in favour of the spiritually fit. His body was cremated near Goindval. A memorial platform (chābar) marks the site to this day |

## Founding of Amritsar (Rāmdāspur)

The transformation of a swampy depression in the central Punjab into the holiest city of the Sikhs is Gurū Rām Dās's most visible legacy. The land — originally a cluster of villages (Tung, Gilvālī, Sultanwind, Gumtāla, and Chāṅgā) — was acquired through a combination of gift, purchase, and imperial grant. The Mughal Emperor Akbar, through his Hindu minister Todar Mal and the local zamīndārs, is said to have facilitated the transaction after hearing of the Guru's reputation.

### The Urban Design

Rām Dās's plan for Rāmdāspur was remarkably modern for its time:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **The Tank (Sarovara)** | A large excavated reservoir fed by a natural spring, lined with brick and faced with stone. The tank measured roughly 150 metres square and was dug below the surrounding ground level so that rainwater would collect naturally |
| **The Causeway Site** | A central platform in the middle of the tank, designed to hold a future shrine — accessible by a single bridge from the west |
| **The Bazaar (Baṭhāk)** | A covered market street connecting the tank precinct to the main approach road, designed to attract both pilgrims and merchants |
| **Residential Blocks (Galleys)** | Planned neighbourhoods with houses for Sikh families, arranged around open courtyards and connected by shaded lanes |
| **The Langar Hall** | A large communal kitchen and dining hall, built to accommodate the growing number of pilgrims |
| **The Waterworks** | Wells, channels, and drainage systems that made the new town self-sufficient in water |

### The Name

The settlement was originally called **Rāmdāspur** — literally "the town of Rām Dās" (or "the servant of God's town"). The name **Amritsar** derived from the tank itself: *Amrit* means "nectar of immortality" and *Sar* means "pool." Over the following decades, the name of the tank came to apply to the entire settlement, and by the time of Gurū Arjan (1581–1606), the city was generally known as Amritsar. The official name remained Rāmdāspur well into the 19th century; the British colonial administration standardised "Amritsar" as the city's name.

### Economic and Demographic Impact

The founding of Rāmdāspur was a masterstroke of economic-religious institution-building. By creating a pilgrimage destination centred on a sacred tank, Rām Dās simultaneously:
- Established a permanent urban base for the Sikh community, reducing dependence on the goodwill of local rulers
- Created a pilgrimage economy that attracted merchants, artisans, and settlers from across the Punjab
- Provided a neutral, non-agricultural economic base for the Sikh community (who were increasingly urban rather than rural)
- Laid the foundation for the Harimandir Sāhib — which would become, under Gurū Arjan, the single most important Sikh shrine and a symbol of Sikh sovereignty

## The Sodhi Succession — The Four Descending Gurus

The first three Gurus of Sikhism — Nānak, Aṅgad, and Amar Dās — were not related by blood. Each Guru appointed his successor on the basis of merit, passing over his own sons. Beginning with Gurū Rām Dās, however, the Guruship passed into a hereditary line within the Sodhi sub-caste of the Khatrīs — a shift with profound implications for the Sikh tradition.

### The Transition

Rām Dās was the first Guru connected to his predecessor by marriage: he was the son-in-law of Gurū Amar Dās. His appointment therefore did not constitute a blood-hereditary succession in the strict sense, but it established the Sodhi family — and specifically the descendants of Rām Dās and Bibi Bhānī — as the locus of Guruship. When Rām Dās in turn appointed his own son Arjan as Guru (passing over his elder son Prithi Chand), the hereditary principle was fully activated.

### The Four Descending Gurus

From Gurū Rām Dās onward, the Guruship passed in an unbroken father-to-son (or, in one case, grandson-to-great-grandson) line spanning **four generations** of direct descent — a unique phenomenon in Sikh history:

| Generation | Guru | Relationship | Tenure |
|------------|------|-------------|--------|
| **1st** | [[Gurū Rām Dās]] | Founder of the Sodhi Guru line | 1574–1581 |
| **2nd** | [[Gurū Arjan]] | Son of Rām Dās | 1581–1606 |
| **3rd** | [[Gurū Har Gobind]] | Son of Arjan | 1606–1644 |
| **4th** | [[Gurū Har Rāi]] | Son of Har Gobind | 1644–1661 |
| **5th** | [[Gurū Har Kriśan]] | Son of Har Rāi | 1661–1664 |

The line continued through [[Gurū Tegh Bahādur]] (son of Har Gobind, born after Har Rāi) and [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]] (son of Tegh Bahādur), but the original "four descending Gurus" — Rām Dās → Arjan → Har Gobind → Har Rāi — represent the first and most consequential consolidation of hereditary authority in Sikh history.

### Significance and Tensions

The shift to hereditary succession brought both stability and conflict:
- **Stability**: The Guruship no longer depended on finding and testing a worthy successor from the wider community; it was embedded in a lineage that could be groomed from childhood.
- **Conflict**: Rival claimants within the Sodhi family — especially Prithi Chand (Rām Dās's eldest son, who set up a rival Guruship known as the Mīṇā or "mimic" sect) — created lasting internal divisions.
- **Theological paradox**: The hereditary principle appeared to contradict Nānak's emphatic meritocratic precedent. Sikh theology resolved this by asserting that the same divine light (*jot*) that illuminated Nānak passed from body to body through the Guru lineage — and that the Sodhi family had been chosen by God to carry that light, merit and heredity working together rather than in opposition.

## Compositions

Gurū Rām Dās contributed **679 hymns** to the Guru Granth Sāhib, arranged in 30 rāgas. His compositions are the work of a poet whose inner life was suffused with nuptial imagery — the soul as bride, God as the divine Bridegroom, and the spiritual path as a marriage of love and surrender.

### Major Compositions

| Composition | Type | Rāga | Location in Guru Granth Sāhib | Theme |
|-------------|------|------|-------------------------------|-------|
| **Ghoṛīāṁ** | Marriage songs (lit. "pony songs") | Multiple rāgas | Various sections | Sung during the pre-wedding rituals; celebrate the arrival of the divine Groom; the soul as a bride adorning herself for union with God |
| **Chhant** | Lyrical praise verses | Rāga Āsā, Rāga Gūjrī, Rāga Suhī | pp. 310–322; 443–446; 486–489 | Extended hymns of praise describing the soul's longing for the Beloved; use extended metaphor of the bride awaiting her husband |
| **Āratī** | Hymn of adoration | Rāga Dhanāsarī | p. 663 | A hymn of the *āratī* (lamp-waving) ritual — but spiritualised: the true offering is not lamps and incense but the heart's devotion. Sung daily at the Harimandir Sāhib during the Sukhāsan ceremony |
| **Vār in Rāga Mājh** | Ballad (heroic/instructional) | Rāga Mājh | pp. 136–150 | A long composition in the *vār* form, describing the qualities of the true Guru, the nature of divine grace, and the path to liberation |
| **Hymns on Marriage and Union** | Various śabads | Rāga Suhī, Rāga Bilāval, Rāga Rāmkalī | Throughout the Granth | The theological heart of Rām Dās's work: the soul's separation from (*biraha*) and union with (*milana*) the divine Beloved — expressed through the raw emotional register of human marriage, separation, longing, and reunion |

### Nuptial Theology

The most distinctive feature of Gurū Rām Dās's hymnody is its sustained use of the marriage metaphor to express the soul's relationship with God. This was not a conventional literary trope but a lived theology: Rām Dās experienced the spiritual life as a bride's longing for her husband, and he invited the Sikh community into the same intimacy. Key themes:

- **Biraha (Separation)**: The soul separated from God experiences an aching, almost unbearable longing — the bride waiting through the night for her husband's return.
- **Milana (Union)**: The moment of divine-grace-bestowed union is described as a wedding feast, with the Guru as the matchmaker and God as the Groom.
- **Suhāg (Blessed Wifehood)**: The soul that has attained union is the *suhāgan* — the blessed wife who dwells in constant joy in her husband's home.
- **The Guru as Friend and Matchmaker**: The Guru is the *sahāī* (helper) who prepares the soul-bride, adorns her with virtues, and leads her to the divine Groom.

This nuptial theology, while drawing on the broader bhakti tradition's use of the *viraha-bhāva* (the mood of separation), is applied with unusual psychological depth and emotional range in Rām Dās's hymns. His compositions are among the most emotionally intense in the Guru Granth Sāhib.

### Place in the Liturgy

Several of Rām Dās's hymns are integrated into the daily Sikh liturgy:
- The **Āratī** is recited during the *Sukhāsan* ceremony (the closing ritual of the day) at the Harimandir Sāhib, played over loudspeakers across the city of Amritsar every evening.
- The **Ghoṛīāṁ** are sung as part of the pre-wedding celebration (*maiyāṁ*) in traditional Sikh weddings, though the core wedding liturgy (the *Lāvāṁ*) is by Gurū Arjan.
- His **Chhant** in Rāga Āsā is part of the evening prayer (*Rahirās*).

## Legacy

### Amritsar — The Eternal City

The founding of Amritsar is Gurū Rām Dās's most palpable legacy. The city that began as Rāmdāspur is today the spiritual and cultural capital of the Sikh world — the Jerusalem, Mecca, and Vārānasī of Sikhism rolled into one. The Harimandir Sāhib (Golden Temple), built on the tank Rām Dās excavated, attracts over 100,000 visitors daily and has been the symbolic centre of Sikh resistance, governance, and identity for over four centuries. Every major institution of Sikhism — the Akāl Takht (the seat of Sikh temporal authority), the Shiromaṇī Gurdwārā Parbandhak Committee (SGPC, the Sikh parliamentary body), the Sikh Reference Library, the central Khalsa schools — is located in Amritsar. Rām Dās did not build the city we see today, but he chose the site, consecrated the water, and set in motion the urban and religious dynamics that made everything else possible.

### The Sodhi Gurū Lineage

By establishing the Guruship in his own family — and by passing it to his son Arjan rather than to an outsider — Rām Dās created the hereditary framework that would sustain the Sikh tradition through the turbulent 17th and 18th centuries. The Sodhi Gurus (Rām Dās through Gobind Siṅgh) navigated the community through Mughal persecution, internecine conflict, and the transformation from a pacifist congregation to a militant Khalsa. The hereditary principle, whatever its theological tensions with Nānak's meritocratic ideal, provided continuity and clarity of leadership in a period when a non-hereditary succession might easily have fragmented the community beyond repair.

### The Hymnody of the Soul-Bride

Gurū Rām Dās's 679 hymns — especially the Ghoṛīāṁ, Chhant, and Āratī — remain among the most frequently sung and emotionally resonant compositions in the Sikh repertoire. His nuptial theology gave the Sikh tradition a distinctive language for describing the soul's relationship with God — a language of intimate longing and joyful union that balances the more austere, philosophical registers of Gurū Nānak and Gurū Arjan. The daily Āratī at the Golden Temple, sung at twilight to the flicker of oil lamps reflected in the sacred tank, is Rām Dās's living voice — a moment of beauty that has continued without interruption for over 400 years.

### The Masand System

Though the masand system was later formalised by [[Gurū Arjan]] and ultimately abolished by [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]] (after it became corrupt), Rām Dās's initial institution of regional representatives was the first attempt to create a Sikh organisational structure beyond the personal presence of the Guru. It foreshadowed the later development of the *pañj takht* (five seats of authority) system and the modern Sikh organisational landscape of gurdwārā committees and regional bodies.

### In Sikh Memory

Gurū Rām Dās is remembered in the Sikh tradition primarily as the **builder of the foundation** — the Guru who chose the site and dug the tank that would become the centre of Sikh spiritual life. His epithet, **Rām Dās** ("servant of God"), is taken as a model of humility: he who calls himself a servant becomes the master. His marriage to Bibi Bhānī is celebrated as a model of Sikh wedded life — partnership-in-service, devotion, and institution-building. The city of Amritsar itself is his enduring monument, and the evening Āratī at the Harimandir Sāhib is his daily offering to the Divine, sung generation after generation.
