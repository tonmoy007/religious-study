# Franz Rosenzweig (פרנץ רוזנצווייג)

One of the most original Jewish philosophers of the modern period. Rosenzweig's magnum opus, *The Star of Redemption*, constructed a systematic Jewish existentialist philosophy that reimagined creation, revelation, and redemption as the fundamental structures of reality. His brief, brilliant career was cut short by a degenerative neurological disease.

## Key Contributions

- ***The Star of Redemption* (Der Stern der Erlösung, 1921):** A philosophical system built around the three pillars of Creation, Revelation, and Redemption — corresponding to God, the human self, and the world. Rosenzweig argues that the old philosophical aspiration to know the All is a delusion; real knowledge begins with fear of death and the irreducible particulars of existence. Judaism and Christianity are two complementary covenants, each with a distinct role in the redemptive process.
- **Translation of the Tanakh with [[Martin Buber]]:** Despite his crippling illness (amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, ALS), Rosenzweig collaborated with Buber on the German translation of the Hebrew Bible. His contributions shaped the translation's linguistic philosophy, aiming to make the Hebrew heard through the German.
- **Founding the Freies Jüdisches Lehrhaus (Free Jewish House of Learning, 1920):** An innovative adult education institute in Frankfurt am Main where Jews of all backgrounds — secular, religious, assimilated — could engage with Jewish texts and ideas in a non-coercive, open environment. It became a model for modern Jewish study and included figures like [[Gershom Scholem]], Erich Fromm, and Leo Strauss as teachers.
- **The New Thinking (Das neue Denken, 1925):** An essay outlining his philosophical method — a turn from idealist abstraction toward concrete, linguistic, relational thinking rooted in speech and dialogue. This "speech-thinking" (Sprachdenken) anticipates much later postmodern and dialogical philosophy.
- **Critique of Hegel:** Rosenzweig's early work *Hegel and the State* (1920, based on his doctoral dissertation) was a magisterial study of Hegel's political philosophy. His break from Hegelian idealism was the intellectual crucible out of which *The Star* emerged.

## Life & Legacy

- Born to an assimilated Jewish family in Kassel; he came close to converting to Christianity before a transformative experience at a Yom Kippur service in 1913
- Served in the German army on the Balkan front in World War I; wrote much of *The Star of Redemption* on military postcards sent home
- Diagnosed with ALS in 1922; he continued to write and teach with extraordinary determination, using an array of devices and the help of his wife Edith
- His thought deeply influenced later Jewish philosophers including [[Rabbi Joseph B. Soloveitchik]] (whose existentialist dimension owes much to Rosenzweig) and Emmanuel Levinas
- Died at age 42 in Frankfurt; his legacy as a philosopher of dialogue and existential commitment endures
