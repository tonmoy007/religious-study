# Elijah & Ilyas — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

Elijah — *Ilyās* in Arabic — is the **fiery prophet of Mt. Carmel** in the Bible and the **righteous herald of God's oneness** in the Qur'an. In the Tanakh he is the greatest miracle-working prophet since Moses — raising the dead, calling down fire, and ascending to heaven in a whirlwind. In the Qur'an he appears in a single brief passage as one in the chain of prophets who called their people to worship God alone. A **genealogical** connection; closely linked to [[John the Baptist and Yahya - A Figure Traced]] (whom Jesus identified as Elijah returned), [[Elisha and Al-Yasa - A Figure Traced]], and [[Moses and Musa - A Figure Traced]].

## The shared figure: a prophet who confronted idolatry

Both scriptures agree on Elijah's core message: he called the people of Israel to abandon Baal-worship and return to the worship of the one God.

> [!quote] 1 Kings 18 — the challenge on Mount Carmel
> **18:21** — "And Elijah came unto all the people, and said, How long halt ye between two opinions? if the **LORD be God, follow him: but if Baal, then follow him.** "
> **18:24** — "The God that answereth by fire, let him be God."
> **18:38–39** — "Then the **fire of the LORD fell**, and consumed the burnt sacrifice… And when all the people saw it, they fell on their faces: and they said, **The LORD, he is the God; the LORD, he is the God.** "

> [!quote] Qur'an — the call to righteousness
> **As-Saaffat 37:123–128** — "And lo! **Elias was verily of those sent (to warn)**. When he said unto his folk: Will ye not ward off (evil)? **Cry ye unto Baal and forsake the best of Creators, Allah, your Lord and Lord of your fathers?** But they denied him, so they surely will be haled forth (to the doom)."

Both texts place him in conflict with **Baal-worship** — a rare case of the Qur'an naming a specific pagan deity (Baal) by name, confirming the shared historical memory.

## Where the Bible's account is vastly richer

The Bible devotes **two full books** (1 Kings 17–19, 2 Kings 1–2) to Elijah, making his story one of the most vivid in the Old Testament:

| Episode | Scripture | Detail |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| **Fed by ravens** | 1 Kings 17:2–6 | Ravens bring him bread and meat by the brook Cherith |
| **Raises the widow's son** | 1 Kings 17:17–24 | His first resurrection miracle |
| **The jar of oil** | 1 Kings 17:8–16 | The widow's supply never runs out |
| **Mt. Carmel contest** | 1 Kings 18:20–40 | Fire from heaven, 450 prophets of Baal slain |
| **Still, small voice** | 1 Kings 19:9–18 | God speaks not in wind/fire but a "still small voice" |
| **Elisha succeeds him** | 1 Kings 19:19–21 | Elijah casts his mantle on Elisha |
| **Ascension** | 2 Kings 2:1–14 | Taken to heaven in a chariot of fire |

The Qur'an compresses this entire prophetic career into **six verses** (37:123–128), mentioning only the call to abandon Baal and the people's denial. No ravens, no fire from heaven, no ascension.

## The unique Qur'anic mention

Beyond the Baal confrontation, the Qur'an also names Ilyās in the prophetic genealogy of [[006 Al-An'aam|Al-An'am 6:85]]:

> [!quote] Al-An'am 6:85 — in the roll of prophets
> "And **Zachariah and John and Jesus and Elias** — each was of the righteous."

Here Ilyās is listed calmly among the prophets — not as the stormy miracle-worker of Kings, but as one righteous figure in a chain stretching from Abraham to Jesus.

## The New Testament: Elijah as the returning forerunner

The Bible adds an entire second layer to Elijah's story. The prophet **Malachi** foretold his return (Malachi 4:5–6), and the Gospels identify **John the Baptist** as Elijah *redivivus* — the one who would "prepare the way of the Lord." Elijah also appears at the **Transfiguration** of Jesus alongside Moses (Matthew 17:1–8, Mark 9:2–8, Luke 9:28–36).

> [!quote] Malachi 4:5 — the prophecy
> "Behold, I will send you **Elijah the prophet before the coming of the great and dreadful day of the LORD**."

> [!quote] Matthew 17:10–12 — Jesus identifies John as Elijah
> "Elias **truly shall first come**, and restore all things. But I say unto you, That **Elias is come already**, and they knew him not… Then the disciples understood that he spake unto them of John the Baptist."

The Qur'an does not make this identification — John/Yaḥyā is a prophet in his own right (covered in [[John the Baptist and Yahya - A Figure Traced]]) — but the *function* of a herald preparing the way resonates across both traditions.

## What it means

Elijah/Ilyās is a study in **scale**: the Bible's most dramatic prophetic career (miracles, fire, ascension) versus the Qur'an's brief, dignified mention as one who "was of those sent to warn." Both agree on his essence — the prophet who confronted Baal-worship and called his people back to the one God. The Bible's Elijah has an **afterlife** (returning as John the Baptist, appearing at the Transfiguration); the Qur'an's Ilyās rests quietly in the prophetic chain, remembered for his message rather than his miracles.
