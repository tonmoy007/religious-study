# Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah (רבי נחוניה בן הקנה)

A Tanna of the first generation who was active during and after the Second Temple period. Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah is best known for the prayer he composed upon entering and leaving the study hall — preserved in the Mishnah (Berakhot 4:2) — and for being the teacher of Rabbi Yishmael, one of the great sages of the second generation. His name, "ben HaKanah," likely indicates descent from a wealthy or priestly family, though the precise meaning is debated.

Rabbi Nechunya was among the older contemporaries of Rabbi Eliezer ben Hyrcanus and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananyah, bridging the first and second generations of Tannaim. He is less frequently quoted in the Mishnah than his student Rabbi Yishmael, but his influence is substantial. The midrashic work *Sifre* (Numbers 12) records that he was the teacher who transmitted the method of the "Thirteen Principles of Exegesis" to Rabbi Yishmael — though these are traditionally attributed to Yishmael himself.

## Key Contributions

- **Prayer Upon Entering and Leaving the Study Hall:** The Mishnah (Berakhot 4:2) records a short but profound prayer composed by Nechunya ben HaKanah: "May it be Your will, Lord my God, that no offence occur through me, that I not err in a matter of law and that my colleagues rejoice in me." Upon leaving, he prayed that he not be led into error or shame. This prayer established the study hall as a sacred space in rabbinic Judaism.
- **Teacher of Rabbi Yishmael:** He was the primary teacher of Rabbi Yishmael, who later developed the Thirteen Principles of Scriptural Interpretation (שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִדּוֹת) — the foundational method of rabbinic exegesis.
- **Mystical Association:** In later Jewish tradition, Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah is sometimes associated with the *Sefer ha-Bahir* — one of the earliest works of Kabbalah — though the historical connection is tenuous. The Bahir attributes its first teaching to him, making him a revered figure in Jewish mystical tradition.
- **Legal Traditions:** His rulings on the laws of vows and the laws of purity are preserved in the Mishnah and Tosefta, and he appears in several key Talmudic discussions about the Temple service.

## In Jewish Tradition

- The Talmud (Berakhot 28b) expands on his prayer, noting that when Rabbi Nechunya left the study hall, he would bless God for "the portion of our ancestors" — the sages who transmitted the Torah
- His teaching method emphasised the connection between legal study and ethical conduct, a theme inherited by his student Rabbi Yishmael
- He is cited in Pirkei Avot (3:6) indirectly, through the principle that "one who takes upon himself the yoke of Torah is relieved of the yoke of worldly cares"
- In the Zohar and other Kabbalistic works, Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah is regarded as a master of esoteric wisdom
