# Ben Zoma (בן זומא)

Shimon ben Zoma — known simply as **Ben Zoma** — was a Tanna of the second generation who is remembered for his profound wisdom, his mystical speculations, and his famous series of aphorisms in Pirkei Avot defining who is truly wise, mighty, rich, and honoured. Although he lived and studied among the sages of Yavneh, he never received formal rabbinic ordination (סמיכה), possibly because of his engagement with esoteric subjects. He died at a young age, and the Talmud suggests his mind was overwhelmed by the intensity of his mystical pursuits.

Ben Zoma was a student of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananyah and a contemporary of Rabbi Akiva and Ben Azzai. The Mishnah (Chagigah 2:1) groups him with Ben Azzai and Elisha ben Avuyah (the "Acher") as one of the four sages who entered the *Pardes* (פַּרְדֵּס) — the orchard of mystical speculation. Of the four, Ben Zoma "looked and died," while Ben Azzai "looked and was harmed," Elisha "cut down the plantings," and only Rabbi Akiva "entered in peace and left in peace."

## Key Contributions

- **The Four Who Entered the Pardes:** Ben Zoma is one of the four sages in the famous Talmudic story of mystical ascent (Chagigah 14b). He "looked and died" — interpreted as meaning that the overwhelming divine light was too much for his soul to bear while still alive.
- **Sayings in Pirkei Avot:** His celebrated aphorisms (Avot 4:1) are among the best-known in rabbinic literature:
  - "Who is wise? One who learns from every person."
  - "Who is mighty? One who conquers one's own inclinations."
  - "Who is rich? One who is happy with one's portion."
  - "Who is honoured? One who honours others."
- **Halakhic Teachings:** Despite lacking ordination, Ben Zoma's legal interpretations are cited in the Mishnah, particularly regarding the laws of Passover and Sabbath limits (עירובין).
- **Mystical Exegesis:** The Talmud records his interpretation of the creation narrative (Genesis 1), in which he engaged in deep speculation about the nature of the upper and lower waters at the time of creation.

## In Jewish Tradition

- The phrase "Ben Zoma is still outside" (שאין בן זומא מבחוץ) became proverbial — he was considered a sage of such stature that even without ordination his opinions carried weight
- Rabbi Yehoshua greeted him with respect despite his youth, saying: "Ben Zoma, from where do you bring these words?" — acknowledging his brilliance (Tosefta Berakhot 6:2)
- His early death is mourned in rabbinic literature as the loss of immense potential; the Sages said that had he lived, he would have been among the greatest of his generation
- He and Ben Azzai are often paired in rabbinic texts as examples of sages who were consumed by Torah and mystical study
