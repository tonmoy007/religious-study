# Samson

> The Nazirite with superhuman strength — Israel's most flawed judge — whose life was a series of compromised gifts and a single redemptive death.

---

## Biography

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| **Tribe** | Dan |
| **Status** | Nazirite (dedicated from birth — no wine, no razor, no dead bodies) |
| **Role** | Judge of Israel (20 years) |
| **Strength source** | His uncut hair — Nazirite vow |
| **Weakness** | Lust, anger, and a pattern of self-serving behavior |
| **Death** | Destroyed the temple of Dagon — killed himself and 3,000 Philistines |

---

## Narrative Arc

1. **The set-apart child** — announced by an angel to a barren woman (Judges 13)
2. **The unrestrained youth** — insists on marrying a Philistine woman, kills a lion, exploits his gift
3. **The betrayed husband** — wife given to another man, burns Philistine crops, kills 1,000 with a jawbone
4. **The fool in love** — visits a prostitute in Gaza, then falls for Delilah — who betrays him for silver
5. **The blind prisoner** — gouged eyes, grinding grain in a Philistine mill
6. **The final act** — prayer: "O Lord God, remember me" — pulls down the pillars

---

## Samson and Islamic Parallels

Samson does not appear in the Qur'an. Some scholars have drawn comparisons with:
- **Samiri** (the magician who made the calf) — both are defined by their actions rather than their faith
- Samson's final prayer — "Remember me" — echoes the Qur'anic pattern of prophets calling on God in extremity
