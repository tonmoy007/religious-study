# Hemacandra (Hemacandra Sūri)

> Born: 1089 CE, Dhandhuka (Gujarat, Chaulukya kingdom)
> Died: 1172 CE (aged 83)
> Title: The Jain Aristotle; the greatest Jain polymath; advisor to King Kumārapāla; author of the Yogaśāstra, the Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra, and the Siddhahemacandra grammar

Hemacandra (Hemacandra Sūri) is the single most versatile intellectual figure in Jain history — a Śvetāmbara Ācārya whose scholarly range spanned grammar, lexicography, poetics, prosody, logic, philosophy, yoga, history, politics, and ethics. He is best known as the royal preceptor (rājaguru) of **King Kumārapāla** of the Chaulukya dynasty (r. c. 1143–1172 CE) in Gujarat, whom he famously converted to Jainism — the last great royal conversion in Indian history. His literary output is staggering in both volume and quality: his **Siddhahemacandra** is considered one of the finest Sanskrit grammars ever composed, his **Yogaśāstra** is the definitive Śvetāmbara manual on Jain yoga, and his **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (Lives of the 63 Illustrious Men) is the master narrative of Jain history, weaving the biographies of all 24 Tīrthaṅkaras, 12 Cakravartins (universal emperors), 9 Vasudevas (Balarāma-like heroes), 9 Prativasudevas (their enemies), and 9 Baladevas into a single epic tapestry. He was called **Kālidāsa of Jains** for his literary grace and **the Jain Aristotle** for the encyclopedic scope of his scholarship. Tradition holds that the goddess Sarasvatī herself appeared to praise his grammar — a sign of his unmatched mastery of language.

## Biography

| Event | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Birth** | 1089 CE in Dhandhuka (modern Ahmedabad district, Gujarat). Born into a humble Śvetāmbara Jain merchant family. His birth name was **Cāṅgadeva** (or Cāṅgaka). His father was a merchant named **Chāca** (or Cāhaḍa), and his mother was **Pāhini** |
| **Early Education** | Showed prodigious intellectual gifts from childhood. He was sent to study under **Devacandra Sūri** (also known as Devabodhi or Devasūri), the head of the **Bṛhadgaccha** monastic lineage, who initiated him at age 8 (or 9) as a Śvetāmbara monk — giving him the name **Somadeva** (later Somacandra, and finally Hemacandra after becoming Sūri) |
| **Monastic Training** | Under Devacandra Sūri's guidance, Hemacandra mastered the Jain Āgamas, Prakrit and Apabhraṃśa literature, and the full Sanskrit curriculum — grammar, logic, poetics, philosophy. He was appointed as an Ācārya (Sūri) of the Bṛhadgaccha lineage at a young age, likely in his late teens or early twenties |
| **Encounter with King Jayasiṃha Siddharāja** | C. 1125–1135 CE. King **Jayasiṃha Siddharāja** (Jayasimha Siddharaja), the Chaulukya king who ruled Gujarat from 1094–1143 CE, was a Śaiva whose court included many Brahmin scholars. When Hemacandra visited the court, the king — sceptical of Jain learning — tested him on grammar. According to the traditional account, Hemacandra not only passed the test but composed a verse in flawless Sanskrit that so impressed the king that he began to favour Jains. However, Jayasiṃha never converted; he remained Śaiva |
| **Royal Preceptor to Kumārapāla** | The pivotal relationship of Hemacandra's life. When **Kumārapāla** (Jayasiṃha's nephew and successor) was in exile fleeing Jayasiṃha's suspicion, Hemacandra took him under his protection. After Kumārapāla ascended the throne c. 1143 CE, he appointed Hemacandra as his royal preceptor (rājaguru). Over the following decades, Hemacandra became the most powerful religious figure in Gujarat |
| **Conversion of King Kumārapāla** | The most famous event in Hemacandra's life — and the last mass-scale royal conversion to Jainism in Indian history. Hemacandra gradually instructed Kumārapāla in Jain doctrine, ethics, and yoga. The king eventually converted to Śvetāmbara Jainism and enforced Jain ethical reforms across his kingdom, including a ban on animal sacrifice and restrictions on alcohol and hunting. The conversion was not a single dramatic event but a gradual process spanning years — a testament to Hemacandra's patient pedagogy |
| **Composition of Major Works** | The bulk of Hemacandra's literary output dates to his years as Kumārapāla's preceptor (c. 1143–1172 CE). He produced his masterworks during this period — the Siddhahemacandra grammar (which according to tradition caused Sarasvatī to speak), the Yogaśāstra (written for Kumārapāla's spiritual practice), the Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra, the Deśīnāmamālā, and numerous other works on poetics, prosody, and logic |
| **Death** | 1172 CE (traditionally 26 December 1172, or the 7th day of the bright half of Pausha, Vikram Saṃvat 1228). He performed *sallekhanā* (the Jain death-rite of fasting unto death) at an advanced age of 83. King Kumārapāla died shortly thereafter — in some accounts, on the same day. Hemacandra's passing was mourned across Gujarat; his funeral was attended by thousands, including the king |

## Conversion of Kumārapāla: The Last Royal Conversion

The conversion of King Kumārapāla is the most consequential political event in medieval Jain history — the last instance of a major Indian king converting to Jainism and enforcing Jain law across his kingdom.

### Background

When **Jayasiṃha Siddharāja** died in 1143 CE, his nephew **Kumārapāla** succeeded to the throne after a period of exile and danger. Kumārapāla had been sheltered by Hemacandra during his exile, and the monk had protected him from assassination attempts by Jayasiṃha's agents. This created a bond of profound trust.

### The Gradual Conversion

Unlike the dramatic conversion stories of earlier figures (such as Haribhadra's debate-induced shamed conversion), Hemacandra's conversion of Kumārapāla was a **pedagogical process**:

| Stage | Activity | Content |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **1. Building trust** | Personal relationship; Hemacandra had saved Kumārapāla's life during exile | The king trusted Hemacandra implicitly before any conversion was attempted |
| **2. Intellectual foundation** | Hemacandra taught Kumārapāla the Yogaśāstra — step by step, as a living text | The king learned the theory of Jain karma, the nature of the self, and the path to liberation |
| **3. Gradual ethical reform** | Rather than demanding immediate conversion, Hemacandra encouraged the king to implement Jain ethical policies | Bans on animal sacrifice, prohibitions on hunting for sport, restrictions on alcohol consumption |
| **4. Formal initiation** | After years of preparation, Kumārapāla took the *sallekhanā* vow and formally adopted Jain practices | The king observed fasting, performed pūjā at Jain temples, and supported the Jain saṅgha with royal patronage |
| **5. The Jain Kingdom** | Gujarat under Kumārapāla became a de facto Jain state — not through forced conversion but through royal policy | Jain temples were built or restored; Jain scholars received court patronage; non-violence was enshrined in law |

### Significance

The conversion is remarkable for what it was **not**: it was not a conquest (as when Aśoka converted to Buddhism after war), not a dramatic personal crisis (as with Haribhadra), and not a political calculation (as with many royal conversions). It was the result of decades of patient intellectual and ethical cultivation — a model of how a philosopher-politician can shape a kingdom through persuasion rather than power.

The reforms Kumārapāla enacted under Hemacandra's influence — especially the ban on animal sacrifice — were revolutionary for 12th-century India. They represent one of the most concrete applications of Jain *ahiṃsā* (non-violence) to state policy in history.

## Major Works

Hemacandra's literary corpus is vast — traditional accounts credit him with over 3,500 works (a number that is almost certainly symbolic but indicates his legendary fecundity). The following are his most important surviving works, grouped by field.

### Yogaśāstra (The Science of Yoga)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Title meaning** | *Yoga* = "spiritual discipline"; *śāstra* = "treatise" or "science" |
| **Date** | Composed c. 1150 CE, during the early years of Kumārapāla's reign, for the king's personal spiritual practice |
| **Structure** | 12 chapters (*prakāśas*) containing approximately 1,100 Sanskrit verses, with Hemacandra's own auto-commentary (Svopajña-Ṭīkā) that is often more detailed than the root text |
| **Core thesis** | A comprehensive manual of Jain yoga for both householders and monks, integrating ethical conduct (śīla), philosophical understanding (jñāna), and meditative practice (dhyāna). The work presents yoga as the practical path to liberation, grounded in the Śvetāmbara understanding of karma |
| **Key content** | (1) Right faith and its types; (2) The nature of the self and non-self; (3) The five great vows (mahāvratas) and the twelve minor vows (aṇuvratas); (4) The six daily duties (āvaśyakas); (5) The eight types of karma and their operation; (6) Meditation (dhyāna) — its types, objects, and progressive stages; (7) The *bhāvanās* (reflections) that support spiritual practice; (8) The *guṇasthānas* (14 stages of spiritual development); (9) The nature of liberation; (10) The characteristics of the accomplished yogin |
| **Significance** | The Yogaśāstra is the **definitive Śvetāmbara text on Jain yoga**. While Haribhadra (8th c.) had written pioneering works on Jain yoga (Yogabindu, Yogadṛṣṭisamuccaya), Hemacandra's Yogaśāstra is more systematic, more practical, and better grounded in the full scope of Jain doctrine. It remains the standard reference for Śvetāmbara yogic practice to this day |
| **Key commentaries** | Hemacandra's own auto-commentary (Svopajña-Ṭīkā) is the primary commentary. Later commentators include Yaśovijaya (17th c.), who wrote a sub-commentary |

### Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra (Lives of the 63 Illustrious Men)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Title meaning** | *Triṣaṣṭi* = "63"; *śalākāpuruṣa* = "illustrious men" or "great beings" (literally "staff-men," beings who mark the ages); *caritra* = "life stories" |
| **Date** | Composed c. 1160–1172 CE, one of Hemacandra's later works |
| **Structure** | 10 *parvans* (books) containing approximately 35,000 Sanskrit verses (some recensions vary); one of the longest works of Sanskrit literature |
| **The 63 Illustrious Men** | The work covers the lives of all 24 Tīrthaṅkaras, 12 Cakravartins (universal emperors), 9 Vasudevas (Balarāma-type heroes), 9 Prativasudevas (their demonic counterparts), and 9 Baladevas (mild heroes associated with the Vasudevas). This framework — a staple of Jain Purāṇic literature — presents the entire sweep of Jain sacred history as an interconnected narrative |
| **Core content** | (1) The lives of the 24 Tīrthaṅkaras, from Ṛṣabha (1st) to Mahāvīra (24th); (2) The parallel lineages of universal emperors and semi-divine heroes who appear in each half of the cosmic time-cycle; (3) Extended treatment of Rāma and Kṛṣṇa as Jain figures — a deliberate Jain appropriation of Hindu epic heroes; (4) The story of Bharata (son of Ṛṣabha) and the naming of India (*Bhāratavarṣa*); (5) Cosmological and geographical descriptions of the Jain universe |
| **Significance** | The Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra is the **master narrative of Jain history** — the single text that integrates all major Jain sacred biographies into a unified chronological and cosmological framework. It is the Jain equivalent of the Purāṇas in Hinduism, providing a complete sacred history that spans the entire cosmic cycle. It is also a major work of Sanskrit literature, celebrated for its narrative elegance, psychological depth, and architectural complexity |
| **Influence** | The text became the standard source for Jain Purāṇic storytelling. It was later translated into Gujarati, Hindi, and other vernaculars. Its treatment of Rāma and Kṛṣṇa as Jain figures — righteous householders who eventually renounce and attain liberation — has deeply shaped the Jain understanding of these pan-Indian heroes |

### Siddhahemacandra (Siddhahemacandra Śabdānuśāsana) — The Grammar

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Title meaning** | *Siddha* = "perfected" or "accomplished"; *Hema* = "golden" (also Hemacandra's name); *śabdānuśāsana* = "instruction on words" |
| **Date** | Composed c. 1135–1145 CE (before the Yogaśāstra); possibly Hemacandra's first major work |
| **Structure** | A comprehensive Sanskrit grammar in 8 *adhyāyas* (chapters), modelled on the Aṣṭādhyāyī of Pāṇini but with significant innovations. It covers phonology (varṇa), morphology (rūpa), syntax (vākya), and semantics (artha) |
| **Core features** | (1) A simplified and regularised treatment of Sanskrit grammar that preserves Pāṇini's insights while eliminating many irregularities; (2) Extensive treatment of the *unādi* suffixes (a class Pāṇini left open-ended); (3) Inclusion of Prakrit and Apabhraṃśa grammatical forms — a unique feature reflecting Jain concern with multiple languages; (4) An auto-commentary (Svopajña) that explains the sūtras with examples |
| **The Sarasvatī Legend** | The most famous story about the grammar: when Hemacandra completed it, he invoked the goddess of learning, Sarasvatī, to test its completeness. The goddess appeared — silent. Hemacandra realised the grammar lacked one rule: the rule that Sarasvatī, being feminine in gender, should be referred to as "sā" (she), not "saḥ" (he). He added the missing rule, and the goddess spoke aloud in praise of his grammar. This story, still told in Jain tradition, signifies the grammar's perfection — it was so complete that even the goddess could find only one flaw, which was immediately corrected |
| **Significance** | The Siddhahemacandra is considered **one of the finest Sanskrit grammars ever composed** — second only to Pāṇini's Aṣṭādhyāyī in authority and arguably superior in pedagogical clarity. It remains a standard text for traditional Sanskrit education in Gujarat and Rajasthan |

### Deśīnāmamālā (The Garland of Local Words)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Title meaning** | *Deśī* = "local" (non-Sanskrit, vernacular); *nāma* = "word"; *mālā* = "garland" |
| **Structure** | A lexicographical work in Prakrit and Apabhraṃśa, listing and defining words of local (non-Sanskritic) origin |
| **Significance** | This is the **earliest and most important dictionary of Prakrit and Apabhraṃśa vocabulary**. It is an invaluable source for Middle Indo-Aryan linguistics, preserving thousands of words that would otherwise be lost. It demonstrates Hemacandra's linguistic range — he was not only a Sanskrit grammarian but a scholar of the vernacular languages in which Jain texts were composed |
| **Historical value** | Modern linguists rely on the Deśīnāmamālā as a primary source for reconstructing the lexical stock of medieval Indo-Aryan languages. It is one of the foundational texts of Prakrit and Apabhraṃśa lexicography |

### Other Major Works

| Work | Field | Description |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Kāvyānuśāsana** | Poetics | A comprehensive treatise on Sanskrit poetics, treating the nature of poetry (kāvya), figures of speech (alaṃkāra), poetic suggestion (dhvani), and the aesthetics of literary composition. Written in the tradition of Daṇḍin and Ānandavardhana but from a Jain perspective |
| **Chandonuśāsana** | Prosody | A manual on Sanskrit and Prakrit metres (chandas), cataloguing hundreds of metrical forms with rules for their construction and examples |
| **Abhidhānacintāmaṇi** | Lexicography | A Sanskrit thesaurus/dictionary, arranged by subject categories (nature, gods, humans, etc.) — a class of dictionary known as a *kośa*. It complements the Deśīnāmamālā by covering the Sanskrit lexical sphere |
| **Anekārthasaṅgraha** | Lexicography | A dictionary of homonyms (words with multiple meanings) in Sanskrit — an essential tool for poets and commentators |
| **Nighaṇṭuśeṣa** | Lexicography | A supplement to earlier Sanskrit lexicons, filling gaps left by Amarakośa and other standard works |
| **Pramāṇamīmāṃsā** | Logic / Epistemology | A work on Jain logic and epistemology, dealing with the theory of valid knowledge (pramāṇa) and the means by which the self can correctly understand reality |
| **Vītarāgastotra** | Devotional poetry | A hymn of praise to the passionless Jinas (vītarāga), written in elegant Sanskrit verse; a classic of Jain devotional literature |
| **Sthavirāvalī** | History | A lineage list of Jain teachers (ācāryas) from Mahāvīra to Hemacandra's own time — an invaluable source for Jain ecclesiastical history |
| **Upadeśamālā** | Ethics | A collection of moral and spiritual instructions in Prakrit verse, designed for laypeople and monastics |
| **Dvātriṃśad-daśaka** | Ethics / Devotion | Thirty-two groups of ten verses each, covering ethical duties, Jina attributes, and meditation topics |

## Significance: Hemacandra's Achievement

### The Jain Aristotle

The comparison between Hemacandra and Aristotle — first made by modern scholars — rests on genuine parallels:

| Domain | Hemacandra | Aristotle |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Grammar** | Siddhahemacandra — one of the great Sanskrit grammars, studied for a millennium | Categories and De Interpretatione — foundational works on language and logic |
| **Poetics** | Kāvyānuśāsana — comprehensive treatise on literary aesthetics | Poetics — the foundational work of Western literary theory |
| **Logic** | Pramāṇamīmāṃsā — Jain epistemology and logic | Organon — the foundational corpus of Western logic |
| **Ethics** | Yogaśāstra — practical manual of spiritual ethics | Nicomachean Ethics — systematisation of virtue ethics |
| **Politics** | Advised Kumārapāla; shaped state policy through Jain ethical principles | Tutored Alexander the Great; wrote Politics on ideal governance |
| **Biology / Natural history** | Cosmological descriptions in the Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra | Historia Animalium — systematic study of animal life |
| **Encyclopedic scope** | Mastered and wrote on nearly every field of knowledge in 12th-century India | Mastered and wrote on nearly every field of knowledge in 4th-century BCE Greece |
| **Relationship to power** | Royal preceptor who shaped a king's policy and conversion | Tutor to Alexander; his student conquered the known world |
| **Mode of thought** | Systematiser and synthesizer — took existing traditions and perfected them | Systematiser and synthesizer — took existing traditions and perfected them |
| **Posthumous reputation** | "The Jain Aristotle" — praised for range and depth | "The Philosopher" — the reference point for all subsequent Western thought |

### Hemacandra's Place in Jain Intellectual History

| Acharya | Field | Hemacandra's Relationship |
|---------|-------|--------------------------|
| **[[Umaswāmi]]** | Systematiser of Jain doctrine (Tattvārtha Sūtra) | Hemacandra presupposes Umaswāmi's framework; his Yogaśāstra applies it to spiritual practice |
| **[[Kundakunda]]** | Radical philosopher of the self (Samayasāra) | Hemacandra's Yogaśāstra internalises the inward turn Kundakunda pioneered, but in a Śvetāmbara framework |
| **[[Haribhadra]]** | Ecumenical philosopher and yoga theorist (Yogabindu, Yogadṛṣṭisamuccaya) | Hemacandra continues Haribhadra's project of systematising Jain yoga, but produces a more practical and systematic manual |
| **Hemacandra** | Polymath who integrated all strands of Jain learning | The culmination of the Śvetāmbara scholastic tradition |

### Innovation in Each Field

Hemacandra's achievement is remarkable because he was not merely a compiler but an innovator in every field he touched:

1. **Grammar**: The Siddhahemacandra regularised Pāṇini's system, made it pedagogically accessible, and was the first major grammar to systematically include Prakrit and Apabhraṃśa forms — a reflection of the Jain commitment to vernacular access.

2. **Yoga**: The Yogaśāstra is the first Śvetāmbara text to present a complete, step-by-step yogic path that integrates ethics, philosophy, and meditation in a single systematic framework. It remains the authoritative Śvetāmbara yoga manual.

3. **Sacred History**: The Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra is the most ambitious single-work narrative of Jain sacred history ever composed — a novelistic tapestry that makes the complex Jain cosmology emotionally and narratively accessible.

4. **Lexicography**: The Deśīnāmamālā is the foundational text of Prakrit-Apabhraṃśa lexicography, preserving a linguistic heritage that no other scholar of his era thought to document.

5. **Poetics**: The Kāvyānuśāsana adapts the Sanskrit poetic tradition — which was overwhelmingly Hindu — to a Jain worldview, creating a space for Jain literary aesthetics.

### What Makes Hemacandra Unique

Among this remarkable company, Hemacandra holds a distinctive position:

1. **He is the only Jain among them** — his work is rooted in the Jain worldview, with its distinctive commitments to non-violence, karma theory, anekānta, and the liberation of the individual self. His polymathy is not a secular or neutral intellectual project but a *Jain* project — the application of a supremely gifted mind to the service of the Dhamma.

2. **His range includes sacred history** — no other polymath on this list composed a narrative of sacred history on the scale of the Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra. This is a genre that requires not only literary skill but theological depth, narrative architecture, and the ability to weave multiple biographies into a coherent cosmic story.

3. **His work is both scholarly and practical** — the Yogaśāstra was written for a king to *use*; the grammar was composed for students to *learn from*. Unlike some polymaths whose work remained in the library, Hemacandra's was designed for application — and through Kumārapāla, it shaped the actual governance of a kingdom.

4. **He is a monk-polymath** — unlike Aristotle, Ibn Sīnā, or Leibniz (who were philosophers in courts or universities), Hemacandra composed his entire corpus as a Śvetāmbara monk who had taken vows of poverty, non-violence, and celibacy. His intellectual freedom operated within the constraints of monastic discipline — a remarkable demonstration that renunciation and encyclopedic scholarship are compatible.

5. **His influence on a living tradition continues** — the Yogaśāstra is still studied by Śvetāmbara monks; the Siddhahemacandra is still taught in traditional schools in Gujarat. He is not a historical curiosity but a living intellectual presence in Jain tradition.

### In Jain Philosophy

Hemacandra's place in the Jain philosophical tradition is that of the **great synthesizer**:

1. **Systematiser of Jain yoga**: He took the yogic tradition inherited from Haribhadra (Yogabindu, Yogadṛṣṭisamuccaya) and produced a comprehensive manual — the Yogaśāstra — that integrated ethics, philosophy, and meditation into a single systematic path. This remains the standard Śvetāmbara yoga text.

2. **Grammatical authority**: The Siddhahemacandra grammar is studied to this day as a standard text. Its pedagogical clarity, combined with its inclusion of Prakrit and Apabhraṃśa forms, makes it unique among Sanskrit grammars.

3. **Narrative theologian**: The Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra shaped Jain sacred history for centuries, providing the narrative framework through which educated Jains understood their tradition's place in cosmic history.

4. **Court philosopher-politician**: His relationship with Kumārapāla is the paradigmatic example of Jain engagement with political power — not through violence or coercion but through persuasion and ethical cultivation.

5. **The complete Jain intellectual**: Unlike Kundakunda (pure metaphysics) or Umaswāmi (systematic theology) or Haribhadra (comparative philosophy), Hemacandra's range covered every branch of Jain learning — making him the closest thing to a "complete" Jain intellectual.
