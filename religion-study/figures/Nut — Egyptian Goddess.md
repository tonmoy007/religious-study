# Nut — Egyptian Goddess

## Summary
Nut is the sky goddess, the mother of [[Osiris — Egyptian God|Osiris]], [[Isis — Egyptian Goddess|Isis]], [[Set — Egyptian God|Set]], and [[Nephthys — Egyptian Goddess|Nephthys]], and the daughter of [[Ra — Egyptian God|Ra]] (or in Heliopolitan theology, of [[Geb — Egyptian God|Geb]] (earth) and [[Shu — Egyptian God|Shu]] (air)). She is the celestial canopy arched over the earth, covered in stars, swallowing the sun at dusk and giving birth to it at dawn. In [[The Egyptian Book of the Dead — Stories, Figures and Connections|the Book of the Dead]], Nut is invoked as the **mother of the deceased** — the soul seeks to be reborn through her as Ra is reborn each morning.

## Iconography
- **Arched woman** — her body stretches from horizon to horizon, hands and feet touching the earth
- **Stars across her body** — usually five-pointed stars; she is *the* starry sky
- **Dark blue skin** — the colour of the night sky
- **Often naked** — representing the pure, undifferentiated sky
- **Sycomore tree** — she appears as a tree offering food and water to the dead
- **The celestial cow** — in some accounts Nut transforms into the Heavenly Cow to lift Ra above humanity

## Nut and Ra

The myth of Nut and Ra explains the structure of the cosmos:

1. **Ra decrees that Nut cannot give birth** on any day of the 360-day calendar
2. **Thoth** (the god of wisdom) gambles with the moon and wins **five extra days** (the *epagomenal days*)
3. On these five days, Nut gives birth to **Osiris, Horus the Elder, Set, Isis, and Nephthys** — the five great gods
4. **Ra separates Nut and Geb** — lifting Nut into the sky, where she becomes the firmament

**Theological significance**: Nut's children are born *outside* the normal order of time — they are sacred, set apart, and their births interrupt the normal cosmic schedule.

## The Heavenly Cow

In the [[The Solar Barque — Egyptian Story|Celestial Cow]] myth (Book of the Heavenly Cow, New Kingdom):

1. **Humanity plots against Ra** (who has grown old)
2. **Ra sends his Eye** (Hathor/Sekhmet) to destroy them
3. **Ra repents** but cannot stop the slaughter
4. **Nut becomes a cow** and lifts Ra on her back into the sky — creating the vault of heaven
5. The **four pillars of the sky** (the four corners) hold Nut in place

## Nut as a Funerary Goddess

For the deceased, Nut is:
- **The mother who gives rebirth** — as she gives birth to the sun each morning, so she gives birth to the justified soul
- **The tomb sky** — coffins are painted with Nut on the underside of the lid; the deceased lies beneath her, waiting for rebirth
- **The Tree-Goddess** — she offers food and water to the soul in the Field of Reeds
- **The protectress** — her wings shield the dead, just as the sky shields the earth

## The Book of Nut

A distinct astronomical text, the *Book of Nut* (found in the cenotaph of Seti I and elsewhere), describes:
- The path of the sun through the sky and underworld
- The **decan stars** — 36 star groups that mark the 10-day weeks of the Egyptian year
- The **hours of the night** — each hour is a goddess or spirit through which the sun passes
- The **rising and setting of the sun** — described as Nut swallowing and giving birth
