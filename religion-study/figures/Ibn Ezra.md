# Ibn Ezra (אבן עזרא — Avraham ibn Ezra)

One of the most brilliant and original Jewish biblical commentators of the medieval period. Ibn Ezra combined linguistic mastery, philosophical insight, and a critical, sometimes sceptical, approach to Scripture. His wanderings across Europe and the Mediterranean spread Sephardic learning to the Ashkenazi world.

## Key Contributions

- **Biblical Commentary:** Ibn Ezra wrote commentaries on the entire Tanakh (most of which survive), characterised by a strict commitment to grammatical-literary analysis and a reluctance to accept midrashic interpretation where the plain sense is clear. His commentary on the Torah is among the most frequently cited of all classical Jewish commentaries.
- **Critical-Literary Approach:** He was the first Jewish commentator to openly question the Mosaic authorship of some biblical passages (e.g., the final verses of Deuteronomy describing Moses' death), though he expressed such observations cryptically, only fully decoded centuries later by Spinoza and others.
- **Hebrew Grammar and Linguistics:** Ibn Ezra wrote foundational works on Hebrew grammar, including *Sefer Safah Berurah* (Book of Pure Language), *Sefer ha-Shem* (on the divine names), and *Moznayim* (The Scales — on grammatical principles). He introduced the terminology and methods of Andalusian philology to European Jewry.
- **Mathematics, Astronomy, and Astrology:** Ibn Ezra was a polymath who wrote on arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, and astrology in both Hebrew and Latin. His astronomical works were translated into Latin and influenced medieval European science.
- **Cultural Bridge:** His forced travels (he fled the Almohad persecutions in Spain and never settled permanently anywhere) took him through Italy, France, and England. He transmitted the scholarly achievements of Sephardic Jewry to the nascent Ashkenazi centres.

## Life & Legacy

- Born in Tudela, in the Muslim Taifa of Zaragoza, and educated in Cordoba's intellectual tradition
- Left Spain around 1140, possibly due to the Almohad invasion, beginning a life of wandering
- Lived in Rome, Lucca, Mantua, Verona, Narbonne, Béziers, Rouen, London, and possibly as far as Provence
- His commentary became a fundamental source for later exegetes, especially the Radak (David Kimhi) and the Ramban (Nachmanides)
- Known for his sharp tongue and wry observations about the Jewish communities he visited — and for his aphoristic, sometimes obscure, style
