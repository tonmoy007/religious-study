# Gurū Amar Dās (Amar Dās)

> Birth: 1479 CE, Basarke, Punjab (now in Amritsar district, India)
> Death: 1 September 1574 CE, Goindval (aged 95)
> Title: Third Sikh Guru (1552–1574); the Institutional Architect of the Sikh Panth; the Guru of Equality
> Era: The Consolidation of the Sikh Community under the Mughal Empire (Sur and early Akbari periods)

**Gurū Amar Dās** (1479–1574 CE) was the third of the ten Sikh Gurus, serving from 1552 until his death at the age of 95. His twenty-two-year Guruship is remembered as the period in which the Sikh community (*Panth*) was given its enduring institutional framework. Born into a Hindu Khatri family in the village of Basarke, Amar Dās grew up a devout Vaishnava Hindu, making annual pilgrimages to the Ganges at Haridwar. His life changed dramatically when, at an advanced age (over sixty), he heard his niece **Bibi Amro** singing a hymn of Gurū Nānak. Deeply moved, he sought out her father, **Gurū Aṅgad** (the second Guru), at Khadūr Sāhib. Already past sixty, Amar Dās became a humble disciple of the much younger Gurū Aṅgad, serving him with extraordinary devotion — fetching water, grinding grain, and performing menial tasks without complaint. When Gurū Aṅgad tested his disciples by pouring filthy water over them, only Amar Dās endured without flinching, earning succession to the Guruship. Installed as Guru in 1552 at age 73, he took a community that was still small and vulnerable and transformed it into a structured, disciplined, and expansive religious organisation. He established **Goindval** on the Beās River as his seat, composed the **Anand Sāhib** (the "Song of Bliss") — now one of the five daily prayers of Sikhism — created a network of **twenty-two manjīs** (diocesan preaching districts), appointed **women as preachers and spiritual leaders** (a revolutionary move for 16th-century India), reformed the **langar** (communal kitchen) into a compulsory institution that demanded every visitor — including the Mughal Emperor Akbar — eat alongside the poorest before meeting the Guru, and abolished the practices of *purdah* (veiling of women) and *sati* (widow-burning) among his followers. He compiled the hymns of his predecessors into a preliminary collection (the **Goindval Pothīs**) that foreshadowed the Ādi Granth. He also devised the **peṅgh** system — requiring all who took langar to sit together in rows as equals — which remains the universal Sikh practice. When he died in 1574 at age 95, he nominated **Gurū Rām Dās** (his son-in-law, husband of his daughter **Bibi Bhānī**) as his successor, passing over his own sons — continuing the Gurū Nānak's principle of meritocratic appointment.

## The Anand Sāhib — The Song of Bliss

The **Anand Sāhib** is Gurū Amar Dās's most celebrated composition and one of the most beloved texts in the Sikh canon. Composed in Punjabi in the Rāga Rāmkalī (one of the thirty-one rāgas of the Guru Granth Sāhib), it comprises forty stanzas (*paurīs*) of graduated length and intensity, building toward an ecstatic celebration of union with the Divine.

### Structure and Content

The Anand Sāhib opens with the declaration of bliss that comes through the Guru's grace:

> **Ānand bhaiā merī māe, satgurū mai pāyā** — "I am in bliss, O my mother, for I have found the True Guru."

Each stanza explores a different dimension of spiritual realisation: the destruction of ego, the sweetness of the Divine Name (*Nām*), the grace of the Satgurū, the company of the holy (*satsaṅgat*), and the state of *sahaj* — the effortless, intuitive union with God that is the goal of Sikh spiritual practice.

### Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **The Guru as the Giver of Bliss** | The Satgurū (True Guru) awakens the soul to its true nature; without the Guru, there is only darkness and birth-death cycles |
| **The Dissolution of Haumai (Ego)** | The greatest obstacle to bliss is the sense of the separate self (*haumai*); when the ego dissolves, the soul merges with the Divine as a river merges with the ocean |
| **Sahaj — The State of Spontaneity** | The goal is not effortful striving but *sahaj* — a state of natural, effortless union with God that pervades all activities of daily life |
| **The Company of Saints (Satsaṅgat)** | The community of the faithful is the place where the Name is sung and the ego is dissolved; solitude and asceticism are rejected |
| **Joy in the Midst of the World** | The bliss of the Anand is not otherworldly; it is experienced in the midst of householder life, through work, service, and family — the Sikh ideal of *rājā-yog* (the king-yogi) |

### Liturgical Role

The Anand Sāhib holds a unique place in Sikh worship:

- It is recited as part of the **daily Nitnem** (one of the five prescribed prayers)
- The first five stanzas and the last stanza form the core of the **Anand Kāraj** (Sikh marriage ceremony), during which the couple circumambulates the Guru Granth Sāhib as each stanza is sung
- It is often recited at **funerals and religious gatherings** — any occasion where the community gathers to celebrate the Guru's presence
- The hymn is a **presence of the Guru**; Gurū Amar Dās himself declared that whatever place the Anand Sāhib is recited with devotion, there the Guru himself is present

## Institutional Reforms — The Architecture of the Panth

Gurū Amar Dās's most enduring legacy is the institutional structure he gave the Sikh community. When he became Guru in 1552, the Sikh Panth was a small, localised group concentrated in the Punjab. By his death in 1574, it had become an organised, trans-regional community with a defined hierarchy, a missionary network, a distinctive social practice (the langar), and the textual foundations of a scripture.

### The Twenty-Two Manjīs

The *manjī* system was the administrative backbone of the early Sikh community. A *manjī* (literally a "cot" or "seat") was a preaching district; each was headed by a *masand* (from Persian *masnad* — "one who sits on a seat of authority"), who was the Guru's appointed representative for that region.

| # | Manjī Location | Masand (Preacher) | Notes |
|---|----------------|-------------------|-------|
| 1 | Goindval (headquarters) | Gurū Amar Dās himself | The central seat; all other manjīs reported to it |
| 2 | Khadūr Sāhib | Gurū Aṅgad's family / disciples | Maintained continuity with the second Guru's seat |
| 3–8 | Various in central Punjab | Various early Sikhs | Covered the heartland of the Sikh community |
| 9–14 | Outlying Punjab districts | Appointed preachers | Extended the Panth into the Malwa and Doaba regions |
| 15–18 | Beyond Punjab (Kashmir, Sindh, Kabul) | Missionary preachers | Brought Sikhism to new linguistic and cultural zones |
| 19–22 | Eastern India (Bengal, Bihar) | Appointed preachers | Extended into the Gangetic plain |

The *masands* had multiple responsibilities:
- **Preaching** the Sikh message of Ik Oaṅkār, Nām Simran, and rejection of caste and ritual
- **Collecting** offerings (*dasvandh* — the tithe) from the local sangat for the Guru's treasury
- **Resolving disputes** among local Sikhs according to the Guru's teachings
- **Reporting** to the Guru on the state of the community
- **Representing** the Guru's authority in their district

This system gave the Sikh community an organisational resilience that allowed it to survive persecution, fragmentation, and the eventual absence of a human Guru (after 1708). It was later reformed and sometimes corrupted, leading Gurū Gobind Siṅgh to abolish the masand system in 1699 — but the principle of a structured, trans-local organisation remained.

### Women Preachers — A Revolutionary Innovation

In a society where women were veiled (*purdah*), denied education, and excluded from religious authority, Gurū Amar Dās appointed women as *masands* and preachers. This was among the most radical social reforms of any 16th-century Indian religious leader.

| Woman Preacher | Relationship | Role |
|----------------|-------------|------|
| **Bibi Amro** | Daughter of Gurū Aṅgad; niece of Gurū Amar Dās by marriage | First to introduce Amar Dās to the Sikh faith; appointed as a preacher and spiritual guide |
| **Bibi Bhānī** | Daughter of Gurū Amar Dās; wife of Gurū Rām Dās | Leading preacher at Goindval; managed the langar; later the mother of Gurū Arjan |
| **Bibi Dāni** | Daughter of Gurū Amar Dās | Managed the women's congregation and oversaw women's initiation |
| **Mātā Khīvī** | Wife of Gurū Aṅgad | Managed the langar at Khadūr Sāhib; an exemplar of *sevā* (selfless service) |

The Guru's reforms regarding women extended beyond religious office. He:
- **Abolished purdah** (the veiling of women) among his followers — declaring that women were not impure and need not hide their faces
- **Preached against sati** (widow-burning) — declaring that a woman who burns herself on her husband's pyre is not a true *sati* (virtuous woman); the true *sati* is one who lives with virtue and dies with the Name of God on her lips
- **Allowed widow remarriage** — affirming women's right to a full life after the death of a spouse

These reforms placed the Sikh community a century and a half ahead of mainstream Hindu society, though their full implementation has been uneven in later Sikh history.

### The Goindval Pothīs — The First Sikh Scripture

Gurū Amar Dās compiled the hymns of Gurū Nānak and Gurū Aṅgad (and his own compositions) into a set of manuscripts known as the **Goindval Pothīs**. These were handwritten volumes preserved at Goindval, organised by rāga (musical mode) — the same principle that Gurū Arjan would later use for the Ādi Granth. The Pothīs served as the authoritative textual basis for the community's liturgical life and represent the first attempt at a Sikh scriptural canon. They are still extant and preserved in private collections, bearing witness to the editorial work that preceded the Ādi Granth by half a century.

## The Peṅgh System — The Revolution of the Common Row

The **peṅgh** (Punjabi: *peṅgh* = row, line) system was Gurū Amar Dās's most visible and socially transformative reform. While the langar (communal kitchen) had been instituted by Gurū Nānak at Kartarpur, it was Gurū Amar Dās who gave it its definitive form: **all who wished to see the Guru must first sit in the langar and eat together, seated in a single row, without regard to caste, religion, or social status.**

### The Rule

The Guru's decree was absolute:
- Every visitor — whether **Brahmin or Dalit, Hindu or Muslim, king or beggar, man or woman** — must first sit in the *peṅgh* and share a meal
- Only after eating could one come into the Guru's presence
- The food was to be **simple and identical for all** — usually *roṭī* (whole-wheat bread), *dāl* (lentils), and *sabzī* (seasonal vegetables)
- The meal was to be prepared and served by volunteers (*sevādārs*) as an act of service
- All forms of **caste hierarchy and ritual purity** were suspended: high-caste Hindus who considered themselves polluted by the shadow of a lower-caste person had to sit shoulder-to-shoulder with everyone

### The Test — Emperor Akbar's Visit

The most famous enactment of the peṅgh rule occurred when the **Mughal Emperor Akbar** visited Gurū Amar Dās at Goindval. The Emperor, hearing of the Guru's growing influence, came to meet him. He was met at the outskirts of Goindval and told: the Guru sees no one without first sharing langar. Akbar — known for his liberal religious policies — accepted the condition, ate the simple lentil and bread meal sitting on the floor alongside commoners, and then was admitted to the Guru's presence. The Emperor was so impressed that he offered the Guru a *jāgīr* (land grant) for the langar's support. The Guru politely refused personal gifts but accepted the grant on behalf of the community — the first state recognition of the Sikh institution.

This episode became legendary in Sikh history, symbolising the radical equality that the Guru demanded even from the most powerful man in India. It also demonstrated that the Guru's authority was independent of — and in this case, respected by — the Mughal state.

### Theological Meaning

The peṅgh system is not merely a social-welfare practice but a **sacrament of equality**. The act of eating together:
1. **Annuls caste** — at the Guru's table, the distinction between *touchable* and *untouchable*, Brahmin and Śūdra, is abolished
2. **Creates community** — the shared meal constitutes the congregation (*saṅgat*) as a body that transcends biological kinship and social hierarchy
3. **Teaches humility** — the highest (the Guru) serves the lowest (the guest), and the Emperor sits beside the beggar
4. **Enacts the theology of grace** — the food is God's gift; no one earns it; all receive it freely

Sikh tradition holds that the peṅgh was Gurū Amar Dās's solution to a practical problem — an arrogant Brahmin once refused to eat in the langar before seeing the Guru — but its theological implications are profound and enduring. Every Sikh gurdwārā to this day maintains the peṅgh system: the langar hall is the first building a visitor enters, and the food is always served in rows on the floor, regardless of the status of the guest.

## Legacy

### The Institutional Foundation of the Sikh Panth

Gurū Amar Dās's twenty-two-year Guruship is the period in which Sikhism acquired the organisational backbone that allowed it to survive and grow. Before him, the Sikh community was a circle of disciples around a charismatic teacher; after him, it was a structured, trans-regional community with a clear hierarchy, a missionary network, a standardised social practice (the langar), and the beginnings of a canonical scripture (the Goindval Pothīs). He transformed the Sikh Panth from a **sect** into a **tradition**.

### The Anand Sāhib in Sikh Liturgy

The Anand Sāhib remains among the most recited and beloved texts in the Sikh canon. Its joyful affirmation of the possibility of divine union in the midst of everyday life — the *rājā-yog* ideal — is the emotional heart of Sikh spirituality. Every Sikh marriage, every daily prayer, every congregation that gathers to sing the Guru's hymns, draws on the Anand Sāhib as a source of joy and a declaration of faith.

### The Langar as a Universal Model

The peṅgh system that Gurū Amar Dās instituted has become the most visible and admired Sikh institution worldwide. Every gurdwārā maintains a langar; the largest — the Golden Temple in Amritsar — serves over 100,000 meals daily, all free, all prepared and served by volunteers, all eaten in rows on the floor. The langar has inspired interfaith initiatives, disaster-relief programmes, and community kitchens across the world. It remains the most concrete embodiment of the Sikh teaching that **God's grace is available to all, without discrimination**.

### The Legacy of Women's Leadership

Though the later history of Sikh institutions has not always lived up to the Guru's vision, the principle he established — that women could hold formal religious authority, preach, and lead congregations — remains part of the Sikh tradition's foundational commitment. Bibi Bhānī's role as both a preacher and the mother of Gurū Arjan linked the third, fourth, and fifth Gurus in a lineage of spiritual service that crossed gender lines.

### Comparative Significance

| Dimension | Gurū Amar Dās's Contribution |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| **Institution-building** | Transformed a personal following into an organised community with dioceses, preachers, and a treasury |
| **Social reform** | Abolished purdah and sati among Sikhs; appointed women preachers; enforced caste equality at the langar |
| **Scripture** | Composed 907 hymns (including the Anand Sāhib); created the Goindval Pothīs, the first Sikh scriptural canon |
| **State relations** | Established a peaceful, dignified relationship with Mughal power — accepting state patronage without political submission |
| **Succession model** | Continued the meritocratic principle: passed Guruship to his son-in-law, not his sons |
| **Global influence** | The langar/peṅgh model has inspired modern movements for food justice, radical hospitality, and anti-caste activism |

### The Gurū's Character

Sikh tradition remembers Gurū Amar Dās as a figure of extraordinary humility, patience, and organisational wisdom. The image of the seventy-three-year-old Guru who had spent twelve years grinding grain and fetching water for his own Gurū is held up as the ideal of *sevā* (selfless service). His long life — he lived to 95, serving as Guru for 22 of those years — gave him the time to build institutions that his shorter-lived predecessors could not. The epithet most often applied to him in Sikh hagiography is **Gurū Amar Dās, the Guru of the Peṅgh** — a title that ties his name forever to the revolution of the common table.

### Key Sources in the Sikh Canon

| Source | Type | Significance |
|--------|------|--------------|
