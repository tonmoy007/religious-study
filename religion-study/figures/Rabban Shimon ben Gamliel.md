# Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel II (רבן שמעון בן גמליאל)

A Tanna of the third generation and the Nasi (Patriarch) of the Sanhedrin during the post-Yavneh period centred at Usha and later Shefaram. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel II was the son of Rabban Gamaliel II and the father of Rabbi Yehudah haNasi, the compiler of the Mishnah. He presided over the Sanhedrin during a transitional era of reconstruction following the Bar Kokhba revolt (132–135 CE), when the rabbinic leadership relocated from Yavneh to the Galilean towns of Usha and Shefaram.

Rabban Shimon lived through one of the most traumatic periods in Jewish history — the Bar Kokhba revolt and its brutal suppression by Hadrian. Many of his colleagues, including Rabbi Akiva, were martyred. He became Nasi at a time when the Sanhedrin needed to rebuild its authority, reassemble the scattered sages, and preserve the Oral Law under Roman persecution. His leadership helped lay the groundwork for his son's monumental achievement — the redaction of the Mishnah.

## Key Contributions

- **Leadership After the Bar Kokhba Revolt:** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel II helped reassemble the surviving sages at Usha and later in the Galilee. Under his leadership, the Sanhedrin issued a series of takkanot (ordinances) addressing the new realities — including decrees about marriage, divorce, and the calendar.
- **Preservation of the Oral Law:** He worked to preserve and transmit the corpus of teachings that had survived the destruction of Jerusalem and the Bar Kokhba revolt. His efforts directly enabled his son, Rabbi Yehudah haNasi, to compile the Mishnah.
- **Restoration of Hillelite Authority:** Like his father and grandfather, Rabban Shimon defended the authority of the Hillelite dynasty of the Nesi'im against rival claims. His title "Rabban" signals his elevated status as Nasi.
- **Usha Synod:** He convened the sages in Usha, where major halakhic enactments were passed, including the requirement to marry off children at a young age and regulations about communal reading of the Megillah.

## In Jewish Tradition

- The Talmud refers to him with the honorific title "Rabban" (preserved for the Nesi'im of Hillel's dynasty)
- He is quoted in Pirkei Avot: "I have found nothing better for the body than silence; study is not the most important thing but practice; and one who talks excessively brings sin" (Avot 1:17)
- Some sources identify him with the "Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel" who appears in the story of the Ten Martyrs — though historically that figure may be Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel I (his grandfather), who lived during the destruction of the Second Temple
- His teachings are preserved in the Mishnah, Tosefta, and the Halakhic Midrashim
