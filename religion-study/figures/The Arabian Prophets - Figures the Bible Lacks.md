# The Arabian Prophets — Figures the Bible Lacks

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **reverse-direction** deep dive

Every other deep dive in this collection traces a figure the Bible and Qur'an
**share**. This one runs the *other* way: the Qur'an contains major prophets with
**no biblical counterpart at all** — **Hūd**, **Ṣāliḥ**, and **Shuʿayb**,
preachers sent to ancient **Arabian** peoples. They show that the Qur'an is not
simply retelling the Bible; it has its **own prophetic landscape**, rooted in
Arabian memory. (Contrast with the shared figures in
[[Shared Abrahamic Figures]].)

## The shared template
All three appear in [[011 Hud|Surah Hud]] (which is *named* after one of them),
each following an identical formula — the Qur'an's signature "punishment story":
*a prophet warns his people → "Serve Allah, ye have no other god" → they reject
him → divine destruction*. It is the **same template** the Qur'an applies to
[[Noah and Nuh - A Figure Traced|Nuh]] and [[Lot and Lut - A Figure Traced|Lut]].

### Hūd — to the people of ʿĀd
> [!quote] Hud 11:50
> "And unto (the tribe of) **A'ad** (We sent) their brother, **Hud**. He said: O my people! **Serve Allah! Ye have no other Allah save Him.** Lo! ye do but invent."

ʿĀd were a powerful people of southern Arabia (associated with the lost city of
*Iram of the pillars*, 89:7). For their arrogance they are destroyed by a
furious wind.

### Ṣāliḥ — to the people of Thamūd, and the she-camel
> [!quote] Hud 11:61, 64–65
> **11:61** — "And unto (the tribe of) **Thamud** (We sent) their brother **Salih**… Serve Allah, ye have no other Allah save Him."
> **11:64** — "This is the **camel of Allah**, a token unto you, so suffer her to feed… and **touch her not with harm**."
> **11:65** — "But they **hamstrung her**… Enjoy life in your dwelling-place three days! This is a threat that will not be belied."

Thamūd (carvers of houses in the rock, 7:74) are given a miraculous **she-camel**
as a test; when they hamstring it in defiance, an earthquake/blast destroys them.

### Shuʿayb — to Midian, the prophet of honest trade
> [!quote] Hud 11:84–85
> **11:84** — "And unto **Midian** (We sent) their brother **Shu'eyb**… Serve Allah… and **give not short measure and short weight**."
> **11:85** — "Give **full measure and full weight in justice**, and wrong not people in respect of their goods. And do not evil in the earth, causing corruption."

Shuʿayb (often linked by tradition to the biblical **Jethro**, Moses'
father-in-law, though the Qur'an does not equate them) preaches against
**commercial fraud** — a strikingly *economic* ethics. Midian, too, is destroyed
when it refuses him.

## Why these figures matter for the comparison
| | The shared prophets | The Arabian prophets |
|---|---|---|
| In the Bible? | yes (Adam, Noah, Abraham…) | **no counterpart** |
| Setting | Mesopotamia, Canaan, Egypt | ancient **Arabia** (ʿĀd, Thamūd, Midian) |
| Function | retell & reinterpret biblical events | root prophecy in **Arabian** history |
| Shared logic | — | same warn-reject-destroy template as Nuh & Lut |

Two takeaways:
1. **The Qur'an is not a derivative text.** Alongside the figures it shares with
   the Bible, it asserts an **independent line of prophecy** to peoples the Bible
   never mentions — making the universal claim that *every* nation received a
   warner (16:36).
2. **The template is consistent.** Whether the prophet is the shared Noah/Lot or
   the Arabian Hud/Salih/Shuʿayb, the Qur'an tells them in the *same shape* —
   which is itself strong evidence of how the Qur'an *thinks* about prophecy
   (see the synthesis discussion in [[Connections Between Religious Texts]]).
