# Shared Abrahamic Figures

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]]

The Tanakh, the Christian Bible, and the Qur'an are not three unrelated books —
they retell the **same cast of prophets**. This is the clearest kind of
connection (**genealogical**): the Qur'an *knowingly* engages the earlier
scriptures, calling Jews and Christians "People of the Book." See also
[[Abrahamic Traditions]].

## The Qur'an names the prophets in its surah titles
Whole surahs of the [[Quran Index|Qur'an]] are *named after* biblical figures —
a structural sign of how central they are:

| Surah | Figure | Biblical name |
|---|---|---|
| [[011 Hud\|Hud (11)]] / Nuh (71) | Nūḥ | Noah |
| Ibrahim (14) | Ibrāhīm | Abraham |
| Yusuf (12) | Yūsuf | Joseph |
| Yunus (10) | Yūnus | Jonah |
| [[019 Maryam\|Maryam (19)]] | Maryam | Mary |

Across the Qur'an these and other shared figures — Adam, Mūsā (Moses), Hārūn
(Aaron), Dāwūd (David), Sulaymān (Solomon), ʿĪsā (Jesus) — recur **hundreds of
times**, woven through dozens of surahs.

## Same figure, three theologies
The figure stays; the *meaning* shifts with each tradition:

> [!quote] Abraham / Ibrahim
> - **Judaism** — the covenant-father. In [[scriptures/tanakh-jps1917/01 Genesis|Genesis (Tanakh)]], God makes an everlasting covenant with him and his seed.
> - **Christianity** — the model of *faith* justified before the Law (Paul, Romans 4).
> - **Islam** — the first pure monotheist (*ḥanīf*) and a *muslim* (one who submits); rebuilder of the Kaʿba (Qur'an 2:124–129).

The same person is the *ancestor* (Judaism), the *exemplar of faith*
(Christianity), and the *first submitter* (Islam) — one story, three lessons.

## Jesus: shared figure, divergent claims
- **Christianity** — the incarnate Son of God, crucified and risen (the Gospels: [[40 Matthew]], [[43 John]]).
- **Islam** — ʿĪsā is a revered prophet and Messiah, born of the virgin Maryam, but **not** divine (see [[019 Maryam|Maryam]]) — the sharpest theological fork in the shared family.

## What kind of connection?
**Genealogical and explicit.** Unlike a chance parallel, the Qur'an openly
positions itself as confirming and correcting the earlier books. The interesting
study is not *whether* they connect but *how each reshapes* the shared figure.

## Threads to pull
- Trace **Joseph / Yusuf**: Genesis 37–50 vs. Surah 12 (the Qur'an's most sustained single narrative).
- Trace **Moses / Mūsā**: the most-mentioned figure in the Qur'an — compare Exodus with the scattered Qur'anic retellings.
