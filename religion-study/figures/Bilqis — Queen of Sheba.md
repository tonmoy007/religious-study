# Bilqis — Queen of Sheba

The wise queen of Sheba (Saba') who ruled a wealthy kingdom in Yemen, tested the Prophet Sulayman, and ultimately submitted to the one God. Her story in Surah An-Naml is one of the most detailed portraits of a political leader in the Qur'an — a woman of intelligence, diplomacy, and spiritual discernment.

---

## Biography

- **Name:** Bilqis (the name is from Islamic tradition; not given in the Qur'an)
- **Kingdom:** Saba' (Sheba) — centred in modern Yemen (capital: Marib)
- **Known for:** Wisdom, diplomacy, sovereignty, conversion
- **Identified with:** The Queen of Sheba in the Bible (1 Kings 10, 2 Chronicles 9) — the same figure, but the Qur'anic version has a different narrative emphasis

---

## The Story

See the full narrative: [[Sulayman and Bilqis — The Queen of Sheba]]

Key events:
1. The Hoopoe brought news of a kingdom ruled by a queen with a magnificent throne
2. Sulayman sent a letter: "Do not exalt yourselves against me, but come to me in submission"
3. Bilqis consulted her council — she chose diplomacy, sending gifts
4. She visited Sulayman — he tested her faith
5. She was shown her own throne (miraculously transported), and when she entered the glass palace, she bared her legs, thinking it was water
6. She declared: "My Lord, I have wronged myself, and I submit with Sulayman to Allah, Lord of the worlds"

---

## Key Tafsir Insights

**A model leader:** The Qur'an shows her consulting her council (shura) before deciding — a model of consultative leadership.

**Diplomat before warrior:** She chose gifts over war, negotiation over confrontation — the Qur'an validates this approach.

**Women in leadership:** The Qur'an does not condemn her rule — only her sun-worship. Once she submitted, her gender was never a disqualification.

**The Hoopoe's role:** The bird that brought the news is named *hudhud* — a messenger of truth. The story validates non-human messengers as bearers of divine intelligence.
