# Rabbi Ishmael (רבי ישמעאל)

Rabbi Ishmael ben Elisha was a leading Tanna of the second and third generations and one of the most influential legal minds of the Mishnaic period. He is best known for formulating the **thirteen hermeneutical principles** (שְׁלוֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִדּוֹת — the Thirteen Middot) by which the Torah is interpreted, a system that remains embedded in the daily morning liturgy and serves as the foundation of rabbinic exegesis. He was a colleague and frequent methodological opponent of Rabbi Akiva.

Rabbi Ishmael came from a priestly family of high standing — tradition identifies him as a kohen whose grandfather was a High Priest in the Temple. His interpretive approach emphasised the plain meaning of the text (דֶּרֶךְ — derekh), arguing that the Torah speaks in human language and that its details should not be over-extended. This put him in direct opposition to Rabbi Akiva, who derived laws from every grammatical particle. The Mekhilta de-Rabbi Ishmael, a midrash halakhah on Exodus, bears his name and reflects his method. He was among the Ten Martyrs executed by the Romans after the Bar Kokhba revolt.

## Key Contributions

- **The Thirteen Middot:** The most influential set of hermeneutical principles in Judaism. They include inferences from the greater to the lesser (קַל וָחוֹמֶר), from an analogous expression (גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה), and from general principles followed by specifics (כְּלָל וּפְרָט). These principles are recited daily in the morning prayers.
- **Method of Derekh (Plain Meaning):** Ishmael's principle that "the Torah speaks in human language" (דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה כִּלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם) limited the extent to which superfluous words or particles could be used for legal derivation — a direct challenge to Akiva's method.
- **Mekhilta de-Rabbi Ishmael:** A foundational midrash halakhah on the Book of Exodus, organised according to the thirteen middot and preserving the interpretive tradition of his school.
- **Opposition to Akiva's System:** While Akiva derived laws from every אֵת and גַּם in the Torah, Ishmael insisted that such particles often have ordinary grammatical functions and should not be overloaded with legal meaning.

## In Jewish Tradition

- The Thirteen Middot are recited every morning during the Pesukei de-Zimra and again before Selichot — more widely known than virtually any other Tannaitic teaching
- The Mekhilta is one of the three oldest Tannaitic midrashim, alongside the Sifra (Akivan) and Sifre
- His martyrdom is narrated as part of the Ten Martyrs liturgy recited on Yom Kippur and Tisha B'Av
- The school of Rabbi Ishmael and the school of Rabbi Akiva are the two great streams of Tannaitic interpretation that shaped the Mishnah
