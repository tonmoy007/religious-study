# Rabban Gamaliel (רבן גמליאל)

Rabban Gamaliel II of Yavneh, the grandson of Hillel the Elder, served as the Nasi (Patriarch) of the Sanhedrin in the decades following the destruction of the Second Temple. He succeeded Rabban Yohanan ben Zakkai as the head of the Yavneh academy and led the Jewish people during a formative period of reconstruction after the catastrophic loss of the Temple. His most enduring achievement was the establishment of the fixed Hebrew calendar, a development that ensured Jewish unity across the diaspora.

Gamaliel was known for his strict and at times autocratic leadership style. He famously clashed with his colleagues — especially Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananyah — humiliating him publicly on multiple occasions. This behaviour led to a crisis in which the sages deposed him as Nasi and appointed Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah in his place. He was later reconciled and reinstated, and the episode became a foundational legal precedent for accountability of communal leaders. Gamaliel's family lineage, running through Hillel, would continue to hold the Patriarchate for centuries.

## Key Contributions

- **Fixed Calendar:** Gamaliel established a systematic, observation-based calendar that regulated the months and festivals for all Jewish communities, ending reliance on Temple-based announcements. This was critical for Jewish unity after the destruction of the Temple.
- **Deposition Precedent:** His conflict with Rabbi Yehoshua, culminating in his temporary removal as Nasi (Berakhot 27b–28a), established the principle that even the highest-ranking sage is subject to the authority of the collective body of scholars.
- **Intercalation Authority:** He personally oversaw the process of adding an extra month (Adar II) to keep the lunar calendar aligned with the solar year — a power that became a defining function of the Patriarchate.
- **Oversight of the Yavneh Academy:** Under his leadership, the academy formalised the Mishnaic tradition, settled disputes between the Houses of Hillel and Shammai, and consolidated the canon of biblical books.

## In Jewish Tradition

- He is cited in the Mishnah as the authority who decreed that witnesses who saw the new moon must travel even on Shabbat to testify before the Sanhedrin (Mishnah Rosh Hashanah 1:9–2:9)
- The Talmud records that he had a diagram of the moon phases on the wall of his upper chamber to question witnesses (Rosh Hashanah 24a)
- His deposition story is taught as a model of how rabbinic consensus functions — even the Nasi is not above the law
- Rabban Gamaliel appears in Christian tradition in Acts 22:3 as the teacher of Paul (Saul of Tarsus), though this identification is debated
