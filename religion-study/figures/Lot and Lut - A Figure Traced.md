# Lot & Lut — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

Abraham's nephew Lot is the lone righteous man in a city destroyed for its sin —
the archetypal **judgment narrative** in both scriptures. The plot is nearly
identical (visitors, a mob, escape, fire from heaven, a wife lost looking back),
but the Qur'an reframes Lut from a **family member in a saga** into a
**warning-prophet** in a series. A **genealogical** connection (see
[[Shared Abrahamic Figures]]).

## The shared story
| Beat | [[scriptures/bible-kjv/01 Genesis\|Genesis 19]] | Qur'an (Hud 11 / Al-A'raf 7) |
|---|---|---|
| Heavenly visitors | "two angels to Sodom at even" (19:1) | messengers come to Lut (11:77) |
| The mob's demand | "**bring them out unto us, that we may know them**" (19:5) | "his people came… running… they used to commit abominations" (11:78) |
| Lot offers his daughters | "I have two daughters… let me bring them out" (19:8) | "Here are my daughters! They are purer for you" (11:78) |
| The destruction | "the LORD **rained** upon Sodom… **brimstone and fire**" (19:24) | "We **rained upon it stones of clay**, one after another" (11:82) |
| The wife who looks back | "his wife **looked back**… became a **pillar of salt**" (19:26) | "his wife… she was of those who stayed behind" (7:83) |

The skeleton — *guests → predatory mob → Lot's plea → fiery destruction → the
wife left behind* — matches closely.

## The signature divergences

**1. The blinding (Bible only).** Genesis has the angels strike the mob blind:
"they **smote the men that were at the door… with blindness**" (19:11). The
Qur'an's account omits this.

**2. The pillar of salt (Bible only).** The Bible's haunting image of Lot's wife
**turned to a pillar of salt** for looking back (19:26) has no Qur'anic
equivalent — the Qur'an simply says she "stayed behind" and perished with the
city.

**3. Lot as patriarch vs. Lut as prophet.** In Genesis, Lot is part of
**Abraham's family saga** — his rescue flows from Abraham's bargaining with God
(Genesis 18, "wilt thou destroy the righteous with the wicked?"), and the story
continues into the troubling episode of Lot and his daughters (19:30–38). In the
Qur'an, **Lut is a sent prophet** who explicitly *preaches* against his people's
sin before they reject him — one entry in the Qur'an's recurring pattern of
*prophet warns → people reject → divine destruction* (the same template as Nuh,
Hud, Salih, Shu'aib). The Qur'an also contains **none** of the daughters
episode.

| | Genesis | Qur'an |
|---|---|---|
| Lot's role | nephew of Abraham, a **patriarch** | a **warning-prophet** in a series |
| Why he's saved | Abraham's intercession | his own righteousness & prophethood |
| The mob blinded | yes (19:11) | not mentioned |
| Wife's fate | **pillar of salt** | "stayed behind," destroyed |
| Aftermath | Lot & daughters (19:30–38) | absent |

## What kind of connection?
**Genealogical** — the Qur'an knowingly retells Sodom, preserving the core
judgment while (as with [[First Murder - Cain and Abel|Cain & Abel]] and the
[[Flood Narratives Compared|flood]]) reshaping the protagonist into a preaching
prophet and dropping the morally awkward family material. The pattern is
consistent across the whole [[Shared Abrahamic Figures|shared corpus]].
