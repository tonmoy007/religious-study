# Banda Siṅgh Bahādur (Banda Bahādur)

> Birth: 27 October 1670, Rajouri (Jammu region), born Hindu as Lachman Dev (Madho Dās)
> Death: 9 June 1716, Delhi — executed by the Mughal Empire
> Title: First Sikh military commander to lead an armed uprising against the Mughals; established the first independent Sikh state at Lohgarh (1710–1715); baptised into the Khalsa by [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]] in 1708; known as *Banda Bahādur* (the Brave One)

Banda Siṅgh Bahādur (Punjabi: ਬੰਦਾ ਸਿੰਘ ਬਹਾਦਰ), born Lachman Dev and originally known as Madho Dās before his initiation, was the first Sikh military commander to wage an organised war against the Mughal Empire. A former Bairagi ascetic who underwent a radical transformation after meeting Gurū Gobind Siṅgh in 1708, he led a popular uprising of the Punjabi peasantry (predominantly Jats) that shattered Mughal authority in the Sirhind region, established the first Sikh state at Lohgarh, issued the first Sikh coinage, and inflicted a series of devastating defeats on imperial forces. Though ultimately captured and executed in Delhi after fierce resistance, his brief five-year campaign (1709–1715) fundamentally altered the political landscape of northern India — it demonstrated that Mughal power in the Punjab was vulnerable, it galvanised the Khalsa into a coherent military force, and it inspired a century of Sikh resistance that would culminate in the Sikh Empire of Raṇjīt Siṅgh. Banda's legacy is profoundly contested: Sikhs revere him as a martyr and the first sovereign of the Sikh commonwealth, while Mughal chroniclers condemn him as a rebel whose atrocities — including the execution of Wazīr Khān at Sirhind — violated the norms of imperial warfare.

## From Ascetic to Warrior

No figure in Sikh history undergoes a more dramatic transformation than Banda Siṅgh Bahādur. His life divides into two halves: forty years as a celibate, quietist Hindu ascetic, followed by five years as a ferocious Khalsa general.

### Early Life in Rajouri

Lachman Dev was born into a Minhas Rajput family in Rajouri, a small principality in the Poonch hills (now Jammu and Kashmir). His father, Rājā Rām Dev, was a local chieftain. Traditional accounts say his father died when he was a boy, and his mother raised him with a strong religious bent. As a youth, he excelled at hunting and martial arts — skills that would later serve him well — but his heart was drawn to the spiritual life.

At around age 15, he encountered a Vaiṣṇava Bairagī ascetic named Janaki Prasād (or in some versions Rām Dās), who initiated him into the renunciant order. He adopted the name **Madho Dās** and began the wandering life of a sadhu — dwelling in monasteries (*akhaṛās*), practising severe austerities, and studying the Hindu scriptures.

### Life as Madho Dās — The Ascetic

For the next quarter-century, Madho Dās lived the classic life of a Bairagi renunciant. He settled for a period at the **Panchale monastery** on the banks of the Yamunā in present-day Uttarakhand, then moved to **Kankhal** near Haridwar (a major centre of asceticism), and finally to a hermitage on the **Godāvarī River** in the Deccan. His reputation for piety and learning grew, and he gathered a small following of his own.

According to Sikh tradition (*Gurbilās Pātshāhī 10*), Madho Dās possessed extraordinary physical strength and an intense personality — even as a renunciant, he was known to carry a heavy iron *chakkar* (a spiked discus) as his meditation aid. This latent warrior energy, sublimated for decades into ascetic discipline, would explode into action when the Guru touched his life.

### The Meeting at Nanded (1708)

In September 1708, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh was encamped at **Nanded** on the Godāvarī River (in present-day Maharashtra), having travelled south for his meeting with Emperor Bahādur Shāh. Madho Dās, whose hermitage was nearby, came to visit the Guru out of curiosity — he had heard of this "saintly warrior" from the north.

The encounter is one of the most pivotal in Sikh history. The Guru is said to have asked the ascetic:

> "You renounced the world — but can you renounce your own salvation for the sake of the oppressed?"

Madho Dās was stunned. No one had ever framed renunciation that way. After several days of discourse, the Guru revealed to him the history of the Sikhs, the martyrdom of Gurū Arjan and Gurū Tegh Bahādur, the tyranny of Wazīr Khān of Sirhind, and the need for a leader who could unite the scattered Khalsa and strike at the heart of Mughal power.

Madho Dās committed himself entirely. The Guru baptised him into the Khalsa with the name **Banda Siṅgh Bahādur** — "Banda" meaning servant or slave (of God), a deliberate counterpoint to the pride of asceticism. He was given the Five K's, a sword, five arrows from the Guru's own quiver, a drum (*naqqāra*) and a banner (*nishān sāhib*). The Guru also assigned him five Sikh companions as advisors: Bhāī Baj Siṅgh, Bhāī Dayā Siṅgh (one of the Pañj Piāre), Bhāī Rām Siṅgh, Bhāī Fateh Siṅgh, and Bhāī Vahīrī.

The Guru's parting instructions were clear:

> **"Go to the Punjab. Take up the sword. Protect the saints. Punish the tyrant of Sirhind. Establish righteousness. I give you the sovereignty of the Khalsa — you shall be the first to wield it in my name."**

Gurū Gobind Siṅgh died less than a month later (7 October 1708) from wounds inflicted by a Pathan assassin. Banda Siṅgh Bahādur became the living sword of the Guru's legacy.

## The Mission to the Punjab

### Journey North (1708–1709)

After the Guru's death, Banda travelled north from Nanded toward the Punjab, a journey of nearly 1,500 kilometres through Mughal territory. He assumed the disguise of a Bairagi ascetic again, moving undetected through hostile country, while his five Sikh companions scouted ahead and established contact with sympathetic Jat villages.

The journey took nearly a year. Along the way, Banda sent letters to the scattered Sikh congregations (*sangats*) in the Punjab — a call to arms that electrified the peasantry. He reached the outskirts of Delhi by August 1709, then proceeded to a gathering point near the town of **Bairagarh** (named after his former identity) in Haryana, where thousands of Sikhs had assembled at the very mention of a Khalsa army.

### The First Victories

| Battle | Date | Location | Opponent | Outcome |
|--------|------|----------|----------|---------|
| **Sāmāna** | 12 November 1709 | Sāmāna (Patiala district) | A Mughal garrison | Sikh victory — the town fell; the killers of the younger Sāhibzāde (Zorāwar and Fateh Siṅgh) were executed |
| **Kaithal** | November 1709 | Kaithal (Haryana) | Local Mughal forces | Sikh victory; the city opened its gates peacefully |

Within a month of his first engagement at Sāmāna, Banda had captured six towns and established a secure base of operations. The Mughal administration was caught completely off guard — they had dismissed the "sadhu turned soldier" as a minor disturbance.

### The Battle of Chapar Chiri and the Fall of Sirhind (May 1710)

The crowning military achievement of Banda's campaign was the capture of **Sirhind** — the city that symbolised Mughal oppression in Sikh memory because its governor Wazīr Khān had ordered the walling alive of Gurū Gobind Siṅgh's two younger sons in 1705.

Wazīr Khān assembled a large Mughal army of perhaps 30,000 men, including artillery and cavalry. The Sikh army — estimated at 10,000–15,000, predominantly Jat peasantry armed with swords, spears, and matchlocks — met the Mughals in the field at **Chapar Chiri**, a village 20 kilometres from Sirhind.

The battle began on 12 May 1710. The Mughal army had the advantage in numbers and artillery. But the Sikhs fought with a ferocity born of religious fervour and a desire for vengeance. According to contemporary accounts, Banda himself led the charge, mounted on a horse, wielding the Guru's sword. The Sikhs broke through the Mughal centre, captured the artillery positions, and sent the imperial forces fleeing.

Wazīr Khān was captured as he tried to escape. Banda ordered him executed on the spot — the sword that had ordered the death of the Sāhibzāde was now used to end his own life. The body was hung from a tree at the site, and the Sikhs chanted *Fateh Darshan* (Victory to the Vision of the Guru).

Sirhind itself fell without further resistance. Banda took the city and ordered the destruction of the fort, the mint, and the governor's palace, as well as the demolition of the mosque from which the orders for the Sāhibzādes' death had been issued. The city was given over to the peasantry — a symbolic reversal of the Mughal order.

## The First Sikh State (1710–1715)

### Establishment at Lohgarh

After the capture of Sirhind, Banda marched to **Lohgarh** (in present-day Haryana, near Yamunanagar), a fortified town that he made his capital. Here he established the **first sovereign Sikh state** — a political entity independent of the Mughal Empire, with its own administration, coinage, revenue system, and army.

### Coinage

The most potent symbol of sovereignty was the minting of coins. Banda struck silver and copper coins in the name of the Gurus, with the inscription:

> **Sikkah zad bar har do 'ālam, tegh-i Nānak va Gobind Siṅgh**
> ("The coin is struck in both worlds, the sword of Nānak and Gobind Siṅgh")

The coins bore the date **1709** (the year of the Sikh victory at Sirhind) instead of the Mughal regnal year — a direct assertion of sovereignty. On the reverse, they carried the legend:

> **Zarb-i Lohgarh** (Struck at Lohgarh)

This was the first Sikh coinage in history, issued a full century before Raṇjīt Siṅgh's Sikh Empire would mint the Nanakshahi series.

### Administrative Reforms

Banda's administration was a radical departure from the Mughal system:

- **Land redistribution** — He abolished the zamindari system in the territories under his control and granted land directly to the peasant cultivators, freeing them from the exactions of Mughal tax-farmers.
- **Remission of taxes** — The heavy Mughal land tax (kharaj) was replaced by a voluntary contribution (*dasvandh*) modelled on the Sikh tradition of tithing.
- **Replacement of officials** — Mughal administrators, qazis (judges), and revenue collectors were replaced by Sikhs and local Jat leaders loyal to the Khalsa.
- **Social levelling** — The low-caste peasantry, particularly the Jats, were elevated to positions of authority. Formerly marginalised groups — sweepers, tanners, labourers — were appointed to administrative posts, a deliberate inversion of the Mughal caste hierarchy.
- **Religious freedom** — No mosque was destroyed in Banda's territories (despite later Mughal propaganda claiming otherwise). The destruction at Sirhind was selective and symbolic — the mosque from which Wazīr Khān had governed was razed, but other places of worship were left untouched.

### The Reaction of the Mughal Empire

The Mughal court at Delhi was initially dismissive, then panicked. Emperor Bahādur Shāh (who had succeeded Aurangzeb in 1707) was in the Deccan when news of Sirhind's fall reached him. He immediately recognised the gravity of the situation — a popular uprising led by a Khalsa commander had established an independent state within a month's march of Delhi.

The Emperor issued a firman ordering the governors of Lahore, Jammu, and Delhi to combine forces and crush the rebellion. Over the next five years, the Mughals would mount four major military campaigns against Banda — each of which he repelled through a combination of guerrilla tactics, fortress warfare, and the extraordinary morale of his troops.

## The Military Campaigns (1710–1715)

### First Mughal Campaign (June–December 1710)

Governor of Lahore and the newly appointed commander **Khān Bahādur** (also known as Zabardast Khān) marched against Lohgarh with a force of 20,000 men, including artillery. Banda evacuated Lohgarh — the classic guerrilla tactic of refusing a pitched battle against a superior force — and retreated into the hills. His army harried the Mughal supply lines and launched night raids.

When Khān Bahādur occupied Lohgarh in September 1710, he found the treasury empty and the fort stripped of all military value. The Sikhs had melted into the countryside. The Mughals declared victory and withdrew — at which point Banda immediately reoccupied Lohgarh and re-established his administration.

### Second Mughal Campaign (1711)

The Emperor himself took the field in 1711, marching from Delhi with a massive army said to be 60,000 strong along with heavy artillery. Banda again adopted Fabian tactics — he evacuated Lohgarh, retreated to the foothills of the Shivalik range, and harassed the imperial army's flanks. The Emperor's army occupied Lohgarh but could not hold it. The campaign was a stalemate, and by 1712, the Emperor returned to Delhi, his attention diverted by the impending succession crisis.

### The Battle of Rahon (1713–1714)

In 1713, after Bahādur Shāh's death and the accession of Jahāndār Shāh (and then the brief rule of Farrukhsiyar from 1713), the new Emperor appointed **Abdul Samad Khān** as governor of Lahore, assisted by **Zakariyā Khān** (later governor in his own right), and ordered a renewed campaign.

The Mughals besieged the Sikh fortress at **Rahon** (near Jalandhar). Banda held out for three months, his garrison reduced to eating the bark of trees. The fort eventually fell, but Banda escaped to the hills again — the pattern of defeat and evasion that made him impossible to destroy.

### The Siege of Gurdās Naṅgal (December 1715)

The final act of Banda's military career took place at the village of **Gurdās Naṅgal** (near Gurdāspur). With his army reduced to perhaps 2,000 followers, Banda fortified a small mud-and-brick enclosure and prepared to make his last stand.

The Mughal army — commanded by Abdul Samad Khān and Zakariyā Khān — besieged the stockade with perhaps 20,000 troops and artillery. The siege lasted from December 1714 until December 1715 — a full year. The Sikhs endured unimaginable conditions: starvation, thirst, disease, and constant bombardment.

Accounts describe the defenders eating grass, leather, and the flesh of dead horses. Many died of starvation or disease. Yet they refused to surrender. The Mughals attempted to storm the stockade repeatedly and were beaten back each time by desperate Sikh sorties.

Finally, in December 1715, with his followers reduced to a few hundred and the stockade reduced to rubble, Banda was persuaded to negotiate a surrender. The Mughal commander swore on the Qur'an that the Sikhs would be allowed to leave unharmed if they laid down their arms.

The Sikhs emerged. The Mughals — true to their precedent with Gurū Gobind Siṅgh at Anandpur — broke their oath immediately. The Sikhs were chained, their arms confiscated, and Banda was taken in chains to **Lahore**, then paraded to **Delhi**.

## Trial and Execution (1716)

### The Victory Procession

Banda Siṅgh Bahādur and approximately 740 of his followers were brought to Delhi in February 1716. The Mughal court ordered a **victory procession** that was part triumphal parade, part public humiliation. Banda was placed on a camel — some accounts say he was seated on a woman's palanquin to mock him — and paraded through the streets of Delhi as crowds jeered and threw rubbish.

The Emperor Farrukhsiyar, seated in the Diwān-i-ʿĀm (Hall of Public Audience) at the Red Fort, personally interrogated Banda. The Mughal chronicler Muhammad Qāsim Lāhorī records the exchange:

> Emperor: "You were a Bairagi ascetic. Why did you abandon your religion to follow the Sikh faith?"
> 
> Banda: "I was a worshipper of God as a Bairagi. I am still a worshipper of God as a Sikh. God is one. The difference is that now I worship Him with my sword drawn."

The Emperor asked him to convert to Islam to save his life. Banda refused.

### The Torture

The Mughal court ordered a public execution designed to be as cruel as possible — a spectacle of imperial power intended to terrify the Sikhs into submission. The execution took place on **9 June 1716** in the **Nāhakī Ghāṭī** (the area near the Delhi Gate of the Old City).

The following account is drawn from contemporary Mughal sources (including the *Tārīkh-i-Muhammadī* by Mīrzā Muhammad Qāsim Lāhorī) and Sikh tradition (*Srī Gurū Panth Prakāsh*):

1. **First day** — Banda's 4-year-old son (named Ajay Siṅgh in some accounts) was first brought before him and **torn apart limb from limb** by the executioners while Banda was forced to watch. According to Sikh tradition, the child was offered as a bribe — "Convert to Islam and your son lives." Banda refused. The executioners then grabbed the boy by his arms and legs and pulled until his body was ripped apart. Banda is said to have gazed steadily at the sky, reciting the Mūl Mantar.

2. **Banda's own execution** — After his son's death, Banda was subjected to a prolonged torture. The executioners cut pieces of his flesh with red-hot pincers. His arms, legs, and hands were severed one by one. Through it all, Sikh tradition holds that he continued to recite the Gurū's banī, never once crying out in pain or begging for mercy.

3. **Final death** — His heart was cut out and thrown in his face. His body was then quartered — the standard Mughal punishment for rebellion — and the pieces were hung at the gates of Delhi as a warning.

4. **The 740 followers** — The same day, 740 of Banda's captured followers were executed — beheaded in batches outside the Red Fort. Sikh tradition records that they went to their deaths singing hymns from the Guru Granth Sāhib, their bodies forming a pile of heads and torsos that the Mughals left to rot for days.

The site of the execution is today marked by **Gurdwārā Banda Bahādur** in the Nāhakī Ghāṭī area of Delhi, near the Delhi Gate.

### The Beheading of Bhāī Baj Siṅgh

A particularly revered episode from the executions: Banda's companion **Bhāī Baj Siṅgh** was flayed alive before his execution. When his skin was removed, he is said to have asked the executioners, "Have I turned pale? Has the colour of the Khalsa faded?" This gruesome loyalty has become a symbol of Sikh *shahīdī*.

## Theological and Historical Significance

### In Sikh History

Banda Siṅgh Bahādur's place in Sikh history is unique. He was not a Guru, yet he acted with the full authority of the Guru. He was not born a Sikh, yet he became the greatest military champion the Khalsa had seen. His five-year campaign accomplished what the Gurus had not been able to achieve in a century of Mughal conflict:

- **He established Sikh sovereignty** — The first Sikh state, however brief, proved that the Khalsa could govern as well as fight.
- **He struck the first Sikh coin** — The coinage of Lohgarh declared the political existence of the Sikh people in the language of imperial legitimacy.
- **He broke Mughal invincibility** — The Punjab had been firmly under Mughal control since Akbar's reign. Banda's victories showed that the empire could be beaten in the field.
- **He democratised the Khalsa** — His social reforms, elevating low-caste Jats to positions of authority, gave the Sikh Panth a popular base it had previously lacked among the peasantry.

### In Sikh Theology

Banda's life embodies the Sikh ideal of the *sant-sipāhī* (saint-soldier) more completely than perhaps any figure outside the Gurus themselves:

- **Detachment** — He spent 40 years renouncing the world in the forest, then renounced even his salvation for the sake of the community. This is the highest form of *sevā* (selfless service) in Sikh ethics.
- **Transformation** — His journey from celibate Bairagi to Khalsa general is the most dramatic individual conversion in Sikh history, demonstrating that the Guru's *amrit* can awaken the warrior in even the most withdrawn ascetic.
- **Martyrdom** — His death, with its unflinching refusal to recant even when his son was killed before his eyes, is the standard of *shahīdī* after which all subsequent Sikh martyrs have been measured.

### The Controversy

Banda is not without controversy in Sikh memory. His execution of Wazīr Khān, the destruction of the Sirhind mosque, and the inversion of caste hierarchies provoked criticism even among some contemporary Sikhs. The established Sikh aristocracy — largely Khatris and upper-caste traders — was uneasy with Banda's social radicalism. After his death, the Khalsa was divided between a faction that saw Banda's model of popular sovereignty as the true Sikh path and another faction that sought accommodation with the Mughal successors.

Modern historians also debate Banda's actions. Mughal chroniclers accuse him of indiscriminate violence — they claim that after the capture of Sirhind, Muslims were massacred wholesale and mosques destroyed. Sikh sources reject these claims as imperial propaganda designed to justify the brutal suppression of the uprising. The truth likely lies between: Banda targeted symbols and agents of Mughal authority but did not, on the evidence of the territories he controlled, conduct a general persecution of Muslims.

## Legacy

### In Sikh Memory

Banda Siṅgh Bahādur is remembered by Sikhs as the **first sovereign of the Sikh commonwealth** — the man who proved that the Khalsa could not only fight but rule. His name appears in every Sikh history as the bridge between the era of the Gurus (1469–1708) and the era of Sikh sovereignty (1760s–1849). Key elements of his legacy:

- **Gurdwārā Banda Bahādur** — Delhi — marks the site of his execution in the Nāhakī Ghāṭī, near the Delhi Gate. A gurdwārā built in the late 20th century commemorates his martyrdom.
- **Gurdwārā Gurdās Naṅgal** — In Gurdāspur district, the site of his last siege and capture, is a pilgrimage site.
- **Lohgarh** — The site of his capital remains a symbol of Sikh sovereignty, though the fort itself is in ruins.
- **Fateh Darshan** — The site of the Battle of Chapar Chiri (Sirhind) has a memorial gurdwārā and a 300-foot-tall flagpole (*nishān sāhib*) erected in 2010.

### Commemoration

- **9 June** — The date of Banda's execution is observed as **Shahīdī Diwas** (Martyrdom Day) at Gurdwārā Banda Bahādur in Delhi and at Gurdwārā Gurdās Naṅgal.
- His name is recited in Sikh *ardās* (the daily prayer) alongside the Gurus and other martyrs.
- The phrase **"Banda Bahādur"** (the Brave Slave/One) has entered the Punjabi language as an epithet for any fearless fighter.

### In Historiography

Banda Siṅgh Bahādur has been the subject of intense historiographical debate:

- **British colonial historians** (e.g., Joseph Davey Cunningham) portrayed him as a fanatic whose rebellion was crushed by the "superior civilisation" of the Mughals — a narrative that served British imperial ideology by presenting the Sikhs as a dangerous military power in need of British control.
- **Indian nationalist historians** (e.g., Hari Ram Gupta) cast Banda as a proto-nationalist rebel who led an early peasant revolt against Muslim imperialism, part of a larger 18th-century pattern of anti-Mughal resistance.
- **Sikh historians** (e.g., Professor Ganda Singh) have focused on Banda's role in Sikh history, seeing him as the first military sovereign of the Khalsa who acted on the direct mandate of the Tenth Guru.
- **Marxist historians** (e.g., Irfan Habib) have analysed Banda's uprising as a class-based peasant revolution — a "Jat rising" against the Mughal feudal order, with Sikh religious ideology providing the unifying framework for class struggle.
- **Post-colonial historians** have recently re-examined Banda's social reforms — particularly the elevation of low-caste Jats — as an example of caste-based agrarian radicalism, comparing it to similar movements in early modern Europe and China.

### In Popular Culture

- The 2019 Punjabi film *Banda Siṅgh Bahādur* (directed by Baljinder Singh) dramatised his life.
- His figure appears in Sikh-themed literature, children's books, and kīrtan (devotional music) compositions.
- The **Banda Siṅgh Bahādur International Foundation** (UK-based) promotes his legacy and the history of Sikh sovereignty.

### The Unfinished Sovereignty

Banda's independent state lasted barely five years. But it planted a seed that would ripen over the following decades:

- The **Dal Khalsa** (the Sikh confederacy) emerged within a generation of his death.
- By the 1760s, the Sikhs had expelled the Mughals from the Punjab and established the **Sikh Misls** (twelve sovereign chiefdoms).
- In 1801, **Maharaja Raṇjīt Siṅgh** established the Sikh Empire — the full flowering of the political sovereignty Banda had first claimed at Lohgarh.

When Raṇjīt Siṅgh issued his **Nanakshahi coins** in the early 19th century, he was consciously continuing the tradition Banda had begun:

> "The coin is struck in both worlds, the sword of Nānak and Gobind Siṅgh"

— the same words, the same claim, a century later.
