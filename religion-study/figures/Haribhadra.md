# Haribhadra (Haribhadra Sūri)

> Born: c. 720 CE, Citrakūṭa (modern Chittor, Rajasthan)
> Died: c. 795 CE
> Title: The Jain philosopher who knew all systems (sarvadarśanakovida); great Śvetāmbara Ācārya

Haribhadra (Haribhadra Sūri) is the most ecumenical philosopher in Jain history — a Śvetāmbara monk who systematically studied all six orthodox schools of Indian philosophy (Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika, Sāṅkhya, Yoga, Mīmāṃsā, and Vedānta) and wrote about them with remarkable fairness and accuracy. His **Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya** (Compendium of Six Systems) is the earliest known Jain doxography — a genre that surveys competing philosophical systems side by side. Beyond doxography, he authored major works on yoga (Yogabindu, Yogadṛṣṭisamuccaya), ethics (Dharma Bindu), and logic (Anekāntajayapatākā). Originally a Brahmin from a scholarly family, he converted to Jainism after a humbling debate with Ācārya Jinadatta, an event that shaped his lifelong conviction that no tradition holds a monopoly on truth. His signature contribution to Jain thought was his application of **anekāntavāda** (non-one-sidedness) to inter-religious dialogue — a position that allowed him to affirm the validity of other paths while remaining firmly rooted in the Jain Dhamma. His famous dictum — *Dharma is one; only the perspectives differ* — anticipates the comparative philosophy of the modern era by more than a millennium.

## Conversion Story: The Brahmin Who Became a Jain

Haribhadra's conversion is the most famous story of its kind in Jain hagiography — a tale that encapsulates the Jain values of humility, intellectual honesty, and non-violence even in disputation.

### The Traditional Account

The story, preserved in medieval Śvetāmbara sources such as the *Prabhāvaka Carita* (c. 13th century CE), unfolds in several stages:

**Stage 1 — The Proud Brahmin.** Haribhadra (still bearing his birth name, which tradition does not preserve with certainty) was a young Brahmin of prodigious intellect. He had studied all six orthodox systems and had defeated every scholar who crossed his path. He heard of a Jain Ācārya named Jinadatta who was gaining fame in the region and, burning with competitive pride, resolved to humiliate him.

**Stage 2 — The Debate.** Haribhadra approached Jinadatta and demanded a debate. The Jain monk, seeing the young Brahmin's arrogance, initially declined — saying that a true philosopher does not seek to defeat others but to understand them. Haribhadra insisted. The debate began, and it was a rout — but in the opposite direction Haribhadra had anticipated. Jinadatta systematically dismantled his arguments, not by using Jain-specific premises but by turning Haribhadra's own logical tools against him. Worse, Jinadatta showed gaps in Haribhadra's knowledge of his own tradition — nuances in Nyāya logic, subtleties in Vedāntic interpretation — that the Brahmin had never noticed.

**Stage 3 — Despair and Rescue.** Humiliated beyond endurance, Haribhadra fled to a nearby river and attempted to drown himself. Jinadatta, perceiving the situation through his clairvoyant powers or by following him, pulled him from the water and spoke to him with extraordinary gentleness: *"You sought victory. I sought only truth. If you are truly a seeker of knowledge, come with me — not as a defeated opponent but as a student."*

**Stage 4 — Initiation.** Haribhadra took initiation as a Jain monk. But unlike many conversion stories where the convert renounces the past wholesale, Haribhadra remained a scholar of all systems — he simply became a Jain scholar of all systems. His previous learning was not discarded; it was integrated into a Jain framework.

### The Significance of the Story

The Haribhadra conversion story is remarkable in Jain tradition for what it emphasises and what it does not:

- **No demonisation of the past.** Haribhadra is not portrayed as having been wrong or evil before his conversion — only incomplete. His Brahminical learning is presented as real knowledge, which Jainism completes rather than replaces.
- **The Jain opponent is generous.** Jinadatta saves Haribhadra's life and welcomes him as a student. This stands in stark contrast to conversion stories in some traditions where the old faith is portrayed as false and the convert must repudiate it entirely.
- **Conversion as integration, not rupture.** Haribhadra's identity as a scholar of all systems does not change; what changes is his framework. He becomes the philosopher who can explain every system from within, while holding the Jain perspective as the most comprehensive.
- **The story prefigures his philosophy.** Haribhadra's ecumenical approach — his willingness to grant each system its own validity within its own sphere — is already enacted in how the Jain teacher treats him: not as an enemy to be destroyed but as a truth-seeker to be guided.

## The Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya (Compendium of Six Systems)

### What It Is

The **Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya** (Compendium of Six Systems) is Haribhadra's most famous work and the earliest extant Jain doxography — a genre that systematically surveys and compares competing philosophical schools. The text is written in elegant Sanskrit verse and comprises approximately 90 (depending on recension) verses with Haribhadra's own commentary (vṛtti).

### The Six Systems Surveyed

| School | Key Tenet (as presented by Haribhadra) | Haribhadra's Treatment |
|--------|---------------------------------------|----------------------|
| **Nyāya** (Logic) | Knowledge through inference and reasoning; sixteen categories of debate; the reality of the external world is established through perception and inference | Presented with genuine respect for its logical methodology; Haribhadra uses Nyāya terminology throughout his own works, showing deep engagement |
| **Vaiśeṣika** (Atomism) | Reality consists of seven categories (substance, quality, action, generality, particularity, inherence, non-existence); atoms (paramāṇu) are the building blocks of the material world | Summarised accurately, including the atomistic theory that has parallels to Jain atomism (though Jains reject the Vaiśeṣika concept of a creator God) |
| **Sāṅkhya** (Enumeration) | Evolution of the universe from primordial matter (prakṛti) and pure consciousness (puruṣa); twenty-five tattvas; the world is real but evolves mechanically | Treated with notable sympathy, as Sāṅkhya's dualism of matter and consciousness resonates with the Jain distinction between jīva and ajīva |
| **Yoga** (Discipline) | Practical means of liberation through meditation, concentration, and ethical discipline; the eight limbs (aṣṭāṅga) of Patañjali's system | Haribhadra was deeply influenced by Yoga; his own Yogabindu and Yogadṛṣṭisamuccaya are Jain adaptations of yogic practice |
| **Mīmāṃsā** (Ritual Exegesis) | The Vedas are eternal and authorless; dharma is known only through Vedic injunction; the performance of ritual produces unseen results (apūrva) | Presented fairly, though Haribhadra gently critiques the exclusive focus on external ritual over internal transformation |
| **Vedānta** (End of the Vedas) | Brahman is the sole reality; the world is an appearance (māyā); the individual self (ātman) is ultimately identical with Brahman | Summarised accurately, though Haribhadra critiques the monistic conclusion from the Jain dualist perspective |

### Haribhadra's Method

The Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya is remarkable for the fairness of its presentations. Unlike many doxographies in Indian tradition — which often describe rival schools only to refute them — Haribhadra's work is genuinely descriptive:

- Each school is presented **in its own terms**, using its own technical vocabulary.
- Haribhadra **does not polemicise** within the descriptive section. The Jain perspective is presented separately, at the end, as the synthesis that resolves the partial truths of the six systems.
- The text assumes that **each system is a valid approach to truth from a specific angle** — an application of the Jain principle of *anekānta* (non-one-sidedness) to the history of philosophy.

### Influence

The Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya became the model for later Indian doxographies, including:

- **Sarvadarśanasaṅgraha** (Compendium of All Systems) by Sāyaṇa-Mādhava (14th c.) — a Vedāntic doxography that surveys sixteen schools
- **Ṣaḍdarśanasaṅgraha** by Rāghava (c. 14th c.) — another doxography following Haribhadra's structure
- Various Jain doxographies by later authors such as **Gunaratna Sūri** (15th c.) and **Siddharṣigaṇi**

It remains a core text in Indian philosophy curricula to this day, valued precisely for its fairness and accuracy.

## Other Major Works

### Yogabindu

A manual on **Jain yoga** that lays out the practical path of spiritual development for the Jain householder and monk. The work adapts the framework of Patañjali's Yoga Sūtras to Jain metaphysical principles, replacing the theistic element (īśvara) with the Jain conception of the perfected soul (siddha) as the object of meditation. Key topics:

- The nature of right faith (samyaktva)
- The vows (vratas) as the foundation of yogic practice
- The role of austerity (tapas) in purifying karma
- Meditation (dhyāna) on the Jinas
- The stages of spiritual purification

### Yogadṛṣṭisamuccaya (Compendium of Yogic Perspectives)

A more advanced sequel to the Yogabindu, this work describes **eight stages of yogic vision** (dṛṣṭi) that correspond to progressively higher levels of spiritual realisation. The eight stages — *mitrā* (friendly), *tārā* (guiding), *balā* (powerful), *dīptā* (radiant), *sthira* (stable), *kāntā* (desired), *prabhā* (luminous), and *parā* (supreme) — offer a Jain version of the yogic path that is thoroughly integrated with the Jain karma theory. Each stage is described with:

- Its characteristic mental state
- The level of karmic purification achieved
- The type of meditation appropriate
- The resulting spiritual powers (ṛddhis)
- The relationship to the Jain *guṇasthānas* (stages of spiritual development)

### Dharma Bindu

A concise ethical text on the nature of **dharma** (righteousness, duty, religious merit). The work classifies dharma according to the Jain framework:

- **Dharma as virtue** (puṇya-dharma) — ethical actions that produce meritorious karma
- **Dharma as practice** (cāritra-dharma) — the specific observances of the Jain monastic and lay path
- **Dharma as liberation** (mokṣa-dharma) — the ultimate dharma of transcending all karmic action

The Dharma Bindu is notable for its ecumenical definition of dharma: *"Dharma is that which leads to the welfare of all beings"* — a formulation that any Indian tradition could affirm, yet one that carries specifically Jain commitments when fully unpacked.

### Anekāntajayapatākā (The Victory Banner of Non-One-Sidedness)

A sophisticated work on Jain logic and epistemology that defends the doctrine of **anekāntavāda** against objections from other schools. The text uses the full apparatus of Indian logic (inference, example, reductio ad absurdum) to show that any philosophical position that claims to be the whole truth is self-refuting — because the act of claiming exclusivity contradicts the nature of reality, which is complex and multi-dimensional.

Haribhadra's literary output is vast and includes, by traditional count, over 1,400 works (though many are lost and the figure is almost certainly symbolic). Accepted attributions include:

## Ecumenical Philosophy

### Anekānta as Interreligious Method

Haribhadra's most distinctive contribution is his application of **anekāntavāda** — the Jain doctrine of multiple viewpoints — to the interpretation of other traditions. While earlier Jain thinkers had used anekānta to analyse internal philosophical problems (the relationship between substance and modes, permanence and change), Haribhadra turned it outward, using it as a hermeneutical tool for understanding why non-Jain traditions exist and what validity they possess.

His core argument, developed across multiple works, runs as follows:

1. **Reality is complex** — no finite statement can capture it entirely.
2. **Each philosophical system** grasps one aspect of reality correctly, within its own scope and assumptions.
3. **The errors of a system** are not that it is wholly false but that it mistakes its partial truth for the whole truth.
4. **The Jain perspective** is not a different truth from the others but the framework that can encompass and harmonise all partial truths — because anekānta is the meta-position that acknowledges partiality as such.
5. **Therefore**, a Jain does not need to refute other traditions; they need to *situate* them — showing where they are right and where they exceed their proper scope.

### The Harmony of Dharmas

Haribhadra's approach is captured in a famous verse (from the Dharma Bindu or attributed to him in commentary tradition):

> *"eko dharmaḥ — dṛśyante tu darśaneṣu prabbhedāḥ / yathā tathā ca syād anekānta-mārgaḥ"*
> ("Dharma is one — differences appear only in the perspectives. Such is the path of non-one-sidedness.")

This is not a simple relativism (all views are equally valid) but a **perspectival integration** (each view is valid *within its proper domain*, and the full truth is the integration of all valid perspectives). This mirrors the Jain epistemological concept of *syādvāda* (conditional predication): every statement is true only from a particular standpoint and under particular conditions.

### How He Differs from Other Synthesizers

| Aspect | Haribhadra's Approach | Typical Syncretic Approaches |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| **Relationship to other systems** | Acknowledges their validity within their own scope; does not ask them to become Jain | Often attempts to absorb or subordinate other systems to one's own |
| **Mechanism of integration** | Anekānta preserves the distinctness of each view while showing how they relate | Typically collapses differences into a single overarching framework |
| **Attitude to error** | Error is overextension (taking a partial truth for the whole) | Error is falsehood or ignorance |
| **Goal of inter-tradition study** | Understanding and situating — not refuting or converting | Often apologetic or missionary |
| **Jain identity** | Firmly Jain — anekānta is specifically Jain; the meta-perspective is not neutral | Often blurs boundaries between traditions |

## In Jain Tradition

### Iconography

Haribhadra is depicted in Jain art and manuscript illustrations with the following features:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Posture** | Seated in meditation (padmāsana) or teaching posture (vyākhyāna mudrā), often with a manuscript in hand |
| **Appearance** | Depicted as a mature monk with a shaven head, wearing a white robe (Śvetāmbara convention); sometimes shown with a pointed beard |
| **Attributes** | A book (the Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya or another of his works) and a water pot (kamaṇḍalu); sometimes a *vyākhyāna-mudrā* hand gesture indicating teaching |
| **Distinguishing symbol** | Often shown with six scrolls or six symbols representing the six darśanas he mastered — a unique iconographic motif that marks him as the *sarvadarśanakovida* |
| **Expression** | Calm and intellectual — a philosopher in contemplation, not an ascetic in extreme austerity |

### Temples & Pilgrimage

- **Citrakūṭa (Chittor, Rajasthan)** — His traditional birthplace; a Jain temple complex commemorates him, though the exact site is contested between two locations in the Chittor region
- **Śatruñjaya (Palitana, Gujarat)** — The most sacred Śvetāmbara pilgrimage site; includes shrines to Haribhadra alongside the Tīrthaṅkaras
- **Mount Ābū (Rajasthan)** — The Dilwara temples complex, the finest example of Jain temple architecture, includes iconographic references to Haribhadra
- **Jalore (Rajasthan)** — A temple dedicated to Haribhadra; the region was a centre of his monastic lineage

### In Jain Philosophy

Haribhadra's philosophical significance in Jain tradition is threefold:

**1. Doxographer.** He created the template for comparative philosophy in India. Before Haribhadra, philosophical texts typically either expounded one's own system or refuted others. The Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya created a third genre — the neutral survey — that made it possible to study multiple traditions without immediately taking sides.

**2. Ecumenist.** He articulated a Jain theology of religious pluralism that remains relevant. His position — that other traditions are valid as partial perspectives and that the Jain view harmonises but does not negate them — is arguably the most sophisticated pre-modern Indian argument for inter-religious respect that does not collapse into relativism.

**3. Yoga theorist.** He integrated yogic practice (especially the Pātañjala tradition) into a Jain framework, creating a systematic Jain yoga that later Jain authors (including the great Hemacandra) would develop further. His Yogadṛṣṭisamuccaya remains one of the most detailed descriptions of yogic stages in any Indian tradition.

## Comparative: Ecumenical Philosophers Across Traditions

Haribhadra belongs to a distinctive type in intellectual history: the scholar who comprehensively studies the competing systems of his age and attempts to harmonise or situate them without denying their validity. The following table compares him with analogous figures.

| Aspect | Haribhadra (Jain) | Śaṅkara (Advaita Vedānta) | Vācaspati Miśra (Brahminical) | Sāyaṇa-Mādhava (Vedāntic) | Al-Fārābī (Islamic) | Nicholas of Cusa (Christian) |
|--------|------------------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|---------------------------|---------------------|------------------------------|
| **Tradition** | Śvetāmbara Jainism | Advaita Vedānta (Hindu) | Mīmāṃsā / Vedānta (Hindu) | Advaita Vedānta (Hindu) | Islamic Peripatetic (Falsafa) | Roman Catholic (Christian) |
| **Period** | c. 720–795 CE | c. 700–750 CE | c. 900–980 CE | 14th c. (d. 1387 CE) | c. 872–950 CE | 1401–1464 CE |
| **Project** | Survey the six orthodox darśanas and situate them within the Jain anekānta framework | Write definitive commentaries on the Upaniṣads, Brahma Sūtras, and Bhagavad Gītā that establish Advaita as the correct interpretation | Write sub-commentaries (subsequent interpretations) on all six orthodox systems, harmonising them under a Vedāntic umbrella | Compile a comprehensive doxography of all known systems (Sarvadarśanasaṅgraha) to demonstrate Vedāntic supremacy | Synthesise Aristotelian philosophy with Islamic theology; create a hierarchical model of knowledge that integrates religion and reason | Argue for a "coincidence of opposites" (coincidentia oppositorum) in which all religious traditions converge on the same truth despite different rites |
| **Method** | Descriptive doxography + Jain anekānta as meta-framework | Commentarial — reading the scriptures through the Advaita lens; subordination of other schools as lower levels of truth | Commentarial — harmonising the six systems by showing they address different levels of reality or different types of inquiry | Encyclopedic compilation — presenting each system in its own words, then refuting it from the Vedānta standpoint | Hierarchical integration — assigning each philosophical discipline its proper place within an Islamic cosmos | Speculative theology — arguing that all religious perspectives are partial expressions of the infinite God, who transcends all finite descriptions |
| **Attitude to other systems** | Accepts partial validity; error is overextension, not falsehood | Subordination — other schools are valid at their own level but lead to contradiction when pushed to the ultimate | Harmonisation — apparent contradictions are resolved by distinguishing the domain of each system | Refutation by incorporation — each system is presented and then shown to be incomplete | Integration — Greek philosophy is a valid precursor to Islamic truth, needing only correction where it contradicts revelation | Convergence — all religions point to the same truth; differences are cultural and ritual, not essential |
| **Does the meta-framework belong to one tradition?** | Yes — anekānta is explicitly Jain; the framework is not neutral | Yes — Advaita Vedānta is the only complete truth | Yes — Vedānta (specifically Bhāṭṭa Mīmāṃsā/Vedānta) is the harmonising framework | Yes — Advaita Vedānta is the final truth that encompasses all others | Yes — Islam (as understood by Fārābī's Peripatetic synthesis) is the completion of all prior philosophy | Yes — the Christian (Catholic) revelation is the fullest expression of the truth that all religions partially grasp |
| **Key work** | Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya | Brahmasūtrabhāṣya, commentaries on the ten Upaniṣads | Bhāmatī, Nyāyasūcīnibandha, Tattvakaumudī, Tattvavaiśāradī | Sarvadarśanasaṅgraha | Kitāb al-Milla (Book of Religion), Kitāb al-Jamʿ (Harmony of the Two Philosophers), Iḥṣāʾ al-ʿUlūm (Enumeration of the Sciences) | De Pace Fidei (On the Peace of Faith), De Docta Ignorantia (On Learned Ignorance) |
| **Number of systems studied** | 6 (Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika, Sāṅkhya, Yoga, Mīmāṃsā, Vedānta) | Primarily Vedānta; critiques of Sāṅkhya, Yoga, Mīmāṃsā, Buddhism, and Jainism embedded in his commentaries | All 6 orthodox systems + commentaries on major texts of each | 16 systems in the Sarvadarśanasaṅgraha (including Jainism, Buddhism, Cārvāka) | Greek philosophy (Plato, Aristotle, Neoplatonism), Islamic kalām, Islamic law, Sufi spirituality | Patristic theology, Neoplatonism, medieval scholasticism, Islamic thought, Jewish Kabbalah (limited) |
| **Influence on own tradition** | Defined the Jain approach to comparative philosophy; still the reference point for Jain inter-religious dialogue | Defined classical Advaita; all later Vedānta responds to his framework | Defined the scholastic method of Indian philosophy; his commentaries are still studied | Standard reference for Indian philosophical systems in late medieval and modern scholarship | Shaped Islamic philosophy's understanding of the relationship between reason and revelation; influenced Ibn Sīnā and Maimonides | Developed the concept of "learned ignorance"; influenced later Christian ecumenism and Renaissance humanism |
| **Key limitation** | Does not engage with Buddhist philosophy (which was flourishing in his era); no detailed treatment of Cārvāka materialism | Does not engage fairly with rival schools; his doxography is embedded in refutation | Primarily synthetic rather than original; adds little new philosophical content | A compendium, not an original synthesis; the refutations are brief and formulaic | His synthesis was rejected by orthodox Islamic theologians (e.g., al-Ghazālī) as too rationalistic | His ecumenism was more abstract than practical; his influence was limited outside intellectual circles |

### Why This Comparison Matters

Haribhadra is unique among these figures in several respects:

- **He is the earliest** systematic doxographer in Indian philosophy. Śaṅkara was roughly contemporary but wrote commentaries, not surveys. Vācaspati Miśra came a century later. Sāyaṇa-Mādhava was six centuries later.
- **His doxography is genuinely descriptive**, not polemical. Unlike Śaṅkara (whose doxography is embedded in refutation) or Sāyaṇa-Mādhava (who presents and then refutes), Haribhadra's Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya describes each system on its own terms before offering the Jain perspective as a separate synthesis.
- **His ecumenism arises from a specific doctrine** — anekāntavāda — rather than from a generalised goodwill or political necessity. This gives his pluralism a philosophical grounding that many other ecumenical projects lack.
- **His integration is symmetric**: other systems are not "preparations" for Jainism (as Greek philosophy is a preparation for Islam in Fārābī, or as lower schools are preparations for Advaita in Śaṅkara) but *valid alternative perspectives on the same reality*. This is a genuinely pluralist position, not a disguised supersessionism.
- **His identity remains firmly Jain.** He is not a syncretist who blurs boundaries but a pluralist who maintains his own tradition while granting others their integrity. This is arguably more difficult and more honest than the syncretic approach.
