# Sthūlabhadra

> Born: c. 410 BCE (traditional), Magadha (modern Bihar)
> Died: c. 300 BCE (traditional)
> Title: The Śvetāmbara Ācārya who preserved the Jain canon in the north; compiler of the Cheda Sūtras; convener of the first codification of the Jain Āgamas

Sthūlabhadra is the pivotal figure for the Śvetāmbara tradition — the elder who preserved the Jain canon in Magadha while his teacher [[Bhadrabāhu]] led the southern migration during the Great Famine (c. 350 BCE). As Bhadrabāhu's foremost disciple, he chose to remain in the famine-stricken north when his teacher predicted the catastrophe, and he later presided over the **Council of Pāṭaliputra** (c. 300 BCE), the first systematic attempt to compile the Jain Āgamas in written form. This act — the codification of the surviving oral canon — is remembered as the decisive moment that anchored the Śvetāmbara tradition. He is also traditionally regarded as the author or compiler of the **Cheda Sūtras**, the Jain monastic disciplinary texts. Where Bhadrabāhu represents the last perfect memory of the complete canon, Sthūlabhadra represents the necessary transition from oral to written preservation, from the ideal of perfect memory to the practical work of textual survival. Without him, the Śvetāmbara Āgamas — the only surviving Jain canon — would have been lost.

---

## Biography

The traditional account of Sthūlabhadra's life is preserved in Śvetāmbara sources, especially the *Sthavirāvalī* (lineage list) within Bhadrabāhu's Kalpa Sūtra and later hagiographies such as Hemacandra's *Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra* (12th c.). The following table synthesises the traditional narrative.

| Event | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Name | "Sthūlabhadra" (Sanskrit: *Sthūla* = "great/substantial" + *bhadra* = "auspicious/blessed"; sometimes rendered as *Sthūlabhadra* or *Sthūlabhādra* in Prakrit sources). In some traditions he is also called **Saṃbhūta** or **Vimala** in early references, though Sthūlabhadra is the standard name |
| Birth | c. 410 BCE in Magadha (modern Bihar), the heartland of Jainism. Born into a Brāhmaṇa family, according to Śvetāmbara tradition. Some sources describe him as a prince or a wealthy householder before ordination |
| Early Life | Educated in the Vedic tradition, he was drawn to the Jain monastic order through the influence of [[Bhadrabāhu]], the 8th and final Śruta Kevalin. He became Bhadrabāhu's principal disciple within the lineage of oral transmission descending from [[Mahāvīra]] through [[Jambūsvāmin]] and the chain of Śruta Kevalins |
| Famine Prophecy | When Bhadrabāhu perceived through his *avadhijñāna* (clairvoyant knowledge) that a 12-year Great Famine was about to strike Magadha, he resolved to lead the monastic community south to Karnataka. Sthūlabhadra faced a defining choice: follow his teacher to the south, or remain in Magadha to maintain the Dhamma where it was established |
| Decision to Remain | Sthūlabhadra chose to stay. According to the tradition, he reasoned that the laity could not be abandoned in a time of crisis and that the Dhamma must be kept alive in its original homeland. When he asked Bhadrabāhu's permission, his teacher replied with a prophecy: "You will be the one who preserves what can be preserved. I go south to keep the pure stream; you stay north to keep the living stream." Bhadrabāhu is said to have transferred to Sthūlabhadra what knowledge of the canon he could, knowing that the fourteen *Pūrvas* (the most ancient scriptures) would be largely lost |
| During the Famine | Sthūlabhadra led the remaining monastic community in Magadha through the 12-year famine with immense difficulty. Many monks perished; the oral transmission chain was broken for the most ancient texts (the fourteen *Pūrvas*). Sthūlabhadra weathered the crisis and maintained what could be maintained of the *Aṅgas* (the core canonical texts) and the supplementary literature |
| The Council of Pāṭaliputra | After the famine ended, Sthūlabhadra convened a council of surviving monks at **Pāṭaliputra** (modern Patna, Bihar), then the capital of the Mauryan Empire, under the patronage of Emperor Candragupta Maurya or his successor Bindusāra. This was the first systematic codification of the Jain canon. The monks recited what they could remember of the *Aṅgas* from memory; Sthūlabhadra authenticated and compiled the surviving texts. The council produced the first written recension of eleven *Aṅgas* (of the original twelve), though the fourteen *Pūrvas* were acknowledged as lost |
| Compilation of the Cheda Sūtras | Sthūlabhadra is traditionally credited with authoring or compiling the **Cheda Sūtras** — the six texts of monastic discipline (*Daśāśrutaskandha, Bṛhatkalpa, Vyavahāra, Niśītha, Mahāniśītha, and Jītakalpa*). These texts govern monastic conduct, confession, penance, and the procedures of the Jain monastic order. Their authoritative compilation was essential for maintaining discipline in the post-famine period when the community had been scattered and confused |
| Death (Nirvāṇa) | Sthūlabhadra died at an advanced age (traditional records place his death around c. 300 BCE) after a life devoted to preserving and systematising the Jain canon. The Śvetāmbara tradition commemorates him as a great Ācārya and the patriarch of the Śvetāmbara textual lineage |

## The Decision to Stay: Defining the Śvetāmbara Tradition

The choice Sthūlabhadra made — to remain in Magadha while Bhadrabāhu migrated south — is the single most consequential decision in Jain sectarian history. It is remembered not as a failure of loyalty but as a complementary act of preservation.

### Comparison of the Two Decisions

| Aspect | Bhadrabāhu (Migration South) | Sthūlabhadra (Stay in Magadha) |
|--------|------------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Role** | The 8th Śruta Kevalin; the last human to know the complete canon by heart | The chief disciple; the Ācārya who preserved what could be preserved |
| **Rationale** | The Saṅgha cannot survive 12 years of famine; the pure tradition must be preserved in a new land | The Dhamma must remain in its homeland; the laity need guidance in crisis; the canon must be maintained where it was established |
| **What was taken / left** | Took the deep knowledge of the Pūrvas and the complete Āgamas in memory | Kept the Aṅgas and the practice traditions of the Magadha monastic community |
| **Risk** | The migration itself could fail; the community could be lost in an unfamiliar region | The community could starve; the oral chain could break without enough surviving monks |
| **Outcome** | Established the Digambara tradition in South India; the complete canon died with Bhadrabāhu | The Śvetāmbara canon was codified in written form at Pāṭaliputra; the Āgamas survive in Śvetāmbara recensions |
| **Sectarian consequence** | Digambara tradition: the original canon is lost; existing texts are later reconstructions | Śvetāmbara tradition: 11 of 12 Aṅgas are preserved; the canon exists in written form |
| **Attitude to nudity** | Maintained strict nudity (*digambara* = "sky-clad") as the original ascetic ideal | Accepted white robes (*śvetāmbara* = "white-clad") as a practical adaptation for settled monastic life |

### Why Sthūlabhadra's Decision Matters

Sthūlabhadra's choice reflects a distinctly Śvetāmbara understanding of the monastic life:

1. **Continuity over purity**: Rather than preserve the pure tradition in isolation (the Digambara ideal), Sthūlabhadra chose to keep the tradition alive in its original context, even at the cost of some textual loss.
2. **The community over the individual**: Staying meant likely death for many monks, but it also meant maintaining the Jain presence in the Gangetic heartland — the region where kings and patrons could support the Saṅgha.
3. **Adaptation for survival**: The acceptance of white robes (and other adaptations) is rooted in this moment. For Sthūlabhadra, the survival of the Dhamma mattered more than the strict maintenance of every external form.

## The Council of Pāṭaliputra (c. 300 BCE)

### Historical Context

The Council of Pāṭaliputra was the first known attempt to codify the Jain Āgamas in written form. It took place in the Mauryan capital, Pāṭaliputra (modern Patna), likely during the reign of Candragupta Maurya (c. 321–297 BCE) or his son Bindusāra (c. 297–273 BCE). The council was a direct response to the crisis of transmission caused by the Great Famine.

### Proceedings

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Convenor** | Sthūlabhadra, as the senior surviving Ācārya of the northern community |
| **Location** | Pāṭaliputra (modern Patna, Bihar) — the capital of the Mauryan Empire |
| **Date** | c. 300 BCE (traditional); exact dating is debated, with some scholars placing it earlier (c. 350–340 BCE) or later (c. 250 BCE) |
| **Participants** | Surviving monks of the Magadha community who had retained portions of the Āgamas through oral memory |
| **Patron** | Traditionally associated with Candragupta Maurya, though the connection is not universally accepted |
| **Method** | The monks recited what they could remember of the twelve *Aṅgas*; Sthūlabhadra presided, authenticated the recitations, and oversaw the compilation |
| **Result** | Eleven of the twelve *Aṅgas* were compiled. The twelfth *Aṅga* (the *Dṛṣṭivāda*, which contained the fourteen *Pūrvas*) was acknowledged as lost — no monk present could recite it fully |
| **Format** | The canon was committed to writing, marking the transition from purely oral transmission to written preservation. This is the origin of the Śvetāmbara Āgamic corpus |

### Significance

The Council of Pāṭaliputra is understood differently by the two Jain traditions:

- **Śvetāmbara view**: The council successfully preserved the core canon. The eleven *Aṅgas* compiled at Pāṭaliputra are the authentic teaching of Mahāvīra, transmitted through the chain of Śruta Kevalins and now fixed in writing. The loss of the *Dṛṣṭivāda* is regrettable but does not undermine the authority of the surviving texts.
- **Digambara view**: The council failed. Without Bhadrabāhu — the last Śruta Kevalin — the monks could not recall the authentic canon. The eleven *Aṅgas* compiled at Pāṭaliputra are at best partial reconstructions. The true Jain canon was lost with the death of Bhadrabāhu.

This difference remains the most significant textual disagreement between the two sects.

## The Cheda Sūtras

Sthūlabhadra is traditionally regarded as the author or compiler of the **Cheda Sūtras** (Prakrit: *Chedasutta*), a group of six texts within the Śvetāmbara Jain canon that deal specifically with monastic discipline, penance, and the procedures of the monastic order.

### The Six Cheda Sūtras

| Text | Prakrit Name | Content | Significance |
|------|-------------|---------|-------------|
| **Daśāśrutaskandha** | *Dasāsuyakkhandha* | Ten chapters on monastic conduct, including rules for the *cāturmāsa* (rainy-season retreat), the procedure for initiation (*dīkṣā*), and the conduct of nuns | The most comprehensive single text on monastic discipline; covers ten foundational topics |
| **Bṛhatkalpa** | *Mahākappa* | The "Great Code" — detailed rules on expiation and penance (*prāyaścitta*) for various monastic offences | The most authoritative text on monastic penance; establishes the graduated system of punishments |
| **Vyavahāra** | *Vavahāra* | Procedural rules for the monastic community: how offences are to be judged, how cases are to be decided, and how reconciliation is achieved | The "legal code" of the Jain Saṅgha — analogous to the Vinaya Piṭaka in Buddhism |
| **Niśītha** | *Nisīha* | Rules regarding prohibitions and the consequences of violating them; a companion to the Vyavahāra | Establishes what is forbidden and the penalties for transgression |
| **Mahāniśītha** | *Mahānisīha* | An expanded version of the Niśītha with additional materials; sometimes considered a supplement | Contains detailed lists of offences and their corresponding penances |
| **Jītakalpa** | *Jīyākappa* | Rules of conduct for ascetics regarding the acceptance of food, alms, and the use of possessions | Focuses on the practical details of the mendicant life — what a monk may accept, when, and from whom |

### Importance in the Śvetāmbara Tradition

The Cheda Sūtras are foundational to Śvetāmbara monastic practice:

- They provide the **legal framework** within which the monastic order operates — analogous to the Vinaya Piṭaka in Buddhism.
- They establish the **system of penance** (*prāyaścitta*) that allows monks to confess and purify transgressions, maintaining the purity of the order.
- They define the **hierarchy of offences** — from minor infractions requiring simple confession to grave offences requiring temporary or permanent expulsion.
- They codify the **procedures for initiation, censure, reconciliation, and readmission** — the corporate life of the Saṅgha.
- Their attribution to Sthūlabhadra gives them **authoritative lineage** — they are seen as the authentic monastic code of the Śvetāmbara tradition, directly connected to Mahāvīra through the chain of Ācāryas.

## In Jain Tradition

### Iconography

Sthūlabhadra is depicted in Śvetāmbara art with the following features:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Posture** | Seated in meditation (*padmāsana*) or in a teaching posture; often shown with a manuscript or palm-leaf book in his hands — the Cheda Sūtras or the Āgamas |
| **Clothing** | White robes, consistent with Śvetāmbara convention; sometimes shown with a shaven head and a thin beard |
| **Attributes** | A manuscript (representing the Āgamas he compiled) and a water pot (*kamaṇḍalu*); sometimes depicted with a *vyākhyāna mudrā* (teaching gesture) |
| **Expression** | Contemplative and serene, with a focus on intellectual concentration rather than extreme asceticism |
| **Distinguishing feature** | Often shown in the company of [[Bhadrabāhu]] as his disciple, or alone receiving the canon from the surviving monks at Pāṭaliputra |
| **Symbolic colour** | White or golden, signifying purity and wisdom |

### Temples & Pilgrimage

- **Pāṭaliputra (Patna, Bihar)** — The site of the council; no specific monument marks the event, but the city is venerated as the place where the Āgamas were first codified in writing.
- **Śatruñjaya (Palitana, Gujarat)** — The most sacred Śvetāmbara pilgrimage site; includes shrines to Sthūlabhadra alongside the Tīrthaṅkaras and other great Ācāryas.
- **Mount Ābū (Rajasthan)** — The Dilwara temples include iconographic references to the early Ācāryas, including Sthūlabhadra.
- **Various Jain temples in Bihar** — Sites associated with the early post-Mahāvīra Saṅgha in Magadha, some of which preserve traditions of Sthūlabhadra's presence.

### Key Texts

| Text | Author / Attribution | Significance |
|------|---------------------|--------------|
| **Cheda Sūtras** (six texts) | Sthūlabhadra (traditional) | The monastic disciplinary code of the Śvetāmbara tradition; foundational for the legal and penitential framework of the Saṅgha |
| **Kalpa Sūtra** (Sthavirāvalī section) | Bhadrabāhu | Includes the lineage list that records Sthūlabhadra's succession and his role as Bhadrabāhu's chief disciple |
| **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (Lives of the 63 Illustrious Men) | Hemacandra (12th c.) | Contains the most complete Śvetāmbara hagiography of Sthūlabhadra |
| **Āvaśyaka Niryukti** | Attributed to Bhadrabāhu but expanded by later Ācāryas | Includes references to Sthūlabhadra's role in the Pāṭaliputra Council |
| **Nandī Sūtra** | Anonymous Śvetāmbara text | A catalog of canonical knowledge that mentions Sthūlabhadra as a key figure in the transmission history |

### Festivals & Commemorations

- **Sthūlabhadra Jayantī** — Observed by the Śvetāmbara community, especially in Gujarat and Rajasthan, with recitations of the Cheda Sūtras and lectures on his role in preserving the canon.
- **Paryuṣaṇā** — The annual 8-day festival (August–September) is intimately connected to Sthūlabhadra's legacy: the central ritual of this festival — the recitation of the Kalpa Sūtra — is possible only because the textual tradition he preserved survives.
- **Āgama Pūjā** — Ritual honouring of the Jain scriptures, especially in Śvetāmbara temples; includes commemoration of the Pāṭaliputra Council as the moment when the canon was fixed.

### In Jain Philosophy

Sthūlabhadra's philosophical significance is more institutional than doctrinal — he did not innovate philosophically but created the conditions for all subsequent Jain philosophy.

| Contribution | Explanation |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Fixation of the canon** | Before Sthūlabhadra, the Jain teaching existed primarily as an oral tradition. After the Pāṭaliputra Council, the Śvetāmbara tradition had a fixed written text that could be studied, commented upon, and transmitted across generations |
| **Śvetāmbara identity** | Sthūlabhadra's decision to stay in Magadha and his work at the council created the distinct Śvetāmbara textual identity — a canon that is preserved in written form, adapted to settled monastic life, and centred on the eleven surviving Aṅgas |
| **Legal foundation** | His compilation of the Cheda Sūtras provided the disciplinary backbone of the Śvetāmbara Saṅgha — the rules by which monks live, confess, and maintain the purity of the order |
| **Bridge figure** | Sthūlabhadra bridges the period of the Śruta Kevalins (perfect oral memory) and the period of written scripture. His decision to codify the canon in writing marks the end of the "age of memory" and the beginning of the "age of the book" in Jain history |
| **Model of adaptation** | In choosing to adapt (by staying, by accepting white robes, by codifying imperfect memory rather than insisting on perfect preservation), Sthūlabhadra exemplified a characteristically Śvetāmbara approach to crisis: preservation through adaptation rather than preservation through isolation |

## Comparative: Canon-Preserving Sthaviras / Elders Across Traditions

Sthūlabhadra belongs to a specific type of religious figure: the elder (Sanskrit: *sthavira*; Pāli: *thera*) who, at a moment of crisis, takes responsibility for gathering, authenticating, and preserving the community's authoritative texts. The following table places him alongside analogous elder figures from other traditions.

| Aspect | Sthūlabhadra (Jain Śvetāmbara) | Mahākassapa (Buddhist Theravāda) | Sāriputta (Buddhist) | Ezra (Jewish) | Peter / Clement (Christian) | Zayd ibn Thābit (Islamic) |
|--------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------|---------------------|---------------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Tradition** | Śvetāmbara Jainism | Theravāda Buddhism | Buddhism (pre-sectarian) | Second Temple Judaism | Early Christianity | Sunni Islam |
| **Title / Role** | Ācārya, convener of the Pāṭaliputra Council | President of the First Buddhist Council (Rājagaha); foremost in ascetic practices (*dhutaṅga*) | The Buddha's chief disciple in wisdom; systematiser of the Dhamma | Scribe and priest who restored the Torah after the Babylonian Exile | Apostles; Peter as the "rock" of the Church; Clement as early Church father who transmitted apostolic tradition | The Prophet's scribe; compiler of the first written Quranic codex |
| **Crisis** | The Great Famine (c. 350 BCE) — monastic community scattered, oral transmission chain broken, the complete canon on the verge of being lost | The Buddha's death (c. 483 BCE) — Subhadda's remark that the Dhamma could now be ignored; risk of the teaching fragmenting among different disciples | The Buddha's death — no single authority left; risk that the Dhamma would be interpreted in contradictory ways | Babylonian Exile (c. 586–539 BCE) — the Temple destroyed, the Torah scrolls lost or neglected, national and religious identity threatened | The death of Jesus (c. 30 CE) — the apostles scattered, risk of the teachings being distorted or lost as the community spread across the Roman world | The Battle of Yamāma (632 CE) — many *qurrā'* (Quran reciters) killed; risk of the Quranic text being lost or corrupted through the death of memorisers |
| **Action** | Remained in Magadha during the famine; convened the Pāṭaliputra Council to compile the Āgamas in written form; compiled the Cheda Sūtras | Convened and presided over the First Council of Rājagaha; questioned Ānanda (Suttas) and Upāli (Vinaya) to authenticate each teaching, suppressing the monk Subhadda | Served as the Buddha's right-hand disciple during his lifetime; systematised the Dhamma into the framework that later became the Abhidhamma Piṭaka | Led the return to Jerusalem; read the rediscovered Torah to the assembled people; led a religious covenant renewal; established the Great Assembly that canonised the Hebrew Bible | Preserved the apostolic teaching through oral and written transmission; Peter's role as the authoritative witness of Jesus's ministry; Clement's letters established early Christian textual authority | Commissioned by Abu Bakr to collect the Quranic revelations into a single codex; later supervised ʿUthmān's standardisation of the text |
| **What was preserved** | Eleven *Aṅgas* of the Jain Āgamas (out of twelve); the Cheda Sūtras (monastic discipline); the Śvetāmbara tradition itself | The Sutta Piṭaka (discourses) and the Vinaya Piṭaka (monastic rules) as recited at the First Council | The framework of the Dhamma — the categories, lists, and analytical structures that later became the Abhidhamma | The Torah (Pentateuch) in a definitive written form; the foundation for the later canonisation of the Prophets and Writings | The *regula fidei* (rule of faith) — the core apostolic teaching — and the Gospels and Epistles that embodied it | The Quran in a single written codex (the *muṣḥaf*); later standardised by ʿUthmān |
| **Method** | Oral recitation + written compilation — the first written fixation of the Jain canon | Oral cross-examination — questioning monks who had heard the Buddha's discourses; recitation verified by group consensus | Oral teaching and systematisation during the Buddha's lifetime — creating the analytical frameworks that would organise the canon | Reading from a rediscovered scroll; public proclamation; priestly teaching | Oral tradition (the *paradosis* or "handing-down") and written letters; Peter as the "witness" (martys) of Jesus's life and resurrection | Written compilation — collecting scattered palm-leaf and bone fragments, oral recitations from memorisers, and the testimony of witnesses who heard the Prophet |
| **Post-crisis canon status** | The Śvetāmbara canon survives in written form; the Digambara tradition does not accept its authenticity; the fourteen Pūrvas are acknowledged as lost by both sects | The Pāli Tipiṭaka becomes the definitive Theravāda canon; all schools accept the First Council's recensions in principle, though the actual texts developed over centuries | The Dhamma was transmitted intact; Sāriputta's analytical approach influenced the development of the Abhidhamma, though its final form postdates him | The Torah is restored and becomes the central scripture of Judaism; the canon of the Hebrew Bible is formally closed in later centuries | The New Testament canon emerges over the next three centuries; Peter and Clement's authority becomes foundational for the apostolic succession of bishops | The Uthmanic codex becomes the universal Quranic text; all variant readings are suppressed |
| **Sectarian significance** | The founder-figure of the Śvetāmbara textual tradition; his decision to stay defines the Śvetāmbara approach to preservation through adaptation | The founder-figure of the Theravāda monastic tradition; his ascetic rigour and concern for textual purity shape the Theravāda identity | Revered across all Buddhist schools; the systematisation of the Dhamma that he began is foundational for all later Buddhist philosophy | Revered in both Jewish and Christian traditions; the model of the scribe-priest who restores Scripture after calamity | Peter is the foundational figure for Roman Catholic claims of papal authority; Clement is numbered among the Apostolic Fathers | The Uthmanic codex is accepted by all Muslims as the authentic Quran; Shīʿa tradition has a different narrative about the compilation but accepts the same text |
| **Relationship to the last "perfect knower"** | Disciple of Bhadrabāhu (the last Śruta Kevalin); represents the transition from perfect oral memory to written preservation | Successor to the Buddha (the perfectly awakened one); the council ensured the teaching survived the death of its source | Worked alongside the Buddha during his lifetime; the systematisation began before the Master's death | Restored the Torah after the loss of the First Temple; the prophets had ceased and the written word replaced living prophecy | Succeeded Jesus (the incarnate Word); the apostolic tradition replaced the living presence of the Teacher | Compiled the Quran after the Prophet's death; the written text replaced living revelation |
| **Key difference** | Sthūlabhadra presided over a partial and acknowledged loss — the Śvetāmbara canon is explicitly incomplete; this shapes Jain humility about textual authority | Mahākassapa successfully transmitted the canon substantially intact; the Theravāda tradition claims unbroken transmission from the First Council | Sāriputta's work was done during the Buddha's lifetime, not after a crisis of survival; his systematisation was seen as deepening the Dhamma, not rescuing it | Ezra successfully restored the Torah; the canon was not only preserved but expanded in the post-exilic period | The Christian canon was not closed in Peter's or Clement's lifetime; the process of canonisation took centuries | ʿUthmān's standardisation closed the text definitively; Islamic tradition holds that the Quran is perfectly preserved, with no acknowledged loss |

### Why This Comparison Matters

Sthūlabhadra is unique among these elder-preserver figures in several respects:

1. **He is the only figure who presided over a *partial preservation*.** Unlike Mahākassapa (whose council produced the complete Tipiṭaka), Ezra (who restored the full Torah), or Zayd (whose codex became the definitive Quran), Sthūlabhadra's council codified eleven out of twelve Aṅgas and acknowledged that the fourteen Pūrvas were lost forever. The Śvetāmbara tradition is the only major religious tradition that formally acknowledges the permanent loss of part of its original scripture.

2. **His work is explicitly contested.** The Digambara tradition does not accept the Śvetāmbara canon as authentic. No Christian tradition rejects the New Testament canon, no Jewish tradition rejects the Torah, and no Muslim tradition rejects the Quranic text. Only in Jainism is the very act of canon-preservation disputed between two major traditions.

3. **He chose adaptation over purity.** Every other figure in the comparison operated within a consensus about what preservation meant — collect the authentic texts, fix them, transmit them faithfully. Sthūlabhadra had to make a different kind of decision: preserve what you can, accept what has been lost, and adapt the form of the tradition (written over oral, clothed over naked, settled over wandering) to ensure survival.

4. **His decision created a tradition.** Without Sthūlabhadra, there would be no Śvetāmbara Jainism — or at least no Śvetāmbara canon. The textual tradition that defines the Śvetāmbara community — the eleven Aṅgas, the Cheda Sūtras, and the entire commentarial literature that depends on them — traces its institutional origin to his choice.

5. **He represents the "elder" type in its purest form.** The term *sthavira* (elder) in Indian tradition denotes not merely seniority but the specific authority of one who has preserved and transmitted the teaching accurately. Sthūlabhadra embodies this ideal: an elder who did not create new doctrine but ensured the survival of the old.

## Sthūlabhadra in Śvetāmbara-Digambara Dispute

Sthūlabhadra is a contested figure between the two Jain traditions:

| Aspect | Śvetāmbara View | Digambara View |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Status** | A great Ācārya and the saviour of the canon; the second most important figure after Bhadrabāhu in the transmission chain | A well-meaning but limited monk who could not preserve the complete canon; his council produced only partial and unreliable texts |
| **Decision to stay** | An act of courage and compassion — sustaining the Dhamma where it was needed most | A failure of discipleship — he should have followed his teacher and preserved the pure tradition |
| **The Pāṭaliputra Council** | The foundational act of the Śvetāmbara canon; an authoritative codification | A failed attempt that produced a corrupt version of the teaching; the authentic canon was lost with Bhadrabāhu |
| **Cheda Sūtras** | Authentic works of Sthūlabhadra, preserving the ancient monastic discipline | Later compositions not connected to the original teaching |
| **White robes** | Accepted by Sthūlabhadra as a practical necessity; the Śvetāmbara tradition follows his example | Evidence of compromise; the Digambara tradition rejects robe-wearing as a deviation from Mahāvīra's original teaching |
| **Overall significance** | The Śvetāmbara tradition exists because of him | The Śvetāmbara tradition exists *despite* him — it is a diminished version of the original Dhamma |

Despite this disagreement, no Jain tradition denies Sthūlabhadra's historical reality or his importance. He is acknowledged by both traditions as the decisive figure who determined the shape of the northern, Śvetāmbara tradition.
