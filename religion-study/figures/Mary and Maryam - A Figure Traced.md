# Mary & Maryam — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

Mary is the rare shared figure the Qur'an arguably honours *more* than the New
Testament does: she is the **only woman named in the entire Qur'an**, and a
whole surah ([[019 Maryam|Maryam, 19]]) bears her name. Both scriptures present
her as the chosen, pure virgin mother of Jesus. The pairing extends
[[Birth of Jesus Across Scriptures]] by focusing on **Mary herself**. A
**genealogical** connection (see [[Shared Abrahamic Figures]]).

## "Blessed among women" / "chosen above all women"
The two scriptures use almost interchangeable language of election:

> [!quote] Luke 1 — highly favoured, blessed among women
> **1:28** — "Hail, thou that art **highly favoured**, the Lord is with thee: **blessed art thou among women**."

> [!quote] Al-Imran 3:42 — chosen, purified, preferred
> "O Mary! Lo! Allah hath **chosen thee and made thee pure**, and hath **preferred thee above (all) the women of creation**."

Both single Mary out from all women; both stress her **purity** and her
acceptance of an overwhelming calling.

## Her own piety, not just her motherhood
The Qur'an gives Mary a devotional life **before** the annunciation — raised in
the temple under the prophet Zachariah, miraculously provided for:

> [!quote] Al-Imran 3:37, 43
> **3:37** — "made **Zachariah her guardian**. Whenever Zachariah went into the sanctuary where she was, he found that she **had food**… It is from Allah."
> **3:43** — "O Mary! Be obedient to thy Lord, **prostrate thyself and bow** with those who bow (in worship)."

The Gospels say little of Mary's childhood (that material is in the later
*Protoevangelium of James*, not the canonical text). The Qur'an makes her
temple upbringing and personal worship central.

## Her response: the Magnificat vs. the vow of silence
Here the portraits diverge in tone. In Luke, Mary answers with **submission and
then song** — the Magnificat, one of the great hymns of the Bible:

> [!quote] Luke 1:38, 46–48
> "Behold the **handmaid of the Lord**; be it unto me according to thy word."
> "**My soul doth magnify the Lord**… from henceforth **all generations shall call me blessed**."

In the Qur'an, after the lonely birth beneath the palm-tree (see
[[Birth of Jesus Across Scriptures]]), Mary is told to answer her accusers not
with words but with a **vow of silence**, letting the infant speak for her:

> [!quote] Maryam 19:20, 26
> **19:20** — "How can I have a son when **no mortal hath touched me**, neither have I been unchaste?"
> **19:26** — "if thou meetest any mortal, say: Lo! I have **vowed a fast** unto the Beneficent, and **may not speak this day** to any mortal."

Luke's Mary **proclaims**; the Qur'an's Maryam **keeps silence** and is
vindicated by the miracle of her speaking child.

## Same woman, shared reverence
| | Gospels (Luke) | Qur'an |
|---|---|---|
| Election | "blessed among women" | "chosen… above all women" |
| Virginity | affirmed | affirmed |
| Upbringing | not described in canon | raised in the temple by Zachariah |
| Her response | the **Magnificat** (she sings) | a **vow of silence** (the baby speaks) |
| Status | mother of the Lord | only woman named in the Qur'an; a surah bears her name |

Mary is the figure where the two traditions are perhaps **least** in conflict —
the disagreement is about her *son's* nature (see
[[Jesus and Isa - A Figure Traced]]), not about her. A reminder that the
Abrahamic family shares not only stories but genuine, overlapping reverence.
