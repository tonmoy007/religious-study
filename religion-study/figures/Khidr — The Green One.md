# Khidr — The Green One

**al-Khidr** ("the Green One") — the mysterious, wise servant of God who taught Moses patience and divine wisdom in the strangest journey of the Qur'an. He is one of the most enigmatic figures in Islamic tradition — not explicitly called a prophet, but possessing knowledge beyond prophecy.

---

## Identity

The Qur'an does not name Khidr. It calls him **one of Our servants** to whom God gave **mercy and knowledge** (18:65).

Islamic tradition names him **al-Khidr** ("the Green One") because wherever he sat, the earth turned green. He is identified by some as:

- A **prophet** (Ibn Abbas: he was a prophet)
- A **righteous servant** (not a prophet, but *walī* — a friend of God)
- The son of Adam (minority view)
- The same as the biblical **Melchizedek** (some comparative scholars)
- A being who will remain alive until the end of time (Sufi tradition)

---

## The Journey with Moses

See the full narrative: [[Moses and al-Khidr — The Hidden Wisdom]]

Moses was asked: "Who is the most knowledgeable of people?" He replied: "I am." God revealed that there was one who knew more — and sent Moses to find Khidr.

The journey involved three acts:

1. **Sinking the boat** — Khidr damaged a boat, seemingly without reason. Explanation: the boat belonged to poor fishermen, and a tyrant was seizing all seaworthy vessels — Khidr made it seem defective so they could keep it.

2. **Killing the boy** — Khidr killed a young boy, seemingly a senseless murder. Explanation: the boy would have grown to oppress his believing parents — God would replace him with a better child.

3. **Restoring the wall** — Khidr rebuilt a wall in a hostile town that had refused them hospitality. Explanation: the wall concealed a treasure belonging to two orphan boys — its righteous father had built it, and God willed it restored for them.

---

## Key Tafsir Insights

**Theodicy's defence:** The story answers the deepest question: "Why does God allow bad things?" Each act reveals a hidden mercy — damage to prevent worse damage.

**Patience as the lesson:** Moses couldn't stop questioning. Khidr's refrain: "Did I not tell you you cannot bear with me?" The story teaches that divine wisdom may be inscrutable but always just.

**Knowledge beyond prophecy:** Khidr had *ladunnī* (direct from God) knowledge — not from scripture or law, but from divine intimacy. This is the basis of the Sufi concept of esoteric knowledge (*bāṭin*).

---

## Legacy

- Central figure in **Sufi mysticism** — symbol of direct divine knowledge
- Said to be alive, wandering the earth, appearing to saints
- Honoured in annual festivals in some Muslim cultures
- The subject of Rumi and Ibn Arabi's most profound commentaries
