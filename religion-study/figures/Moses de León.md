# Moses de León (משה די ליאון — Moshe ben Shem-Tov)

The author or compiler of the *Zohar* (ספר הזוהר — Sefer ha-Zohar, "Book of Radiance"), the foundational text of [[Kabbalah|Kabbalistic]] mysticism. Moses de León produced the Zohar in the late 13th century, attributing it to the 2nd-century sage Rabbi [[Shimon bar Yochai]]. His work transformed Jewish spirituality and remains the central text of Jewish mystical tradition.

## Key Contributions

- ***Sefer ha-Zohar* (The Zohar, c. 1280–1290 CE):** A multi-volume mystical commentary on the Torah written largely in Aramaic. It presents the inner, esoteric dimension of Torah — the *sodot* (secrets) of creation, the nature of God (Ein Sof and the ten *Sefirot*), the structure of the cosmos, the soul's journey, and the redemptive meaning of history.
- **Kabbalistic Innovations:** The Zohar introduced or developed core kabbalistic concepts: the *Sefirot* as divine emanations, *Ein Sof* (the Infinite), *tzimtzum* (divine contraction — though this is more Lurianic), *gilgul neshamot* (reincarnation of souls), and *tikkun* (cosmic repair).
- **Pseudepigraphic Attribution:** De León presented the Zohar as the teachings of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai (Rashbi), transmitted secretly for a millennium. This attribution gave the work ancient authority but has been questioned since de León's own time.
- **Bridge Between Philosophic and Mystic Judaism:** The Zohar synthesised earlier Jewish mystical traditions (Sefer Yetzirah, Heikhalot literature) with the intellectual frameworks of medieval Jewish philosophy, creating a comprehensive mystical theology.

## Life & Legacy

- Born in León in the Kingdom of Castile, he spent most of his life in Guadalajara and Ávila
- He circulated the Zohar manuscripts from around 1280, claiming they were copies of an ancient text brought from the Land of Israel
- A story in Isaac of Acre's writings recounts a wealthy man who offered to support de León's son-in-law; the son-in-law reportedly confessed that his father-in-law had composed the Zohar from his own mind
- The controversy over authorship has never fully settled — traditional Kabbalists accept Shimon bar Yochai as author; critical scholars view de León as the primary author or compiler
- The Zohar profoundly influenced Isaac Luria's school in Safed, early Hasidism, and virtually all later Jewish mysticism
