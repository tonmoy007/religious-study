# Hannah Senesh (חנה סנש — Hannah Szenes)

A poet, paratrooper, and national heroine whose brief life became a symbol of courage, sacrifice, and the Zionist ideal of self-determination. Senesh volunteered for a British military mission to rescue Hungarian Jews during the Holocaust, was captured, tortured, and executed at age 23. Her poetry — especially the haunting *Eli, Eli* — has become iconic in Israeli and Jewish culture.

## Key Contributions

- **Paratrooper Rescue Mission:** In 1944, Senesh joined a special British Army unit (the Special Operations Executive, SOE) that parachuted Jewish volunteers into Nazi-occupied Europe to organise resistance and rescue Jews. Her mission to Hungary aimed to reach Budapest and help organise escape routes for Hungarian Jews being deported to Auschwitz.
- ***Eli, Eli* (My God, My God):** Her most famous poem, written in 1942, evokes the pain of individual suffering against the vastness of the universe. The line "Eli, Eli, shelo yigamer le'olam" (My God, my God, may it never end — the sand and the sea, the rustle of the water, the lightning of the heavens, the prayer of man) has been set to music countless times and is sung at Israeli memorial ceremonies and Holocaust commemorations worldwide.
- ***Halikha LeKesariya* (A Walk to Caesarea):** Another of her poems, written in 1942 near Caesarea in British Palestine, captures her sense of wonder at the Mediterranean landscape. It too became a famous song, recorded by many Israeli artists.
- **Diary and Letters:** Senesh's diary and letters, published posthumously as *Hannah Senesh: Her Life and Diary*, document her intellectual and spiritual journey from a Hungarian Jewish childhood through Zionist awakening, immigration to Palestine, and her final months as a paratrooper. They are among the most widely read personal accounts of the Holocaust generation.
- **Symbol of Zionist Heroism:** Senesh became the most iconic symbol of the Yishuv's response to the Holocaust — the determination to fight back, to rescue, and to assert Jewish agency. Her story is taught in Israeli schools and commemorated in numerous museums, monuments, and cultural works.

## Life & Legacy

- Born into an assimilated, well-to-do Jewish family in Budapest; her father was a playwright and journalist who died when she was young
- Experienced growing antisemitism in Hungary and developed a passionate Zionism, immigrating to British Palestine (Eretz Yisrael) in 1939 at age 18
- Trained at the agricultural school in Nahalal before joining the Haganah and then the British SOE paratrooper program
- Parachuted into Yugoslavia in March 1944, crossed into Hungary, and was captured by Hungarian authorities in June 1944
- Imprisoned and tortured for months but refused to reveal any operational details; executed by firing squad on 7 November 1944
- Her remains were brought to Israel in 1950 and reinterred on Mount Herzl in Jerusalem; a moshav (Yad HaSenesh) and several streets are named after her
