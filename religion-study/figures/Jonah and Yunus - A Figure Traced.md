# Jonah & Yunus — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

The prophet swallowed by a great fish is one of the most **tightly matched**
stories across the Bible and the Qur'an — and important enough that the Qur'an
names a **whole surah** after him ([[010 Yunus|Yunus, 10]]). The shared shape is
exact; the difference is in *emphasis*. A clear **genealogical** connection (see
[[Shared Abrahamic Figures]]).

## The shared story
| Beat | [[bible-kjv/32 Jonah\|Book of Jonah]] | Qur'an (As-Saaffat 37 / Al-Anbiya 21) |
|---|---|---|
| He flees his mission | "Jonah rose up to **flee unto Tarshish** from the presence of the LORD" (1:3) | "When he **fled** unto the laden ship" (37:140) |
| Cast into the sea | "they… **cast him forth into the sea**" (1:15) | (the ship; he is thrown to the fish) (37:141–142) |
| Swallowed by the fish | "the LORD had prepared a **great fish** to swallow up Jonah… **three days and three nights**" (1:17) | "the **fish swallowed him** while he was blameworthy" (37:142) |
| Saved by prayer | Jonah prays from the fish's belly (ch. 2) | "had he not been one of those who **glorify (Allah)** he would have tarried in its belly…" (37:143–144) |
| Cast out | "it **vomited out Jonah** upon the dry land" (2:10) | "We **cast him on a desert shore** while he was sick" (37:145) |
| The city repents | "the **people of Nineveh believed God**… put on sackcloth" (3:5) | "We sent him to **a hundred thousand or more**; and **they believed**" (37:147–148) |

The skeleton — *flight → sea → fish → prayer → deliverance → the great city
repents* — is essentially identical, beat for beat.

## The prayer in the darkness
The Qur'an's most memorable Jonah moment names him **Dhū'n-Nūn** ("the man of
the fish") and gives his cry from inside the fish a famous formula:

> [!quote] Al-Anbiya 21:87
> "And (mention) **Dhu'n-Nun**, when he went off in anger… but he **cried out in the darkness**, saying: **There is no Allah save Thee. Be Thou Glorified! Lo! I have been a wrong-doer.**"

This prayer of repentance-in-the-depths is recited by Muslims in distress. The
Book of Jonah's chapter-2 psalm is longer and more poetic ("out of the belly of
hell cried I"), but the spiritual beat — **a sinking man turning to God** — is
the same.

## Where the emphasis differs
- **Jonah's anger and the gourd.** The Bible's most distinctive material is the
  *ending* the Qur'an doesn't dwell on: Jonah **resents** God's mercy to Nineveh,
  sulks under a withered gourd, and God rebukes his lack of compassion (Jonah 4).
  The whole book is a lesson about **God's mercy extending even to enemies** —
  delivered as a comic rebuke of the prophet himself.
- **The Qur'an's focus** is Jonah as a warning about **impatience**: he left his
  post "in anger," and the moral is *ṣabr* (patience) and trust — "be not like
  him of the fish" (Al-Qalam 68:48).

| | Book of Jonah | Qur'an |
|---|---|---|
| Core lesson | God's **mercy** reaches even Nineveh | the prophet's **patience** & repentance |
| Distinctive scene | the gourd & Jonah's sulking (ch. 4) | the cry in the darkness (21:87) |
| Tone | ironic, almost satirical | admonitory |

## A New Testament echo
Jonah is also a bridge *within* the Bible: Jesus invokes "the **sign of the
prophet Jonas**" — three days in the fish prefiguring three days in the tomb
(Matthew 12:40) — making this one figure resonate across all three layers:
Tanakh, Gospel, and Qur'an. (See [[Jesus and Isa - A Figure Traced]].)
