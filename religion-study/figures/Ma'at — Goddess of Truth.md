# Ma'at — Goddess of Truth

## Summary
Ma'at is the goddess of truth, justice, balance, and cosmic order — the most abstract and philosophically important deity in Egyptian religion. She is not merely a goddess but a **principle**: the fundamental order that makes the cosmos possible. In [[The Egyptian Book of the Dead — Stories, Figures and Connections|the Book of the Dead]], Ma'at is the feather against which the heart is weighed, the ethical standard the deceased must meet, and the cosmic law that even gods must obey. She is at once a personal goddess and an impersonal principle — a unique theological achievement.

## Ma'at in the Book of the Dead

| Occurrence | Context |
|------------|---------|
| **Chapter 30B** | "Let my heart be led to the judgment... O thou that weighest the heart, let my heart be weighed in the presence of the Great God" — invoking Ma'at's standard |
| **Chapter 125** | **The central judgment**: The heart is weighed against the feather of Ma'at; "I have offered bread to the hungry, water to the thirsty, clothing to the naked" — ethical acts are the evidence of Ma'at |
| **Chapter 125 (Negative Confession)** | "I have not committed sin... I have not stolen... I have not killed... I have not defiled" — 42 ethical declarations, each is a statement of Ma'at |
| **Chapter 94** | "Grant me the sweet breath of the north wind — I am the Lord of Ma'at" |
| **Chapter 17** | The deceased says: "I am the Lord of Ma'at" — claiming union with the principle |
| **Vignette (Ch. 125)** | Ma'at is a small feather on the balance — or a tiny figure of the goddess perched on the pedestal before Osiris |

## Iconography
- A **single ostrich feather** — the headdress of the goddess is the feather itself
- **Seated goddess** with the feather on her head, holding an *ankh* (life) and a *was*-sceptre
- Sometimes she stands on a **pedestal before Osiris** in the judgment vignette — she is the *token* of the truth Osiris upholds
- In amulet form: a **small golden feather** — worn by the living and buried with the dead
- The **shu-feather** (the feather of Shu, the air god) — connecting Ma'at to breath and life

## Ma'at as a Dual Concept

Ma'at operates on three levels simultaneously:

| Level | Meaning | Application |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| **Cosmic** | The order of the universe — the fixed stars, the seasons, the rising of the Nile | The gods themselves live by Ma'at; even Ra must pass through the Tuat each night |
| **Social** | Justice, law, truth in human affairs — reciprocity, fairness, social harmony | The pharaoh "rules by Ma'at" — justice is the foundation of kingship |
| **Personal** | Ethical integrity — truthfulness, right action, purity of heart | The Negative Confession lists 42 ways to live by Ma'at |

## Ma'at and the Pharaoh

The pharaoh was not above Ma'at — he was *subject to her*:

> "I have done Ma'at. I have spoken Ma'at. I have brought Ma'at. I have made harmony in the land."

The pharaoh's primary duty was to "establish Ma'at" in the land — to maintain justice, protect the weak, and ensure that the cosmic order was reflected in society. A pharaoh who failed to uphold Ma'at caused chaos (*isfet*) — famine, invasion, civil war.

The annual **Heb Sed** (jubilee festival) involved the pharaoh running a ritual race to demonstrate his physical fitness to maintain Ma'at — if he could not run, he could not rule.

## The Negative Confession and Ma'at

See full treatment at [[The Negative Confession — Egyptian Story]].

The 42 declarations of the Negative Confession are addressed to the 42 Assessors (judge-gods) of the Hall of Righteousness. Each is a declaration of having *done Ma'at* in a specific domain:

**Categories of Ma'at** (from the Book of the Dead Chapter 125):

| Category | Examples of Declarations |
|----------|------------------------|
| **Against the gods** | "I have not blasphemed. I have not diminished the offerings." |
| **Against other persons** | "I have not killed. I have not stolen. I have not committed adultery." |
| **Against the land** | "I have not dammed the Nile. I have not stolen land." |
| **Against speech** | "I have not lied. I have not gossiped. I have not eavesdropped." |
| **Against the dead** | "I have not dispossessed the dead. I have not torn the bandages of the dead." |

## Ma'at in the Scales

The physics of the judgment:

1. **The heart (*ib*)** — contains the record of every thought and deed
2. **The feather of Ma'at** — pure, light, the standard of truth
3. **The balance** — not a mechanism of punishment but of *revelation*
4. **The result** — if the heart balances with Ma'at, the soul is *ma'a kheru* ("true of voice") and passes into the Field of Reeds

> "The heart is the witness. Ma'at is the standard. The scales do not judge — they *reveal*."

If the heart is heavier than the feather (burdened by sin/[[isfet]]), [[Ammut — The Devourer|Ammut]] destroys it. The "Second Death" — the soul ceases to exist.

## The Theological Uniqueness of Ma'at

Ma'at is one of the few cases in ancient religion where an **abstract principle is fully personified as a goddess** without losing one pole of the duality. Compare:

| Tradition | Principle | Personification? |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Egyptian | Ma'at — truth/order | Yes — a goddess who is also the feather |
| Greek | *Logos* — reason/order | No — impersonal (though Stoics divinize it) |
| Greek | *Dike* — justice | Yes — goddess, but not the same as the legal principle |
| Hindu | *Rta* / *Dharma* — cosmic order | Partially — as *Dharma-devi* in later tradition |
| Zoroastrian | *Asha* — truth | Yes — *Asha Vahishta* as an Amesha Spenta |
| Chinese | *Tao* — the way | No — impersonal |
| Jewish | *Torah* — instruction | Personified as *Chokhma* (Wisdom) in Proverbs 8 |
