# Devadatta

> The Buddha's rival cousin — the monk who turned disciple into adversary, whose name became synonymous with treachery in the Buddhist world.

---

## Biography

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| **Role** | The Buddha's rival cousin, would-be usurper of the Saṅgha |
| **Relation** | First cousin and brother-in-law to the Buddha |
| **Clan** | Sakya (Śākya) |
| **Ordination** | Joined the Saṅgha alongside Ānanda at the Buddha's return to Kapilavatthu |
| **Known For** | Attempting to assassinate the Buddha and split the monastic community |

---

## The Schism

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **The Five Demands** | Devadatta proposed five ascetic rules stricter than the Buddha's: (1) monks must dwell in forests, never in villages; (2) live only on alms food, never accept invitations; (3) wear only rag-robes, not lay-robe donations; (4) dwell at the foot of trees, never under a roof; (5) never eat fish or meat. The Buddha refused. |
| **Assassination Attempts** | (1) Hired archers to kill the Buddha — all converted on meeting him. (2) Rolled a boulder down a hill at the Buddha — it split in two. (3) Sent Nālāgiri, a rampaging royal elephant, against the Buddha — the Buddha tamed it with loving-kindness. |
| **The Split** | Devadatta performed miracles before Prince Ajātasattu, won his patronage, then led 500 newly-ordained monks away to Gayāsīsa, declaring himself the true Buddha. Sāriputta and Moggallāna went to retrieve them and succeeded. |

---

## His Powers

Devadatta was no ordinary troublemaker — he possessed formidable **psychic powers** (iddhi) attained through meditation before his fall:

- He levitated, walked on water, and appeared in multiple places at once
- He used these powers to dazzle **Prince Ajātasattu** (later King Ajātasattu), winning the prince's allegiance, lavish patronage, and a private monastery
- Ajātasattu built him a monastery at Gayāsīsa and attended him daily with 500 chariots
- His powers corrupted him — he grew prideful and began to believe he, not the Buddha, should lead the Saṅgha

---

## Death

According to the Buddhist canon (Cullavagga), after his failed schism Devadatta fell gravely ill. He set out to see the Buddha one last time, hoping for reconciliation. As he was carried on a litter toward Jetavana Monastery, he stopped near a pond. When he stepped down to drink, the earth opened and swallowed him alive. He was reborn in the Avīci hell — the deepest and most torturous realm — where he remains for the duration of a kalpa (an unimaginably long cosmic age).

Some traditions say that in a distant future life, he will eventually become a Paccekabuddha (silent Buddha) named Aṭṭhissara, after having exhausted his karmic debt.

## Comparison with Other Traditions

| Figure | Parallel to Devadatta |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Judas Iscariot** | Both were close insiders who betrayed their teacher; Judas for 30 silver pieces, Devadatta for royal patronage. Both are archetypes of the trusted disciple turned betrayer. |
| **Satan / Iblis** | Like Iblis who refused to bow to Adam out of pride, Devadatta was undone by spiritual pride — his psychic powers convinced him he was the Buddha's equal. Both fell from grace through arrogance. |
| **The Pharisees** | Like the Buddha, Jesus was challenged by religious authorities who demanded stricter adherence to traditional rules — the parallel lies in Devadatta's demand for more ascetic regulations than the Buddha taught, a legalism the Buddha saw as attachment rather than liberation. |
