# Māra

> The personification of Death, Desire, and Delusion — the tempter who stalked the Bodhisatta from his renunciation to his final breath, and whose defeat is the template for every victory of awakening.

## The Three Forms of Māra

Māra is not merely an external demon; Buddhist tradition analyses him as four overlapping principles:

| Form | Meaning | Description |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Kilesamāra** | Māra as Defilements | Greed, hatred, delusion — the internal temptations that bind beings to saṃsāra |
| **Khandhamāra** | Māra as Aggregates | The five khandhas (form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness) themselves are a death-trap when clung to |
| **Devaputtamāra** | Māra as Deity | The anthropomorphic tempter-deity who confronts the Buddha — a literal being in the Paranimmitavasavatti heaven |
| **Maccumāra** | Māra as Death | Physical death itself, the ultimate lord from whom no being is free until nibbāna |

Only *Devaputtamāra* is a personal being; the other three are impersonal structures of existence.

## The Great Assault at Enlightenment

As Siddhattha Gotama sat under the Bodhi Tree on the night of his awakening, Māra arrived with his vast army — a cosmic terror spanning leagues in every direction. The army is catalogued in the *Padhāna Sutta* (Sn 3.2):

> "Your army is the first — sense-desires. Your second — discontent. Your third — hunger and thirst. Your fourth — craving. Your fifth — sloth and torpor. Your sixth — cowardice. Your seventh — doubt. Your eighth — denigration and stubbornness. Your ninth — gain, praise, honour, and ill-gotten fame. Your tenth — extolling oneself and disparaging others."

Māra challenged the Bodhisatta's right to sit on the seat of awakening. The Bodhisatta touched the earth — the **Bhūmisparśa Mudrā** — calling the earth goddess to witness his countless lifetimes of generosity. The earth trembled in response, and Māra's army fled. The Morning Star rose, and the Buddha had won.

## Māra's Later Appearances

Māra did not vanish after the defeat at Enlightenment. He haunts the Suttas as a persistent stalker:

- **After the meal with the brahmin** (SN 4.2) — The Buddha had returned from alms in a brahmin village where no offering was given. Māra appeared, mocking: "Did you get any almsfood, or not?" The Buddha replied: "Even if I get nothing, I live in joy — like the radiant gods who feed on rapture."

- **The 'snake' appearance** (SN 4.5) — Māra took the form of a huge snake and lay across the Buddha's path. The Buddha recognised him: "Empty is the inside, empty the outside — the world is empty. How will you find me, O Māra?"

- **Māra making the Saṅgha noisy** (SN 4.21) — As monks were reciting the Dhamma, Māra manifested as a huge bull to disrupt them. The Buddha told him: "You think: 'I have power over them.' But I and my disciples are beyond your reach."

## His Daughters' Failed Temptation

After Māra's defeat at the Bodhi Tree, his three daughters — **Taṇhā** (Craving), **Aratī** (Aversion), and **Ragā** (Lust) — approached the Buddha, taking on every seductive form imaginable: young, old, and every age between. They danced, they smiled, they offered every delight of the senses.

The Buddha remained utterly unmoved:

> "Why do you trouble one who is free of passion? The Tathāgata has no use for lust. Let Māra's host attack with banners raised — I stand fearless, beyond birth and death."

The daughters withdrew, defeated. The episode is a profound statement that the Buddha's liberation is complete — not merely ascetic suppression but genuine uprooting of desire.

## Comparison with Other Traditions

| Tradition | Figure | Parallel |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Christianity** | Satan / the Devil | Both are personified tempters who test a central holy figure in the wilderness; both oppose the divine plan |
| **Islam** | Iblis / Shayṭān | Both refuse to submit to God's command out of pride; both lead beings astray through whisperings (waswās) |
| **Zoroastrianism** | Ahriman (Angra Mainyu) | The destructive spirit who opposes Spenta Mainyu — a genuinely dualistic adversary with his own army, closest structural parallel to Māra's army at Enlightenment |
| **Hinduism** | Kāma (the god of desire) | Māra is sometimes called *Kāma* in Buddhist texts; both personify the force of craving that binds beings |

Unlike Satan in the Abrahamic traditions (a fallen angel acting within God's ultimate sovereignty), Māra's existence is firmly within the Buddhist understanding of saṃsāra — he is himself a sentient being in a high heaven, not yet awakened, subject to the same laws of kamma and rebirth. His defeat is not an eschatological battle but an individual awakening.
