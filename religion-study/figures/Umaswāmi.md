# Umaswāmi (c. 1st–5th century CE)

**Also known as:** Umasvāti (Śvetāmbara tradition), Umaswāmi (Digambara tradition), author of the *Tattvārtha Sūtra*

**Tradition:** Jainism (sectarian affiliation disputed — claimed by both Śvetāmbara and Digambara)
**Era:** Likely 2nd–4th century CE (dates vary widely by tradition; earliest possible c. 1st century CE, latest c. 5th century CE)
**Region:** India (likely Mathura or ancient Indian scholastic centers)
**Key Work:** *Tattvārtha Sūtra* (also *Tattvārthādhigama Sūtra*) — "That Which Is"

---

## Significance

Umaswāmi is the single most important systematizer in Jain philosophy. His *Tattvārtha Sūtra* is the **only text accepted as authoritative by all Jain sects** — Śvetāmbara and Digambara alike — a unique status in Jain history. The work is often referred to as the **"Jain Gītā"** (or "Jain Bible") for its comprehensive, aphoristic codification of Jain doctrine.

While the Jain tradition traces its roots to the 24 *Tīrthaṅkaras* (most recently Mahāvīra, c. 6th century BCE), it was Umaswāmi who first produced a systematic, sutra-style synthesis of Jain philosophy — comparable to what Bādarāyaṇa did for Vedānta or Patañjali for Yoga.

---

## Life & Historical Context

Very little is known about Umaswāmi's life with certainty. Traditional accounts differ between the two main Jain sects:

- **Śvetāmbara tradition** refers to him as **Umasvāti** (or Umasvāmin), placing him in the late 1st–2nd century CE. He is said to have been a disciple of the Śvetāmbara ācārya **Kundakunda** (though some dispute this chronology), and his name is sometimes interpreted as "Uma's (Pārvati's) teacher" — suggesting possible Brahmanical origins.

- **Digambara tradition** calls him **Umaswāmi** and places him later, around the 2nd–4th century CE. They claim him as one of their own ācāryas, and his name is parsed differently: "Umaswāmi" meaning "lord of Uma" or "master of [the syllable] Om" depending on interpretation.

This sectarian dispute itself testifies to his importance: both traditions want to claim him, because his text is the doctrinal cornerstone each uses.

## Key Work: *Tattvārtha Sūtra*

### Overview

The *Tattvārtha Sūtra* ("That Which Is" or "On the True Nature of Reality") is a collection of **344 (or ~350) aphoristic sūtras** divided into **10 chapters**. It is written in Sanskrit sūtra style, modeled after the grammatical and philosophical sūtra traditions of ancient India.

### Structure & Core Content

| Chapter | Topic |
|---------|-------|
| 1 | **Faith and Knowledge** — Right Faith (*samyag darśana*), Right Knowledge (*samyag jñāna*), and Right Conduct (*samyag cāritra*) — the three jewels (*ratnatraya*); the nature of the soul (jīva) |
| 2–3 | **The Categories of Reality** — The seven *tattvas* (fundamental principles); the nine *padārthas* (categories); the distinction between jīva (soul) and ajīva (non-soul) |
| 4 | **Cosmology** — The Jain universe (loka); the three worlds; the nature of time |
| 5 | **Ajīva (Non-Soul)** — Matter (*pudgala*), the medium of motion (*dharma*), the medium of rest (*adharma*), space (*ākāśa*), time (*kāla*) |
| 6 | **Karmic Bondage** — The influx of karma (*āsrava*), bondage (*bandha*), and the causes of karmic attachment |
| 7 | **The Five Vows** — Non-violence (*ahiṃsā*), truthfulness (*satya*), non-stealing (*asteya*), celibacy (*brahmacarya*), non-possession (*aparigraha*) |
| 8 | **Karmic Types and Their Effects** — The eight types of karma and their fruition |
| 9 | **Stoppage and Shedding of Karma** — *Saṃvara* (stopping karmic influx) and *nirjarā* (shedding accumulated karma) |
| 10 | **Liberation (*Mokṣa*)** — The path to liberation; the stages of spiritual development (*guṇasthānas*) |

### The Seven Tattvas (Fundamental Principles)

The *Tattvārtha Sūtra*'s most famous contribution is the codification of Jain ontology into seven principles:

1. **Jīva** — Living beings / souls
2. **Ajīva** — Non-living entities (matter, space, motion, rest, time)
3. **Āsrava** — Influx of karma into the soul
4. **Bandha** — Bondage of karma to the soul
5. **Saṃvara** — Stoppage of karmic influx
6. **Nirjarā** — Shedding of accumulated karma
7. **Mokṣa** — Liberation from karma and rebirth

These seven are often expanded to **nine padārthas** by adding *puṇya* (merit) and *pāpa* (demerit) as subcategories.

### Philosophical Contributions

- **Anekāntavāda (Non-Absolutism)**: Though not invented by Umaswāmi, the *Tattvārtha Sūtra* provides the framework for Jain epistemology of multiple perspectives (*nayas*) and the doctrine of conditional predication (*syādvāda*).
- **Syādvāda (The Doctrine of "Maybe")**: The seven-fold predication (*saptabhaṅgī*) — the logical expression of anekāntavāda — is grounded in principles laid out in the text.
- **Guṇasthāna Theory**: The 14 stages of spiritual development (guṇasthānas) that map the soul's journey from ignorance to liberation.
- **Karma Theory**: A systematic account of how karma binds to the soul based on actions, passions, and intentions.

## Comparative Table: Philosophical Systematizers

Umaswāmi's role in Jainism parallels that of other great systematizers in Indian and world religious philosophy:

| **Figure** | **Tradition** | **Key Work** | **Role** |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|----------|
| **Umaswāmi** (Umasvāti) | Jainism | *Tattvārtha Sūtra* | Systematized Jain philosophy into seven tattvas and nine padārthas; the only text accepted by all Jain sects |
| **Bādarāyaṇa** | Vedānta (Hinduism) | *Brahma Sūtra* | Systematized Upaniṣadic philosophy into the Vedānta sūtra tradition; foundational text for all Vedānta schools |
| **Patañjali** | Yoga (Hinduism) | *Yoga Sūtra* | Codified the eight-limbed path (aṣṭāṅga yoga) and systematized Yoga philosophy |
| **Buddhaghosa** | Theravāda Buddhism | *Visuddhimagga* (Path of Purification) | Systematized Theravāda doctrine, meditation, and monastic practice into a unified path |
| **Vasubandhu** | Yogācāra Buddhism | *Abhidharma-kośa* / *Triṃśikā* | Systematized Abhidharma and Yogācāra philosophy; bridged earlier and later Buddhist scholasticism |
| **Śaṅkara (Ādi Śaṅkarācārya)** | Advaita Vedānta (Hinduism) | *Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya*, *Upadeśa Sāhastrī* | Systematized Advaita Vedānta and wrote definitive commentaries on prasthāna-traya |
| **Ibn Sīnā (Avicenna)** | Islamic Philosophy | *Kitāb al-Shifāʾ* (Book of Healing) | Systematized Aristotelian and Neoplatonic philosophy within Islamic theology |
| **Thomas Aquinas** | Catholic Christianity | *Summa Theologica* | Systematized Christian theology using Aristotelian philosophy; the definitive scholastic synthesis |

## Sectarian Dispute & Legacy

### Śvetāmbara vs. Digambara Claims

The sectarian tug-of-war over Umaswāmi reflects the deep importance of his text:

| **Aspect** | **Śvetāmbara View** | **Digambara View** |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Name | Umasvāti | Umaswāmi |
| Era | c. 1st–2nd century CE | c. 2nd–4th century CE |
| Teacher | Disciple of Kundakunda (or associated with Śvetāmbara lineage) | A Digambara ācārya in his own right |
| Commentaries | The *Tattvārthādhigama Bhāṣya* (auto-commentary, in Prakrit) is accepted | The *Svopajña Bhāṣya* is the auto-commentary, preserved in Digambara tradition |
| Textual variants | The sūtra count and some wording differ slightly | Emphasizes certain verses that align with Digambara doctrine (e.g., nudity as a requirement for liberation) |

The key doctrinal point of contention: the *Tattvārtha Sūtra* states that a monk who is "naked" (*acelaka*) is superior — Digambaras read this as supporting their practice of nudity, while Śvetāmbaras interpret it symbolically or as a historical description.

### Influence

- The *Tattvārtha Sūtra* has been commented upon by dozens of Jain scholars across both sects, including **Akalaṅka** (Digambara), **Siddhasēna Divākara** (Śvetāmbara), **Pūjyapāda**, and **Vidyānanda**.
- It is the first text a student of Jain philosophy studies, and remains the standard reference for Jain doctrine.
- Buddhist and Hindu scholars also engaged with the text — it was recognized across Indian philosophical schools as the authoritative statement of Jain thought.

## Connections

- **Mahāvīra (Vardhamāna)** — The 24th Tīrthaṅkara; Umaswāmi's text systematizes the teachings attributed to him
- **Kundakunda** — Another major Jain philosopher; dates and relationship to Umaswāmi are debated
- **Anekāntavāda** — The epistemological framework codified (if not originated) by the *Tattvārtha Sūtra*
- **Jain Tattvas** — The seven principles that form the core of Jain metaphysics
- **Guṇasthāna** — The 14 spiritual stages systematized in the text
