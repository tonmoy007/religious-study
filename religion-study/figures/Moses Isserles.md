# Moses Isserles (רמ"א — Rabbi Moshe ben Yisrael Isserles)

Known as the **Rema** (רמ"א), the great Ashkenazi halakhist who wrote *ha-Mappah* (המפה — "The Tablecloth"), a set of glosses that adapted [[Rabbi Joseph Karo]]'s *Shulchan Aruch* for Ashkenazi practice. Together, the Shulchan Aruch and ha-Mappah became the definitive code of Jewish law for all subsequent generations.

## Key Contributions

- ***Ha-Mappah* (The Tablecloth, 1571):** A commentary and set of marginal glosses on Karo's *Shulchan Aruch* that records Ashkenazi customs (*minhagim*), rulings, and practices where they differ from the Sephardic framework. Isserles's motto: "Let them come with their differences — a table cannot stand with only one leg."
- **Unification of Jewish Law:** By supplementing Karo's Sephardic-oriented code with Ashkenazi practice, Isserles made the Shulchan Aruch acceptable to all Jewish communities. The combined work became the universal standard of halakhic decision-making.
- ***Torat ha-Olah* (The Law of the Offering):** A philosophical and kabbalistic work on the meaning of the Temple and its sacrifices, demonstrating Isserles's range beyond legalism into philosophy, mysticism, and science.
- ***Darkhei Moshe* (The Ways of Moses):** A commentary on the *Tur* (Jacob ben Asher's code) that laid the groundwork for ha-Mappah, systematically comparing major halakhic authorities to derive rulings.
- **Yeshiva Leadership:** He established and led a major yeshiva in Kraków that attracted students from across Poland, Lithuania, and Germany. His rulings shaped Ashkenazi Jewish life for centuries.

## Life & Legacy

- Born to a wealthy and scholarly family in Kraków — his father Yisrael was a scholar and communal leader
- Studied under Rabbi Shalom Shachna in Lublin and later returned to Kraków, joining the bet din (rabbinical court)
- Despite his wealth (his father-in-law was a prominent banker), Isserles lived modestly and devoted himself to scholarship
- He was a student of philosophy and science — unusual for his time — and corresponded on astronomy with scholars in Padua
- Died in Kraków at age 42; his ohel (tomb) in the Remuh Cemetery remains a pilgrimage site; the Remuh Synagogue is named in his honour
