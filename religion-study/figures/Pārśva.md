# Pārśva (Parshvanatha)

> Birth: c. 877–872 BCE (traditional), Vārāṇasī (modern Varanasi, Uttar Pradesh)
> Death: c. 777–772 BCE (traditional — nirvāṇa on Mount Sammeta, modern Jharkhand)
> Title: 23rd Tīrthaṅkara of the current avasarpiṇī (descending half of the time cycle)

Pārśva (Parshvanatha), meaning "He Whose Side Emblem is a Serpent" (from *pārśva* — side/flank + *nātha* — lord/protector), is the 23rd Tīrthaṅkara (ford-maker) of Jainism, the immediate predecessor of Mahāvīra. Born a prince of Vārāṇasī to King Aśvasena and Queen Vāmādevī, he renounced the world at age 30 and undertook 83 days of intense asceticism before attaining kevala jñāna (perfect knowledge). He is credited with establishing the *cāturyāma* — the fourfold vow of non-violence (ahiṃsā), truthfulness (satya), non-stealing (asteya), and non-attachment (aparigraha) — which Mahāvīra later expanded into the five great vows (mahāvratas) by adding celibacy (brahmacarya). Pārśva's tradition was already flourishing in the Gangetic basin when Mahāvīra was born, and many of Mahāvīra's earliest and most prominent disciples — including the 11 Ganadharas — came from Pārśva's established order, making him a foundational bridge between the prehistoric Tīrthaṅkaras and the historically-attested Jain community.

The Śvetāmbara tradition recounts that his mother had 14 auspicious dreams before his birth (as with all Tīrthaṅkaras), including dreams of a serpent and a jewelled throne. The serpent-king's wife, Padmāvatī (or Suṣīmā), also coiled around Pārśva's lower body to protect him. This event is the most iconic moment in Pārśva's hagiography — Dharaṇendra's cobra-hood canopy becomes the defining iconographic motif of Pārśva images |
| Kevala Jñāna (Omniscience) | After 83 days of asceticism, standing under a Dhātakī tree (a flowering shrub, Grislea tomentosa or Woodfordia fruticosa), Pārśva attained kevala jñāna — perfect, infinite knowledge encompassing all substances, times, and modes simultaneously. His body became free of all karmic bonds, and he began teaching the eternal Jain Dhamma |
| Ministry | Pārśva wandered the Gangetic plain for 70 years (to age 100), organising a large community of monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen. He established the *cāturyāma dharma* — the fourfold restraint. ## The Fourfold Vow (Cāturyāma)

Pārśva's defining doctrinal contribution is the *cāturyāma* — a fourfold code of conduct that forms the earliest known Jain ethical system. This pre-dates Mahāvīra's five great vows (mahāvratas) by several centuries and differs from it in one crucial respect.

| Vow | Meaning | Pārśva's Formulation | Mahāvīra's Expansion |
|-----|---------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **Ahiṃsā** | Non-violence | Absolute non-harm to all living beings in thought, word, and deed | Identical but more elaborately categorised — earth-bodies, water-bodies, fire-bodies, wind-bodies, plant-bodies, animal-bodies, human-bodies |
| **Satya** | Truthfulness | Speaking only that which is truthful, beneficial, and pleasant | Identical in content |
| **Asteya** | Non-stealing | Taking nothing that is not freely given | Identical in content |
| **Aparigraha** | Non-attachment | Renunciation of possessions and worldly attachments | Identical in content, with greater emphasis on emotional detachment |
| **Brahmacarya** (Celibacy) | — | *Not included as a separate vow* — chastity within marriage was implicitly part of the ethical code but not enumerated as an independent mahāvrata | Explicitly added as the fifth great vow, expanding it to cover complete abstinence from all sensual pleasures for monastics |

The key difference: Pārśva taught a fourfold path for both monastics and laity, while Mahāvīra distinguished between the five *mahāvratas* (great vows) for monks/nuns and the five *aṇuvratas* (limited vows) for laypeople. The addition of brahmacarya as a separate vow reflects the evolution of Jain asceticism toward greater rigour in the monastic community.

### Why Pārśva's Fourfold Vow Matters

The *cāturyāma* is historically significant because:

- It is the earliest attested formulation of a comprehensive ethical code in the Indian sramana tradition, predating Buddhist and Ājīvika codes
- It establishes ahiṃsā as the primary and non-negotiable foundation — a radical departure from the Vedic tradition of animal sacrifice (yajña)
- It proves that Jainism existed as an organised tradition *before* Mahāvīra — Pārśva's community was already a recognisable religious order with monks, nuns, laity, scriptures, and a lineage
- The fourfold vow is still respected in Jain tradition: the Śvetāmbara school maintains that both the fourfold and fivefold formulations are valid, with Pārśva's being appropriate for earlier, less corrupted times

## Dharaṇendra & Padmāvatī — The Serpent Protectors

The narrative of Pārśva's protection by the Nāga king Dharaṇendra during the demon Meghamālin's assault is the most famous story in his hagiography and a cornerstone of Jain iconography.

### The Story

1. **Past Life Connection**: In a previous life, Pārśva was a merchant named Marubhūti, whose brother Kamatha (also called Meghamālin in later lives) killed him out of greed. Marubhūti was reborn as Pārśva, while Kamatha was reborn as a powerful demon (also called Samvara). A Nāga (serpent) who had witnessed Marubhūti's compassion was reborn as Dharaṇendra, king of the Nāgas.

2. **The Attack**: As Pārśva meditated under a Dhātakī tree, the demon Meghamālin — remembering his enmity from the past life — unleashed a furious storm of rain, lightning, hail, and mud, attempting to break Pārśva's concentration or even kill him.

3. **The Protection**: Dharaṇendra, seeing Pārśva's danger, emerged from the earth with his queen Padmāvatī. He spread his massive cobra hood over Pārśva's head like an umbrella, while Padmāvatī coiled around his lower body. They stayed in this posture for three days and nights until the storm passed.

4. **The Outcome**: Pārśva remained completely unmoved, his meditation unbroken. The demon, exhausted and defeated, finally recognised Pārśva's spiritual power and became a devotee. This story symbolises the victory of equanimity (samatā) over aggression, the power of non-violence to overcome violence, and the protective role of Nāga deities in Jain cosmology.

### Theological Significance

- **Dharaṇendra** becomes the perpetual guardian of Pārśva and later the Jain Dhamma. He is venerated as a yakṣa (attendant deity) in Jain temples, often depicted holding a serpent and a fruit.
- **Padmāvatī** becomes one of the most important female yakṣīs (goddesses) in Jainism, associated with protection, prosperity, and the removal of obstacles. She has her own independent cult, especially in southern India.
- The serpent-hood canopy becomes Pārśva's universal identifying symbol — every image of Pārśva includes the seven-headed (or single-headed) cobra hood above his head.

## Pārśva's Place as the 23rd Tīrthaṅkara

### In the Cosmic Cycle

Pārśva belongs to the current descending half of the time cycle (avasarpiṇī), specifically the fourth *ārā* (period) out of six — the age of moral decline where Tīrthaṅkaras still appear but their followers are less capable than in earlier ages. He is the third of the four Tīrthaṅkaras of the current cycle (Neminātha's period having ended before him), separated from Ṛṣabha (1st Tīrthaṅkara) by 22 previous ford-makers.

### Historical Standing

Unlike the first 22 Tīrthaṅkaras, who are largely mythological figures with fantastic lifespans and cosmological rather than historical settings, Pārśva is treated by scholars as a plausible historical figure:

- His dates (c. 9th–8th century BCE) align with the archaeological record of the early Iron Age in the Gangetic plain
- The city of Vārāṇasī was a major urban centre by this period
- Jain texts describe his community as an organised order with specific rules, lineages, and a canon — details that suggest institutional reality rather than pure legend
- Buddhist texts (which are hostile to Jain claims but valuable as contemporary sources) mention a pre-existing Jain community called the *Nirgranthas* (the "unbound ones") when the Buddha was active, which aligns with Pārśva's tradition
- The Śvetāmbara and Digambara traditions agree on the broad outlines of Pārśva's life in a way they do not for earlier Tīrthaṅkaras — though they diverge on details such as his marital status and nudity

### The Bridge Between Ṛṣabha and Mahāvīra

| Aspect | Ṛṣabha (1st Tīrthaṅkara) | Pārśva (23rd Tīrthaṅkara) | Mahāvīra (24th Tīrthaṅkara) |
|--------|--------------------------|---------------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Status** | Mythological — prehistoric civilising hero | Proto-historical — plausible early historical figure | Historical — attested in non-Jain sources |
| **Era** | Yuga of perfect happiness (3rd ārā of utsarpiṇī) | Fourth ārā of avasarpiṇī (age of decline) | Fifth ārā of avasarpiṇī (age of deep decline) |
| **Lifespan** | 8,400,000 years (traditional) | 100 years (traditional) | 72 years (Śvetāmbara) / 70 (Digambara) |
| **Ethical Code** | Taught basic virtues (implicit) | Fourfold vow (cāturyāma) | Fivefold vow (pañca-mahāvrata) |
| **Community size** | Massive (cosmological numbers) | Large (hundreds of thousands) | Large (hundreds of thousands) |
| **Iconographic emblem** | Bull | Serpent (cobra hood) | Lion |

## Connection to Mahāvīra's Order

One of the most important — and often overlooked — facts about the relationship between Pārśva and Mahāvīra is that many of Mahāvīra's earliest and most significant disciples came from Pārśva's existing order of monks.

### The Transfer of Leadership

When Mahāvīra began his ministry after attaining kevala jñāna, Pārśva's tradition had already been established for roughly two centuries. Several prominent monks of Pārśva's order — including some who had been chief disciples (gaṇadharas) under Pārśva — became confused and uncertain after Pārśva's nirvāṇa. They encountered Mahāvīra, were convinced of his spiritual attainment, and joined his community with their entire retinues of followers.

### The Ganadharas — All from Pārśva's Tradition

According to Jain tradition, all 11 Ganadharas of Mahāvīra were originally followers of Pārśva's path:

| Ganadhara | Background |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Indrabhūti Gautama** | A Brahmin scholar and former follower of Pārśva's teachings; he was the first to be converted by Mahāvīra after a famous debate |
| **Agnibhūti** | Brother of Indrabhūti; also a Brahmin and Pārśva follower |
| **Vāyubhūti** | Third brother; same background |
| **Vyākta / Vyaktasvabhāva** | From Pārśva's monastic lineage |
| **Sudharmā** | A former Pārśva śrāvaka (lay follower) who became Mahāvīra's disciple and later the most important Ganadhara for the Śvetāmbara canon |
| (plus 6 others — each from Pārśva's tradition) | |

This means that the entire chain of Jain scriptural transmission — the Āgamas — passes through disciples who first learned the Dhamma from Pārśva's tradition and then received its final form from Mahāvīra.

### Doctrinal Continuity and Change

The relationship between the two traditions is complex:

- **Continuity**: Both taught non-violence, non-stealing, truthfulness, and non-attachment as core ethical principles. Both accepted the eternal nature of the soul (jīva), the reality of karma as a substance, and the goal of mokṣa
- **Addition**: Mahāvīra added brahmacarya (complete celibacy) as a separate, explicit vow — elevating it from an implicit monastic practice to a cardinal principle
- **Systematisation**: Mahāvīra organised the doctrine into the comprehensive framework of the nine tattvas, five types of knowledge, karma theory as physical matter, and the seven/fourteen guṇasthānas (stages of spiritual development)
- **Ritual**: The *cāturyāma* order had simpler rituals; Mahāvīra's tradition developed more elaborate ones including pratikramaṇa (confession), prāyaścitta (repentance), and detailed rules of monastic conduct

### Jain Sectarian Views

The Śvetāmbara and Digambara traditions differ on how they view the Pārśva–Mahāvīra relationship:

| Aspect | Śvetāmbara | Digambara |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Clothing** | Pārśva wore a single garment; Mahāvīra also wore one initially then went naked | Both were completely naked (digambara = "sky-clad") as all Tīrthaṅkaras must be |
| **Marriage** | Pārśva married Prabhāvatī; Mahāvīra married Yaśodā | Both never married; celibate from birth |
| **Doctrinal relationship** | The fourfold vow was for an earlier, less degenerate age; the fivefold vow is the current standard | The fivefold vow was always implied in Pārśva's teaching; brahmacarya was always part of the ascetic code |
| **Female liberation** | (Implied by Pārśva's order having nuns) — women can attain mokṣa | (Strictly) — women cannot attain mokṣa without being reborn as men |
| **Scripture** | Pārśva's teachings were lost; only Mahāvīra's Āgamas survive | Both taught the same eternal Dhamma; no separate Pārśva scripture exists |

## In Jain Tradition

### Iconography

Pārśva's iconography is the most distinctive and immediately recognisable of all Tīrthaṅkaras:

- **Serpent canopy** (Nāga-phuṇā) — A multi-headed cobra (usually 7 heads, sometimes 5 or 9) spread above his head like an umbrella, representing Dharaṇendra's protection. This is the single most identifying feature
- **Posture** — Seated in padmāsana (lotus pose) or standing in kāyotsarga (meditation posture). When standing, the serpent hood rises above him; when seated, it arches behind
- **Colour** — Dark blue, black, or green (śyāma varṇa — "dark-coloured"), reflecting his connection to the serpent and his name (Pārśva = "dark" in some etymologies)
- **Emblem** — A serpent coiled at the base of his throne or pedestal
- **Yakṣa and Yakṣī** — Attended by the yakṣa Dharaṇendra (serpent-king) and the yakṣī Padmāvatī (serpent-queen)
- **Clothing** — Śvetāmbara images show him with a cloth below the navel; Digambara images show him completely naked, seated on a lotus with the serpent hood above
- **Expression** — Calm, serene, inward-turned, with half-closed eyes indicating deep meditative absorption

### Temples & Pilgrimage

- **Sammeta Śikharajī (Pārasnāth Hill)**, Jharkhand — The most sacred Pārśva site; 20 of 24 Tīrthaṅkaras attained nirvāṇa here. The hill bears 20 śikharas (peaks) each marked by a Jain temple, with Pārśva's temple at the highest point
- **Dilwara Temples**, Mount Abu, Rajasthan — The world-famous marble temple complex includes images of Pārśva with exquisite serpent-hood carvings
- **Pārśvanātha Temple**, Khajuraho — One of the best-preserved Jain temples at Khajuraho, with detailed Pārśva sculptures
- **Chandragiri Hill**, Shravanabelagola, Karnataka — Contains shrines to Pārśva alongside Bāhubali and other Jain saints
- **Pārśvanātha Temple**, Girnar, Gujarat — A major pilgrimage destination for Śvetāmbaras

### Key Texts

- **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** — The earliest Jain canonical text, sometimes interpreted as containing layers from Pārśva's tradition alongside Mahāvīra's
- **Sūtrakṛtāṅga** — Mentions the followers of Pārśva (the "Pārśvāpatyas") as a distinct group that existed before and during Mahāvīra's time
- **Uttarādhyayana Sūtra** — A late-canonical text that includes a famous dialogue between Pārśva and Mahāvīra's followers, discussing the difference between the fourfold and fivefold vows
- **Āvaśyaka Sūtra** with commentaries — Contains extended narratives of Pārśva's life and the Dharaṇendra story
- **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** by Hemacandra — The vast compendium of the "Lives of the 63 Illustrious Men," which includes a detailed hagiography of Pārśva
- **Pārśvacaritra** — Medieval hagiographical works dedicated specifically to Pārśva (e.g., by Śrīcandra, by Devabhadra)

### Festivals

- **Pārśvanātha Bhaktāmbara Stotra** — A famous hymn composed by Mānatuṅga Sūri (c. 7th–8th century), recited daily by Jains for protection and spiritual progress
- **Nirvāṇa Kalyanaka** — The anniversary of Pārśva's liberation on Mount Sammeta, commemorated with special prayers, fasting, and pilgrimages
- **Śvetāmbara annual pilgrimage** (Śatrunjaya Māhātmya) — Includes Pārśva as one of the central figures honored during the pilgrimage to Palitana

### In Jain Philosophy

Pārśva's teachings are understood as the same eternal Dhamma that all Tīrthaṅkaras teach, adapted to the spiritual capacity of the age. The fact that he taught a fourfold rather than fivefold code is interpreted by Śvetāmbara Jains as evidence that the age in which he lived was spiritually more capable — beings needed fewer explicit restrictions because they had fewer passions. Mahāvīra's addition of brahmacarya as a separate vow reflects, in this view, not an innovation but an adaptation to the greater spiritual blindness of the fifth ārā.

The Pārśva tradition (Pārśvāpatyas) maintained a separate identity for some time even after joining Mahāvīra's order. The *Uttarādhyayana Sūtra* records a debate between followers of the two traditions, resolved in Mahāvīra's favour by the Ganadharas. But the continuity was never broken — modern Jainism, in both its Śvetāmbara and Digambara forms, venerates Pārśva as the immediate forerunner and direct link to the historically verifiable Jain community.
