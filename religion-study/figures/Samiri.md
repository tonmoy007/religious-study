# Samiri

The shadowy figure who made the golden calf while Moses was on Mount Sinai. The Qur'an uniquely names *al-Sāmirī* as the culprit — unlike the Bible, which blames Aaron. He represents the archetype of the sorcerer who corrupts sacred knowledge for idolatry.

---

## Identity

The Qur'an does not give Samiri's full lineage. Exegetical traditions suggest:

- He was an **Egyptian Copt** who accompanied the Israelites out of Egypt, outwardly converting but keeping his sorcerer's knowledge
- He belonged to the tribe of **Samira** — a little-known Israelite clan
- His name derives from the same root as **Samaritan** — though the Samaritans emerged centuries later
- Some traditions identify him as **Musa ibn Zafar**, a magician from the court of Pharaoh who survived the crossing

---

## The Sin

See the full story: [[Samiri and the Golden Calf — The Cow That Led Astray]]

When Moses left for 40 days on Mount Sinai, Samiri gathered the Israelites' gold ornaments. He had taken a handful of dust from the footprints of Jibril's (Gabriel's) horse — this dust, when thrown into the fire, gave the golden calf the appearance of life (a lowing sound).

Aaron tried to stop them; they refused. When Moses returned, he found his people worshipping the calf.

Moses confronted Samiri, who confessed:

> *"I saw what they did not see. I took a handful of dust from the messenger's footprint and threw it. Thus my soul prompted me."* — **20:96**

Moses exiled him:

> *"In this life, you shall be exiled, saying: Do not touch me."* — **20:97**

---

## Key Tafsir Insights

**Why the Qur'an exonerates Aaron:** Unlike Exodus 32, the Qur'an insists Aaron was blameless — he tried to stop the worship and was nearly killed. This reflects the Islamic doctrine of prophetic infallibility (*'iṣmah*).

**The dust of the messenger's horse:** A deeply symbolic detail — Samiri used a trace of the sacred (the archangel's footprint) for idolatry. It is the perversion of divine power.

**His punishment:** *Lā misās* ("Do not touch me") — he was isolated from the community, a living symbol of the contamination of idolatry.
