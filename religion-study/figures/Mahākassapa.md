# Mahākassapa

> The foremost in ascetic practices — the Buddha's heir in the Dharma, who convened the First Council and preserved the teaching for posterity.

---

## Key Roles

- **Ascetic Par Excellence** — Wore rag-robes (paṃsukūla) collected from cremation grounds and rubbish heaps; never accepted new robes from laypeople.
- **The Robe Exchange** — The Buddha exchanged robes with Mahākassapa, a unique honour that symbolised spiritual kinship. Seated together, they were mistaken for equals — the Buddha then clarified that Kassapa was his disciple, but the gesture showed his supreme esteem.
- **President of the First Council** — After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, Mahākassapa convened and presided over the council at Rājagaha to codify the Dhamma and Vinaya.

---

## The First Council

Three months after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, Mahākassapa heard the monk **Subhadda** remark dismissively, "Now we are free — no more 'you must do this, you must not do that'." Alarmed by the threat to the Saṅgha's integrity, Kassapa resolved to convene 500 arahants to recite and authorise the Dhamma.

- **Ānanda's Examination** — Though Ānanda had memorised every discourse, Mahākassapa subjected him to a public inquiry and listed his faults (e.g., failing to ask the Buddha which minor rules could be abolished). Ānanda was driven to attain arahantship before the council began.
- **Recitation** — Ānanda recited the Suttas (Dhamma); **Upāli** recited the Vinaya (monastic discipline). Mahākassapa presided, verifying every word.
- **Legacy** — This oral canon remained authoritative across all early Buddhist schools.

## His Asceticism

Mahākassapa is the paradigmatic practitioner of the **13 dhutaṅga** (austere) practices:

1. Wearing rag-robes (not accepting cloth from laity)
2. Having only three robes (te-cīvara)
3. Going for alms (not accepting invitations)
4. Not omitting any house on the alms round
5. Eating only one meal a day
6. Eating only from the alms-bowl (not a second vessel)
7. Refusing food after the meal
8. Living in the forest
9. Living under a tree
10. Living in the open air
11. Living in a charnel ground
12. Using any seat offered (not insisting on one)
13. Sleeping in a seated posture (never lying down)

The Buddha praised him: "Kassapa, you maintain my lineage. You are the pillar of my teaching."

---

## Death and Relic

Mahākassapa is said to have attained **parinirvāṇa** (final release) beneath Mount **Kukkata** (Cock's Mountain). His body was preserved intact inside the mountain, where it awaits the coming of **Metteyya Buddha** — the next Buddha. When Metteyya appears, Kassapa will rise and present the Buddha's robe to him, a symbol of the unbroken transmission of the Dhamma across the aeons.

## Comparison with Other Traditions

| Tradition | Parallel | Difference |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Hinduism** | The ascetic sage (ṛṣi) and forest-dweller | Kassapa remains in the world for the Saṅgha; the ṛṣi typically withdraws entirely |
| **Christianity** | John the Baptist — an ascetic who prepares the way | John heralds a single coming; Kassapa bridges two Buddhas across millennia |
| **Jainism** | The kevalin who observes strict vows (mahāvratas) | Kassapa's dhutaṅgas are rigorous but less extreme than Jain asceticism |
| **Zen** | Bodhidharma — the stern patriarch who guards the teaching | Both emphasise direct, disciplined Dharma transmission |
