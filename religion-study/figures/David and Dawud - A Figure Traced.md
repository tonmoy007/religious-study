# David & Dawud — A Figure Traced

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]] · a **single-figure deep dive**

David is the warrior-king and psalmist of the Hebrew Bible — and a prophet-king
of the Qur'an. Both make him the giant-slayer who receives a divine scripture.
But the Bible gives him a **morally complex, sinning humanity** the Qur'an
removes entirely — the clearest case of the two traditions' differing views of
what a prophet *is*. A **genealogical** connection (see
[[Shared Abrahamic Figures]]).

## Goliath: the shared victory

> [!quote] 1 Samuel 17 — the sling and the stone
> **17:49–50** — "David put his hand in his bag, and took thence a stone, and slang it, and **smote the Philistine in his forehead**… So David prevailed over the Philistine **with a sling and with a stone**… but there was no sword in the hand of David."

> [!quote] Qur'an, Al-Baqara 2:251 — one verse for the whole battle
> "So they routed them by Allah's leave and **David slew Goliath**; and Allah gave him **the kingdom and wisdom**, and taught him of that which He willeth."

The Bible lingers on the underdog drama (the five smooth stones, the taunts, the
severed head); the Qur'an compresses it to a single clause and immediately frames
it theologically — victory "by Allah's leave," then kingdom and wisdom as God's
gift.

## The scripture and the praising creation
Both traditions give David a **revealed book** — the Psalms / *Zabur* — and the
Qur'an adds a vivid miracle: nature itself joins his worship.

> [!quote] Qur'an — the hills echo his psalms
> **Saba 34:10** — "We gave David grace from Us, (saying): O ye **hills and birds, echo his psalms of praise!** And We made the **iron supple** unto him."
> **Al-Anbiya 21:79** — "We subdued the **hills and the birds to hymn (His) praise along with David**."

The Bible's Psalms *are* David's praise (Psalm 148 calls mountains and birds to
praise God); the Qur'an makes them **literally answer back**, and adds his
craft of softening iron into armour — details with no biblical parallel.

## The great divergence: the sinning king vs. the sinless prophet
This is the payload. The Hebrew Bible is unflinching about David's **gravest
sin** — adultery with Bathsheba and the engineered death of her husband Uriah
(2 Samuel 11), for which the prophet Nathan condemns him. David is a flawed,
repentant, fully human king.

The Qur'an contains **no Bathsheba episode**. Dawud is a righteous prophet-king;
where the Qur'an mentions him being "tried" and seeking forgiveness (Sad
38:24–25), it gives no scandal. This reflects the Islamic doctrine of *ʿiṣma* —
the **moral protection (sinlessness) of the prophets** — which is incompatible
with the Bible's portrait of David's crime.

| | Hebrew Bible | Qur'an |
|---|---|---|
| Goliath | extended underdog epic (1 Sam 17) | one verse (2:251), "by Allah's leave" |
| The book | the Psalms (his own laments & praise) | the *Zabur*; hills & birds echo it |
| Miracles | none beyond the victory | praising mountains, supple iron, armour |
| His sin | **adultery & murder** (Bathsheba/Uriah) | **absent** — a sinless prophet |
| Portrait | flawed, repentant **human king** | righteous **prophet-king** |

Same hero, two anthropologies of leadership: the Bible's David is great
*because* he falls and repents; the Qur'an's Dawud is great *because* he never
falls. Compare the same pattern in [[Adam - A Figure Traced|Adam]] (original sin
vs. immediate forgiveness).
