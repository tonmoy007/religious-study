# Gurū Arjan (Arjan Dev)

> Birth: 15 April 1563, Goindval (Punjab)
> Death: 30 May 1606, Lahore (Punjab)
> Title: Fifth Sikh Guru (1581–1606); first Sikh martyr; compiler of the Ādi Granth; builder of the Harimandir Sāhib (Golden Temple)

Gurū Arjan (Punjabi: ਗੁਰੂ ਅਰਜਨ), also known as Arjan Dev, was the fifth Sikh Guru who served from 1581 to 1606 and left an indelible mark on the Sikh tradition as its chief institutional architect and its first martyr. Born the youngest son of Gurū Rām Dās in Goindval, he was chosen as Guru over his elder brother Prithi Chand — a succession dispute that would generate lasting tension and, ultimately, contribute to his death. His short but extraordinarily productive twenty-five-year guruship defined the shape of Sikhism as a world religion: he completed and consecrated the Harimandir Sāhib (the Golden Temple) at Amritsar as a place of pilgrimage open to all, regardless of caste or creed; he compiled the first authorised scripture of the Sikhs, the Ādi Granth, collecting the hymns of the first four Gurus alongside those of Hindu and Muslim saints (Bhagats) such as Kabīr, Nāmdev, and Ravīdās; he composed over 2,200 hymns himself, including the Sukhmanī, Bārāh Māhā, and the wedding hymn Lāvāṁ (now central to the Sikh marriage ceremony); and he institutionalised the collection of dasvandh (the tithe) and the system of masands (regional preachers) that gave the growing community organisational coherence. In 1606, at the orders of the Mughal Emperor Jahāngīr, Gurū Arjan was arrested, tortured, and executed in Lahore — dying five days later in the waters of the Rāvī River — because he refused to alter the Ādi Granth's content and because his growing influence and his sheltering of the rebel Prince Khusrau had made him a political threat to the empire. His death transformed the Sikh community: it initiated the tradition of Sikh martyrdom (shahīdī), militarised the Guruship under his son [[Gurū Har Gobind]], and set the Sikhs on a path of political-military resistance to Mughal authority that would culminate in the Khalsa.

He died on 30 May 1606, his body never recovered. The exact circumstances are debated (see "The Martyrdom" section below) |

## Compilation of the Ādi Granth

The Ādi Granth ("First Book") is the single most consequential text in Sikh history — the scripture that gave the Sikh community its identity before it had a political or military form. Gurū Arjan conceived and executed the project at a time when the community faced two pressures: internal fragmentation caused by rival claimants to the Guruship (especially Prithi Chand's Mīṇā sect, which produced spurious "canons"), and external pressure from the Mughal state. A single, authorised, definitive text was urgently needed.

### The Scope of the Collection

The Ādi Granth contains **5,894 hymns** (śabads) arranged in **31 rāgas** (musical modes), organised by rāga and within each rāga by the author-Guru in chronological order, then by the Bhagats. The contributors include:

| Contributor | Number of Hymns |
|-------------|-----------------|
| **Gurū Nānak** | 974 hymns (including the Japjī Sāhib, Āsā dī Vār, and Sidh Gosti) |
| **Gurū Aṅgad** | 62 hymns |
| **Gurū Amar Dās** | 907 hymns (including the Anand Sāhib) |
| **Gurū Rām Dās** | 679 hymns |
| **Gurū Arjan** | 2,218 hymns (including the Sukhmani Sāhib, Bārāh Māhā, and Lāvāṁ) |
| **Bhagat Kabīr** | 534 hyms (more than any non-Guru contributor) |
| **Bhagat Nāmdev** | 60 hymns |
| **Bhagat Ravīdās** | 40 hymns |
| **Sheikh Farīd** | 130 hymns (Sūfī poet) |
| **Bhagat Trilochan, Dhannā, Benī, Bhaikhan, Surdās, Pīpā, Rāmānand, Parmanand, Sadaḥ, Saina** | Smaller contributions |
| **Bards (Bhaṭṭas)** | 138 hymns praising the Gurus |

Gurū Arjan's editorial principle was inclusive but discerning: only those compositions that expressed the core Sikh teaching of *ik oaṅkār* (One God), rejected caste and ritual, and emphasised nām-simaran (remembrance of the Divine Name) were included. He deliberately excluded his rival Prithi Chand's compositions and also omitted hymns that promoted Hindu avatāra theology or Muslim legalism.

### The Installation Ceremony

On 1 September 1604 (Bhādōṁ Sūdī 1), the completed Ādi Granth was ceremonially installed in the Harimandir Sāhib. Gurū Arjan himself carried the volume on his head and placed it on the throne (takht) that had been prepared. He then bowed before it and proclaimed it the sovereign embodiment of the Guru for the community — a radical declaration that the living authority of the Guru was now invested in the scripture. Bhāī Buddhā was appointed the first Granthī (reader). The congregation heard the first hymn from the Granth — the very first verse of the Japjī Sāhib:

> "Ik Oaṅkār, Satnām, Kartāpurakh, Nirbha'u, Nirvair, Akāl Mūrat, Ajūnī, Saibhan" — one God, Truth by name, Creator, without fear, without enmity, timeless, unborn, self-existent.

With this act, Gurū Arjan established a principle that would distinguish Sikhism from almost every other religious tradition: the scripture itself, not a human successor, would inherit the Guru's authority. This was formalised by [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]] in 1708 when he declared the Guru Granth Sāhib the eternal Guru, but the precedent was set here.

### Editorial Innovations

- **Gurmukhī script** — The Ādi Granth was written in the Gurmukhī script (developed by [[Gurū Aṅgad]]), giving the Punjabi language a standardised written form distinct from the Perso-Arabic script of the Mughal court and the Devanāgarī of Hindu scribes.
- **Musical organisation** — The Granth is arranged by rāga, not by author or theme, reflecting the centrality of music (kīrtan) in Sikh worship. Each rāga section begins with the hymn of the Guru who contributed it, then proceeds to other contributors.
- **Interlinear glosses** — Gurū Arjan sometimes inserted brief glosses or clarifications in the Gurmukhī text, always clearly demarcated.
- **Indexing** — A full index of hymns, authors, and rāgas was included — an advanced editorial feature for the 17th century.

## Construction of the Golden Temple

The Harimandir Sāhib (literally "Temple of God") — commonly called the Golden Temple — is the holiest shrine in Sikhism. Though the site had been consecrated by Gurū Rām Dās, who excavated the Amritsar tank, the central shrine itself was the work of Gurū Arjan.

### Architectural Vision

Gurū Arjan designed the complex with deliberate theological intent:

- **The tank (sarovara)** — The sacred pool of Amritsar ("Pool of Nectar"), excavated by Gurū Rām Dās, was enlarged and lined with brick by Gurū Arjan. The water was believed to have healing properties.
- **The causeway (bridge)** — A single bridge, symbolising the soul's journey from the material world (the four-sided entrance) to the spiritual realm (the sanctuary).
- **The four doors** — The shrine was built with four entrances, one on each side, signifying that the temple was open to all four castes (brāhmaṇa, kṣatriya, vaiśya, śūdra) and to everyone regardless of religion, gender, or nationality. This was a radical departure from Hindu temples, which typically had a single entrance and restricted access.
- **The lower level** — The shrine was constructed below ground level, so that worshippers must descend steps to enter — symbolising humility before the Divine.
- **The sanctum** — The inner sanctum housed the Ādi Granth and served as the focal point for congregational worship.

### Construction Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1574 | Gurū Rām Dās acquires the site and begins excavating the tank |
| 1581 | Gurū Arjan becomes Guru and takes over the project |
| 1588 | Foundation of the central shrine is laid by Mīān Mīr, a Muslim Sūfī saint of Lahore, at Gurū Arjan's invitation |
| 1589–1604 | Construction of the marble-based, multi-domed structure |
| 1604 | The shrine is completed; the Ādi Granth is installed on 1 September |
| 1760s | The upper floors and dome are gilded with gold foil by Maharaja Raṇjīt Siṅgh, giving it the name "Golden Temple" — the gold was added two centuries after Gurū Arjan |

### Symbolism

The Harimandir Sāhib was conceived not merely as a place of worship but as a living embodiment of Sikh theology. Its architecture preaches the same message as the scriptures: God is one, all are equal, humility is the path to the Divine, and the spiritual life is a journey from the material to the transcendent. The temple attracted pilgrims from across India, including Hindus and Muslims, creating a multi-faith centre that further alarmed the Mughal authorities who saw it as a rival pole of allegiance.

## The Martyrdom

The death of Gurū Arjan in 1606 is the pivot around which Sikh history turns. It transformed a pacifist religious community into a martyred faith, and from that martyrdom grew the militant Sikhism of the later Gurus.

### Historical Context

When the Mughal Emperor Akbar died in 1605, his son Jahāngīr ascended the throne after a brief succession struggle. Prince Khusrau, Jahāngīr's rebellious son, attempted to seize the throne and fled eastwards toward Kabul. On his way, he visited Gurū Arjan at Goindval in April 1606, seeking the Guru's blessings and financial support. The Guru gave him a modest donation (some sources say 100 gold coins). When Khusrau was captured and Jahāngīr learned of the visit, he interpreted it as an act of treason.

Jahāngīr's own memoirs (*Tuzuk-i-Jahāngīrī*) state his reasons for punishing the Guru:

> "At Goindwal, which is situated on the bank of the river Beas, there was a Hindu named Arjan who pretended to be a spiritual leader. For a long time he had been seducing the hearts of the simple-minded and calling himself a Guru. For this reason, the foolish people had great faith in him and for twenty years he had been collecting contributions and offerings. I ordered that he be brought into my presence, punished his house and property confiscated, and put him to death."

Jahāngīr's stated motives blend political (sheltering a rebel), financial (the Guru's growing wealth and the system of dasvandh), and religious (fear of an alternative spiritual authority).

### The Torture

The traditional Sikh account (*Gurbilās Chhevīṁ Pātshāhī*, 1718) describes the torture vividly:

- **Day 1** — Gurū Arjan is seated on a red-hot iron plate (*tattī tawī*), with boiling water poured over his body.
- **Day 2** — Burning sand is poured over his scalded flesh.
- **Day 3** — He is forced to sit in a cauldron of boiling water.
- **Day 4** — He is taken to the bank of the Rāvī River, where his body is immersed in the scalding, filth-laden water while being tortured further.
- **Day 5** — 30 May 1606. Gurū Arjan expires in the river. His body is never recovered — the Sikhs say the river carried him away, and the water itself became sacred.

Some sources (including the Jesuit missionary Jerome Xavier, who was present in Lahore) give a less gruesome account — the Guru was simply drowned in the Rāvī after being tortured. The exact details are debated, but all accounts agree on the fact of execution by state authority after torture.

### The Theological Meaning

The martyrdom of Gurū Arjan is not understood by Sikhs as a defeat but as a supreme act of witness (*shahīdī*). Key theological points:

- **Acceptance of God's will (Sabh meṁ sat guru rahiyā samāī)** — The Guru demonstrated absolute submission to the Divine will, refusing to curse his torturers or resist.
- **Non-violence under extreme provocation** — Gurū Arjan did not fight back, even though he had followers who would have died for him. This stand is seen as the perfection of the saintly path (bhaktī/pīrī) that would later be balanced by the political-military path (śaktī/mīrī) of his son [[Gurū Har Gobind]].
- **Purification through suffering** — The Guru's body was "returned to the five elements" (water, earth, fire, air, ether) through the torture — a literal dissolution into the elements as a final act of detachment.
- **The Guru as sacrifice** — Sikh tradition sees Gurū Arjan as a willing sacrificial offering (*bali*), whose death purified the community and consecrated the Sikh path with blood.

### The Aftermath

The martyrdom had immediate and long-term consequences:

- War broke out between the Sikhs and the Mughals within months. Gurū Arjan's son and successor, [[Gurū Har Gobind]], at age 11, declared that he would wear two swords — one for spiritual authority (pīrī) and one for temporal authority (mīrī) — and began arming his followers.
- The Akāl Takht (Throne of the Timeless One) was built directly opposite the Harimandir Sāhib as a political counterpoint to the spiritual shrine — the place where the Guru would sit as a temporal ruler and hold court.
- Sikhs abandoned their pacifist theology. Within a century, [[Gurū Tegh Bahādur]] would be martyred in Delhi (1675), and [[Gurū Gobind Siṅgh]] would create the Khalsa (1699) as a community of saint-soldiers.
- The martyrdom of Gurū Arjan became the founding event of Sikh shahīdī — a tradition of martyrdom that would claim [[Banda Siṅgh Bahādur]] (1716) and countless Sikhs during the 18th-century genocide under the Mughals and Afghans.

## Legacy

Gurū Arjan's legacy is woven into every layer of Sikh identity — scripture, architecture, liturgy, institution-building, and the theology of martyrdom that defines the community's self-understanding.

### In Scripture

Of the 5,894 hymns in the Guru Granth Sāhib, over **2,218 are Gurū Arjan's** — more than any other contributor. His most important compositions include:

- **Sukhmani Sāhib** ("Psalm of Peace") — 24 cantos (ashtpadīs), each with 8 stanzas. A sustained meditation on the nature of God, the human condition, and the path to peace through devotion, recited daily by Sikhs worldwide.
- **Lāvāṁ** — The four stanzas sung during the Sikh marriage ceremony (Anand Kāraj), describing the soul's union with the Divine.
- **Bārāh Māhā** ("The Twelve Months") — A lyrical depiction of the soul's longing for God expressed through the changing seasons of the Punjabi calendar.
- **Āsā dī Vār — contribution** — He expanded [[Gurū Nānak]]'s Āsā dī Vār with his own stanzas and set it to music for the morning liturgy.

### In Architecture

The Harimandir Sāhib (Golden Temple) stands as the physical embodiment of his vision — a place of pilgrimage that attracts over 100,000 visitors daily in the 21st century. The architectural innovations he introduced (four doors, lower-level entrance, causeway, interfaith foundation stone) became the template for all subsequent Sikh gurdwārās.

### In Institutions

- **Masand system** — Gurū Arjan formalised the network of local preachers (masands) who collected offerings (dasvandh — the tenth of one's income), maintained congregation centres, and served as liaisons between the Guru and dispersed communities from Kabul to Bengal.
- **Dasvandh** — The institutionalisation of the tithe gave the Sikh community an independent economic base, funding the construction of the Harimandir Sāhib and the langar (free kitchen).

### In Sikh Identity

The martyrdom of Gurū Arjan is the single most important event in Sikh history after the founding Guruship of [[Gurū Nānak]]. It completed the transition from a pacifist bhaktī movement to a community that would soon take up arms. His death transformed the meaning of "Sikh" (learner/disciple) into something that also implied "witness" and "martyr." Every subsequent Sikh martyr — from [[Gurū Tegh Bahādur]] to Banda Siṅgh Bahādur to the thousands killed in 18th-century genocides and 20th-century pogroms — stands in the lineage of Gurū Arjan's sacrifice.

### In Comparative Religion

Gurū Arjan is increasingly studied alongside figures like Jesus, Imām Ḥusayn, and Socrates as paradigmatic martyrs whose deaths shaped the identity of their communities. His combination of scripture compilation, institution-building, architectural patronage, and sacrificial death makes him a figure of exceptional significance — the theologian, the administrator, the poet, and the martyr all in one life. In the Sikh tradition, he is remembered with an epithet that captures every dimension of his contribution:

> **Gurū Arjan, the Fifth Guru — the compiler of the Word, the builder of the Door of Liberation, the first to seal the faith in blood.**
