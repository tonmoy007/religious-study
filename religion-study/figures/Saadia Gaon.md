# Saadia Gaon (סעדיה גאון)

The first great Jewish philosopher of the medieval period and the most prominent Gaon of the Sura Academy. Saadia's intellectual range was extraordinary: philosopher, biblical exegete, translator, grammarian, polemicist, liturgist, and halakhist. His work marks the beginning of systematic Jewish philosophy and the scientific study of Hebrew.

## Key Contributions

- **Translation of the Tanakh into Arabic (Tafsir):** Saadia produced the first complete translation of the Hebrew Bible into Judeo-Arabic, with commentary. It became the standard version for Arabic-speaking Jewish communities for centuries and influenced Karaite and even Muslim exegetes.
- ***Emunot ve-De'ot* (Beliefs and Opinions, 933 CE):** The first systematic work of Jewish philosophy. Written in Arabic as *Kitāb al-Amānāt wa'l-Iʿtiqādāt*, it reconciles revealed religion with rational philosophy, addressing creation, free will, the soul, resurrection, and the messianic era. It draws on Kalām (Islamic rational theology) while defending rabbinic Judaism.
- **Defender of Rabbinic Judaism against Karaism:** Saadia wrote polemical works refuting the Karaite rejection of the Oral Law. His systematic argumentation established the methodological foundation for all subsequent Rabbinic–Karaite debates.
- **Pioneer of Hebrew Philology:** His *Sefer ha-Egron* is the first known Hebrew dictionary and poetics. He wrote on grammar, developed Hebrew linguistic terminology, and composed liturgical poetry (*piyyutim*) many of which entered the standard prayer book.
- **Gaon of Sura (928–942 CE):** Appointed Gaon against opposition, he revitalised the declining Sura Academy through his scholarship, correspondence, and halakhic responsa that reached Jewish communities across the Islamic world.

## Life & Legacy

- Born in the Fayyum region of Egypt, he left for Eretz Yisrael and then to Babylon, where he studied under the Geonim of the day
- His appointment as Gaon was contested, leading to a bitter dispute with the Exilarch David ben Zakkai that ultimately required Caliphal intervention
- Among his lost works: a comprehensive commentary on the entire Talmud (only fragments survive)
- He pioneered the genre of the philosophical commentary — reading Scripture through a systematic theological lens
- Maimonides praised Saadia as the foundational figure of Jewish philosophy, though he disagreed with him on key points
