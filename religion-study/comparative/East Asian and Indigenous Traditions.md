# East Asian & Indigenous Traditions

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → Comparative Religion

These traditions stretch the very category of "religion" (see the caution in
[[World Religions Overview]]). Many are **non-exclusive** — a person may be
Confucian, Daoist, and Buddhist at once — and several blur the line between
religion, philosophy, and social order.

## Daoism (Taoism)
- **Core**: harmony with the **Dao** (the Way), the spontaneous order of nature.
- **Key ideas**: *wu wei* (effortless action), simplicity, naturalness.
- **Texts**: Dao De Jing (Laozi), Zhuangzi.
- Both a **philosophy** and an organized **religious** tradition (deities, ritual, alchemy).

## Confucianism
- **Core**: cultivation of virtue and right relationships to create social harmony.
- **Key ideas**: *ren* (humaneness), *li* (ritual propriety), filial piety, the *junzi* (exemplary person).
- **Texts**: Analects, Mencius, the Five Classics.
- Debated as ethics/political philosophy vs. religion — a live question for [[Philosophy of Religion]].

## Shinto
- Indigenous tradition of Japan; reverence for **kami** (spirits/sacred presences).
- Emphasis on purity, ritual, and connection to nature and ancestors.
- Historically syncretic with Buddhism.

## Indigenous & Oral Traditions
- Found worldwide (Indigenous American, African, Aboriginal Australian, etc.).
- **Features**: orally transmitted, embedded in land, kinship, and community; ancestor and spirit relations; ritual specialists (shamans).
- Often lack the creed/scripture model — challenging our default definition of religion.
- Examples of major regional clusters:
  - **African Traditional Religions** — hugely diverse (Yoruba, Akan, Igbo, Zulu, Dogon, etc.). Common threads: a High God / creator, lesser deities (orishas, loa), ancestor veneration, divination, initiation rites. Yoruba religion (Ifá) has been exceptionally influential, giving rise to Santería (Cuba), Candomblé (Brazil), and Vodou (Haiti) in the African diaspora. See [[Spread and Transformation of World Religions]] for the diaspora dimension.
  - **Indigenous American** — diverse cosmologies; common themes include a layered cosmos, the shaman as healer/mediator, vision quests, and deep kinship with the natural world (Andean *Pachamama*, Lakota *Wakan Tanka*).
  - **Aboriginal Australian** — **Dreaming** (the ancestral creation epoch); the land itself is sacred and storied; songlines, totems, and ritual cycles maintain the world.

## Syncretism in East Asian Practice
One of the most distinctive features of East Asian religiosity is its **non-exclusive, synthetic character**:
- Chinese popular religion seamlessly blends **ancestor veneration**, **Daoist ritual**, **Confucian ethics**, and **Mahayana Buddhism** — the "three teachings" (sān jiào 三教).
- **Japanese tradition** institutionalized this: Shinto and Buddhism coexisted for centuries (shinbutsu-shūgō); most Japanese participate in both Shinto rites (birth, harvest) and Buddhist ceremonies (death, memorials).
- **Korean shamanism (Muism)** persists alongside imported Buddhism and Confucianism.
- This challenges the Western assumption that religions are mutually exclusive — a key point for [[Philosophy of Religion]] and the definition of "belief" itself.

## Why These Matter for Your Study
- They **test the Abrahamic-shaped definition** of religion.
- They foreground **practice and harmony** over belief and doctrine.
- Confucianism and Daoism connect directly to ethics in [[Religious Ethics and Meaning]].

## Open Questions
- Is "non-exclusive" religiosity a distinct category of its own, or a difference of degree? Do East Asian practitioners truly "believe less" or simply practice differently?
- African Traditional Religions are often grouped under "Indigenous" — but Yoruba cosmology (with its pantheon, theology, and scripture-like oral corpus Ifá) is arguably as sophisticated as any "world religion." Why is the "world religion" label still denied to them?
- Confucianism is often called a "philosophy" rather than a religion — is this a genuinely descriptive distinction, or a legacy of Protestant-influenced definitions of religion?
- Shinto and Buddhism coexisted for over a millennium in Japan — what conditions make syncretism stable, and what causes it to break down (as with the明治-era State Shinto separation)?
- What is lost when oral traditions are written down and studied as texts? Does the academic study of religion inevitably favour scripture-based traditions?
