# World Religions Overview

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → Comparative Religion

A bird's-eye map of the major living traditions before we go deep. The goal is
orientation: who, where, what they hold central, and how scholars group them.

## The "Big" Traditions by Adherents (approx.)
| Tradition | Est. adherents | Core geography | Central orientation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Christianity | ~2.4 billion | Global | Salvation through Christ |
| Islam | ~1.9 billion | MENA, S/SE Asia, Africa | Submission to one God (Allah) |
| Hinduism | ~1.2 billion | South Asia | Dharma, liberation (moksha) |
| Buddhism | ~500 million | E/SE Asia | End of suffering (nirvana) |
| Folk/Indigenous | ~400 million | Worldwide | Local cosmologies, ancestors |
| Sikhism | ~25-30 million | South Asia (Punjab) | Devotion to one God, equality |
| Judaism | ~15 million | Israel, diaspora | Covenant with one God |
| Baha'i | ~5-8 million | Global (orig. Persia) | Unity of God, religion, humanity |
| Zoroastrianism | ~100-200k (historically massive influence) | India, Iran (diaspora) | Cosmic dualism, free choice |

*(Figures are scholarly estimates and vary by source and definition.)*

## How Scholars Group Them
- **Abrahamic** — trace lineage to Abraham; monotheistic; historical revelation. See [[Abrahamic Traditions]].
- **Dharmic (Indian)** — share concepts of karma, dharma, rebirth, liberation. See [[Dharmic Traditions]].
- **East Asian** — Daoism, Confucianism, and syncretic practice; often non-exclusive. See [[East Asian and Indigenous Traditions]].
- **Indigenous / oral** — locally rooted, transmitted orally, embedded in community and land.
- **Iranian / Zoroastrian** — sometimes classed with Abrahamic (due to influence) or treated separately; cosmic dualism, eschatology, free will. See note below.

### Traditions Not in the Table
Some traditions are small today but **historically pivotal**:
- **Zoroastrianism** (~100-200k adherents) — Persia's pre-Islamic faith; profoundly shaped Jewish, Christian, and Islamic eschatology (angels, judgment, resurrection, heaven/hell, messianic saviour). See [[Abrahamic Traditions]] for influence.
- **Manichaeism** (extinct) — a syncretic religion blending Zoroastrian, Christian, and Buddhist elements; once spanning from Rome to China.
- **Various new religious movements** — Mormonism, Cao Dai, Tenrikyo, and modern Pagan revivals are growing fields of study.

## Key Axes of Comparison
Use these dimensions to compare any two traditions:
1. **Concept of the ultimate** — personal God, impersonal absolute, many deities, or none?
2. **Human predicament** — sin, ignorance, suffering, exile?
3. **Path / solution** — faith, works, knowledge, ritual, meditation?
4. **Authority** — scripture, tradition, clergy, direct experience? See [[Sacred Texts Overview]].
5. **Time** — linear (creation → end) vs. cyclical (eternal return)?
6. **Community & ritual** — how belief is lived and reproduced.

## A Note on the Category "Religion"
The very word *religion* is a contested, partly Western-modern construct
(Wilfred Cantwell Smith). Some traditions (e.g., much of Hinduism, Confucianism)
don't map neatly onto a creed-centered model. Keep this in mind — comparison
clarifies, but it can also distort.

## Open Questions
- What are the limits of the "Abrahamic / Dharmic / East Asian" taxonomy? Does it obscure more than it reveals (e.g., Sikhism straddles Abrahamic and dharmic categories)?
- Should Zoroastrianism be considered its own family, a precursor to Abrahamic, or both?
- The "world religions" paradigm has been criticized as a colonial construct (Tomoko Masuzawa). What gets left out when we frame the study this way?
- Why do some traditions globalize while others remain culturally bound?
- How should we count "adherents" for traditions that are non-exclusive (East Asian) or have no formal membership (indigenous)?
