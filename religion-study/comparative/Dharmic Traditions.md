# Dharmic Traditions — Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, Sikhism

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → Comparative Religion

The "Indian" or *dharmic* family arose in South Asia and shares a conceptual
vocabulary — **dharma, karma, samsara, moksha** — even while disagreeing
profoundly about metaphysics. Compared to [[Abrahamic Traditions]], time here is
typically **cyclical** and the goal is **liberation** rather than salvation in
linear history.

## Shared Conceptual Vocabulary
- **Dharma** — cosmic/moral order, duty, or teaching (meaning shifts by tradition).
- **Karma** — moral causation; actions bear fruit.
- **Samsara** — the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth.
- **Moksha / nirvana / kevala** — liberation/release from that cycle (but see below — the traditions disagree on *what is liberated*).
- **Cyclical time** — vast recurring cosmic ages (yugas, kalpas).

> [!note] What liberation looks like differs sharply
> | Tradition | What is liberated? | What is liberation like? |
> |---|---|---|
> | Hinduism (Vedanta) | The eternal self (atman) realizes its identity with Brahman | Blissful unity, beyond duality |
> | Buddhism | There is no atman — liberation is release from the illusion of self | Nirvana: the "blowing out" of craving |
> | Jainism | The eternal jiva (soul) is purified of karmic matter | Kevala: omniscience, isolation from matter |
> | Sikhism | The soul merges with the one God | Union with the divine, through grace |

## Hinduism
- Not one religion but a **family** of traditions (Vaishnava, Shaiva, Shakta, etc.).
- **Texts**: Vedas, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita, epics (see [[Sacred Texts Overview]]).
- **Key ideas**: Brahman (ultimate reality), atman (self), the unity/diversity of the two.
- **Paths (yogas)**: knowledge (jnana), devotion (bhakti), action (karma).

## Buddhism
- Founded on the teaching of the **Buddha** ("the awakened one").
- **Core**: Four Noble Truths; suffering (dukkha) and its cessation.
- **Distinctive**: *anatta* — **no permanent self** (a sharp break from Hindu atman).
- **Vehicles**: Theravada, Mahayana, Vajrayana.

## Jainism
- Radical **non-violence (ahimsa)** and asceticism.
- Reality is eternal; liberation through purifying the soul (jiva) of karmic matter.
- No creator God; the cosmos is uncreated.

## Sikhism
- Founded by **Guru Nanak** (15th c.); often seen as bridging dharmic and Abrahamic ideas.
- **One formless God**, devotion, equality, honest living.
- **Text**: Guru Granth Sahib, treated as the living Guru.

## Comparative Snapshot
| Dimension | Hinduism | Buddhism | Jainism | Sikhism |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ultimate | Brahman | (Often) non-theistic | No creator | One God |
| Self | Atman (real) | Anatta (no fixed self) | Eternal jiva | Soul united with God |
| Goal | Moksha | Nirvana | Kevala (liberation) | Union with God |
| Authority | Vedas + many | Buddha's teaching | Tirthankaras | Guru Granth Sahib |

## The Broader Indian Milieu: Heterodox Schools
The dharmic "family" was never a consensus — it emerged from a vigorous debate among competing schools. Some key voices **outside** the four traditions above:

- **Cārvāka / Lokāyata** — an ancient **materialist** school (c. 600 BCE onward). Rejected the Vedas, the soul, karma, and rebirth. Only matter exists; perception is the only valid source of knowledge. A useful counterpoint: Indian philosophy was not all spiritual.
- **Ājīvika** — a fatalist school led by **Makkhali Gosāla** (contemporary of the Buddha). Taught that all beings are determined by fate (niyati); no free will, no moral causation. Popular in its day, then faded.
- **The six orthodox schools (darśanas)** of Hindu philosophy — Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika, Sāṃkhya, Yoga, Mīmāṃsā, Vedānta — each with different epistemologies and metaphysics, all accepting the Vedas' authority.

This diversity shows that "Hinduism" was always a contested, evolving conversation — not a single creed.

## Open Questions
- The anattā (no-self) doctrine of Buddhism is a radical break from the Hindu ātman — are these genuinely incompatible, or is the difference more about language and perspective?
- Sikhism bridges dharmic and Abrahamic categories — does the Abrahamic/Dharmic binary break down here? Is there a better taxonomy?
- If karma is a law of moral causation, does it function like a "natural law" — or does it presuppose a divine order (or a cosmic accountant)?
- Why did Hinduism absorb and incorporate Buddhist ideas in India (making the Buddha an avatar of Vishnu) while Buddhism largely disappeared from its birthplace?
- The Cārvāka materialists were marginalized — what does that tell us about power and orthodoxy in Indian religious history?
