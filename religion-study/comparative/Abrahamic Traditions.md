# Abrahamic Traditions — Judaism, Christianity, Islam, and Baha'i

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → Comparative Religion

Three (or four) traditions that claim descent from **Abraham**, share an ethical
monotheism, and understand history as the arena of divine revelation. They are
deeply related — and their differences are precisely where the most instructive
comparisons lie. For textual links, see [[Connections Between Religious Texts]].

## Shared Family Resemblances
- **One God** — transcendent, personal, creator, moral lawgiver.
- **Revelation in history** — God acts through events, prophets, and scripture.
- **Linear time** — creation → covenant/redemption → judgment/end.
- **Prophets** — Abraham, Moses, and others appear across all three.
- **Ethical covenant** — relationship with God entails moral obligation.

## Judaism
- **Core**: covenant between God and Israel; Torah as instruction.
- **Texts**: Tanakh (Torah, Nevi'im, Ketuvim); Talmud (rabbinic law & commentary).
- **Emphasis**: orthopraxy (right practice), law (halakha), peoplehood.
- **Key ideas**: covenant, mitzvot (commandments), messianic hope.

## Christianity
- **Core**: God's self-revelation in **Jesus of Nazareth**, believed to be the incarnate Son.
- **Texts**: Hebrew Bible ("Old Testament") + New Testament.
- **Emphasis**: salvation/grace, the Trinity, resurrection.
- **Internal diversity**: Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant — differing on authority, sacraments, salvation.

## Islam
- **Core**: **submission (islām)** to the one God (Allah); Muhammad as the final prophet.
- **Texts**: Qur'an (revelation) + Hadith (prophetic tradition).
- **Emphasis**: tawhid (absolute oneness of God), the Five Pillars, law (sharia).
- **Internal diversity**: Sunni, Shia, Sufi currents.

## Baha'i Faith
- **Origins**: emerged from 19th-century Shi'a Islam in Persia; founded by **Bahá'u'lláh** (1817-1892).
- **Core**: progressive revelation — God reveals His will through a succession of Messengers (Abraham, Moses, Buddha, Jesus, Muhammad, and now Bahá'u'lláh). Unity of God, unity of religion, unity of humankind.
- **Texts**: Writings of Bahá'u'lláh (Kitáb-i-Aqdas, Hidden Words, Seven Valleys) and `Abdu'l-Bahá.
- **Emphasis**: world peace, elimination of prejudice, equality of men and women, harmony of science and religion.
- **Distinctive**: no clergy; independent investigation of truth; administrative order (elected councils) rather than hierarchical authority.
- **Relation to Abrahamic family**: accepts previous prophets but supersedes their dispensations — a position that creates tension with orthodox Islam and Christianity (both view Muhammad/Jesus as final).

## Zoroastrianism — The Abrahamic Precursor
- Though not Abrahamic in origin, Zoroastrianism (founded by **Zarathustra**, likely c. 1200-1000 BCE in ancient Persia) **profoundly shaped** the monotheistic traditions that emerged later.
- **Ideas adopted into Judaism (esp. during the Babylonian exile and Persian period), Christianity, and Islam**:
  - **Eschatology**: a final judgment, resurrection of the dead, and a saviour figure (Saoshyant).
  - **Angels & demons**: the Amesha Spentas (holy immortals) and the adversarial spirit **Angra Mainyu** (later developed into Satan).
  - **Heaven & Hell**: as distinct post-mortem destinations.
  - **Dualism**: cosmic struggle between truth (*asha*) and falsehood (*druj*) — echoed in Christian and Manichaean thought.
- Texts: the **Avesta** (including the Gathas, hymns attributed to Zarathustra himself).
- Modern community small (~100-200k; the Parsis of India), but the theological DNA runs through all Abrahamic faiths.

## Comparative Snapshot
| Dimension | Judaism | Christianity | Islam | Baha'i |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nature of God | One, indivisible | One in Trinity | One, indivisible (tawhid) | One, unknowable essence, revealed through messengers |
| Central figure | Moses / Torah | Jesus Christ | Muhammad / Qur'an | Bahá'u'lláh |
| Human problem | Exile, disobedience | Sin | Forgetfulness, going astray | Distance from God, ignorance |
| Path | Covenant, law | Faith, grace | Submission, righteous deeds | Unified spiritual principles, service |
| Jesus | A teacher (not divine) | God incarnate | A revered prophet (not divine) | A divine messenger, not final |
| Finality of revelation | Torah ongoing interpreted | Christ is final | Muhammad is seal of the prophets | Progressive revelation continues |

## Fault Lines & Dialogue
- The **status of Jesus** is the sharpest theological divide among the Big Three.
- Baha'i's claim of ongoing revelation challenges the finality claims of both Christianity and Islam.
- All three (four) wrestle with **the same figures** (Abraham, Moses) read differently — a rich case study for [[Connections Between Religious Texts]].
- Monotheism shapes a shared engagement with [[The Problem of Evil]].
- Zoroastrian influence raises a fascinating question: how much of "biblical" theology is actually rooted in Persia? See [[Origins and the Axial Age]].

## Open Questions
- The Baha'i Faith claims to be the fulfillment of all prior religions — is this a legitimate claim within the Abrahamic lineage, or does it break with it?
- How should we account for Zoroastrianism's influence: is it a "source" or a "parallel development"?
- The term "Abrahamic" is sometimes contested for implying more unity than exists — does it obscure the deep differences between these traditions?
- How does the Jewish-Christian-Muslim-Baha'i relationship compare to the intra-dharmic relationship (Hinduism-Buddhism-Jainism-Sikhism)? Which family shows more internal tension, and why?
