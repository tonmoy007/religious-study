# Scripture-Based Comparative Study

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → Comparative Religion · [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]]

A systematic comparison of world religions **through their own sacred texts** — letting the scriptures speak side by side. This study covers the major theological themes across Abrahamic, Dharmic, East Asian, and Indigenous traditions, grounded in verbatim passages from the vault's [[Scriptures Library]].

---

## How to Read This Study

Each **theme** below is structured the same way:

1. **Introduction** — the question the theme addresses
2. **Scriptural evidence** — passages from multiple traditions, quoted directly
3. **Comparative analysis** — what is shared, what diverges, and why
4. **Connection type** — genealogical (shared source), diffusion (borrowed), or independent (archetypal convergence)
5. **Links** — to deeper dives in the vault

> [!tip] Use alongside
> The overview notes — [[Abrahamic Traditions]], [[Dharmic Traditions]], [[East Asian and Indigenous Traditions]] — provide the historical context. This study focuses on the **textual evidence**.

## Theme 1: Creation & Origins — The Beginning of All Things

Every tradition tells a story about how the world began. These origin accounts reveal each tradition's deepest assumptions about reality: is the cosmos created by a personal God, emitted from an impersonal absolute, or born from chaos?

### Genesis — The God Who Speaks

> [!quote] Genesis 1:1-3
> "In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth. And the earth was without form, and void; and darkness was upon the face of the deep. And the Spirit of God moved upon the face of the waters. And God said, **Let there be light**: and there was light."
> — [[01 Genesis]]

Creation by **divine speech**. God is transcendent, personal, and sovereign. Time is linear — a beginning, a middle, an end.

### The Rig Veda — The Cosmic Question

> [!quote] Rig Veda 10.129 (Nasadiya Sukta) — from [[The Upanishads (Part I)|The Upanishads]]
> "There was neither non-existence nor existence then; there was neither the realm of space nor the sky which is beyond. What stirred? Where? In whose protection? Was there water, bottomlessly deep?… **Who really knows? Who will here proclaim it?** Whence was it produced? Whence is this creation? The gods came afterwards, with the creation of this universe. **He who knows whence it came — He knows, or maybe even He does not know.**"
> — Rig Veda, Book X, Hymn 129

Not a declaration but a **question**. The origin is a mystery that precedes the gods themselves. This is the most sceptical creation text in any scripture — radically different from Genesis.

### The Qur'an — Creation by Command

> [!quote] The divine fiat
> "He it is Who created the heavens and the earth in six Days — and His Throne was upon the water — that He might try you, which of you is best in conduct. If thou shouldst say: **Lo! ye are indeed raised up after death!** those who disbelieve would surely say: **This is naught but mere magic.**"
> — [[011 Hud|Hud 11:7]]

> [!quote] The creative command
> "His command, when He intendeth a thing, is only that He saith unto it: **Be! and it is.**"
> — [[036 Yaseen|Yaseen 36:82]]

Like Genesis, creation is by **divine command** — but the Qur'an links creation explicitly to judgment ("raised up after death") and moral accountability.

### The Dao De Jing — The Unnameable Origin

> [!quote] Dao De Jing, Chapter 1
> "The **Tao** that can be trodden is not the enduring and unchanging Tao. The name that can be named is not the enduring and unchanging name. (Conceived of as) having no name, it is the Originator of heaven and earth; (conceived of as) having a name, it is the Mother of all things."
> — [[Tao Te Ching]]

The origin is **unnameable**, impersonal, and ineffable. It does not *will* creation — it *is* the spontaneous ground from which all things arise.

### Enuma Elish — Creation from Cosmic Conflict

> [!quote] The Babylonian creation epic
> "When on high the heaven had not been named, Firm ground below had not been called by name, Naught but primordial Apsu, their begetter, (And) Mummu-Tiamat, she who bore them all, Their waters mingling as a single body… Then it was that the gods were formed within them."
> — [[Mesopotamian Scriptures]] (Enuma Elish, Tablet I)

The Mesopotamian account is the **oldest** of those cited here. Creation emerges from the mingling of fresh (Apsu) and salt (Tiamat) waters, followed by divine conflict. This is the tradition the Hebrew authors were likely responding to and reworking.

### Comparison Table

| Tradition | Source of creation | Mode | Time | Role of humanity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Judaism/Christianity | A personal God | Spoken word | Linear | Image-bearers, stewards |
| Islam | Allah | Command ("Be!") | Linear | Vicegerents (khalifa) |
| Hinduism (Vedic) | An unknowable One | Emanation/question | Cyclical | Part of the cosmic order |
| Daoism | The nameless Tao | Spontaneous flow | Cyclical | To harmonize with nature |
| Babylonian | Primordial waters | Divine conflict | Linear | Created to serve the gods |

### What kind of connection?

- **Genealogical**: Genesis → (interpreted by) John's *Logos* → (engaged by) the Qur'an. A shared Abrahamic stream.
- **Independent convergence**: Rig Veda and Dao De Jing both point to an ineffable source — but the former is questioning, the latter is confident. Neither has a personal creator.
- **Contrast of type**: Babylonian (conflict → creation) vs. Hebrew (speech → creation) — the same cultural milieu, radically different theology.

## Theme 2: The Nature of the Ultimate — God, Brahman, Dao, Emptiness

What is the ultimate reality? Is it personal, impersonal, both, or neither? This is arguably the deepest divide between traditions.

### Judaism — The One God

> [!quote] The Shema
> "Hear, O Israel: the LORD our God, the LORD is One."
> — [[05 Deuteronomy|Deuteronomy 6:4]]

> [!quote] The unimageable God
> "Thou canst not see My face, for no man shall see Me and live."
> — [[02 Exodus|Exodus 33:20]]

Radical monotheism. God is one, transcendent, personal, and cannot be depicted.

### Christianity — One God in Three Persons

> [!quote] The Great Commission
> "Go ye therefore, and teach all nations, baptizing them in the **name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost.**"
> — [[40 Matthew|Matthew 28:19]]

> [!quote] The Word made flesh
> "And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us, (and we beheld his glory, the glory as of the only begotten of the Father,) full of grace and truth."
> — [[43 John|John 1:14]]

God is both one (creedally) and three (experientially). The most distinctive claim: God became a particular human being.

### Islam — Absolute Oneness

> [!quote] Surah Al-Ikhlas
> "Say: He is Allah, the One! Allah, the eternally Besought of all! He begetteth not nor was begotten. And there is none comparable unto Him."
> — [[112 Al-Ikhlas|Al-Ikhlas 112:1-4]]

> [!quote] Ayat al-Kursi (The Throne Verse)
> "Allah! There is no deity save Him, the Alive, the Eternal. Neither slumber nor sleep overtaketh Him. Unto Him belongeth whatsoever is in the heavens and whatsoever is in the earth…"
> — [[002 Al-Baqara|Al-Baqara 2:255]]

The sharpest possible rejection of both Trinity (no begotten) and any depiction. God is absolutely one, utterly transcendent.

### Hinduism — Brahman and the Atman

> [!quote] The neti-neti (not this, not that)
> "This (Self) is not this, nor is it this; it is incomprehensible, as it is not comprehended; it is indestructible, as it is not destroyed; it is not attached, as it is not attached; it is unfettered, it does not suffer, it is not injured."
> — [[The Upanishads (Part I)|Brihadaranyaka Upanishad]] 3.9.26

> [!quote] The unity of Atman and Brahman
> "That art thou" (Tat tvam asi)
> — [[The Upanishads (Part I)|Chhandogya Upanishad]] 6.8.7

The ultimate (Brahman) is impersonal, all-pervading, and identical in essence with the deepest self (Atman). This is the most radically non-dual vision of any major tradition.

### Buddhism — The Silence of the Buddha

> [!quote] The unanswerable questions
> "**The world is eternal** — or not — or both — or neither. The world is finite — or not — or both — or neither. The Tathagata exists after death — or not — or both — or neither." The Buddha said: "Whether these views arise or not, there is still birth, decay, death, grief, lamentation, suffering… **I teach suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path.**"
> — [[The Dhammapada|Dhammapada]] / Majjhima Nikaya (paraphrased)

The Buddha refused to answer metaphysical questions. The ultimate concern is not speculation about God but the **diagnosis and cure of suffering**.

### Comparison Table

| Tradition | Ultimate | Personal? | Relation to self |
|---|---|---|---|
| Judaism | YHWH (One) | Yes | Creator vs. creature (absolute distinction) |
| Christianity | Trinity (One in Three) | Yes (incarnate in Jesus) | Creator vs. creature, but Christ bridges |
| Islam | Allah (One, indivisible) | Yes (no incarnation) | Creator vs. servant (absolute submission) |
| Hinduism (Vedanta) | Brahman | Both/nor (nirguna/saguna) | Atman = Brahman (identity) |
| Buddhism | Emptiness (sunyata) / Dharma | No | Anatta (no self to unite) |
| Daoism | The Dao | Impersonal | Align with the Dao |

### What kind of connection?

- **Theistic traditions** (Abrahamic) vs. **non-dual / non-theistic** (Dharmic, Daoist). This is the deepest structural divide in comparative religion.
- **Islam and Judaism** are structurally closer to each other than either is to Christianity (both reject the Trinity).
- **Hinduism's** nirguna (without qualities) Brahman and **Buddhist** emptiness are sometimes compared — but the former is a ground of being, the latter is the absence of any fixed essence. Not the same.

## Theme 3: The Human Predicament — What's Wrong With Us?

Every religion diagnoses a fundamental problem with the human condition. The cure depends on the diagnosis.

### Judaism & Christianity — Sin and Separation

> [!quote] The Fall (Genesis)
> "And the LORD God commanded the man, saying, Of every tree of the garden thou mayest freely eat: But of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, thou shalt not eat of it: for in the day that thou eatest thereof thou shalt surely die."
> — [[01 Genesis|Genesis 2:16-17]]

> "And he said, I heard thy voice in the garden, and I was afraid, because I was naked; and I hid myself."
> — [[01 Genesis|Genesis 3:10]]

> [!quote] The heart's condition (Jeremiah)
> "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked: who can know it?"
> — [[24 Jeremiah|Jeremiah 17:9]]

The human problem is **disobedience** to God, resulting in separation, guilt, and a corrupted nature.

### Christianity specifically — Original Sin

> [!quote] Romans
> "Wherefore, as by one man sin entered into the world, and death by sin; and so death passed upon all men, for that all have sinned."
> — [[45 Romans|Romans 5:12]]

Sin is not just individual acts but a **universal condition** inherited from Adam.

### Islam — Forgetfulness and Going Astray

> [!quote] The human tendency
> "And We had already taken a covenant from Adam, but he forgot; and We found not in him determination."
> — [[020 Ta-Ha|Ta-Ha 20:115]]

> "And if God should touch thee with hurt, there is none to remove it save Him; and if He intendeth good for thee, there is none to repel His bounty."
> — [[010 Yunus|Yunus 10:107]]

The problem is **forgetfulness** (nisyan) and weakness, not original sin. Humans are born innocent but prone to heedlessness.

### Buddhism — Suffering and Craving

> [!quote] The First Noble Truth — from [[The Gospel of Buddha]]
> "Birth is suffering, decay is suffering, sickness is suffering, death is suffering. To be united with the unpleasant is suffering, to be separated from the pleasant is suffering, not to obtain what one desires is suffering. **In short, the five aggregates of clinging are suffering.**"

> [!quote] The Second Noble Truth
> "The cause of suffering is **craving** (tanha) which leads to rebirth, accompanied by delight and passion, seeking pleasure here and there: craving for sensual pleasures, craving for existence, craving for annihilation."
> — [[The Dhammapada|Dhammapada]] / Samyutta Nikaya

The problem is not sin but **craving and ignorance** — the mind's attachment to what is impermanent.

### Hinduism — Ignorance (Avidya)

> [!quote] The root of bondage
> "The self-existent Lord pierced the senses outward; therefore one looks outward, not within. A certain wise man, desiring immortality, turned his eyes inward and saw the Self."
> — [[The Upanishads (Part I)|Katha Upanishad]] 4.1

> [!quote] The bondage of karma
> "As a man acts, so does he become. A man of good deeds becomes good; a man of evil deeds becomes evil."
> — [[The Upanishads (Part I)|Brihadaranyaka Upanishad]] 4.4.5

The human problem is **avidya** (ignorance) — mistaking the temporary for the eternal, the self for the Self. This ignorance fuels karma and keeps one bound to samsara.

### Comparison Table

| Tradition | Problem | Root cause | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Judaism | Disobedience, exile | Free choice | Separation from God |
| Christianity | Sin (inherited + personal) | The Fall, human nature | Spiritual death, condemnation |
| Islam | Forgetfulness, weakness | Human heedlessness | Distance from guidance |
| Hinduism | Ignorance (avidya) | Mistaking maya for reality | Bondage to samsara |
| Buddhism | Suffering (dukkha) | Craving (tanha) | Rebirth and dissatisfaction |
| Daoism | Disharmony | Forcing, over-complicating | Unnaturalness, suffering |

### What kind of connection?

- **Genealogical**: The Jewish concept of sin develops into Christian original sin; Islam pushes back toward a milder view (forgetfulness not corruption).
- **Independent**: The Buddhist craving and Hindu ignorance arise from a shared Indian milieu (karma/samsara framework) — but Buddhism abandons the self that Hinduism seeks to liberate.
- **Structural parallel**: All traditions diagnose a gap between how things are and how they should be — but they name the gap differently (sin, ignorance, craving, disharmony).

## Theme 4: The Path to Deliverance — The Way Out

If the diagnosis differs, the prescribed cure differs too. Here are the paths each tradition prescribes.

### Christianity — Faith and Grace

> [!quote] Salvation by grace
> "For by grace are ye saved through faith; and that not of yourselves: it is the gift of God: Not of works, lest any man should boast."
> — [[49 Ephesians|Ephesians 2:8-9]]

> [!quote] The Greatest Commandment
> "Thou shalt love the Lord thy God with all thy heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy mind. This is the first and great commandment. And the second is like unto it, Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself."
> — [[40 Matthew|Matthew 22:37-39]]

The path is **faith** in Christ's atoning work, expressed in love. Grace precedes human effort.

### Islam — Submission and Righteous Deeds

> [!quote] The path of submission
> "And who is better in religion than he who **submiteth his purpose to Allah** while doing good (to others) and followeth the creed of Abraham, the upright? And Allah chose Abraham for a friend."
> — [[004 An-Nisa|An-Nisa 4:125]]

> [!quote] The Five Pillars (from Hadith)
> "Islam is built upon five: the testimony that there is no god but Allah and that Muhammad is His messenger, the establishment of prayer, the giving of zakah, the fast of Ramadan, and pilgrimage to the House."
> — [[Sahih al-Bukhari|Bukhari 1:7]]

The path combines **submission** (islam), **righteous deeds**, and **observance** of divinely ordained practices.

### Judaism — Covenant and Law

> [!quote] What God requires
> "He hath shewed thee, O man, what is good; and what doth the LORD require of thee, but to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with thy God?"
> — [[33 Micah|Micah 6:8]]

> [!quote] The way of Torah
> "For this commandment which I command thee this day, it is not hidden from thee, neither is it far off… But the word is very nigh unto thee, in thy mouth, and in thy heart, that thou mayest do it."
> — [[05 Deuteronomy|Deuteronomy 30:11,14]]

The path is **living within the covenant** — obeying God's law, pursuing justice, and walking humbly.

### Hinduism — The Three Yogas

> [!quote] The three paths — from the [[Bhagavad Gita (The Song Celestial)|Bhagavad Gita]]
> "Whoso ever shall offer to me with devotion a leaf, a flower, a fruit, or water — that offering of love, of the pure of heart, I accept."
> — Bhagavad Gita 9.26 (the path of **bhakti** / devotion)

> "Better is the vision of knowledge than the constant practice of discipline; and better than knowledge is meditation; but better than meditation is the surrender of the fruit of actions."
> — Bhagavad Gita 12.12 (the path of **karma** / selfless action)

> "When a man has cast away the vain conceit of self, and the strength of his own strength, and the pride of his own knowledge — then he attains to peace."
> — Bhagavad Gita 18.53 (the path of **jnana** / knowledge)

Hinduism offers **multiple paths** suited to different temperaments: devotion (bhakti), selfless action (karma), and knowledge (jnana).

### Buddhism — The Eightfold Path

> [!quote] The Fourth Noble Truth — from [[The Gospel of Buddha]]
> "The truth of the path that leads to the cessation of suffering is the **Noble Eightfold Path**: Right Understanding, Right Thought, Right Speech, Right Action, Right Livelihood, Right Effort, Right Mindfulness, Right Concentration."

> [!quote] The middle way
> "There are two extremes: a life given to pleasures and a life given to mortifications. The Tathagata has discovered the **Middle Path** which gives vision and knowledge and leads to peace, insight, enlightenment, and Nirvana."
> — [[The Dhammapada|Dhammapada]] / Samyutta Nikaya

The path is a **structured discipline** of ethical conduct, mental training, and wisdom — a middle way between indulgence and asceticism.

### Sikhism — Devotion and Service

> [!quote] The path of the Gurus — from the [[Guru Granth Sahib Index|Guru Granth Sahib]]
> "One who recognizes the One God among all beings does not speak of ego. **He alone knows the path who sees the One in all.**"
> — Guru Granth Sahib, Ang 223

> "**Truth is high, but higher still is truthful living.**"
> — Guru Granth Sahib, Ang 1400

The path combines **devotion** (to the one God), **honest living**, and **service** to others.

### Comparison Table

| Tradition | Path summarized | Primary means | Role of grace vs. effort |
|---|---|---|---|
| Christianity | Faith + love | Grace received through faith | Grace initiates, effort responds |
| Islam | Submission + righteous deeds | Observance + intention | God's mercy, human effort required |
| Judaism | Covenant observance | Law (halakha), justice | Both — the covenant is a partnership |
| Hinduism | Yogas (devotion, action, knowledge) | Practice suited to temperament | Self-effort through chosen path |
| Buddhism | Eightfold Path | Mindfulness, ethics, wisdom | Self-effort, but requires "right" conditions |
| Sikhism | Devotion + honest living | Meditation on God's name, service | Grace of the Guru + human effort |
| Daoism | Wu wei (effortless action) | Aligning with nature | Letting go of forcing |

## Theme 5: Love & Compassion — The Highest Virtue

While traditions disagree on metaphysics, they converge remarkably on the supreme ethical virtue.

### Christianity — Agape

> [!quote] 1 Corinthians 13
> "Though I speak with the tongues of men and of angels, and have not **charity (love)** , I am become as sounding brass, or a tinkling cymbal… And now abideth faith, hope, charity, these three; but the greatest of these is charity."
> — [[46 1 Corinthians|1 Corinthians 13:1,13]]

> [!quote] Love your enemies
> "But I say unto you, **Love your enemies**, bless them that curse you, do good to them that hate you, and pray for them which despitefully use you, and persecute you."
> — [[40 Matthew|Matthew 5:44]]

### Islam — Mercy and Compassion

> [!quote] The Bismillah
> "In the name of **Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful**" (Bismillah ir-Rahman ir-Rahim)
> — Opening words of every surah except one

> [!quote] Mercy as divine attribute
> "My mercy encompasses all things."
> — [[007 Al-A'raf|Al-A'raf 7:156]]

### Buddhism — Loving-Kindness

> [!quote] The Metta Sutta — from [[The Dhammapada|Dhammapada]] reflections
> "As a mother would risk her own life to protect her child, her only child, even so toward all living beings one should cultivate a **boundless heart** — loving-kindness for all the world, above, below, and all around, unstinted, without hatred or enmity."
> — Sutta Nipata 1.8 (Metta Sutta)

### Hinduism — The Highest Reach

> [!quote] The Gita on compassion
> "The man who is free from malice, **compassionate** to all beings, free from egotism and self-love, patient in sorrow and happy in joy — he is dear to me."
> — [[Bhagavad Gita (The Song Celestial)|Bhagavad Gita]] 12.13-14

### Confucianism — Ren (Humaneness)

> [!quote] The principle of reciprocity
> "Tsze-kung asked: 'Is there one word which may serve as a rule of practice for all one's life?' The Master said: 'Is not **RECIPROCITY** such a word? What you do not want done to yourself, do not do to others.'"
> — [[The Analects of Confucius|Analects]] XV.24

### The Golden Rule — A Universal

> [!quote] Across traditions
> | Tradition | Form | Source |
> |---|---|---|
> | Judaism | "Love thy neighbour as thyself" | [[03 Leviticus|Leviticus 19:18]] |
> | Christianity | "Do unto others as you would have them do unto you" | [[40 Matthew|Matthew 7:12]] |
> | Islam | "None of you believes until he loves for his brother what he loves for himself" | [[04 Jami\` at-Tirmidhi|Tirmidhi 2585]] |
> | Confucianism | "What you do not want done, do not do to others" | [[The Analects of Confucius|Analects XV.24]] |
> | Buddhism | "All tremble at punishment, all love life — do not kill nor cause slaughter" | [[The Dhammapada|Dhammapada 129-130]] |
> | Hinduism | "This is the sum of duty: do naught to others which would cause you pain if done to you" | Mahabharata (Anushasana Parva) |

## Theme 6: Suffering & Evil — Why Is There Suffering?

The problem of suffering is arguably the most universal theological question. Every tradition must address it.

### The Book of Job — Questioning God

> [!quote] Job's complaint
> "Even to day is my complaint bitter: my stroke is heavier than my groaning. Oh that I knew where I might find him! that I might come even to his seat!"
> — [[18 Job|Job 23:2-3]]

> [!quote] The divine response
> "Where wast thou when I laid the foundations of the earth? declare, if thou hast understanding."
> — [[18 Job|Job 38:4]]

Job receives not an answer but an **encounter**. The message: God's ways exceed human understanding; the proper response is trust, not explanation.

### The Qur'an — Trial and Return

> [!quote] Suffering as trial
> "And surely We shall try you with something of fear and hunger, and loss of wealth and lives and fruits; but give glad tidings to the steadfast — who, when a misfortune overtaketh them, say: **Lo! we are Allah's and lo! unto Him we are returning.**"
> — [[002 Al-Baqara|Al-Baqara 2:155-156]]

> [!quote] No burden beyond capacity
> "Allah tasketh not a soul beyond its scope."
> — [[002 Al-Baqara|Al-Baqara 2:286]]

Suffering is a **test** from God, and every trial is proportionate. The response is patience (sabr) and trust (tawakkul).

### Buddhism — Suffering as the First Truth

> [!quote] The Buddha's diagnosis
> "Now this, O monks, is the noble truth of suffering: birth is suffering, old age is suffering, disease is suffering, death is suffering…"
> — [[The Dhammapada|Dhammapada]] / Samyutta Nikaya 56.11

Suffering (dukkha) is not an anomaly to be explained but the **starting point of the entire path**. The goal is not to understand why suffering exists but to **end it** through the Eightfold Path.

### Hinduism — Karma and Rebirth

> [!quote] The law of karma
> "According as one acts, according as one behaves, so does he become. The doer of good becomes good, the doer of evil becomes evil."
> — [[The Upanishads (Part I)|Brihadaranyaka Upanishad]] 4.4.5

Suffering is the **consequence of past actions** (karma) across lifetimes. This is not punishment by a judge but cosmic cause-and-effect. The solution is to transcend karma entirely through liberation.

### Comparison Table

| Tradition | Why suffering exists | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Judaism/Christianity | Sin entered the world, creation is fallen | Redemption, restoration |
| Islam | Test from God; proportionate | Patience, trust, return to God |
| Hinduism | Karma (moral causation across lives) | Liberation (moksha) from the cycle |
| Buddhism | Craving and attachment | Cessation of craving (nirvana) |
| Daoism | Forcing against the Dao | Wu wei — flow with nature |

### What kind of connection?

- **Karmic traditions** (Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism) share a structural answer: suffering fits a cosmic moral order.
- **Theistic traditions** all wrestle with the tension between an all-good, all-powerful God and the reality of suffering — the "problem of evil."
- **Islam** largely avoids the problem by emphasizing God's sovereignty and wisdom: whatever God wills is just by definition.
- **Job** and the **Buddha** share a surprising parallel: both reject easy answers (the friends' theodicy in Job; speculative metaphysics in Buddhism) and focus on the proper **response** to suffering.

## Theme 7: Eschatology & Afterlife — What Happens After Death?

What lies beyond this life? The answers range from resurrection to reincarnation to extinction of the self.

### Judaism — Resurrection and the World to Come

> [!quote] Daniel's vision
> "And many of them that sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt."
> — [[27 Daniel|Daniel 12:2]]

> [!quote] The Psalmist's hope
> "For Thou wilt not leave my soul to Sheol; neither wilt Thou suffer Thy godly one to see the pit."
> — [[19 Psalms|Psalm 16:10]]

Jewish eschatology develops from a shadowy underworld (Sheol) to a belief in **resurrection** of the righteous.

### Christianity — Judgment and Eternal Life

> [!quote] The final judgment
> "And I saw a great white throne, and him that sat on it, from whose face the earth and the heaven fled away… And the dead were judged out of those things which were written in the books, according to their works."
> — [[66 Revelation|Revelation 20:11-12]]

> [!quote] The resurrection
> "But now is Christ risen from the dead, and become the firstfruits of them that slept. For since by man came death, by man came also the resurrection of the dead."
> — [[46 1 Corinthians|1 Corinthians 15:20-21]]

### Islam — The Day of Judgment

> [!quote] The resurrection
> "And the trumpet is blown, and lo! from the graves they come forth unto their Lord… And the earth shineth with the light of her Lord, and the Book is set up, and the prophets and the witnesses are brought…"
> — [[039 Az-Zumar|Az-Zumar 39:68-69]]

> [!quote] Paradise and Hell
> "Lo! those who believe and do good works, their Lord guideth them by their faith. Rivers will flow beneath them in the Gardens of Delight."
> — [[010 Yunus|Yunus 10:9]]

### Hinduism — Reincarnation and Liberation

> [!quote] The cycle of rebirth
> "As a man casting off worn-out garments takes new ones, so the embodied soul, casting off worn-out bodies, enters into other new ones."
> — [[Bhagavad Gita (The Song Celestial)|Bhagavad Gita]] 2.22

> [!quote] Liberation from the cycle
> "When all the bonds of the heart are broken here on earth, then the mortal becomes immortal."
> — [[The Upanishads (Part I)|Katha Upanishad]] 6.15

### Buddhism — Rebirth without a Self

> [!quote] No self that transmigrates
> "Just as the flame of one lamp lights another, but nothing passes from the first to the second — so the being is reborn without anything passing over."
> — [[The Gospel of Buddha|Gospel of Buddha]] (Milinda Panha analogy)

> [!quote] Nirvana — the end of suffering
> "There is, O monks, a state where there is neither earth nor water, nor heat nor air… neither this world nor another, neither sun nor moon. That I call **neither arising nor passing away, neither standing still nor being born, neither support nor development, without basis — that is the end of suffering.**"
> — [[The Dhammapada|Dhammapada]] / Udana 8.1

### Comparison Table

| Tradition | After death | Final goal | Basis of destination |
|---|---|---|---|
| Judaism | Resurrection of the dead | World to Come (Olam Ha-Ba) | Covenant faithfulness |
| Christianity | Resurrection → judgment | Heaven (eternal life) / Hell | Faith in Christ (works as evidence) |
| Islam | Resurrection → judgment | Paradise (Jannah) / Hell (Jahannam) | Faith + deeds |
| Hinduism | Rebirth (samsara) | Moksha (liberation from the cycle) | Karma accumulated |
| Buddhism | Rebirth (but no self) | Nirvana (blowing out of craving) | Karma + insight |
| Jainism | Rebirth (soul migrates) | Kevala (omniscient liberation) | Purification of karmic matter |

### What kind of connection?

- **Genealogical**: The Zoroastrian framework (resurrection, judgment, heaven/hell) likely shaped Jewish, Christian, and Islamic eschatology during the Persian period. See [[Abrahamic Traditions]].
- **Dharmic stream**: Reincarnation (samsara) is shared across Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism — but Buddhism radically reinterprets *what* is reborn (no self).
- **Deep difference**: Abrahamic traditions see the afterlife as a **divine judgment** by a personal God; Dharmic traditions see it as **automatic consequence** (karma) of one's actions, without a judge.
- **Nirvana** is not "heaven" — it is the cessation of the entire samsaric process, not an eternal life within it.

## Theme 8: Mysticism & Direct Experience — Union with the Ultimate

Beyond doctrine and ritual lies the path of **direct experience** — the mystic's claim to encounter ultimate reality directly.

### The Upanishads — Tat Tvam Asi (That Thou Art)

> [!quote] The identity of self and ultimate
> "This whole world is Brahman. Tranquil, let one worship it as that from which he came forth, as that into which he will be dissolved, as that in which he breathes."
> — [[The Upanishads (Part I)|Chhandogya Upanishad]] 3.14.1

> [!quote] The realized sage
> "When the seer sees the brilliant Maker and Lord of all as the Self — then, having known Him, he becomes wise, and being liberated from all fetters, he attains to liberation."
> — [[The Upanishads (Part I)|Shvetashvatara Upanishad]] 3.13-14

The highest mystical realization in Vedanta: **Atman (self) = Brahman (ultimate)** — not union but recognition of an already-existing identity.

### Sufism — Fana (Annihilation in God)

> [!quote] The Sufi longing — from Rumi
> "I died as a mineral and became a plant,
> I died as a plant and rose to animal,
> I died as animal and I was a man.
> Why should I fear? When was I less by dying?
> Yet once more I shall die as man to soar
> With angels blest; but even from angelhood
> I must pass on: all except God doth perish."
> — Jalaluddin Rumi (not in vault scriptures, but central to Islamic mysticism)

The goal of Sufi mysticism is **fana** — annihilation of the ego-self in the divine presence, followed by *baqa* (subsistence in God).

### Christian Mysticism — The Cloud of Unknowing

> [!quote] The via negativa
> "For of God Himself can no man think, and therefore it is our choice to leave all thought of Him and to let go of all desire for Him, and to enter into that **darkness and cloud of unknowing**, where we may be united with Him by love."
> — Pseudo-Dionysius / [[The Gospel of Buddha|The Cloud of Unknowing]] (paraphrased)

> [!quote] Paul's third heaven
> "I knew a man in Christ above fourteen years ago, (whether in the body, I cannot tell; or whether out of the body, I cannot tell: God knoweth;) such an one caught up to the third heaven."
> — [[47 2 Corinthians|2 Corinthians 12:2]]

### Zen Buddhism — Satori (Awakening)

> [!quote] The direct experience beyond words
> "A special transmission outside the scriptures;
> No dependence on words and letters;
> Direct pointing at the mind of man;
> Seeing one's nature and attaining Buddhahood."
> — Attributed to Bodhidharma

> [!quote] The Heart Sutra
> "Form is emptiness, emptiness is form. Emptiness is not separate from form, form is not separate from emptiness."
> — Prajnaparamita (not yet in vault library)

### Mysticism Compared

| Tradition | Goal of mysticism | Method | Relation to Ultimate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vedanta Hinduism | Recognize Atman = Brahman | Meditation, discernment (neti-neti) | Identity (already the case) |
| Sufi Islam | Fana — annihilation in God | Dhikr (remembrance), love, poetry | Annihilation then subsistence |
| Christian mysticism | Unio mystica — union with God | Contemplation, via negativa | Union but not identity |
| Zen Buddhism | Satori — awakening | Zazen (sitting), koans | Direct seeing, no "self" to unite |
| Daoism | Wu wei — effortless harmony | Spontaneity, simplicity | Alignment, not union |

### What kind of connection?

- **Independent convergence**: Mystics across all traditions describe the ultimate as **ineffable** — beyond language, beyond concepts.
- **Real difference**: For Vedanta, the goal is realizing **you are already that**. For Abrahamic mystics, the goal is **union with a God who remains distinct**. For Buddhism, the goal is **seeing through the illusion of self altogether**.
- **Daoist wu wei** is a unique third way: not union, not annihilation, but **flowing with the pattern of nature**.

## Theme 9: Scripture & Revelation — How the Text Itself Is Understood

Traditions differ not only in *what* their scriptures say but in *what they believe scripture is*.

### The Qur'an — Literal Divine Speech

> [!quote] The Qur'an on itself
> "And lo! it is a **revealed Scripture** — We have revealed it in a night of power — And what will convey unto thee what the Night of Power is! The Night of Power is better than a thousand months. The angels and the Spirit descend therein, by the permission of their Lord, with all decrees. It is peace until the rising of the dawn."
> — [[097 Al-Qadr|Al-Qadr 97:1-5]]

> "A Book whereof the verses are explained in detail — **a Qur'an in Arabic, for people who understand**."
> — [[041 Fussilat|Fussilat 41:3]]

The Qur'an is understood as the **literal speech of God** (kalam Allah), delivered through the Angel Gabriel. Its Arabic is integral to its identity — translation is interpretation, not the Qur'an itself.

### The Vedas — Shruti (That Which Is Heard)

> [!quote] The revealed origin
> "The Vedas are the breath of the Eternal."
> — [[The Upanishads (Part I)|Brihadaranyaka Upanishad]] 2.4.10

The Vedas are **shruti** — "that which is heard" — not composed by humans but **apprehended** by ancient seers (rishis) in deep meditation. They are eternal and authorless (apaurusheya).

### The Bible — Inspired Testament

> [!quote] The nature of scripture
> "**All scripture is given by inspiration of God**, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness."
> — [[55 2 Timothy|2 Timothy 3:16]]

> [!quote] Prophecy as origin
> "For the prophecy came not in old time by the will of man: but holy men of God spake as they were moved by the Holy Ghost."
> — [[61 2 Peter|2 Peter 1:21]]

The Bible is seen as **inspired** by God but written through human authors. The degree of inspiration varies across denominations (from verbal inerrancy to general guidance).

### The Guru Granth Sahib — The Living Guru

> [!quote] The Guru as Word
> "The Word, the Bani, is the Guru. And the Guru is the Bani. Within the Bani, all the ambrosial nectars are contained."
> — [[Guru Granth Sahib Index|Guru Granth Sahib]], Ang 982

The Guru Granth Sahib is not merely a book — it is treated as a **living Guru**, the embodiment of divine authority. It is installed in the gurdwara, addressed as sovereign, and consulted for guidance.

### The Dao De Jing — The Unwritten Way

> [!quote] The limits of writing
> "The Tao that can be trodden is not the enduring and unchanging Tao. The name that can be named is not the enduring and unchanging name."
> — [[Tao Te Ching]], Chapter 1

In a paradox, the Dao De Jing opens by asserting that its **own subject cannot be captured in words**. The Dao itself is prior to all texts. Scripture points beyond itself.

### Comparison Table

| Tradition | What scripture is | Status of the text | Can it be translated? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Islam | Literal divine speech | Inimitable (i'jaz), uncreated | No (Arabic is essential) |
| Judaism | Inspired, but humanly written | Divine-human partnership | Yes |
| Christianity | Inspired by God through authors | Divine-human authorship | Yes (widely translated) |
| Hinduism | Shruti — "heard," authorless | Eternal, not composed | Traditionally oral preservation |
| Sikhism | The living Guru | Supreme authority | Studied in original, taught in translation |
| Buddhism | Recorded teachings of the Buddha | Authoritative but not divine | Translation is essential (spread of Dharma) |
| Daoism | Pointers to the ineffable | Provisional, not ultimate | Yes |

## Cross-Cutting Conclusions

### What the Scriptures Agree On

1. **Compassion is central** — every major tradition elevates love, mercy, or kindness as the highest virtue. The [[The Golden Rule Across Traditions|Golden Rule]] is the nearest thing to an ethical universal.
2. **The ultimate is beyond full description** — whether God, Brahman, or the Dao, all traditions acknowledge that language reaches its limit.
3. **The human condition is flawed** — whether sin, ignorance, craving, or disharmony, no tradition says we are fine as we are.
4. **A path exists** — every tradition prescribes a transformative discipline to move from the current state to the liberated one.

### Where They Fundamentally Diverge

| Divide | Side A | Side B |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature of the Ultimate** | Personal God (Abrahamic) | Impersonal / non-theistic (Dharmic, Daoist) |
| **Self** | Eternal soul (Hindu, Christian, Muslim) | No permanent self (Buddhist) |
| **Time** | Linear (Abrahamic) | Cyclical (Dharmic) |
| **Problem** | Sin / disobedience | Ignorance / craving |
| **Solution** | Grace / faith / submission | Knowledge / insight / practice |
| **Afterlife** | Resurrection / judgment | Rebirth / liberation from rebirth |
| **Scripture** | Revealed by a personal God | Eternal truth "heard" or discovered |

### The Most Instructive Comparisons

- **Job and the Buddha** — both reject easy theodicies and focus on response to suffering.
- **Ecclesiastes and the Dhammapada** — both see impermanence, one counsels joy-within-limits, the other counsels release.
- **Genesis 1 and Rig Veda 10.129** — one declares, the other questions. Two stances toward cosmic origins.
- **Jesus on the cross and the Buddha under the Bodhi tree** — two models of confronting and transcending suffering: one through redemptive surrender, one through awakened insight.
