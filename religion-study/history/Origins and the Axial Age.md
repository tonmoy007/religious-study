# Origins and the Axial Age

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → History of Religions

How did religion begin, and why did so many enduring traditions crystallize in a single remarkable window? This note traces the arc from the deepest prehistoric glimmerings of the sacred to the "Axial Age" — the period that shaped the world's living philosophical and religious traditions.

For what happened next, see [[Classical and Medieval Religious History]] and [[Spread and Transformation of World Religions]].

---

## Part I: Understanding "Origins"

The question "how did religion begin?" is itself contested. There is no single origin — only archaeological inferences and competing theories:

- **Tylor's animism** (1871): religion begins with the belief in spirits (*anima*) — the earliest humans attributing souls to natural phenomena.
- **Eliade's sacred** (1957): religion begins with *hierophanies* — experiences of the sacred breaking into ordinary reality. Humans respond by creating sacred space, sacred time, and ritual.
- **Durkheim's social theory** (1912): religion begins when a community projects its own collective power onto a totemic symbol. "God" is society transfigured.
- **Cognitive approaches** (modern): religion is a by-product of evolved cognitive modules — agency-detection (seeing purpose in random events), theory of mind (attributing intentions to unseen beings), and hazard-precaution systems.

> These are not mutually exclusive. Early religion likely involved all of these dimensions — social, experiential, cognitive, and explanatory.

## Part II: Prehistoric Religion — The Deep Past

### Paleolithic Evidence (c. 100,000–10,000 BCE)

The evidence is fragmentary but suggestive:

| Evidence | Date | What it suggests |
|---|---|---|
| **Deliberate burials** (e.g., Qafzeh, Skhul, Shanidar) | ~100,000–60,000 BCE | Care for the dead; possible belief in an afterlife |
| **Venus figurines** (e.g., Willendorf, Hohle Fels) | ~40,000–25,000 BCE | Fertility symbolism; female divine imagery |
| **Cave art** (Chauvet, Lascaux, Altamira) | ~37,000–12,000 BCE | Ritual spaces; possibly shamanic trance visions |
| **Shanidar flower burial** (Iraq) | ~60,000 BCE | Ritual offering of flowers with a burial |

The **shamanic hypothesis** (associated with David Lewis-Williams and Jean Clottes) argues that much Paleolithic art depicts visions seen in altered states of consciousness — geometric patterns, hybrid human-animal figures. If correct, shamanism would be the oldest known form of religious practice.

### The Neolithic Revolution (c. 10,000–3,500 BCE)

The shift to agriculture transformed religion as fundamentally as it transformed society:

- **Göbekli Tepe** (c. 9500 BCE, Turkey) — the oldest known ritual complex, predating pottery, writing, and agriculture. Massive T-shaped stone pillars carved with animals, arranged in circles. It suggests that organized religion may have *preceded* and *enabled* the Neolithic Revolution, not the other way around.
- **Çatalhöyük** (c. 7500 BCE, Anatolia) — a dense settlement with wall paintings of bulls, hunting scenes, and possible goddess figurines. Domestic shrines suggest religion was household-centered.
- **Temple communities** — the first cities (Uruk, Ur, Jericho) were organized around temple complexes. The priesthood emerged as a specialized class managing offerings, calendars, and public works.

### River-Valley Civilizations (c. 3500–1000 BCE)

The great early civilizations developed complex pantheons, creation myths, and state religions:

> **Mesopotamia**: A pantheon of anthropomorphic gods (Anu, Enlil, Enki, Inanna) ruling a cosmic state. Humans were created from clay mixed with divine blood to serve the gods. Ziggurats linked heaven and earth. See [[Mesopotamian Scriptures]].

> **Egypt**: A cosmos created from the primordial waters (Nun) by Atum or Ptah. The pharaoh was a living god who guaranteed *ma'at* (cosmic order). Elaborate mortuary religion promised an afterlife for those judged worthy. The *Book of the Dead* provided spells for navigating the underworld. See [[The Egyptian Book of the Dead]].

> **Indus Valley** (Harappa, Mohenjo-Daro, c. 2600–1900 BCE): Little is decipherable, but ritual bathing tanks, seal imagery (a horned figure reminiscent of Shiva), and phallic symbols (proto-linga) suggest continuity with later Hinduism.

> **Vedic Religion** (Indo-European pastoralists, c. 1500–500 BCE): Fire sacrifice (*yajna*), hymns to the gods (*devas* — Indra, Agni, Varuna), and the oral composition of the Rig Veda, the oldest surviving scripture. This is the direct ancestor of classical Hinduism. See [[Sacred Texts Overview]].

> **Shang China** (c. 1600–1046 BCE): Ancestor worship and divination using oracle bones. The high god *Shangdi* — and later *Tian* (Heaven) — legitimized the ruler's mandate.

## Part III: The Axial Age (c. 800–200 BCE)

The term **Axial Age** (*Achsenzeit*) was coined by philosopher **Karl Jaspers** (1949) to describe an extraordinary convergence. Across several distinct civilizations, roughly simultaneously, new ways of thinking emerged that broke with the mythic, ritual-centered religion of the Bronze Age:

> [!quote] Karl Jaspers, *The Origin and Goal of History* (1949)
> "The spiritual process which occurred between 800 and 200 BCE seems to constitute such an axis. It was then that the man with whom we live today came into being… In this age were born the fundamental categories within which we still think."

The central innovations: **inwardness** (the self), **transcendence** (a reality beyond the gods), **universalism** (truth for all, not just one tribe), and **ethical reflection** (the question "how should I live?").

### India — The Shramana Revolution

- **Upanishads** (c. 800–400 BCE): The late Vedic texts that shift focus from external sacrifice to inner knowledge. The self (*atman*) is ultimately identical with ultimate reality (*Brahman*). This is the foundation of Vedanta, the dominant philosophical school of Hinduism.
- **Mahavira** (c. 599–527 BCE or 480–410 BCE): Founder of Jainism. Extreme non-violence (*ahimsa*), asceticism, and the eternity of the individual soul (*jiva*).
- **Gautama Buddha** (c. 480–400 BCE): Rejected the self entirely (*anatta* — no-self). Diagnosed suffering and prescribed the Eightfold Path as the way to liberation (*nirvana*). Founded a monastic order that would become a world religion.
- **The Shramana movement**: A broader renunciant culture that rejected Vedic sacrifice and caste hierarchy, pursuing liberation through asceticism, meditation, and philosophical inquiry.

> See [[Dharmic Traditions]] for the full comparison of these traditions.

### China — The Hundred Schools of Thought

- **Confucius** (Kong Qiu, 551–479 BCE): Restored social harmony through ritual (*li*), filial piety (*xiao*), and humaneness (*ren*). His *Analects* became the core text of China's governing philosophy. See [[The Analects of Confucius]].
- **Laozi** (traditional 6th c. BCE, possibly legendary): Attributed with the *Dao De Jing*, which teaches yielding, spontaneity, and harmony with the Dao (the Way) — the nameless source of all. See [[Tao Te Ching]].
- **Mozi** (c. 470–391 BCE): Universal love (*jian'ai*) and meritocracy. A rival to Confucianism that eventually faded.
- **Legalists** (e.g., Han Fei, c. 280–233 BCE): Human nature is bad; only strict law can maintain order. The philosophy that unified China under Qin Shi Huang.
- **The "Warring States" context**: The chaos of the period (403–221 BCE) drove thinkers to ask fundamental questions about order, human nature, and the good society.

> See [[East Asian and Indigenous Traditions]] for the later development of these ideas.

### Israel — The Prophetic Revolution

- **The Hebrew prophets** (c. 800–500 BCE): Amos, Hosea, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel — not fortune-tellers but social critics speaking for God. They transformed Israelite religion from a temple-based sacrificial cult into **ethical monotheism**:
  - God demands justice, not just sacrifice ("I desire mercy, not sacrifice" — Hosea 6:6).
  - God is universal, not tribal (Amos 9:7 — the Ethiopians are as dear to God as Israel).
  - History is the arena of divine purpose — the Exile is punishment, but restoration will follow.
- **Deuteronomic reform** (c. 622 BCE): Centralization of worship in Jerusalem; the "book of the law" (possibly Deuteronomy) becomes the foundation of Jewish identity.
- **The Exile** (586–539 BCE): The destruction of the Temple forced a transition from sacrificial religion to **scripture-based religion** — the Torah becomes the portable temple.

> See [[Abrahamic Traditions]] for the broader context.

### Iran — Zoroaster (dates debated)

- **Zarathustra** (Zoroaster): His dates are fiercely contested — estimates range from 1200 BCE to 600 BCE. He taught a cosmic dualism between the wise lord (Ahura Mazda) and the destructive spirit (Angra Mainyu). Humans must choose sides; history ends with a final judgment, resurrection, and restoration.
- **Influence on Judaism, Christianity, Islam**: Debated but plausible — concepts of angels, a devil, a final judgment, and bodily resurrection may have entered Judaism during the Persian period (539–332 BCE) through Zoroastrian influence.

### Greece — The Birth of Philosophy

- **Pre-Socratics** (Thales, Anaximander, Heraclitus, Parmenides, 6th–5th c. BCE): The first to ask about the *archē* (first principle) of all things without appealing to myth. This is the birth of **philosophy** as a rational discipline distinct from religion.
- **Socrates** (469–399 BCE): The examined life. The pursuit of virtue through rational questioning. His method — and his execution — define the Western philosophical tradition.
- **Plato** (428–348 BCE): The theory of Forms — transcendent ideals of which the physical world is a shadow. The immortality of the soul. His thought deeply shaped Christianity (via Augustine).
- **Aristotle** (384–322 BCE): Logic, ethics, politics, and biology. His metaphysics of the "Unmoved Mover" shaped medieval theology in both Christianity (Aquinas) and Islam (Averroes).
- **Difference**: Greek philosophy is the **secular** offspring of the Axial Age — it asks the same questions (the good, the real, the just) but answers them through reason, not revelation.

### Common Threads Across the Axial Age

| Theme | India | China | Israel | Greece |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ultimate reality** | Brahman (impersonal) | Dao (the Way) | God (personal) | The Forms / the Good |
| **Human problem** | Ignorance, attachment | Disorder, selfishness | Sin, disobedience | Ignorance, vice |
| **Solution** | Knowledge, detachment | Ritual, humaneness | Repentance, covenant | Reason, virtue |
| **Ideal human** | The *moksha*-seeker | The *junzi* (noble person) | The righteous | The philosopher |
| **Social vision** | Beyond caste (Buddhism) | Harmonious hierarchy | Covenant community | The just polis |

## Part IV: Debates and Critiques

**Did the Axial Age really happen?** The thesis is widely used but also criticized:

- **Eurocentrism**: Jaspers' "Axial" civilizations suspiciously match the ones important to Western self-understanding. What about the Mayan, the Norse, or the Polynesian religious developments? Were they "non-Axial" — and does that imply they were less advanced?
- **Artificial convergence**: Did similar things *really* happen in parallel, or did Jaspers select data to fit a pattern? Socrates, the Buddha, and Jeremiah were doing very different things.
- **The "Second Axial Age" thesis** (Ewert Cousins, Karen Armstrong): argues that the *current* period of globalization constitutes a second Axial shift — this time a global, not regional, transformation.
- **Alternative**: Rather than one Axial Age, perhaps religious history is marked by **multiple transitions**: the Neolithic, the Urban Revolution, the Axial Age, the Medieval synthesis, the Reformation, the Modern encounter — each reshaping religion in its image.
