# Indigenous Religious History

> The religious traditions of the world's Indigenous peoples are not "primitive" precursors to the "world religions" — they are complete, internally coherent systems of meaning that have evolved over millennia, often with sophisticated theologies, ethics, and cosmologies encoded in oral tradition, ritual, and art.

---

## Part I: What "Indigenous Religion" Means

The term "Indigenous religion" is a modern academic category — no community calls themselves "Indigenous" in the sense of their religion. It refers to:

- **Oral traditions**: transmitted through storytelling, song, and ritual, not written scripture
- **Place‑based**: tied to specific landscapes, ancestors, and ecosystems
- **Ancestral**: The dead remain part of the community — they are fed, consulted, and honoured
- **Relational**: The human is one being among many — animals, plants, spirits, and landforms are all kin
- **Dynamic**: Indigenous religions adapt — they are not frozen in a pre‑contact "pristine" state

## Part II: The Americas

### Pre‑Columbian Civilisations

#### Mesoamerica

| Civilisation | Period | Religious Features |
|-------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Olmec** | c. 1400–400 BCE | Earliest known ballgame; jaguar‑shamanism; colossal heads |
| **Maya** | c. 250–900 CE (Classic) | Complex calendar; the Popol Vuh creation myth; blood sacrifice by rulers; multiple layers of heaven and underworld |
| **Aztec (Mexica)** | 1325–1521 CE | Huitzilopochtli (war/sun god); human sacrifice as cosmic maintenance; *flor y canto* — the ideal of the poet‑warrior |

The **Popol Vuh** of the Maya (transcribed in the 16th century) is the most complete pre‑Columbian scripture preserved — the story of the Hero Twins defeating the lords of the underworld, and the creation of humans from maize dough.

#### The Andes

| Civilisation | Period | Religious Features |
|-------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Chavín** | c. 900–200 BCE | Underground temple complex; jaguar and serpent iconography; likely a pilgrimage centre |
| **Moche** | c. 100–800 CE | Extensive iconography of ritual combat and human sacrifice |
| **Inca** | 1438–1533 CE | Inti (the sun god); the Sapa Inca as the son of Inti; *huacas* (sacred places); the mummies of dead emperors consulted as oracles |

#### North America

The religious traditions of North America's Indigenous peoples varied enormously — from the **Pueblo** kachina ceremonies of the Southwest to the **potlatch** (ritual redistribution) of the Pacific Northwest, to the **plains vision quest** of the Lakota.

**Common themes**:
- **The Great Spirit** — a high creator (Wakan Tanka for the Sioux, Gitche Manitou for the Algonquian)
- **Vision quests** — fasting alone in the wilderness to receive a guardian spirit
- **The sacred pipe** — the calumet as a ritual object that establishes relationship with the spirits
- **Coyote / Trickster** — a figure who is both sacred and ridiculous; creator and destroyer

### Colonial Impact

- The Spanish conquest destroyed the Aztec and Inca state religions — temples were levelled, codices burned, priests killed
- The **Dance of the Dead** (Pueblo, 1680) and the **Ghost Dance** (Lakota, 1890) were millenarian movements — attempts to restore the pre‑contact world through ritual
- **Peyote religion** (Native American Church, est. 1918): A syncretic tradition blending Christian elements with the ritual use of peyote as a sacrament
- **Resilience**: Despite centuries of forced conversion, Indigenous traditions survive — often embedded within Christianity (the Pueblo feast day, the Maya cofradía)

## Part III: Africa

### Features of Traditional African Religions

Despite enormous diversity, most African traditional religions share:

- **A High God**: a creator who is distant, not actively involved in daily life (often associated with the sky)
- **Lesser spirits**: associated with natural features (rivers, forests, mountains) and with ancestors
- **Ancestor veneration**: The living‑dead remain part of the community — they are consulted, fed, and honoured. Neglecting them brings misfortune
- **Divination**: Systems for discovering the will of the spirits — the Yoruba Ifá system (one of the most complex, with 256 *odu* or signs) is a sophisticated intellectual tradition
- **Ritual specialists**: Diviners, healers, and priests — often the same person
- **No conversion**: Traditional African religions are not missionary — you are born into them

### Major Regional Traditions

| Region | People / Tradition | Key Features |
|--------|-------------------|--------------|
| **West Africa** | Yoruba (Nigeria, Benin) | Orishas (deities) — Ogun (iron/war), Shango (thunder), Oshun (rivers/love). Ifá divination system. Flourished despite colonial suppression |
| **West Africa** | Akan (Ghana, Ivory Coast) | Nyame (High God); abosom (lesser spirits); the Golden Stool — sacred symbol of the nation |
| **West Africa** | Dogon (Mali) | Complex cosmology: the Nommo (ancestral spirits), the star Sirius as the centre of creation |
| **Central Africa** | Kongo | Nzambi Mpungu (High God); *nkisi* (power objects); ritual specialists as healers and judges |
| **East Africa** | Maasai, Kikuyu, etc. | Enkai (High God, sky); age‑grade rituals; cattle as sacred wealth |
| **Southern Africa** | San (Bushmen) | Trance dance — the oldest known shamanic practice (over 20,000 years of rock art continuity) |
| **Southern Africa** | Zulu | Unkulunkulu (the old‑old‑one, creator); ancestor communication through dreams |

### Colonial Impact

- **Mission Christianity** (19th–20th c.): massive conversion; indigenous traditions were suppressed as "heathen" and "primitive"
- **African Independent / Initiated Churches** (AICs): The most important religious development in modern Africa — Christian in form, Indigenous in spirit. Prophets (like **Simon Kimbangu** in Congo, 1921) received revelations for their communities; healing, music, and ancestral sensitivity were retained
- **Syncretism**: Candomblé, Santería, and Vodou are Afro‑Atlantic religions — the orishas and *loa* were hidden behind Catholic saints during slavery

## Part IV: Oceania

### Aboriginal Australia

The world's oldest continuous religious tradition (c. 65,000 years):

- **The Dreaming** (*Tjukurpa*): The primordial period when ancestral beings (the Rainbow Serpent, the Wandjina, the Seven Sisters) shaped the land, created the laws, and then transformed into the landscape itself
- **Songlines**: Routes across the continent encoded in songs — the land *is* the song. To walk the songline is to re‑create the Dreaming
- **Initiation**: Ritual procedures (circumcision, subincision, tooth‑knocking) that transform a child into an adult who can carry the law
- **Totemism**: Every person has a totem (a species, a weather pattern, a place) that connects them to the Dreaming
- **Stolen Generations** (1910–1970s): Aboriginal children forcibly removed from their families — a deliberate assault on cultural and religious transmission

### Polynesia, Micronesia, Melanesia

| Culture | Features |
|---------|----------|
| **Hawaiʻi** | *Kapu* (sacred law) system; human sacrifice; the volcano goddess Pele; the *hula* as a ritual dance |
| **New Zealand (Māori)** | *Tapu* (sacred/prohibited) and *noa* (ordinary); *tiki* carvings; the war dance (*haka*) as ritual challenge |
| **Easter Island (Rapa Nui)** | The *moai* (stone heads) — ancestor representations; bird‑man cult later replaced the ancestor cult |
| **Melanesia (Papua New Guinea, Vanuatu)** | "Cargo cults" — millenarian movements that emerged in the 20th century in response to Western goods arriving during WWII |

## Part V: Siberia and the Arctic

Siberian shamanism is the classic form that gave the English language the word **shaman** (from Evenki *šaman*):

- **The shaman**: A ritual specialist who enters altered states of consciousness to travel to the spirit world, heal the sick, and guide the dead
- **Three worlds**: Upper world (gods), Middle world (humans), Lower world (spirits and the dead) — connected by the **World Tree** or **World Pillar**
- **Drum and costume**: The shaman's drum is a "horse" or "canoe" that carries them to the spirit world; the costume replicates the skeleton — a symbol of death and rebirth
- **Arctic peoples**: The Inuit (*inuksuit* as sacred markers; Sedna, the sea woman); the Sami (noaidi shamanism, the *yoik* song tradition)

## Part VI: Challenges and Resilience

### Threats to Indigenous Religions

1. **Colonial suppression**: Active persecution of Indigenous ceremonies (e.g., the potlatch ban in Canada, 1885–1951; the prohibition of Native American religious practices in the US until 1978)
2. **Missionary conversion**: The great "world religions" — Christianity and Islam — have converted the majority of Indigenous peoples
3. **Language loss**: Indigenous religions are embedded in their languages; language death is religious death
4. **Environmental destruction**: Sacred sites are destroyed by mining, logging, dams — without the land, the religion cannot function
5. **Urbanisation**: Young Indigenous people move to cities, away from elders, land, and ritual

### Contemporary Resurgence

- **Native American Religious Freedom Act** (1978, US) — legal protection for Indigenous religious practice
- **Aboriginal land rights** (Mabo decision, Australia, 1992) — recognition of Indigenous connection to the land
- **Cultural revival**: Language revitalisation, the rebuilding of ritual traditions (the Makah whale hunt revival, 1999; the revival of the Hawaiian *hula*)
- **Self‑determination**: Indigenous religions are increasingly studied *by* Indigenous scholars, not *about* them. The insider perspective is centred
