# Spread and Transformation of World Religions

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → History of Religions

From local cults to global traditions: the mechanisms by which religions spread, split, and reinvented themselves. Builds on [[Origins and the Axial Age]] and [[Classical and Medieval Religious History]].

---

## Part I: Mechanisms of Spread

Religions do not diffuse like a gas — they travel through specific human channels, each with its own logic.

### 1. State Adoption & Imperial Patronage

The most powerful accelerant: when a ruler converts, the people follow — or are encouraged to.

- **Ashoka and Buddhism** (3rd c. BCE): The Mauryan emperor Ashoka converted after the bloody Kalinga war. He erected edicts across South Asia promoting Buddhist ethics, sent missionaries to Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, and the Hellenistic world, and established Buddhism as a trans-regional force.
- **Constantine and Christianity** (4th c. CE): Christianity went from a persecuted minority to the favored religion of the Roman Empire within a generation. The Edict of Milan (313) legalized it; Theodosius (380) made it the state religion. The imperial apparatus — roads, law, bureaucracy — became the vehicle of Christianization.
- **The early Islamic state** (7th c.): Unlike Buddhism and Christianity, Islam spread first through political conquest — the Arab-Muslim state that emerged from Medina. Within a century, it ruled from Spain to Central Asia. The state collected the jizya (tax on non-Muslims), built mosques, and gradually transformed the demographic and religious character of its territories.
- **Confucianism as state ideology**: In China, the examination system (established under the Sui/Tang, 7th c.) made Confucian classics the gateway to power. For over a millennium, ambitious families invested in Confucian education because it led to government office — a uniquely effective mechanism of cultural reproduction.

### 2. Trade Routes

Trade carried religions across political and cultural boundaries without conquest:

- **The Silk Road** (2nd c. BCE–15th c. CE): The network of routes connecting China, Central Asia, India, Persia, and the Mediterranean. Buddhism traveled from India to China via Central Asian merchants and monks (1st c. CE). Nestorian Christianity and Manichaeism also spread along the Silk Road, reaching as far as Tang dynasty China.
- **Indian Ocean trade**: Islam spread to Southeast Asia (Malaysia, Indonesia) not by Arab conquest but by Muslim traders who established communities in port cities from the 8th century onward. The conversion of Sumatra (13th c.) and Java (15th–16th c.) was gradual and peaceful — a contrast with Islam's expansion in the Middle East.
- **Trans-Saharan trade**: Islam spread into West Africa through trade routes connecting the Maghreb to the Sahel. The empires of Ghana, Mali, and Songhai adopted Islam through contact with Muslim merchants — though often blended with indigenous traditions.
- **The Hajj**: The annual pilgrimage to Mecca functioned as a network connecting Muslim communities across the globe — returning pilgrims brought books, ideas, and news.

### 3. Missionary Movements

Deliberate, organized conversion efforts — distinct from mere cultural diffusion.

- **Buddhist missions**: Ashoka's missionaries (*dharmaduta*) are the earliest recorded missionary movement. Later, Chinese and Korean monks traveled to India to retrieve scriptures (e.g., Faxian, 4th c.; Xuanzang, 7th c.). Tibetan Buddhism sent missionaries to Mongolia. Buddhist missionaries generally relied on persuasion, translation, and accommodation — not coercion.
- **Christian missions**: The Catholic Church commissioned mendicant orders (Franciscans, Dominicans) to convert non-Christians in Asia, Africa, and the Americas from the 13th century onward. The Jesuit missions in China (Matteo Ricci, 16th c.) and India (Roberto de Nobili, 17th c.) attempted to adapt Christianity to local cultures — a strategy called *accommodation*. Protestant missions exploded in the 19th century, following European colonialism, and created a truly global Christian presence.
- **Islamic da'wa**: While early Islamic expansion was political, later propagation occurred through Sufi orders, whose wandering preachers adapted Islam to local contexts — often incorporating saint veneration, music, and local customs that the legalistic ulama disapproved of.

### 4. Conquest and Colonization

The most violent mechanism — religious change imposed by force.

- **The Crusades** (1095–1291): Christian military expeditions to reclaim the Holy Land. Failed in their primary goal but deepened the Christian–Muslim divide.
- **The Reconquista** (c. 718–1492): The Christian reconquest of Spain; followed by the expulsion or forced conversion of Jews and Muslims (1492).
- **European colonialism** (1492–20th c.): The Spanish and Portuguese brought Catholicism to the Americas (with mass conversion, often coerced, and the destruction of indigenous temples). In Latin America, a distinctive syncretic Christianity emerged, blending Catholic saints with indigenous deities. Protestant empires (British, Dutch) carried their varieties of Christianity to Africa, Asia, and the Pacific.
- **Note**: Colonial Christianity was deeply entangled with racism, exploitation, and cultural destruction. Modern theologians (e.g., postcolonial theology, liberation theology) continue to reckon with this legacy.

### 5. Migration and Diaspora

Religions travel with people — and diaspora communities often preserve traditions more conservatively than the homeland.

- **Jewish diaspora**: After the Roman destruction of Jerusalem (70 CE), Jewish communities spread across Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East. Diaspora Judaism developed distinctive liturgical traditions (Ashkenazi, Sephardi, Mizrahi). In modern times, migration created major Jewish centers in the United States and Israel.
- **Hindu diaspora**: Hindu communities moved as indentured laborers to the Caribbean, Fiji, Mauritius, South Africa, and Southeast Asia (19th–20th c.). Today, diaspora Hinduism is a growing force — and the site of debates about caste, gender, and tradition.
- **The "New Diasporas"** (20th–21st c.): Muslim migration to Europe; Buddhist and Hindu communities in the West; the global spread of Pentecostalism. Every major tradition now has a significant diaspora population, and these communities are reshaping the traditions themselves.

## Part II: Schism and Diversification

Religions divide — over doctrine, authority, practice, and politics.

### Christianity's Great Divides

| Schism | Date | Cause | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| **East–West (Great Schism)** | 1054 | Papal authority, *filioque*, cultural divergence | Catholic vs. Orthodox |
| **Protestant Reformation** | 1517 onward | Indulgences, sola scriptura, sola fide | Catholic vs. Protestant (Lutheran, Reformed, Anabaptist) |
| **Anglican split** | 1534 | Henry VIII's annulment, royal supremacy | Church of England |
| **Puritan / Radical Reformation** | 16th–17th c. | Baptism, church governance, separation of church and state | Baptists, Congregationalists, Quakers |
| **Methodist revival** | 18th c. | Personal holiness, Arminianism, social reform | Methodism |
| **Pentecostalism** | 1906 | Baptism of the Holy Spirit, speaking in tongues | The fastest-growing Christian movement today |

> Each division created new churches; the divisions also produced **renewal** — reform movements that revitalized the parent tradition (Catholic Counter-Reformation, Orthodox revival).

### Islam's Divisions

- **Sunni–Shia split** (7th c.): Originating in the succession dispute after Muhammad's death. Sunnis (~85%) accept the elected Caliphs; Shia (~15%) believe Ali (the Prophet's cousin and son-in-law) and his descendants (Imams) were divinely appointed. Political and theological differences deepened over centuries.
- **Kharijites** (7th c.): An early sect that held that any Muslim, even a slave, could be Caliph if qualified. Survives today as the Ibadi tradition in Oman and parts of Africa.
- **Sunni schools**: Four legal schools (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali) — differing in method but recognizing each other as orthodox.
- **Shi'i schools**: Twelvers (Iran, Iraq, Lebanon), Ismailis (South Asia, East Africa), Zaydis (Yemen).
- **Sufi orders**: Not a division from Islam itself but a mystical movement within it. Sufi orders (tariqas) cross sectarian lines and have been the primary vehicle of Islamic spread in many regions.

### Buddhism's Three Vehicles

| Vehicle | Also known | Dominant in | Emphasis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Theravada** | "Way of the Elders" | Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia | Monasticism, Pali canon, individual liberation |
| **Mahayana** | "Great Vehicle" | East Asia (China, Korea, Japan, Vietnam) | Bodhisattva path, universal salvation, new sutras |
| **Vajrayana** | "Diamond Vehicle" | Tibet, Mongolia, Bhutan | Tantric techniques, visualizations, guru devotion |

These are less mutually exclusive than Christian denominations — a Buddhist may draw on all three, and the divisions are more about emphasis and text than ecclesiastical authority.

### General Dynamics of Schism

1. **Orthodoxy vs. adaptation**: Traditions that resist change (orthodoxy) preserve clarity but risk irrelevance; those that adapt gain followers but risk losing their identity.
2. **Center vs. periphery**: Movements that succeed often begin on the margins and are later absorbed (Franciscans, Sufi orders, Methodism).
3. **Reform always produces reaction**: Every reformation generates a counter-reformation (Catholic vs. Protestant; Islamic modernism vs. Salafism).
4. **Political entanglement**: Schisms that seem theological are often rooted in politics, ethnicity, and geography (the Great Schism; the Sunni–Shia split).

## Part III: Encounters with Modernity

Modernity — the complex of science, capitalism, the nation-state, and pluralism — has posed the deepest challenge to traditional religion since the Axial Age.

### The Secularization Thesis

The classic argument (Weber, Durkheim, Berger, 1960s): as societies modernize, religion declines. Causes:
- **Rationalization**: Science and technology explain what religion once explained.
- **Differentiation**: Religion becomes one sphere among many, not the organizer of all life.
- **Pluralism**: Exposure to many traditions undermines the taken-for-granted status of any one.

**But**: the thesis has been heavily criticized and largely abandoned in its strong form:
- The United States is highly modern and highly religious.
- The global South is experiencing explosive religious growth (Pentecostalism, Islam).
- Secularization applies best to Western Europe — which may be the exception, not the rule.
- **Peter Berger**, once the leading secularization theorist, recanted: "The world today is as furiously religious as ever."

### Religious Responses to Modernity

> [!tip] A spectrum of responses
> Traditions vary in how they respond to the modern challenge. The same tradition may contain all three responses:

| Response | Strategy | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Traditionalist/ Fundamentalist** | Reject modernity, return to "original" sources | Salafi Islam, Christian fundamentalism, Orthodox Judaism |
| **Modernist/Liberal** | Reinterpret tradition in light of modern knowledge | Reform Judaism, liberal Christianity, progressive Islam |
| **Neo-Traditionalist** | Embrace modern tools but uphold traditional values | Catholic traditionalism, Neo-Confucianism |
| **Syncretic/New Religious** | Blend elements of multiple traditions | Baháʼí, New Age, Soka Gakkai |

### Modernity's Key Challenges to Religion

1. **Science**: Evolution vs. creation; the historical-critical study of scripture; the cognitive science of religion. The "conflict thesis" (science and religion are at war) is one model — there are also models of independence, dialogue, and integration.
2. **The nation-state**: Modern states claim authority over law, education, and welfare — powers that religions once held. The result is ongoing conflict over religious law (sharia in Muslim states; religious exemptions in secular states).
3. **Human rights**: The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) enshrines individual rights that often conflict with religious traditions — gender equality, LGBTQ+ rights, freedom of conscience (including the freedom to leave a religion).
4. **Pluralism**: For the first time in history, most people live in societies where multiple religions are present and cannot be ignored. Pluralism raises the question: can multiple traditions all be true, or is only one correct? The theology of religions (exclusivism, inclusivism, pluralism) is the most urgent theological question of our time.

### New Religious Movements (NRMs)

The modern period has also produced entirely new religions:
- **Mormonism / LDS** (1830) — new scripture, new prophet, new revelation.
- **Baháʼí Faith** (1863) — progressive revelation; unity of all religions.
- **Jehovah's Witnesses** (1870s) — restorationist Christian; distinctive eschatology.
- **New Age** (1970s onward) — eclectic, individualistic, drawing on Eastern and Western esoteric traditions.
- **Scientology** (1953) — self-help meets religion, with a cosmic narrative.
- **Religious "nones"** (the fastest-growing category in many Western countries): not "atheist" necessarily, but unaffiliated — the largest "religious" demographic shift of the 21st century.

### Globalization and the Future

- **Reverse mission**: Once-receiving regions now send missionaries — African Pentecostals to Europe, Korean Christians to the Middle East, South Asian Muslims to the West.
- **Deterritorialization**: Religions are no longer tied to specific lands. A Tibetan Buddhist center in New York; a Hindu temple in Dubai; a megachurch in Seoul.
- **Digital religion**: Online worship, scripture apps, virtual pilgrimages, religious influencers. The internet is reshaping religious authority and community.
- **Climate and religion**: Growing movements to ground environmentalism in religious terms — "creation care" (Christianity), *khalifa* (stewardship in Islam), the interconnectedness of all life (Buddhism, Indigenous traditions).

## Key Historical Dynamics to Watch

1. Religion as a force for **both** unity and division — the same scriptures that inspire compassion have been used to justify violence.
2. The tension between **orthodoxy** (preserving the past) and **adaptation** (surviving the present) — and the cost of getting the balance wrong.
3. The entanglement of religion with **power, identity, and politics** — religion is rarely "pure" belief; it is always embedded in social realities.
4. The law of **unintended consequences**: Constantine's conversion led to the Crusades and the Reformation; the printing press enabled both the Bible in every home *and* the radical fragmentation of Protestantism.
