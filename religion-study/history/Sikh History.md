# Sikh History: From Nānak to the Global Diaspora

> The youngest of the major world religions, founded in the Punjab region of the Indian subcontinent. Sikhism emerged from the convergence of Sant (devotional) and Sufi traditions, but developed a distinct identity through the institution of the Guru, the revelation of scripture, and the creation of the Khalsa — a community defined by discipline, egalitarianism, and the ideal of the saint-soldier (*sant-sipāhī*). Sikh history is a story of persecution, resilience, empire, reform, and a rapidly growing global diaspora.

---

## Part I: The Ten Gurus (1469–1708)

The Sikh tradition is built around the lives and teachings of ten human Gurus, from Gurū Nānak (1469–1539) to Gurū Gobind Siṅgh (1666–1708). Each Guru contributed distinct institutions, doctrines, and responses to the political challenges of their time.

### Overview of the Ten Gurus

| # | Guru | Life | Key Contribution | Key Event |
|---|------|------|-----------------|-----------|
| 1 | **Gurū Nānak** | 1469–1539 | Founder; taught *Ik Oaṅkār* (One God), *nām simran* (meditation on the Name), *seva* (selfless service); composed Japjī Sāhib; undertook four great journeys (*Udāsīs*) | Revelation at the river Vien (c. 1499): "There is no Hindu, no Muslim" |
| 2 | **Gurū Aṅgad** | 1504–1552 (Guru 1539–1552) | Developed the Gurmukhī script; collected and preserved Nānak's hymns; institutionalised the *langar* (community kitchen) | Formalised the succession system — the spirit of Nānak, not his blood |
| 3 | **Gurū Amar Dās** | 1479–1574 (Guru 1552–1574) | Composed the *Anand Sāhib*; organised the Sikh community into 22 *mañjīs* (dioceses); appointed women preachers; made langar a compulsory institution where all sat together | Emperor Akbar visits and eats in the langar, impressed by its egalitarianism |
| 4 | **Gurū Rām Dās** | 1534–1581 (Guru 1574–1581) | Founded Rāmdāspur (later Amritsar); began excavation of the sacred pool *(Amritsar)*; initiated construction of the Harimandir Sāhib | Composed the *Lāvāṅ* marriage hymns used in Sikh weddings |
| 5 | **Gurū Arjan** | 1563–1606 (Guru 1581–1606) | Compiled and installed the **Ādi Granth** (1604) at Harimandir Sāhib; completed the Golden Temple; **first Sikh martyr** — executed by Emperor Jahāngīr on charges of supporting a rebel prince | **Martyrdom, 30 May 1606** — tortured for five days, died in the Rāvī River; the turning point that militarised the Sikh community |
| 6 | **Gurū Har Gobind** | 1595–1644 (Guru 1606–1644) | Introduced **Mīrī-Pīrī** (temporal + spiritual authority); wore two swords; built the **Akāl Takht** (Throne of the Timeless) opposite the Harimandir | Raised a standing army; fought four battles against Mughal forces (Amritsar, Mehraj, Kartarpur, Gurusar) |
| 7 | **Gurū Har Rāi** | 1630–1661 (Guru 1644–1661) | Maintained the Sikh army but avoided direct conflict; sheltered the Mughal prince Dārā Shikoh (elder brother of Aurangzeb); known for his herb garden and medical knowledge | Provided medicinal herbs to Dārā Shikoh, earning Aurangzeb's displeasure |
| 8 | **Gurū Har Kriśan** | 1656–1664 (Guru 1661–1664) | The youngest Guru at age 5; blessed the smallpox-stricken in Delhi; died of smallpox himself | Called to Delhi by Aurangzeb to disprove claims of his divinity; died before meeting him |
| 9 | **Gurū Tegh Bahādur** | 1621–1675 (Guru 1665–1675) | Composed 116 hymns (the *Baṛī Sāhib*); travelled extensively across India; stood up for the religious freedom of Kashmiri Pandits | **Martyrdom, 11 Nov 1675** — beheaded in Chandni Chowk, Delhi, for refusing to convert to Islam |
| 10 | **Gurū Gobind Siṅgh** | 1666–1708 (Guru 1675–1708) | Created the **Khālsā** (1699); finalised the **Guru Granth Sāhib** as the eternal Guru; composed the **Dasam Granth** and the **Zafarnāmā** (Epistle of Victory) | **Vaisākhī 1699**: founded the Khalsa; declared the Guru Granth Sāhib as permanent Guru (1708) |

### The Compilation of the Ādi Granth (1604)

Gurū Arjan oversaw the first official compilation of Sikh scripture:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Compiler** | Gurū Arjan, with Bhāī Gurdās as scribe |
| **Location** | Amritsar, in the Harimandir Sāhib complex |
| **Languages** | Sant Bhāṣā (Old Punjabi, Braj, Khari Boli), Persian, Arabic, Marathi |
| **Contents** | 5,894 hymns: compositions of Sikh Gurus (Nānak, Aṅgad, Amar Dās, Rām Dās, Arjan) and 15 *bhagats* (poet-saints including Kabīr, Nāmdev, Ravidas, and Farīd) |
| **Installation** | 16 August 1604, with Bhāī Buddhā as the first *granthī* |
| **Significance** | Established the **Guru as scripture** — a radical departure from the model of a living Guru |

### The Martyrdom of Gurū Arjan (1606)

The execution of Gurū Arjan under Emperor Jahāngīr transformed the Sikh community:

- **Immediate cause**: Gurū Arjan was accused of blessing the rebel prince Khusrau (Jahāngīr's son) and of including non-Sikh hymns in the Ādi Granth
- **Method**: Tortured for five days (forced to sit on a hot iron plate; boiling sand poured on him), then immersed in the Rāvī River
- **Consequence**: The Sikh community, until then pacifist, began to militarise under Gurū Har Gobind — the doctrine of **Mīrī-Pīrī** was born

### The Creation of the Khalsa (Vaisākhī 1699)

The single most transformative event in Sikh history:

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Date** | 30 March 1699 (Vaisākhī) |
| **Location** | **Anandpur Sāhib** |
| **Founding Act** | Gurū Gobind Siṅgh called for a volunteer; five men (*Pañj Piāre* — the Five Beloved Ones) offered their heads one by one |
| **Initiation** | The Guru administered *Khaṇḍē dī Pāhul* (Amrit initiation with a double-edged sword stirred in an iron bowl) |
| **The Five Ks** | *Kesh* (uncut hair), *Kaṅghā* (wooden comb), *Kirpān* (sword/dagger), *Kachherā* (cotton shorts), *Karā* (steel bracelet) |
| **Names** | All male initiates received the surname **Siṅgh** (lion); all female initiates received the surname **Kaur** (princess) |
| **Constitutional Provisions** | The Khalsa was to have no Guru after the ten human ones; the Guru Granth Sāhib would be the spiritual Guru; the Guru Panth (the collective Khalsa) would be the temporal body |
| **The Five Beloved Ones** | Daya Rām Sohan (Bhai Daya Siṅgh), Dharam Dās (Bhai Dharam Siṅgh), Mohkam Chand (Bhai Mohkam Siṅgh), Himmat Rām (Bhai Himmat Siṅgh), Sāhib Chand (Bhai Sāhib Siṅgh) — from different castes and regions |

### The Guru Granth Sāhib as Eternal Guru (1708)

Before his death in 1708, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh declared that after him there would be no more human Gurus. The Guru Granth Sāhib would be the living Guru for all time:

> "All Sikhs are commanded to accept the Granth as their Guru. Consider the Granth as the embodiment of the Gurus. He who would see the Guru, let him see the Granth."

This act **closed the line of living Gurus** and made scripture the ultimate authority — a unique development among world religions.

## Part II: The 18th Century — Persecution and Rise of the Misls (1708–1799)

The death of Gurū Gobind Siṅgh inaugurated a century of extreme persecution, but also of Sikh consolidation, military organisation, and the rise of the **Misls** (sovereign confederacies).

### Banda Siṅgh Bahādur (1710–1716)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Background** | Born Lachman Dev in Rajouri (Jammu); became a Bairagi ascetic; met Gurū Gobind Siṅgh in Nanded (1708) and was baptised into the Khalsa as **Banda Siṅgh Bahādur** |
| **Campaign** | Led the first armed Sikh uprising: captured Sirhind (May 1710), executed Wazīr Khān (the governor responsible for the deaths of the Guru's sons), established a Sikh state at Lohgarh |
| **Administration** | Abolished the *zamīndārī* system, granted land to peasants, struck coins in the name of Gurū Nānak and Gurū Gobind Siṅgh |
| **Fall** | After initial successes, the Mughal army under Farrukhsiyar crushed the rebellion; Banda was captured, taken to Delhi in chains |
| **Martyrdom** | **9 June 1716** — tortured for six months, then executed in Delhi (his son Ajay Siṅgh was killed before his eyes, his body torn apart with hot irons) |

### Mughal Persecution under Zakariya Khan (1726–1745)

After Banda's execution, the Mughal governor of Punjab, **Zakariya Khan**, pursued a systematic policy of Sikh extermination:

- **1726**: Proclamation offering rewards for the heads of Sikhs
- **1733**: Brief period of reconciliation (the Mughals granted a *jāgīr* to the Sikhs and the title "Nawab" to their leader **Kapur Siṅgh**) — but this was short-lived
- **1730s–1740s**: Continuous persecution; Sikhs were hunted like animals; they took refuge in forests and deserts

### The Dal Khalsa and the Rise of the Misls

During this period, the Sikhs organised themselves into two groups:
- **Buddhā Dal** (the army of the elders) — the veteran fighters
- **Tarunā Dal** (the army of the young) — the younger recruits

These eventually developed into the **12 Sikh Misls** (confederacies), each controlling a territory in Punjab:

| Misl | Founder | Territory |
|------|---------|-----------|
| **Bhangi** | Chhajja Siṅgh (Dhari) | Amritsar, Gujranwala, Sialkot |
| **Kanhaiya** | Jai Siṅgh Sandhu | Batala, Gurdaspur |
| **Nakkaí** | Hīra Siṅgh Sandhu | Nakka, Kasur |
| **Sukkerchakia** | Naudh Siṅgh | Gujranwala, Eminabad |
| **Ahluwalia** | Jassā Siṅgh Ahluwalia | Kapurthala |
| **Ramgarhia** | Jassā Siṅgh Ramgarhia | Sri Hargobindpur, Moga |
| **Dallewala** | Gulāb Siṅgh | Rahon, Phillaur |
| **Fyzullapuria** | Khushāl Siṅgh | Jalandhar, Hoshiarpur |
| **Krori** | Karorī Malik | Gurdaspur |
| **Shaheedan** | Dīpa Siṅgh | Damdama, Muktsar |
| **Nishanwala** | Sangat Siṅgh | Ambala |
| **Sindhanwala** | Dīdār Siṅgh | Kasur, Sheikhupura |

### The Chhota Ghallughara (1746) and Vadda Ghallughara (1762)

Two genocidal campaigns that nearly wiped out the Sikhs:

| Name | Date | Perpetrator | Casualties | Notes |
|------|------|-------------|------------|-------|
| **Chhota Ghallughara** (Small Holocaust) | 1746 | Lakhpat Rai (Diwan of Lahore) under Zakariya Khan's successor | ~7,000 Sikhs massacred near Kahnuwan; Sikh holy places desecrated | Lakhpat Rai acted in revenge for Sikh raids |
| **Vadda Ghallughara** (Great Holocaust) | 5 Feb 1762 | Ahmad Shāh Abdālī (Durrani emperor of Afghanistan) | ~20,000–30,000 Sikhs killed at Kup (near Malerkotla) | Abdālī had invaded Punjab for the sixth time; the Sikhs were caught off-guard returning from a campaign |

Despite these massacres, the Sikh confederacies regrouped and continued to expand. The period **1762–1799** saw the Misls consolidate control over most of Punjab, regularly extracting tribute (*rakhī* or protection tax) from towns and villages.

## Part III: The Sikh Empire (1799–1849)

### Maharaja Ranjit Singh's Unification (1780–1839)

**Ranjit Singh** (1780–1839), leader of the Sukkerchakia Misl, unified the fractious Misls into a single empire:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Birth** | 13 Nov 1780, Gujranwala (to Mahān Siṅgh, Sukkerchakia chief) |
| **Accession** | 1792 (age 12); early regency of his mother-in-law Sada Kaur |
| **Capture of Lahore** | 7 July 1799 — the city gates were opened to him by the Mughal governor |
| **Unification** | 1801–1810: absorbed the Bhangi, Kanhaiya, Nakkaí, Ahluwalia, and other Misls through diplomacy, marriage, and conquest |
| **Coronation** | 12 April 1801 (Vaisākhī) — proclaimed **Maharaja of Punjab** |
| **Titles** | *Sher-e-Punjāb* (Lion of Punjab); respected as a secular ruler |
| **Empire at its peak** | Extended from the Khyber Pass in the west to the Sutlej in the east, from Kashmir in the north to Sindh in the south |

### Administration of the Sikh Empire

Ranjit Singh's rule is remembered for its **religious pluralism** and **modernisation**:

| Area | Features |
|------|----------|
| **Government** | Centralised monarchy with Mughal-style bureaucracy; French, Italian, and British officers trained the army (the **Fauj-i-Khās**, or elite corps) |
| **Military** | Modernised artillery; the **Fauj-i-Ain** (regular army) of ~60,000; the **Fauj-i-Khās** (royal guard) of ~3,500 |
| **Religious policy** | Allowed Muslims, Hindus, and Sikhs to worship freely; employed Muslim scholars and Hindu pandits; reversed previous iconoclasm of temples and mosques |
| **Economy** | Flourished under Sikh rule; trade routes to Central Asia, Persia, and China; the Amritsar mint struck the Nanakshahi coinage |
| **Justice** | Combination of Sikh customary law, Mughal *qānūn* (state law), and local *jirga* (tribal councils) |
| **Key monuments** | Renovated the Harimandir Sāhib in gold leaf (the **Golden Temple**); built the **Lahore Fort**, **Hazūrī Bāgh**, and **Ranjit Siṅgh's Samādhi** |

### The Anglo-Sikh Wars and the Fall of the Empire

After Ranjit Singh's death in 1839, the empire collapsed into factional infighting:

| Event | Date | Detail |
|-------|------|--------|
| **Ranjit Singh dies** | 27 June 1839 | Succession crisis; four rulers in five years |
| **First Anglo-Sikh War** | 1845–1846 | British victory at **Battles of Mudki, Ferozeshah, Aliwal, and Sobraon**; Treaty of Lahore (1846) reduced the Sikh state to a British protectorate |
| **Treaty of Bhairowal** | 1846 | British Resident posted at Lahore; the Maharaja (Dalip Siṅgh, age 7) was a puppet |
| **Second Anglo-Sikh War** | 1848–1849 | **Battle of Chillianwala** (13 Jan 1849) — a pyrrhic British victory; **Battle of Gujrat** (21 Feb 1849) — decisive British victory |
| **Annexation** | 29 March 1849 | The Punjab was annexed by the British East India Company; the Koh-i-Noor diamond was seized and presented to Queen Victoria |

## Part IV: Colonial Period (1849–1947)

### The Shock of Annexation

The loss of sovereignty was a profound trauma for the Sikh community. The British initially favoured Sikhs as a "martial race" for military recruitment, but the dismantling of Sikh institutions and the erosion of Khalsa identity led to a period of decline and fragmentation.

### The Singh Sabha Reform Movement (1873–1905)

The most significant Sikh revivalist movement, founded in response to **Christian missionary activity**, **Hindu reform movements (Arya Samaj)**, and the **degeneration of Sikh institutions**:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **First Singh Sabha** | **Amritsar** (1873) — led by conservative, tradition-minded Sikhs; focused on scriptural study and *kīrtan* |
| **Lahore Singh Sabha** | **Lahore** (1879) — more reformist, western-educated Sikhs; promoted education, printing, and social reform |
| **Unification** | 1880s: the two factions merged under **Chief Khalsa Diwan** (1902) |
| **Key leaders** | **Bhai Vir Siṅgh** (reformed Punjabi literature), **Professor Gurmukh Siṅgh**, **Bhai Kāhn Siṅgh Nābha** (author of *Gurū Shabad Ratnakar Mahān Kosh* — the Encyclopaedia of Sikhism), **Bhai Talwār** |
| **Achievements** | Revived Khalsa identity, reformed Sikh ceremonies (removed Hindu accretions), standardised the *Rehat Maryādā* (Sikh Code of Conduct), established schools and colleges (Khalsa College, Amritsar, 1892) |
| **Publications** | The *Khalsa Advocate* (journal), the *Khalsa Samachar*, and dozens of books in Punjabi, English, and Urdu |

### The Gurdwara Reform Movement / Akali Movement (1920–1925)

A mass movement to reclaim Sikh places of worship from corrupt, hereditary *mahants* (hereditary priests) who were controlled by the British:

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Trigger** | The **Nankana Sāhib massacre** (20 Feb 1921): the *mahant* of Nankana Sāhib had **Akali protesters locked inside a building and burned alive** — ~200 killed |
| **Demand** | Control of all historical Gurdwaras to be handed to elected Sikh committees |
| **Key events** | **Keys of the Golden Temple** were surrendered by the British after a peaceful Satyagraha (1922); **Guru-ka-Bagh** (1922) — Sikhs stood firm against police beatings while cutting wood for the langar |
| **Legislation** | **Sikh Gurdwaras Act 1925** — created the **Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee (SGPC)** as the elected body for managing historical Gurdwaras |
| **Political wing** | The **Shiromani Akali Dal** (SAD, founded 1920) became the political voice of the Sikhs — a position it maintains |

### The Komagata Maru (1914)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Incident** | A Japanese ship carrying 376 Punjabi (mostly Sikh) passengers arrived in Vancouver, Canada (23 May 1914); they were denied entry due to racist immigration policies |
| **Return** | The ship was forced back to India; when it docked at **Budge Budge** (Calcutta) on 29 Sept 1914, British police tried to force the passengers onto a train to Punjab |
| **Violence** | A riot broke out; 20 passengers were shot dead (Sikhs and Hindus) |
| **Significance** | Galvanised the Ghadar Party (Indian revolutionary movement) and fuelled anti-colonial sentiment among Sikhs |

### The Partition of Punjab (1947)

The partition of India in 1947 was catastrophic for Punjab:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Demographics** | Pre-partition Punjab was ~35% Muslim, ~35% Hindu, ~15% Sikh (west-east gradient: Muslims in west, Sikhs in centre, Hindus in east) |
| **Radcliffe Line** | The boundary commission split Punjab arbitrarily; the entire Sikh heartland (Lahore, Rawalpindi, Lyallpur, Montgomery) was awarded to Pakistan |
| **Migration** | Up to **10 million** people crossed the border; **2.2 million** Sikhs moved from West Punjab to Indian Punjab |
| **Violence** | Estimated 500,000–1,000,000 dead in the Punjab massacres; Sikhs suffered disproportionately as they were concentrated in the most violently contested regions |
| **Losses** | Sikhs lost: the birthplace of Gurū Nānak (Nankana Sāhib), the city of Ranjit Singh (Lahore), the Guru's seat (Kartarpur), and hundreds of Gurdwaras |

## Part V: Contemporary Sikhism (1947–Present)

### The Punjabi Suba Movement (1948–1966)

After partition, Sikhs in the Indian state of East Punjab demanded a state where Punjabi (Gurmukhī script) would be the official language:

| Phase | Date | Event |
|-------|------|-------|
| **Demand** | 1948 | The Akali Dal under **Master Tara Siṅgh** demanded a Punjabi-speaking state |
| **Reorganisation of States Commission** | 1955 | Rejected the demand for a Punjabi linguistic state |
| **Protest** | 1955–1966 | Mass agitations, including the **Punjabi Suba Morcha** — thousands were arrested, including Master Tara Siṅgh who undertook a 48-day fast |
| **Resolution** | 1966 | The Indian government agreed: **Punjab** was divided into **Punjabi-speaking Punjab**, **Hindi-speaking Haryana**, and a **Himachal Pradesh** union territory (later a state) |
| **Result** | Chandigarh became the shared capital of Punjab and Haryana; Sikhs formed a majority in the new Punjab state |

### Operation Blue Star (1984)

The most traumatic event in modern Sikh history:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Background** | **Jarnail Siṅgh Bhindranwale** (a charismatic preacher) and his followers had fortified the Akāl Takht within the Golden Temple complex in Amritsar, demanding greater autonomy for Punjab |
| **Operation** | **3–8 June 1984** — the Indian Army under General Kuldip Siṅgh Brar launched an assault on the Golden Temple (code-named **Operation Blue Star**) |
| **Casualties** | Official: 576 killed (including 245 military); unofficial estimates: 1,000–5,000+ (civilians, militants, and army) |
| **Damage** | The Akāl Takht was severely damaged by tank and artillery fire; the Harimandir Sāhib (the sanctum) was damaged by bullets |
| **Aftermath** | Bhindranwale and most of his followers were killed; large-scale army operations across Punjab |

### The Assassination of Indira Gandhi (31 October 1984)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Event** | Prime Minister Indira Gandhi was assassinated by her two Sikh bodyguards, Satwant Siṅgh and Beant Siṅgh |
| **Cause** | Personal vengeance for Operation Blue Star |
| **Consequence** | **Anti-Sikh riots** (1–7 Nov 1984) — thousands of Sikhs were murdered in Delhi and other cities (official: 2,733; unofficial: 8,000–17,000) |
| **Justice** | Very few perpetrators were ever convicted; a 2018 report by the Delhi police admitted that the riots were "organised" and many of the accused were connected to the ruling Congress party |

### The Khalistan Movement

A separatist movement demanding an independent Sikh state (*Khalistan*, "Land of the Pure"):

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Origins** | First demanded in 1940 by Dr. V. S. Bhatti; revived after the 1966 Punjabi Suba and intensified after Operation Blue Star |
| **Peak** | 1980s–early 1990s: armed insurgency in Punjab; estimated 25,000–50,000 killed in the conflict (1984–1995) |
| **Diaspora support** | Strong in the UK, Canada, USA, and Australia; the **Sikh Federation** (UK) and **World Sikh Organization** (Canada) advocate for Khalistan |
| **Referendums** | Unofficial Khalistan referendums held in diaspora communities (2020–2023); rejected by the Indian government as illegal |
| **Current status** | The insurgency has been largely suppressed in Punjab; the movement remains a political fringe within India but a significant mobilising force in the diaspora |

### The Sikh Diaspora

The twentieth-century transformation of Sikhism from a regional to a global religion:

| Region | Population (est.) | Key Communities |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|
| **India** | ~23 million (1.7% of population) | Punjab (majority), Haryana, Delhi, Rajasthan, Chandigarh |
| **Canada** | ~800,000 | Brampton, Surrey, Vancouver, Calgary, Edmonton |
| **United Kingdom** | ~540,000 | Southall (London), Birmingham, Leicester, Glasgow |
| **United States** | ~500,000 | Yuba City (California), New York, Chicago, Fresno |
| **Malaysia** | ~120,000 | Penang, Kuala Lumpur, Perak |
| **Australia** | ~200,000 | Sydney, Melbourne, Adelaide |
| **Italy** | ~60,000 | Brescia, Reggio Emilia, Vicenza |
| **East Africa** | ~50,000 | Kenya (Nairobi), Uganda (Kampala), Tanzania |
| **Rest of world** | ~150,000 | Singapore, Thailand, Pakistan (declining), Gulf states (migrant workers) |

### Contemporary Challenges and Debates

| Issue | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Rehat Maryādā vs. modern life** | Tensions between the 1950 Sikh Code of Conduct and contemporary secular practices; debates over the *dastār* (turban) in schools/workplaces |
| **Caste** | Despite the Khalsa's egalitarian founding, caste prejudices persist, especially among Jat Sikhs (dominant landowning caste) and Dalit Sikhs (Mazhabi Sikhs) |
| **Gender** | The role of women in the Panth (Sikh community); debates over female Amritdhari (initiated) leaders, and the status of Kaur as an identity |
| **Diaspora identity** | The pressure of assimilation, *pāgṛī* (turban) bans in France/Quebec, and the question of whether Sikhism is a distinct religion or a reform of Hinduism (resolved by Indian law in 2005, but still debated in global contexts) |
| **Khalistan** | Divergence between the diaspora (more supportive) and Sikhs in India (largely opposed to violent methods but sympathetic to demands for autonomy) |
| **Scripture in the digital age** | Digital editions of the Guru Granth Sāhib; apps, websites, and social media kīrtan — the Guru's word accessible globally |
| **Environmental justice** | The Sikh principle of *sarbat da bhalā* (welfare of all) applied to climate activism; langar as a model for food justice |
