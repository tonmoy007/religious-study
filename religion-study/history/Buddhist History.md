# Buddhist History: From the Buddha to a World Religion

> The arc of the dharma — from a wandering ascetic in northeastern India to the fourth largest religion in the world, with a presence in nearly every country on earth.

---

## Part I: The Life of the Buddha (c. 5th Century BCE)

### The Buddha's Biography

Accounts of the Buddha's life are a mix of history and hagiography. The scholarly consensus places his dates around **c. 480–400 BCE** (earlier than traditional dates of 563–483 BCE):

| Stage | Event | Significance |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| **Birth** | Prince Siddhārtha Gautama born in Lumbinī (modern Nepal) | Son of King Śuddhodana; prophesied to become either a great king or a great renunciant |
| **The Four Sights** | Sees an old man, a sick man, a corpse, and an ascetic | Encounters with suffering shatter his sheltered life |
| **The Great Renunciation** | Leaves the palace at 29, cutting his hair and donning the yellow robe | Renounces worldly life to seek liberation |
| **Asceticism** | Six years of extreme fasting with 5 companions | "I have practised such austerity that my bones stuck to my skin — and I did not reach enlightenment" |
| **Enlightenment** | Sits under the Bodhi tree at Bodh Gayā, resolves not to rise until he understands | Attains *nirvāṇa* (cessation of suffering); becomes the **Buddha** ("the Awakened One") |
| **First Sermon** | Deer Park at Sārnāth | Sets in motion the **Wheel of Dharma** — the Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path |
| **Parinirvāṇa** | Dies at Kuśīnagara at age 80 | Enters final *nirvāṇa* — "Decay is inherent in all compounded things; strive with diligence" |

### The Four Noble Truths

1. **Suffering (*duḥkha*)** exists
2. Suffering arises from **craving (*tṛṣṇā* — "thirst")**
3. Cessation is possible
4. The **Eightfold Path** is the way: right view, intention, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, concentration

### The Saṅgha

The Buddha established a monastic order (*saṅgha*) open to all, regardless of caste or gender (though the order for women was established reluctantly). The saṅgha became the vehicle for preserving and transmitting the dharma.

## Part II: The Early Buddhist Councils (c. 5th–3rd Century BCE)

| Council | Location | Date | Outcome |
|---------|----------|------|---------|
| **First** | Rājagṛha | Shortly after the Buddha's death | 500 arhats recited the Dharma and Vinaya. The canon begins |
| **Second** | Vaiśālī | c. 383 BCE | Dispute over monastic discipline; the first schism — Sthaviravādins ("Elders") vs. Mahāsāṅghikas ("Great Saṅgha") |
| **Third** | Pāṭaliputra | c. 250 BCE (under Aśoka) | Purification of the saṅgha; compilation of the Kathāvatthu (points of controversy). The tradition that became Theravāda crystallises |
| **Fourth** | Sri Lanka (Tāmraparṇī) | c. 1st c. BCE | The Pāli canon (*Tipiṭaka*) is written down for the first time, after centuries of oral transmission |

---

## Part III: Aśoka and the First Buddhist Mission (c. 268–232 BCE)

The Mauryan emperor **Aśoka** converted after the bloody Kalinga War (c. 260 BCE). His legacy:

- **Edicts**: Inscribed on pillars and rocks across South Asia — promoting *dharma* (moral law), non‑violence, and religious tolerance
- **Missions**: Sent missionaries (*dharmadūta*) as far as Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, Central Asia, and the Hellenistic world (to the Greek kings of Bactria, Syria, and Egypt)
- **Patronage**: Funded monasteries, stupas, and the Third Council

> [!quote] Aśoka, Rock Edict XII
> "The king, beloved of the gods, honours all sects — whether ascetics or householders — with gifts and honour."

## Part IV: The Rise of Mahāyāna (c. 1st Century BCE–5th Century CE)

Around the turn of the millennium, a new vision of the Buddhist path emerged — **Mahāyāna** ("Great Vehicle"):

| Feature | Early Buddhism (Nikāya / "Śrāvakayāna") | Mahāyāna |
|---------|------------------------------------------|----------|
| **Goal** | Arhat (one who attains *nirvāṇa* for themselves) | Bodhisattva (one who delays liberation to help all beings) |
| **Buddha** | A historical teacher | Transcendent: many Buddhas in many realms |
| **Texts** | Pāli canon (*suttas*) | New *sūtras* — Perfection of Wisdom (*Prajñāpāramitā*), Lotus Sūtra, Pure Land Sūtras |
| **Philosophy** | Abhidhamma analysis | *Śūnyatā* (emptiness), *tathāgatagarbha* (Buddha‑nature) |
| **Key figure** | Śāriputra, Moggallāna | Nāgārjuna (c. 150–250 CE, founder of Madhyamaka), Asaṅga (c. 4th c., Yogācāra) |

Mahāyāna became the dominant form of Buddhism in **East Asia** (China, Korea, Japan, Vietnam) and **Tibet**.

## Part V: The Transmission to East Asia (c. 1st–8th Century CE)

### China

- **Arrival** (c. 1st c. CE): Buddhist merchants and monks travelled the Silk Road from India to China. Translation began — initially using Daoist terminology (*wuwei* for *nirvāṇa*, *dao* for *dharma*)
- **Adaptation**: Buddhism confronted Confucian ancestor‑worship (the celibate monk seemed unfilial) and Daoist naturalism. Out of this encounter emerged **Chan** (Zen) Buddhism — "direct pointing to the mind; no dependence on words" (see [[East Asian Religious History]])
- **Pure Land**: Faith in Amitābha Buddha for rebirth in the Western Paradise — the most accessible, popular form of Chinese Buddhism

### Korea

- Buddhism arrived in the **4th century CE** (via China)
- Became the **state religion** under the Three Kingdoms period and the Goryeo dynasty (918–1392)
- The **Joseon dynasty** (1392–1910) replaced it with Neo‑Confucianism — monks were persecuted, temples forced into the mountains
- Modern Korea has a strong Buddhist revival, with the **Jogye Order** as the largest school

### Japan

- Buddhism arrived in the **6th century CE** (via Korea)
- **Prince Shōtoku** (574–622) promoted Buddhism as the state religion
- Distinctive Japanese schools emerged: **Shingon** (esoteric, Kūkai), **Pure Land** (Hōnen, Shinran), **Nichiren** (chanting the Lotus Sūtra), **Zen** (Dōgen, Eisai)
- Buddhism in Japan **syncretised** with Shintō — the two traditions coexisted for over a millennium (see [[East Asian Religious History]])

## Part VI: The Rise of Vajrayāna (c. 7th Century CE Onward)

**Vajrayāna** ("Diamond Vehicle") — also called **Tantric Buddhism** — emerged in India around the 7th century and became dominant in Tibet:

- **Tantric techniques**: visualisation of deities, *mantra* recitation, elaborate rituals, and the transformation of desire into wisdom
- **Guru devotion**: The *guru* (lama) is essential — one's realisation depends on the transmission
- **Mahāsiddhas**: Eccentric, enlightened masters (e.g., Tilopa, Nāropa) who often broke monastic conventions

### Buddhism in Tibet

| Period | Key Event |
|--------|-----------|
| 7th–9th c. | **First transmission**: Padmasambhava and Śāntarakṣita establish Buddhism; the Nyingma ("Ancient") school |
| 10th–12th c. | **Second transmission**: New tantras; the Kagyü, Sakya, and Kadam schools |
| 14th–16th c. | **Geluk school**: Tsongkhapa (1357–1419) systematises monastic education. The Dalai Lama lineage begins |
| 1642–1959 | **Geluk‑led government**: Dalai Lamas rule Tibet under Qing dynasty patronage |
| 1959 | **Diaspora**: The 14th Dalai Lama flees to India; Tibetan Buddhism spreads globally |

---

## Part VII: The Decline of Buddhism in India (c. 7th–13th Century)

Buddhism, which was born in India and flourished there for over a millennium, **largely disappeared from its homeland** — one of history's great religious transformations. Causes:

1. **Hindu revival**: The Bhakti movement and Śaṅkara's Advaita Vedānta absorbed Buddhist ideas while offering accessible devotional alternatives
2. **Monastic isolation**: Indian Buddhism retreated into large, land‑owning monasteries (Nālandā, Vikramaśīla) — disconnected from lay society
3. **The Muslim invasions** (c. 12th–13th c.): The great monasteries were sacked (Nālandā, 1202); monastic Buddhism in India never recovered
4. **Syncretic absorption**: Buddhists were gradually absorbed into the Hindu caste system; the Buddha became the 9th avatāra of Viṣṇu

> By the time the British arrived (17th–18th c.), Buddhism survived in India only in the Himalayan regions (Ladakh, Sikkim, Arunachal). The **mass conversion of Dalits** (1956, led by B. R. Ambedkar) was a modern revival, not a continuation.

---

## Part VIII: Buddhism in the Modern World

- **Theravāda revival** in Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, Cambodia, Laos — often entangled with nationalism
- **19th‑century Western fascination**: The "discovery" of Buddhism by European scholars, followed by early converts (H. P. Blavatsky, Christmas Humphreys)
- **Engaged Buddhism** (20th c.): Socially engaged Buddhism — **Thích Nhất Hạnh** (Vietnam), the **Dalai Lama** (Tibet), **Aung San Suu Kyi** (Myanmar, though contested)
- **Western Buddhism**: Mindfulness as a secular practice; meditation centres; Buddhist philosophy without rebirth (controversial within traditional circles)
- **Demographics**: c. 500 million Buddhists globally; 4th largest religion after Christianity, Islam, Hinduism
