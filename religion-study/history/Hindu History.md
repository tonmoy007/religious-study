# Hindu History: From the Vedas to Modernity

> The arc of the tradition now called "Hinduism" is the longest continuous religious history in the world — spanning four millennia, from the fire sacrifices of the Indus Valley to the global diaspora of the 21st century.

---

## Part I: The Indus Valley and Vedic Period (c. 2600–500 BCE)

### Indus Valley Civilisation (c. 2600–1900 BCE)

The script of the IVC remains undeciphered, so its religion must be inferred from archaeology:

- **Ritual bathing tanks** (the "Great Bath" of Mohenjo-daro) — suggesting purification rites, later central to Hinduism
- **Seal imagery**: a horned figure seated in a yogic posture (proto-Śiva? "Paśupati" seal); trees, animals, and phallic symbols (proto‑liṅga?)
- **Mother goddess figurines** — widespread, but their meaning is debated
- **Continuities** with later Hinduism are plausible but unproven; scholars warn against projecting later categories onto Bronze Age artefacts

> [!warning] The IVC–Hinduism connection
> Claims of direct continuity ("the Indus Valley was Hindu") are politically loaded in modern India. The evidence supports *some* continuities (bathing, yogic postures, reverence for certain animals) but does not prove that IVC religion was "Hindu" in any recognisable sense.

### The Vedic Period (c. 1500–500 BCE)

Indo‑Aryan pastoralists migrated into Northwest India, bringing a ritual‑centred religion known through the four Vedas:

- **Rig Veda** (c. 1400–1000 BCE): hymns to the gods (*devas*) — Indra (war, rain), Agni (fire, sacrifice), Varuṇa (cosmic order, *ṛta*), Soma (the sacred drink). The oldest surviving Indo‑European scripture. See [[Sacred Texts Overview]].
- **Yajur Veda**: prose formulas for the sacrifice (*yajña*)
- **Sāma Veda**: chants for the sacrifice
- **Atharva Veda**: spells, healing, domestic rituals

The *yajña* (fire sacrifice) was the centre of religion — performed by priests (*brahmins*) on behalf of patrons (*yajamānas*). The cosmos was sustained by sacrifice. The social order was encoded in the *puruṣa sūkta* (Rig Veda 10.90), which describes the *varṇa* (caste) system emerging from the body of the cosmic man.

> The four varṇas: Brahmins (mouth — priests), Kṣatriyas (arms — warriors), Vaiśyas (thighs — merchants/farmers), Śūdras (feet — labourers).

### The Upanishads (c. 800–400 BCE)

The late Vedic texts that mark the **shift from external sacrifice to inner knowledge** — the philosophical heart of the Axial Age in India:

- The self (*ātman*) is ultimately identical with ultimate reality (*Brahman*)
- Liberation (*mokṣa*) comes through knowledge (*jñāna*), not ritual
- The cycle of rebirth (*saṃsāra*) driven by action (*karma*)

This is the foundation of **Vedānta** — the dominant philosophical school of later Hinduism. See [[Origins and the Axial Age]] for the broader context.

## Part II: The Epic and Puranic Age (c. 500 BCE–600 CE)

This is the period in which Hinduism as we know it *crystallised* — the great epics were composed, the Purāṇas were compiled, and the devotional relationship with Viṣṇu, Śiva, and Devī became central.

### The Epics (Itihāsa)

Two vast narratives that encode dharma in story form:

- **Mahābhārata** (c. 400 BCE–400 CE): The longest epic in world literature (100,000 verses). The story of the Kuru war — layered with philosophy (the Bhagavad Gītā), myth, law, and morality. See [[Hindu Stories MOC]] for the narrative arc.
- **Rāmāyaṇa of Vālmīki** (c. 500–100 BCE): The story of Rāma and Sītā, the ideal king and queen. Shorter, more unified, and one of the most beloved texts in Hindu literature.

### The Purāṇas

Encyclopaedic texts (18 major Purāṇas) covering cosmology, genealogy, mythology, law, and pilgrimage. Key examples:

- **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** (c. 900 CE): Devoted to Viṣṇu and his avatāras — especially Kṛṣṇa. The most influential Purāṇa for Vaiṣṇava devotion.
- **Śiva Purāṇa** and **Liṅga Purāṇa**: Śaiva theology and mythology.
- **Devī Māhātmya** (part of Mārkaṇḍeya Purāṇa, c. 5th–6th c.): The first full articulation of the Goddess (Devī) as supreme.

### The Rise of Bhakti

During this period, **bhakti** (devotion to a personal god) emerges as the dominant religious emotion — distinct from Vedic ritual and Upanishadic knowledge. See Part IV below for the later Bhakti movement.

## Part III: Classical Theology (c. 600–1300 CE)

The systematic elaboration of Hindu philosophy, in dialogue with Buddhism and Jainism:

| School (Darśana) | Founder / Key Figure | Core Teaching |
|------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Advaita Vedānta** | Ādi Śaṅkara (c. 788–820) | Non‑dualism: Brahman alone is real; the world is illusion (*māyā*); liberation through knowledge |
| **Viśiṣṭādvaita** | Rāmānuja (1017–1137) | Qualified non‑dualism: God, soul, and matter are real but form one whole; devotion is the path |
| **Dvaita** | Madhva (1238–1317) | Dualism: God and soul are eternally distinct; liberation through grace and devotion |
| **Śaiva Siddhānta** | — | Śiva as supreme; dualistic theology common in South India |
| **Kashmir Śaivism** | Abhinavagupta (c. 950–1020) | Non‑dual Śaivism: the universe is the play of Śiva's consciousness |

## Part IV: The Bhakti Movement (c. 600–1700 CE)

A devotional revolution that swept across India, democratising religious experience:

| Region | Poet‑Saints | Deity | Contribution |
|--------|-------------|------|-------------|
| **Tamil Nadu** | Āḻvārs (6th–9th c.) | Viṣṇu/Kṛṣṇa | 4,000 hymns of passionate love for God |
| **Tamil Nadu** | Nāyaṉārs (6th–9th c.) | Śiva | 63 saints; their hymns form the Śaiva canon |
| **Karnataka** | Basava (12th c.) | Śiva (Liṅgāyat) | Rejected caste, temple, and ritual |
| **Maharashtra** | Jñāneśvar (1275–1296), Tukārām (17th c.) | Viṭṭhala (Kṛṣṇa) | Vernacular poetry of ecstatic devotion |
| **North India** | Kabīr (1440–1518) | Formless God | Rejected both Hindu and Muslim orthodoxy |
| **North India** | Mīrābāī (1498–1547) | Kṛṣṇa | Rajput princess who abandoned royal life for devotion |
| **Bengal** | Caitanya (1486–1534) | Kṛṣṇa | Ecstatic *saṅkīrtana* (chanting); ISKCON's spiritual root |
| **Punjab** | Gurū Nānak (1469–1539) | One God | Foundation of **Sikhism** — bhakti meets Islamic monotheism |

The Bhakti movement transformed Hinduism: it made **vernacular languages** liturgically central, challenged caste hierarchy, and shifted the centre of religious life from the temple to the heart.

## Part V: Colonial Encounter (c. 1750–1947)

### European Critiques

- **Missionaries** (William Carey, etc., early 19th c.): attacked "idolatry", satī (widow‑burning), caste — producing a defensive response among Hindu intellectuals
- **Orientalists** (William Jones, Max Müller, late 18th–19th c.): "discovered" the Vedas and Upanishads, translating them for Europe. For Hindus, this revived interest in their *own* classical texts
- **Colonial law**: The British codified "Hindu law" — freezing a fluid tradition into rigid categories

### Reform Movements

| Movement | Founder | Response to Modernity |
|----------|---------|----------------------|
| **Brahmo Samaj** | Ram Mohan Roy (1772–1833) | Monotheistic, rationalist, anti‑idolatry; drew on Upanishads |
| **Arya Samaj** | Dayananda Saraswati (1824–1883) | "Back to the Vedas"; rejected Purāṇic Hinduism; promoted cow protection and *śuddhi* (re‑conversion) |
| **Ramakrishna Mission** | Ramakrishna (1836–1886) / Vivekananda (1863–1902) | Universalist: all religions lead to the same goal; social service as worship |
| **Theosophical Society** | Blavatsky / Olcott (1875) | Esoteric Hinduism meets Western occultism |
| **Neo‑Hinduism** | Aurobindo (1872–1950) | Evolutionary spirituality; integral yoga |

### Key Figures

- **Swami Vivekananda** (1893 Parliament of the World's Religions): "Sisters and brothers of America" — presented Hinduism as a universal, rational, and tolerant religion. A watershed moment for Hindu self‑presentation in the West.
- **Mahatma Gandhi** (1869–1948): Not a reformer of Hinduism *per se*, but his *ahiṃsā* (non‑violence), *satyāgraha* (truth‑force), and *sarvodaya* (welfare of all) were deeply rooted in Hindu ethics.

## Part VI: Hinduism in the Modern World

- **Hindutva**: A political ideology (Savarkar, 1923; RSS, 1925) defining Hindu identity in cultural/national terms. The Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP, est. 1980) brought Hindutva to power in India (2014–present).
- **Caste in modern India**: Formally abolished (1950 Constitution), but persists in practice. Dalit (formerly "Untouchable") movements — B. R. Ambedkar's mass conversion to Buddhism (1956) — challenge the tradition from within.
- **Global Hinduism**: The Hare Krishna movement (ISKCON, est. 1966), yoga as a global practice, Hindu diaspora communities. Hinduism is now a global religion — no longer confined to South Asia.
