# Jain History: From Ṛṣabha to the Modern World

> The oldest śramaṇa tradition — an unbroken chain of 24 Tīrthaṅkaras spanning aeons. Jainism has outlasted empires, famines, and iconoclasm through the relentless discipline of ahiṃsā (non-violence), the patronage of merchants, and one of the most sophisticated philosophical systems in Indian thought.

---

## Part I: Prehistory and the Ṛṣabha Age

### Jain Cosmology of Time

Jainism divides time into two repeating half-cycles of immense duration:

| Cycle | Direction | Phase | Character |
|-------|-----------|-------|-----------|
| **Utsarpiṇī** (ascending) | Progress | 6 *āras* | Increasing happiness, stature, lifespan; 1 Tīrthaṅkara per *āra* in the last 3 |
| **Avasarpiṇī** (descending) | Decline | 6 *āras* | Decreasing happiness, stature, lifespan; 24 Tīrthaṅkaras appear in the 3rd–4th *āras* |

We are currently in the **5th *āra* of Avasarpiṇī** (*duṣama* — "unhappy") — a period of suffering in which no new Tīrthaṅkara can arise. The next, the 24th Tīrthaṅkara of this cycle, will appear only in the next ascending half-cycle.

### The 24 Tīrthaṅkaras

A Tīrthaṅkara ("ford-maker") is a human being who, through perfect ascetic practice, destroys all karmic bondage and reveals the eternal dharma. Jain tradition names 24 for each half-cycle:

| # | Name | Emblem | Colour | Height (traditional) | Lifespan |
|---|------|--------|--------|---------------------|----------|

| 2 | **Ajitanātha** | Elephant | Golden | 450 *dhanuṣa* | — |
| 3 | **Sambhavanātha** | Horse | Golden | 400 *dhanuṣa* | — |

| 10 | **Śītalanātha** | Śrīvatsa | Golden | 90 *dhanuṣa* | — |
| 11 | **Śreyāṃśanātha** | Rhinoceros | Golden | 80 *dhanuṣa* | — |

> [!note] Only the last two — Pārśvanātha (c. 9th c. BCE?) and Mahāvīra (6th–5th c. BCE) — are generally accepted by historians as having lived. The preceding 22 belong to the mythological prehistory of the tradition, though some scholars explore possible Indus Valley connections for the bull emblem of Ṛṣabha.

### Ṛṣabha and the Indus Valley Hypothesis

Several Jain scholars and a minority of archaeologists have proposed a link between the **seal of the "Paśupati"** (a seated figure surrounded by animals) from Mohenjo-daro (c. 2000 BCE) and Ṛṣabha's bull emblem or Mahāvīra's lion. The evidence is inconclusive, but:

- Bull imagery dominates Indus seals — Ṛṣabha's emblem is the bull (*vṛṣabha*)
- A nudity emphasis in some Indus figurines aligns with the Digambara ideal
- The "standing figure" seals may represent proto-Jain ascetics

Most historians remain cautious, placing Jainism's **verifiable origins** with Pārśva and Mahāvīra.

## Part II: Mahāvīra and the Foundation of the Saṅgha (6th–5th c. BCE)

### Mahāvīra's Biography

| Stage | Event | Significance |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| **Birth** | Vardhamāna born in **Kuṇḍagrāma** (near Vaiśālī, modern Bihar) to King Siddhārtha and Queen Triśalā | Second son of a kṣatriya clan; twin auspicious dreams foretold his birth |
| **Renunciation** | At age 30, after the death of his parents, he distributes his wealth and becomes a homeless ascetic | Joins the Pārśva tradition — removes all clothes (Digambara tradition) or wears a single robe (Śvetāmbara tradition) |
| **Asceticism** | 12½ years of extreme penance: fasting, silence, meditation, enduring abuse | "During the first year I did not sleep for a single night" — *Ācārāṅga Sūtra* |
| **Kevala Jñāna** | Under a **śāla tree** on the bank of the Ṛjupālikā River (near Jṛmbhikagrāma) | Attains **omniscience** (*kevala jñāna*); becomes a **Jina** ("Victor"), a **Tīrthaṅkara** |
| **First Sermon** | On Mount Vipula (near Rājagṛha) | Delivers the first sermon to the 11 Ganadharas; establishes the fourfold community |
| **Nirvāṇa** | At **Pāvāpurī** (modern Bihar) at age 72 | Enters final liberation; the event is celebrated as **Dīpāvalī** (Jain New Year) |

### Dates: Traditional vs. Scholarly

| Aspect | Traditional Date | Scholarly Consensus |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Birth** | 599 BCE | c. 490–480 BCE |
| **Nirvāṇa** | 527 BCE | c. 410–400 BCE |
| **Total life** | 72 years | 72 years |
| **Contemporary of Buddha** | Yes (Buddha died 543 BCE trad.) | Likely (Mahāvīra probably slightly older) |

> The traditional dates of 599–527 BCE are still used in Jain liturgy and calendar. The scholarly revision to c. 490–410 BCE aligns better with the archaeological evidence of the Buddha's dates and the chronology of the Magadhan kings.

### The 11 Ganadharas

Immediately after his enlightenment, Mahāvīra converted **Indrabhūti Gautama** (his chief disciple), Agnibhūti, Vāyubhūti, Vyākta, Sudharman, Maṇḍitaputra, Mauryaputra, Akampita, Acalabhrātā, & Metārya. These 11 **Ganadharas** (heads of the monastic order) compiled the original teachings into the **12 Aṅgas** (limbs).

### The Fourfold Order

| Group | Male | Female |
|-------|------|--------|
| **Monks** (*sādhus / munis*) | Yes | Yes |
| **Nuns** (*sādhvīs*) | — | Yes (Śvetāmbara has nuns; Digambara denies women can attain mokṣa without rebirth as a man) |
| **Laymen** (*śrāvakas*) | Yes | Yes |
| **Laywomen** (*śrāvikās*) | — | Yes |

> Notably, Mahāvīra admitted women to the monastic order — a radical step for his time. The Śvetāmbara tradition maintains that Mahāvīra's disciple **Candanā** (or Candrabālā) was the first nun; the Digambara tradition holds that women cannot attain *mokṣa* from a female body.

### The Śramaṇa Context

Mahāvīra's movement was one of several **śramaṇa** (striver) traditions that flourished in the Gangetic plain during the Axial Age:

| Tradition | Founder (or key figure) | Key Teaching |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------|
| **Jainism** | Mahāvīra (preceded by Pārśva) | Ahiṃsā, asceticism, karma, anekāntavāda |
| **Buddhism** | Siddhārtha Gautama | Middle Way, Four Noble Truths, Eightfold Path |
| **Ājīvika** | Makkhali Gosāla | Fatalism (*niyati*); no karma |
| **Lokāyata/Cārvāka** | Bṛhaspati (legendary) | Materialism; no afterlife |
| **Agnostic/Sceptical** | Sañjaya Belaṭṭhiputta | Suspension of judgement on metaphysical questions |

Mahāvīra was associated with **Gosāla** (the Ājīvika leader) for six years before they parted ways — a period recounted in the Jain *Bhagavatī Sūtra*.

## Part III: The Early Saṅgha and the Spread (5th–3rd c. BCE)

### The Succession of Śruta Kevalins

After Mahāvīra's *nirvāṇa*, the saṅgha was led by a succession of **Śruta Kevalins** — those who knew the entire canon by heart:

1. **Sudharman** (the last surviving Ganadhara)
2. **Jambūsvāmin**
3. **Prabhava**
4. **Śayyambhava**
5. **Yaśobhadra**
6. **Sambhūtavijaya**
7. **Bhadrabāhu** (the 7th — key figure in the split)

This oral line preserved the teachings intact for several generations.

### The First Schism: Makkhali Gosāla

Some sources record that the first break came in Mahāvīra's own lifetime, when **Gosāla** formed the **Ājīvika** sect after a dispute. For Jainism proper, the first internal schism is traditionally placed in the **Mauryan period** (see Part IV).

### Candragupta Maurya's Conversion (Digambara Tradition)

The Digambara tradition recounts that **Candragupta Maurya** (founder of the Maurya Empire, r. c. 321–297 BCE) became a Jain lay disciple and eventually a monk, abdicating his throne to follow **Bhadrabāhu** southward to Karnataka. This narrative:

- Is accepted by Digambaras as historical
- Is disputed by Śvetāmbaras (who say Candragupta remained a follower of the Brahmanical tradition)
- Is the basis for a claim to Mauryan royal patronage

If historical, Candragupta's conversion would be the first major royal endorsement of Jainism, parallel to Aśoka's conversion to Buddhism.

### Jain Trade Networks

From the early centuries, Jainism spread along trade routes. The **Śrāvaka** (lay Jain) communities were predominantly **mercantile** — a pattern that continues today. The reasons:

- Ahiṃsā precluded agriculture (killing insects in ploughing)
- Trade allowed wealth without violence
- Jain monks required lay support for food, shelter, and manuscripts

## Part IV: The Great Famine and the Split (c. 350–300 BCE)

### Bhadrabāhu's Prophecy

According to the Digambara *Bhadrabāhu Carita*, the 7th Śruta Kevalin **Bhadrabāhu** foresaw a 12-year famine in Magadha and led a migration of 12,000 monks southward to Karnataka and the Deccan. Among them was **Candragupta Maurya** (as a monk).

### The Council of Pāṭaliputra (c. 300 BCE)

Those who stayed behind in Magadha — led by **Sthūlabhadra** — convened a council at Pāṭaliputra to compile the teachings while the elders were absent. Key outcomes:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Chair** | Sthūlabhadra |
| **Texts compiled** | 11 Aṅgas (the 12th, the *Dṛṣṭivāda*, was lost — tradition says only Bhadrabāhu knew it) |
| **Canonical status** | Śvetāmbaras accept this as the beginning of their canonical corpus |

### The Emerging Division

When Bhadrabāhu's party returned from the South, they rejected the Pāṭaliputra compilation as incomplete — the *Dṛṣṭivāda* was missing. This disagreement set the stage for the permanent **Śvetāmbara-Digambara** schism, though the split likely took several centuries to harden.

| Feature | Digambara (Sky-clad) | Śvetāmbara (White-clad) |
|---------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Clothing** | Monks wear no clothes (nakedness = non-possession) | Monks wear white robes |
| **Women** | Cannot attain mokṣa from female body; no nuns | Women can attain mokṣa; full order of nuns |
| **Canon** | Rejects the Śvetāmbara Āgamas as corrupt; relies on later works (Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama, Kundakunda) | Accepts the 45 Āgama texts (11 Aṅgas, 12 Upāṅgas, etc.) |
| **Mahāvīra's life** | Never married; renounced without permission | Married, fathered a daughter; renounced with family's consent |
| **Kevalin's needs** | A kevalin does not need food | A kevalin still needs minimal food to sustain the body |

> [!note] A third sect, the **Yāpanīya**, existed from roughly the 2nd c. BCE to the 14th c. CE. They followed Śvetāmbara canon but Digambara monastic practice (nudity). They are now extinct.

## Part V: The Age of Councils and Consolidation (3rd c. BCE–5th c. CE)

### The Council of Valabhī (453–467 CE)

Under the presidency of **Devarddhigaṇi Kṣamāśramaṇa**, the Śvetāmbara council at **Valabhī** (in modern Gujarat) finalised the written canon:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Texts fixed** | 45 Āgama texts (11 Aṅgas, 12 Upāṅgas, 10 Prakīrṇakas, 6 Chedasūtras, 4 Mūlasūtras, 2 Cūlikā Sūtras) |
| **Language** | Ardha-Māgadhī Prākrit |
| **Significance** | This is the canonical basis for all later Śvetāmbara Jainism |

The Digambara tradition did not participate in Valabhī and developed its own canon in later centuries, centred on the **Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama** (attributed to Puṣpadanta and Bhūtabali, c. 2nd c. CE) and the **Kasāya Pāhuḍa** (Guṇadhara).

### The Mauryan and Post-Mauryan Period

| Dynasty | Relation to Jainism |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Maurya** (321–185 BCE) | Candragupta (trad. Jain monk); Bindusāra (possibly); Aśoka (Buddhist, but Jain sources claim patronage) |
| **Śuṅga** (185–73 BCE) | Brahmanical revival; some persecution of Jains 🜄 (the king Puṣyamitra Śuṅga reportedly destroyed Jain monasteries) |
| **Kṣatrapa** (1st–4th c. CE) | Jainism thrived in western India under the Western Kṣatrapas (Śaka rulers) — many Jain inscriptions from this period |
| **Gupta** (320–550 CE) | Generally tolerant; Jainism flourished alongside Buddhism and Hinduism |

### The Extinct Yāpanīya Sect

The Yāpanīyas bridged the two main traditions:

- **Canon**: Accepted Śvetāmbara Āgamas
- **Monastic practice**: Nudity (Digambara-style)
- **Women**: Allowed nuns; taught that women could attain *mokṣa*
- **Decline**: Last mentioned in inscriptions from the 14th century

## Part VI: The Classical Age — Growth and Philosophy (5th–12th c. CE)

### Foundational Philosophers

| Figure | Tradition | Work | Contribution |
|--------|-----------|------|-------------|
| **Umāsvāmi** (c. 2nd–5th c. CE) | Both (Digambara/Śvetāmbara claim him) | *Tattvārtha Sūtra* | The first systematic Jain philosophy text; the *sūtras* accepted by all sects |
| **Kundakunda** (c. 2nd–3rd c.) | Digambara | *Samayasāra*, *Pravacanasāra* | Dual perspective: *niścaya* (real) vs. *vyavahāra* (conventional) truth; interiorised karma doctrine |
| **Siddhasena Divākara** (c. 5th c.) | Śvetāmbara | *Nyāyāvatāra*, *Dvātriṃśikā* | First Jain logician; synthesis of Jain thought with Buddhist *pramāṇa* (logic) |
| **Haribhadra** (c. 700–770) | Śvetāmbara | *Ṣaḍdarśanasamuccaya*, *Yogabindu* | Doxographer who analysed all six Indian philosophical systems from a Jain standpoint; author of over 1,400 works (trad.) |
| **Akalaṅka** (c. 720–780) | Digambara | *Tattvārtha Rājavārtika*, *Aṣṭaśatī* | Leading Digambara logician; systematised *anekāntavāda* (many-sidedness) |
| **Vidyānanda** (c. 9th c.) | Digambara | *Tattvārtha Ślokavārttika* | Further refined Jain epistemology |
| **Hemacandra** (1089–1172) | Śvetāmbara | *Yogaśāstra*, *Siddhahemaśabdānuśāsana*, *Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacarita* | Polymath: grammar, logic, poetry, history, yoga. Last royal Jain conversion (Kumārapāla) |

### Key Philosophical Tenets

| Doctrine | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| **Ahiṃsā** (non-violence) | Supreme dharma — the central Jain virtue extended to all living beings (including plants, microbes, and elementals) |
| **Anekāntavāda** (many-sidedness) | Reality has infinite aspects; no single viewpoint captures the whole truth |
| **Syādvāda** (conditional predication) | Every proposition is true only "in a certain sense" — the seven-fold predication (*saptabhaṅgī*) |
| **Karma** | A material substance (*karma-pudgala*) that clings to the soul (*jīva*); bondage and liberation are physical processes |
| **Jīva / Ajīva** | Two fundamental categories: souls (infinite, conscious) and non-souls (matter, space, time, motion, rest) |

### The Ācārya Lineages

Both traditions developed elaborate lineages of **Ācāryas** (monastic heads). In the Digambara tradition, the line runs from Mahāvīra through the Śruta Kevalins to:

- Kundakunda → Umāsvāmi → Samantabhadra → Akalaṅka → Vidyānanda → Nemicandra (author of *Gommaṭasāra*, the Digambara encyclopedia)

In the Śvetāmbara tradition, the *Sthānakavāsi* and *Mūrtipūjaka* branches both trace through the Valabhī Ācāryas.

### Hemacandra and the Chaulukya Age

**Hemacandra** (1089–1172 CE) was the court scholar of King **Kumārapāla** of the Chaulukya dynasty of Gujarat under whose patronage Jainism became the de facto state religion:

- Kumārapāla converted from Śaivism to Jainism (the last royal conversion in Indian history)
- He enforced vegetarianism and non-violence across his kingdom
- Hemacandra wrote the *Yogaśāstra*, a systematic Jain meditation manual
- He also composed the *Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacarita* ("Lives of the 63 Illustrious Persons"), a Jain universal history

### The Gangas and the Kadambas of Karnataka

The **Gaṅga** dynasty of Talkad (c. 350–1000 CE) and the **Kadambas** were major Digambara patrons:

| Patron | Region | Monument |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **Camuṇḍarāya** (minister of the Gaṅga king Mārasimha) | Śravaṇabeḷagoḷa, Karnataka | **The great Bāhubalī statue** — 18m (57 ft) monolith of Lord Bāhubalī (Gommeśvara), installed 983 CE |
| **Gaṅga kings** | Talkad | Numerous *basadis* (Jain temples) and cave shelters |
| **Kadamba kings** | Banavasi | Jain cave temples in the Western Ghats |

The Bāhubalī statue at Śravaṇabeḷagoḷa is the **largest monolithic Jain statue in the world**, carved from a single block of granite. Every 12 years, the **Mahāmastakābhiṣeka** (head-anointing festival) draws hundreds of thousands of pilgrims.

### Jain Art and Architecture

| Period | Examples |
|--------|----------|
| **Cave temples** (2nd c. BCE–5th c. CE) | Udayagiri-Khandagiri (Odisha), Aihole (Karnataka), Sittannavasal (Tamil Nadu) |
| **Temple cities** (10th–13th c.) | Dilwara temples (Mount Abu, Rajasthan) — exquisite white marble; Ranakpur (Rajasthan) — 1,444 marble pillars; Palitana (Gujarat) — 863 temples on a hilltop |
| **Colossal statues** (10th c.) | Śravaṇabeḷagoḷa (Bāhubalī, 983 CE); Kārkoḷa (Bāhubalī, 12th c.) |

## Part VII: Decline and Survival (13th–18th c. CE)

### The Turkic Invasions and Temple Destruction

The Turkic invasions of North India (c. 12th–14th c.) devastated Jain institutions:

- **Delhi Sultanate** (1206–1526): Systematic desecration of Jain temples; some Jain ministers served under Muslim rulers (e.g., the Āgarā family at Delhi) to protect the community
- **Gujarat**: The Chaulukya kingdom fell to the Delhi Sultanate; many Jain temples at Somnath, Cambay, and Patan were destroyed or converted into mosques
- **Karnataka**: The Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646) was largely tolerant, but Digambara centres in the North declined

> Despite the destruction, several Jain **ministers and merchants** gained high positions in the Delhi Sultanate courts, negotiating protection for temples and the right to build new ones — a pattern of survival through diplomacy.

### The Decline of Digambara Centres in the North

By the 15th century, Digambara monastic presence in North India had shrunk dramatically. The centre of gravity shifted to **South India** (Karnataka, Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu), while Śvetāmbaras consolidated in **Gujarat and Rajasthan**.

### Jain Commercial Success in Gujarat and Rajasthan

Controlled by the Turkic rulers but economically indispensable, Jain merchants flourished:

- **Banking and trade**: Jains dominated long-distance trade in Gujarat, linking India to the Persian Gulf, East Africa, and Southeast Asia
- **Textiles**: Jain financiers controlled the textile markets of Ahmedabad and Cambay
- **Mughal patronage**: Under Akbar (r. 1556–1605), the Jain monk **Hīravijayasūri** gained imperial favour. Akbar prohibited animal slaughter on Jain holy days and honoured Jain scriptures

### Śvetāmbara Mūrtipūjaka-Sthānakavāsi Split (15th–17th c.)

| Aspect | Mūrtipūjaka (idol-worshipping) | Sthānakavāsi (non-idol-worshipping) |
|--------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Origins** | Traditional Śvetāmbara practice | Reform movement founded by **Loṅkā Śāh** (c. 1475) and later **Lavaka** (c. 1650) |
| **Idol worship** | Central — elaborate temple rituals | Rejected as non-canonical; prayer halls (*sthānakas*) instead |
| **Monks** | White-robed; accept the full Valabhī canon | White-robed; reject parts of the canon as interpolated; cover mouth (*muhpattī*) at all times |
| **Nuns** | Full order of nuns | Smaller nun tradition |

> A further reform movement, the **Terāpantha** (c. 1760), emerged from the Sthānakavāsi tradition under **Ācārya Bhīkṣu**. They are strictly non-idolatrous, with tightly disciplined monastic hierarchy.

### The Āgama Rediscovery and Manuscript Preservation

Between the 13th and 17th centuries, Jain monks and lay scholars undertook a massive campaign of **manuscript copying and preservation**:

- **Bhaṇḍāras** (libraries) in Gujarat and Rajasthan accumulated tens of thousands of manuscripts on palm leaf and paper
- The **Jaisalmer Bhaṇḍāra** and **Patthar-ki-Haveli** (Jaipur) hold among the largest collections of pre-modern Indian manuscripts
- These libraries preserved not just Jain texts but also Hindu, Buddhist, and secular works that would otherwise have been lost

## Part VIII: Modern Jainism (19th c.–Present)

### Colonial Encounters and the Census Classification

| Year | Development |
|------|------------|
| **1871–1872** | First British census of India classifies Jains as a sub-sect of Hinduism |
| **1901** | Census Commissioner **Herbert Risley** lists Jains as a "religion" for the first time (under protest from Hindu organisations) |
| **1911** | A separate Jain category is finally established in the census — 1.2 million counted |

The British categorisation forced Jains to articulate what made their tradition distinct from Hinduism — catalysing a modern **Jain identity movement**.

### The Jain Identity Movement

| Figure / Organisation | Contribution |
|-----------------------|-------------|
| **Ātmānanda** (19th c.) | Advocated for Jain legal identity separate from Hindu law |
| **Jain Śvetāmbara Conference** (founded 1879) | United Śvetāmbara voices on political and religious issues |
| **Vīra Samvat 2500** (1974) | Major celebration of Mahāvīra's 2,500th *nirvāṇa* anniversary; global awareness campaign |
| **Ācārya Śrī Sushil Kumar** (1926–1994) | First Jain monk to teach in the West; founded the International Mahavir Jain Mission |
| **Ācārya Mahāprajña** (1920–2010) | Prekṣā meditation revival; modernisation of Terāpantha |

### The Jain Diaspora

| Region | Wave | Population (approx.) |
|--------|------|---------------------|
| **East Africa** (Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania) | Late 19th–early 20th c. | c. 60,000 (peak); many later migrated to UK/USA |
| **United Kingdom** | 1960s–1970s (from East Africa) | c. 50,000 |
| **United States** | 1970s–present | c. 150,000 |
| **Canada** | 1980s–present | c. 30,000 |
| **Europe** | 1980s–present | c. 20,000 |

> The diaspora has prompted a major renegotiation of Jain identity: inter-sect marriage (historically taboo), the role of technology in monastic life, and the adaptation of Jain ethics to secular, pluralist societies.

### Eco-Jainism and Contemporary Ahiṃsā Movements

Jainism's environmental implications have received renewed attention:

| Movement / Initiative | Focus |
|-----------------------|-------|
| **Jain declarations on climate change** | Statements from Jain Ācāryas urging plant-based diets and reduced consumption |
| **Ladnu Jain hospital** (Rajasthan) | The *Ahimsa Samvardhan Samiti* — animal rescue and vegan advocacy |
| **Jain ecological vegetarianism** | Extended beyond personal practice to environmental activism |
| **Prekṣā Dhyāna** (Terāpantha meditation) | A modern, systematic practice drawing on ancient Jain yogic techniques — taught globally |

> Contemporary issues debated within Jain communities include: organ donation (does it harm the body?), assisted dying (can a monk voluntarily fast to death — *sallekhanā* — be reconciled with modern ethics?), and the status of Jainism in Indian law (the 2005 Supreme Court ruling that Jainism is a separate religion from Hinduism).

## Timeline

| Date | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Cyclical prehistory** | 22 Tīrthaṅkaras before Pārśva | Mythological foundation |
| **c. 9th c. BCE (trad.)** | **Pārśvanātha** (23rd Tīrthaṅkara) | Establishes the *cāturyāma dharma* — four vows (later became five) |
| **599–527 BCE (trad.) / c. 490–410 BCE (scholarly)** | Mahāvīra's life | 24th and last Tīrthaṅkara; formal foundation of the Jain saṅgha |
| **c. 350–300 BCE** | Great Famine; Bhadrabāhu's migration to Karnataka | Beginning of the Digambara-Śvetāmbara split |
| **c. 300 BCE** | Council of Pāṭaliputra | First compilation of the Śvetāmbara canon |
| **c. 268–232 BCE** | Aśoka's reign | Buddhist patronage dominant; Jain communities survived |
| **c. 185–73 BCE** | Śuṅga period | Alleged persecution under Puṣyamitra |
| **c. 1st c. BCE–1st c. CE** | *Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama* compiled | Digambara canonical foundation |
| **c. 2nd–5th c. CE** | Umāsvāmi writes *Tattvārtha Sūtra* | First systematic Jain philosophy — accepted by all sects |
| **453–467 CE** | **Council of Valabhī** | Śvetāmbara canon fixed in writing |
| **c. 700–800 CE** | Akalaṅka, Haribhadra, Siddhasena Divākara | Golden age of Jain logic and doxography |
| **983 CE** | **Bāhubalī statue** consecrated at Śravaṇabeḷagoḷa | World's largest monolithic Jain statue |
| **c. 1089–1172 CE** | Hemacandra's career; Chaulukya king Kumārapāla converts | Last royal conversion; synthesis of Jain yoga |
| **c. 1200–1400 CE** | Turkic invasions; destruction of northern Jain centres | Decline of North Indian Jainism |
| **c. 1475 CE** | Loṅkā Śāh's reform | Beginnings of Sthānakavāsi tradition |
| **c. 1556–1605 CE** | Akbar honours Hīravijayasūri | Mughal patronage for Jainism |
| **1760 CE** | Terāpantha founded by Ācārya Bhīkṣu | Stricter non-idolatrous reform |
| **1871–1911 CE** | British census classification | Jainism recognised as a separate religion |
| **1956 CE** | Indian Supreme Court rules Jains as Hindu (later reversed in 2005) | Legal identity debate |
| **1974 CE** | Vīra Samvat 2500 celebration | Global profile raised |
| **2005 CE** | Supreme Court of India: Jainism is a distinct religion | Legal recognition of separate identity |
| **Present** | Jain diaspora c. 500,000–600,000 worldwide | Global presence |
