# Classical and Medieval Religious History (c. 200 BCE–1500 CE)

> Part of [[Religion Study MOC]] → History of Religions

The long millennium between the [[Origins and the Axial Age|Axial Age]] and the early modern world shaped the religious landscape we still inhabit. This note follows the major traditions through the classical empires, the rise of new world religions, and the medieval synthesis — up to the threshold of modernity (covered in [[Spread and Transformation of World Religions]]).

---

## Part I: The Hellenistic World & Roman Empire (c. 330 BCE–500 CE)

### Hellenistic Religion (323–30 BCE)

Alexander's conquests (d. 323 BCE) unleashed a wave of cultural fusion across the Mediterranean and Near East:

- **Syncretism**: Greek gods were identified with local deities (Zeus-Ammon, Hermes-Thoth). The **interpretatio graeca** reframed Egyptian, Syrian, and Anatolian cults for Greek-speaking audiences.
- **Mystery cults**: The Eleusinian Mysteries, the cult of Isis (from Egypt), Mithraism (from Persia) offered personal salvation, secret initiations, and a sense of belonging that the public state cults did not. These were the "spiritual marketplace" of the Hellenistic world.
- **Philosophy as religion**: Stoicism (cosmic reason, inner virtue), Epicureanism (the gods are indifferent), Neoplatonism (emanation from the One). Philosophical schools became the primary spiritual path for educated elites.
- **The Septuagint** (c. 250 BCE): The Hebrew Bible translated into Greek at Alexandria — a pivotal moment that made Jewish scripture accessible to the Hellenistic world and provided the Bible for early Christianity.

### Judaism in the Second Temple Period (c. 515 BCE–70 CE)

- **Diaspora**: Jewish communities thrived in Alexandria, Rome, Babylon — developing interpretive traditions, the synagogue as a center of worship and study, and a scriptural canon in formation.
- **Hellenistic Judaism**: Philo of Alexandria (c. 20 BCE–50 CE) reinterpreted the Torah through Platonic philosophy — a model later adopted by Christian theologians.
- **Sects**: Pharisees (interpretive tradition, oral law), Sadducees (Temple-centered, literal), Essenes (Qumran, apocalyptic), Zealots (revolutionary). This diversity shaped the environment into which Christianity was born.
- **The Destruction of the Second Temple** (70 CE): The catastrophe that forced Judaism to reinvent itself — see below.

### The Rise of Christianity (1st–5th Centuries CE)

- **Jesus of Nazareth** (c. 4 BCE–30/33 CE): A Jewish apocalyptic preacher in Galilee. His followers proclaimed him the Messiah ("Christ"), crucified by Rome and resurrected by God.
- **Paul of Tarsus** (c. 5–67 CE): A Hellenistic Jew who transformed a small messianic sect into a universal religion. His letters are the earliest Christian documents. Key innovations: salvation by faith, not law; inclusion of Gentiles without requiring circumcision or kosher observance.
- **Persecution to establishment**:
  - Sporadic persecution under Nero (64 CE), Decius (250 CE), Diocletian (303 CE).
  - **Constantine's conversion** (312 CE) and the Edict of Milan (313 CE) — Christianity legalized.
  - **Theodosius I** (380 CE) — Christianity made the state religion of the Roman Empire.
- **Defining orthodoxy**: The ecumenical councils (Nicaea 325, Constantinople 381, Chalcedon 451) defined the Trinity, the divinity and humanity of Christ, and the canon of the New Testament. Those who disagreed (Arians, Nestorians, Monophysites) were deemed heretics — but many of these communities survived and spread eastward.

> [!quote] The Nicene Creed (325 CE)
> "We believe in one God, the Father Almighty, Maker of all things visible and invisible. And in one Lord Jesus Christ, the Son of God… of one substance with the Father."

- **Monasticism**: From the Egyptian desert fathers (Anthony, Pachomius) in the 3rd–4th centuries, monasticism spread as a radical alternative to worldly Christianity. The Rule of St. Benedict (c. 530 CE) became the standard for Western monasticism.
- **Augustine of Hippo** (354–430 CE): The most influential theologian for Western Christianity. *Confessions*, *City of God*, his doctrines of original sin, grace, and predestination shaped Catholic and (later) Protestant theology.

### Rabbinic Judaism (70 CE onward)

The destruction of the Temple in 70 CE ended sacrificial worship. Judaism reinvented itself around **text, study, and practice**:

- **Yavneh Academy**: The rabbis gathered at Yavneh (c. 70–90 CE) to reconstruct Judaism without a Temple. Prayer replaced sacrifice; the synagogue replaced the Temple; study of Torah became the highest act of worship.
- **The Mishnah** (c. 200 CE): The first major work of rabbinic Judaism — 63 tractates organizing oral law into 6 orders. Compiled by Rabbi Judah ha-Nasi. See [[Mishnah Index]].
- **The Talmud**: Two versions — the Jerusalem Talmud (c. 400 CE) and the Babylonian Talmud (c. 500 CE). The Gemara (commentary on the Mishnah) plus the Mishnah itself. The Babylonian Talmud became the authoritative text of rabbinic Judaism. (Not yet in the vault — see [[Scriptures Library]] for availability.)
- **Key features**: Halakha (law), Aggadah (narrative), midrash (interpretation), and the principle of the **Oral Torah** — the belief that Moses received both the Written Torah (Tanakh) and the Oral Torah (later encoded in the Talmud) at Sinai.

## Part II: The Rise of Islam (610–750 CE)

- **Muhammad** (c. 570–632 CE): A merchant of Mecca who received revelations from God through the angel Gabriel from 610 CE onward. These revelations became the Qur'an. See [[Quran Index]].
- **The Hijra** (622 CE): Migration to Medina — marks the beginning of the Islamic calendar. In Medina, Muhammad became both a prophet and a statesman, uniting the Arabian tribes under Islam.
- **The Rashidun Caliphs** (632–661 CE): Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali. The rapid expansion of the Islamic state into Syria, Egypt, Persia, and North Africa.
- **The Sunni–Shia split**: Arises from the question of succession after Muhammad's death. Sunnis accepted the Caliphs; Shia believed leadership should remain in the Prophet's family, specifically Ali and his descendants.
- **The Umayyad Caliphate** (661–750 CE): Capital at Damascus. Continued expansion into Spain, Central Asia, and the Indus Valley. Arabic became the administrative language. The Dome of the Rock built in Jerusalem (691 CE).
- **The Abbasid Revolution** (750 CE): Overthrew the Umayyads, moved the capital to Baghdad, and inaugurated the Islamic Golden Age (see below).

### Early Islamic Theology and Law

- **Qur'anic codification**: Under Caliph Uthman (c. 650 CE), the Qur'an was standardized into the text known today. Variant readings were suppressed.
- **Hadith collection**: The prophetic traditions were collected and authenticated in the 8th–9th centuries — the "Six Books" (Bukhari, Muslim, Abi Dawud, Tirmidhi, Nasa'i, Ibn Majah) plus three other major collections. See [[Hadith Library]].
- **Four Sunni madhhabs** (legal schools): Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali — all established by the mid-9th century.
- **Shi'i Islam**: Developed its own legal tradition and theology around the Imamate — the belief that divinely guided Imams from Ali's lineage lead the community.

## Part III: Buddhism's Expansion (c. 250 BCE–1000 CE)

- **Ashoka** (r. 268–232 BCE): The Mauryan emperor who converted to Buddhism and sent missionaries across Asia — to Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, Central Asia, and the Hellenistic world. His edicts are the earliest surviving Buddhist inscriptions.
- **The Silk Road** (1st c. CE onward): Buddhism traveled the trade routes from India to China. It arrived in China via Central Asian merchants and monks around the 1st century CE — a gradual, peaceful transmission through translation, adaptation, and syncretism with Daoist terminology.
- **Mahayana Buddhism** (c. 1st c. BCE onward): A new vision of the Buddhist path emphasizing the *bodhisattva* (one who delays their own liberation to help others), universal salvation, and new sutras. Mahayana became the dominant form in East Asia.
- **Theravada Buddhism**: The "Way of the Elders" — the oldest surviving school — became dominant in Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia (Myanmar, Thailand, Cambodia, Laos).
- **Vajrayana Buddhism** (c. 7th c. CE onward): Tantric Buddhism, incorporating esoteric rituals and visualizations. Dominant in Tibet and Mongolia. The Tibetan Buddhist canon (Kangyur, Tengyur) is among the world's largest scriptural collections.
- **Chan/Zen Buddhism** (6th c. CE onward): A Chinese school emphasizing direct meditation over textual study. Spread to Japan as Zen. See [[East Asian and Indigenous Traditions]].

## Part IV: The Medieval Period (c. 500–1500 CE)

### Christendom

- **Papacy and the Holy Roman Empire**: After the fall of the Western Roman Empire (476 CE), the Church became the guardian of Roman civilization. The Pope emerged as the supreme spiritual authority; Charlemagne (crowned 800 CE) revived the title of Emperor in the West.
- **The Great Schism** (1054 CE): The split between the Roman Catholic West and the Eastern Orthodox East — over papal authority, the *filioque* clause, and cultural differences. The two churches remain divided.
- **The Crusades** (1095–1291 CE): A series of military expeditions to reclaim the Holy Land. Deeply shaped Christian–Muslim relations and left a legacy of mistrust.
- **Monastic reform movements**: Cluny (10th c.), Cistercians (12th c.), Franciscans and Dominicans (13th c.) — periodic reforms against worldly excess in the Church.
- **Scholasticism** (12th–13th c.): The attempt to reconcile Christian faith with Aristotelian philosophy. **Thomas Aquinas** (1225–1274) authored the *Summa Theologica*, the masterpiece of Catholic theology. His synthesis of faith and reason remains the official position of the Catholic Church.
- **Cathedrals and universities**: The first universities (Bologna, Paris, Oxford) grew out of cathedral schools. Education, like religion, was under Church authority.

### Islam's Golden Age (c. 750–1258 CE)

- **Baghdad's House of Wisdom**: Translation movement that preserved and expanded Greek philosophy, medicine, mathematics, and astronomy — knowledge that later fueled the European Renaissance.
- **Islamic theology and philosophy**:
  - **Kalam** (dialectical theology): The Mu'tazilites (rationalists, argued the Qur'an was created) vs. the Ash'arites (the Qur'an is uncreated, God's attributes are real but not identical to His essence).
  - **Falsafa** (Hellenistic philosophy in Islam): **Al-Farabi**, **Ibn Sina (Avicenna)**, **Ibn Rushd (Averroes)** — who argued that reason and revelation are two paths to the same truth. Averroes' commentaries on Aristotle shaped Christian scholasticism.
  - **Al-Ghazali** (1058–1111): *The Incoherence of the Philosophers* — a devastating critique of the *falasifa*, arguing that reason alone cannot reach the highest truths. His own path led him to Sufism.
- **Sufism**: Islamic mysticism. The search for direct experience of God through love, poetry, and spiritual disciplines. Key figures: **Rabi'a al-Adawiyya** (d. 801, the saint of divine love), **Mansur al-Hallaj** (executed 922 for saying "I am the Truth"), **Jalal al-Din Rumi** (1207–1273, the *Mathnawi*), **Ibn al-Arabi** (1165–1240, the unity of being).
- **The Mongol sack of Baghdad** (1258 CE): The end of the Abbasid Caliphate. A profound trauma for Islamic civilization, sometimes framed as the end of the Golden Age — though Islamic culture continued to flourish elsewhere (Mamluk Cairo, Mughal India, Ottoman Turkey).

### The Bhakti Movement in India (c. 600–1700 CE)

- A devotional revolution that swept across India, emphasizing **personal love for a personal God** over ritual, caste, and theological learning.
- **Alvars and Nayanars**: Tamil poet-saints (6th–9th c.) devoted to Vishnu and Shiva. Their hymns are still sung in temples.
- **Key figures**:
  - **Ramanuja** (1017–1137 CE): Philosopher of Vishishtadvaita (qualified non-dualism) — the soul is one with God but distinct, like a wave is one with the ocean.
  - **Basava** (12th c. Karnataka): Lingayat movement — rejected caste and ritual.
  - **Kabir** (1440–1518): A weaver-poet who rejected both Hindu and Muslim orthodoxy, singing of a God beyond names.
  - **Mirabai** (1498–1547): A Rajput princess who abandoned her royal life for devotion to Krishna.
  - **Guru Nanak** (1469–1539): Founder of Sikhism — a tradition that blended Bhakti devotion with Islamic monotheism. See [[Dharmic Traditions]].
- **Impact**: The Bhakti movement democratized religious experience, challenged caste hierarchy, and produced a vast literature of devotional poetry in vernacular languages.

### East Asian Religious Synthesis

- **China**: Confucianism, Daoism, and Buddhism coexisted and intertwined — the "Three Teachings" (*San Jiao*). The state sponsored Confucian ethics for governance, Daoist alchemy and meditation for health and longevity, and Buddhist rituals for the dead. Neo-Confucianism (Zhu Xi, 1130–1200 CE) synthesized all three into a comprehensive philosophy.
- **Japan**:
  - **Shinto**: The indigenous animist tradition focused on *kami* (spirits) — coexisted with Buddhism since its arrival in the 6th century.
  - **Japanese Buddhism**: Developed distinctive schools — **Pure Land** (faith in Amida Buddha for salvation), **Nichiren** (chanting the Lotus Sutra), **Zen** (meditation, influenced samurai culture).
  - **Syncretism**: *Shinbutsu-shugo* — the fusion of Shinto kami and Buddhist bodhisattvas. It was not unusual for a person to be born Shinto, married Christian, and buried Buddhist (and to see no contradiction).
- **Korea**: Buddhism arrived in the 4th century and became the state religion. The Joseon dynasty (1392–1910) replaced it with Neo-Confucianism as the official ideology.

### The European High Middle Ages & Transitions

- **The Black Death** (1347–1351): Killed 30–50% of Europe's population. Shook faith in the Church's authority and contributed to the conditions that produced the Reformation.
- **John Wycliffe** (1320s–1384) and **Jan Hus** (c. 1370–1415): Forerunners of the Reformation — challenged papal authority, translated the Bible into vernacular languages.
- **The Fall of Constantinople** (1453 CE): The Byzantine Empire (the eastern half of Christendom) fell to the Ottoman Turks. Greek scholars fled to Italy with manuscripts, fueling the Renaissance.
- **The Printing Press** (c. 1450, Gutenberg): Made books — including the Bible — widely available. A necessary condition for the Reformation.

## Open Questions

- Why did Buddhism, which arose in India, largely disappear from its homeland (c. 13th century) while flourishing elsewhere?
- How should we assess the role of violence in the spread of Christianity and Islam — was it essential or incidental?
- Is the concept of a "medieval" period useful for non-European histories, or is it an imposition of Western periodization?
- The Bhakti and Sufi movements arose independently but share striking similarities — are these convergent or connected?
