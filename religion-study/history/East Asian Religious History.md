# East Asian Religious History: China, Japan, and Korea

> The religious history of East Asia is distinct from the Abrahamic and Indian worlds — defined not by exclusive allegiance but by **synthesis and coexistence**. In China, the "Three Teachings" (*San Jiao*) — Confucianism, Daoism, and Buddhism — intertwined for two millennia. In Japan, Shintō and Buddhism cohabitated so thoroughly that a separate term (*shinbutsu-shūgō*) describes their fusion. Korea's trajectory was shaped by its role as a bridge between China and Japan.

---

## Part I: China — The Three Teachings

### The Indigenous Foundations (Pre‑Axial)

Before the Axial Age, Chinese religion centred on:

- **Ancestor worship**: The living maintained the ancestors' spirits through offerings — in return, the ancestors guided and protected the family
- **Divination**: Oracle bones (Shang dynasty, c. 1600–1046 BCE) — questions carved on turtle shells and ox bones, heated until they cracked, interpreted as answers from the spirits
- **Shangdi** ("High God") and later **Tian** ("Heaven"): a supreme power that legitimised the ruler's "Mandate of Heaven"

### The Axial Age: Confucianism and Daoism (c. 6th–3rd Century BCE)

The chaos of the Warring States period (403–221 BCE) generated a burst of philosophical creativity — the **Hundred Schools of Thought**.

**Confucianism** (Kǒngzǐ / Confucius, 551–479 BCE):
- Humans are perfectible through ritual (*lǐ*), humaneness (*rén*), filial piety (*xiào*), and education
- The ideal society is a **hierarchy of mutual obligation** — ruler/minister, parent/child, husband/wife, elder/younger, friend/friend
- The *Analects* became the core text of China's governing philosophy for 2,000 years
- Key later figures: **Mencius** (Mèngzǐ, 4th c. BCE — human nature is good) and **Xunzi** (Xúnzǐ, 3rd c. BCE — human nature is bad; only ritual and law can correct it)

**Daoism** (Lǎozǐ / attributed author of the *Dào Dé Jīng*, 6th c. BCE?):
- The *Dào* (the Way) is the nameless source of all existence — "the Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao"
- **Wuwei** ("non‑action"): acting spontaneously, in harmony with nature, without force or contrivance
- **Zhuāngzǐ** (4th c. BCE): humorous, paradoxical stories undercutting fixed categories and celebrating freedom
- **Later, religious Daoism** (2nd c. CE onward) developed a pantheon of gods, alchemical practices, and the quest for physical immortality — very different from the philosophical Daoism of Lǎozǐ and Zhuāngzǐ

### The Arrival of Buddhism (c. 1st Century CE Onward)

Buddhism entered China via the Silk Road around the 1st century CE. Initially, it was "translated" into Daoist terms — a process called *géyì* (matching meanings):

- *Nirvāṇa* was equated with *wuwei*
- *Dharma* was rendered as *dao*
- The Buddha was imagined as a Daoist immortal

Over centuries, Buddhism became thoroughly Chinese. See [[Buddhist History]] for the full story.

### The Three Teachings in Tension and Harmony

| Period | Dominant Tradition | Key Development |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Han dynasty** (206 BCE–220 CE) | Confucianism as state ideology | Civil service exams based on the Confucian classics |
| **Six Dynasties** (220–589) | Buddhism and Daoism flourish | Monasteries gain wealth and influence; Daoism organises into a religion |
| **Tang dynasty** (618–907) | Buddhism at its peak — state patronage | Grand translation projects; pilgrimage (Xuanzang); persecution (845) |
| **Song dynasty** (960–1279) | **Neo‑Confucianism** (Zhu Xi) | Synthesises Confucian ethics, Daoist metaphysics, and Buddhist psychology |
| **Yuan–Ming–Qing** (1279–1912) | Three Teachings integrated | The *sānjiào* idea is explicit — "Confucianism for government, Daoism for health, Buddhism for death" |

### Neo‑Confucianism

The most important intellectual development in later Chinese history. **Zhu Xī** (1130–1200 CE) created a comprehensive system:

- **Li** (principle) — the rational pattern behind all things
- **Qi** (material force) — the psycho‑physical stuff of existence
- Humans can become fully realised (*chéng*, "sincerity") by investigating things and quiet‑sitting (meditation)

Neo‑Confucianism became the orthodox state ideology for the next 600 years — from Song China to Joseon Korea to Tokugawa Japan.

## Part II: Japan — Kami and Buddha

### Shintō — The Indigenous Tradition

**Shintō** ("the way of the *kami*") is Japan's indigenous religion — a diffuse, decentralised tradition focused on:

- **Kami**: spirits of nature, ancestors, and extraordinary beings (not "gods" in the Western sense — they are ambiguous, capricious, and closer to animistic spirits)
- **Purity**: Ritual purification (*misogi*, *harae*) to remove pollution (*kegare*)
- **Sacred sites**: Natural features — mountains (Fuji), rocks, waterfalls, groves — marked by torii gates
- **No founder, no scripture, no doctrine**: Shintō is defined by practice, not belief

### The Arrival of Buddhism (6th Century CE)

Buddhism arrived in Japan from Korea in **538 CE** (traditional date). The Soga clan championed it; the imperial court adopted it.

The result was not replacement but **fusion**:

- *Shinbutsu‑shūgō*: the syncretic blending of Shintō *kami* and Buddhist *bodhisattvas*
- The *kami* were understood as local manifestations of Buddhist deities (the *honji‑suijaku* theory — Buddhas are the "original ground," *kami* are the "trace manifestations")
- Shintō shrines stood within Buddhist temple complexes; priests served both

### The Kamakura Schools (12th–13th Century)

A period of political crisis produced new, accessible Buddhist movements:

| School | Founder | Core Practice |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| **Jōdo (Pure Land)** | Hōnen (1133–1212) | *Nembutsu* — chanting "Namu Amida Butsu" for rebirth in the Pure Land |
| **Jōdo Shinshū** | Shinran (1173–1263) | Faith alone — one recitation with true faith is enough |
| **Nichiren** | Nichiren (1222–1282) | Chanting "Namu Myōhō Renge Kyō" — devotion to the Lotus Sūtra |
| **Sōtō Zen** | Dōgen (1200–1253) | *Zazen* (seated meditation) — "just sitting" |
| **Rinzai Zen** | Eisai (1141–1215) | *Kōan* practice — paradoxical riddles to break the rational mind |

### The Tokugawa Period (1603–1868)

- **Christianity suppressed** (after the Shimabara Rebellion, 1637‑38); Japan closed to the outside world
- The **danka system**: every household was registered with a Buddhist temple — not for belief but for state surveillance. Religion became a tool of social control
- **National learning (*kokugaku*)**: Scholars (Motoori Norinaga, 1730–1801) rediscovered Shintō and Japan's indigenous traditions, partly to counter Chinese (Confucian/Buddhist) influence

### Meiji Restoration (1868) and State Shintō

- The new Meiji government separated Buddhism from Shintō by force (*shinbutsu bunri* — 1868)
- **State Shintō**: Shintō was re‑invented as a non‑religious civic cult; the emperor was declared divine
- Buddhism was persecuted (temples destroyed, monks defrocked) — the cost of centuries of state‑aligned Buddhism
- The **Pacific War** (1937–45) was fought in part under State Shintō ideology
- **1945**: The Allied occupation disestablished State Shintō; the emperor renounced his divinity

### Post‑War Japan

- Religion in Japan is fluid — most people participate in both Shintō (births, weddings, festivals) and Buddhism (funerals, ancestor rites) without feeling any contradiction
- **New religions**: Sōka Gakkai (based on Nichiren's Lotus Sūtra) — one of the largest lay Buddhist movements in the world
- **Secularisation**: Formal religious affiliation is low, but religious *practice* (visiting shrines, ancestor offerings) remains high

## Part III: Korea — The Confucian Transformation

### Early Buddhism

Buddhism arrived in Korea in the **4th century CE** (from China). It became the **state religion**:

- **Goguryeo** (372), **Baekje** (384), **Silla** (527): Each kingdom adopted Buddhism
- **Unified Silla** (668–935) and **Goryeo** (918–1392): Buddhism under state patronage — the *Tripitaka Koreana* (carved 81,258 woodblocks) was a Goryeo project

### The Joseon Dynasty (1392–1910): Confucian Hegemony

The Joseon dynasty replaced Goryeo and established **Neo‑Confucianism** as the sole orthodox ideology:

- Buddhism was actively **suppressed**: monks were pushed into the mountains; temples were stripped of land and power
- Confucian rituals (ancestral rites, the *seonggyungwan* shrine) became the centre of family and state life
- The civil service exam was based on the Confucian classics — as in China, ambition flowed through Confucian channels

### Modern Korea

- **Japanese colonial period** (1910–45): Buddhism was restructured under Japanese control; tensions between married (Japanese‑style) and celibate monks
- **Post‑1945**: Both North and South Korea suppressed organised religion — North Korea through state atheism; South Korea through secularisation and the explosive growth of Christianity (now ~30% of the population, unusually high for East Asia)
- **Buddhist revival**: The Jogye Order (celibate, Seon/Zen) is the largest school; Buddhism remains the second‑largest religion in South Korea

## Part IV: Common Threads Across East Asia

| Theme | China | Japan | Korea |
|-------|-------|-------|-------|
| **Indigenous base** | Confucianism + Daoist folk religion | Shintō | Shamanic tradition + Confucian ritual |
| **Buddhist arrival** | 1st c. CE (Silk Road) | 6th c. CE (via Korea) | 4th c. CE (via China) |
| **State ideology** | Confucian exam system (from Han) | Buddhist / Shintō alternating | Neo‑Confucian (Joseon) |
| **Clerical celibacy** | Always respected | Married clergy (Jōdo Shinshū, post‑Meiji) | Controversy under Japanese rule |
| **Foreign religions** | Christianity limited (persecuted under Qing) | Christian minority (2%) — "hidden Christians" under Tokugawa | Explosive Christian growth (30%) |
| **Modern condition** | Officially atheist; revival of Buddhism and folk religion | Pluralistic; low affiliation, high practice | Christian + Buddhist + "no religion" |
