# The Reformation and Early Modern Religion (1500–1800 CE)

> The Reformation shattered a thousand years of Christian unity in the West, triggering wars, reform, and a new plurality — while elsewhere, the Gunpowder Empires (Ottoman, Safavid, Mughal) shaped Islamic civilisation, and Europe's colonial expansion rewrote the global religious map.

---

## Part I: The Protestant Reformation (1517–c. 1650)

### Background

By 1500, the Catholic Church was Europe's dominant institution — but deeply troubled:

- **Corruption**: Simony (buying church offices), absentee bishops, the sale of indulgences
- **Papal authority challenged**: Conciliarism (councils > pope), national monarchies resisting papal interference
- **The Printing Press** (c. 1450): Made the Bible, pamphlets, and vernacular theology widely available — a necessary condition for the Reformation
- **Humanism**: Erasmus (1466–1536) and the *philosophia Christi* — a return to the sources (*ad fontes*), including the Greek New Testament

### Martin Luther (1483–1546)

An Augustinian monk and professor of theology at Wittenberg. His **95 Theses** (31 October 1517) — against the sale of indulgences — triggered a chain reaction that broke Western Christendom.

| Luther's Core Ideas | Catholic Counter‑position |
|---------------------|--------------------------|
| **Sola fide** — faith alone saves | Faith + works |
| **Sola scriptura** — scripture is the sole authority | Scripture + Tradition + Papal authority |
| **Priesthood of all believers** | Sacerdotal priesthood |
| **Two sacraments only** (Baptism, Eucharist) | Seven sacraments |
| **Consubstantiation** (Christ is "in, with, and under" the bread) | Transubstantiation (bread becomes body) |
| Rejection of monastic vows, clerical celibacy | Monastic and clerical discipline |

### The Spread of Protestantism

| Branch | Founder / Key Figure | Region | Distinctive Feature |
|--------|---------------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Lutheran** | Martin Luther (1517) | Germany, Scandinavia | State‑church model; liturgy retained |
| **Reformed / Calvinist** | John Calvin (Geneva, 1536) | Switzerland, France (Huguenots), Netherlands, Scotland (John Knox) | Predestination; iconoclasm; presbyterian governance |
| **Anabaptist** | (Various, 1520s) | Switzerland, Germany, Netherlands | Adult baptism; pacifism; separation of church and state; ancestors of Mennonites, Amish |
| **Anglican** | Henry VIII (1534) | England | Royal supremacy; via media — Catholic structure, Protestant theology |

### The Catholic Counter‑Reformation

The Catholic Church responded with reform and reaffirmation:

- **Council of Trent** (1545–1563): Reaffirmed all seven sacraments, transubstantiation, and the authority of the Pope; mandated better clerical education; banned the sale of indulgences
- **Jesuits** (Society of Jesus, founded 1540 by Ignatius of Loyola): An elite, educated missionary order that spread Catholicism to Asia, Africa, and the Americas — and staffed the best schools in Europe
- **Index of Prohibited Books** and the **Roman Inquisition**: Tools of censorship and enforcement

### The Wars of Religion

| Conflict | Date | Parties | Outcome |
|----------|------|---------|---------|
| German Peasants' War | 1524–25 | Peasants vs. princes (Luther sided with the princes) | Tens of thousands killed |
| French Wars of Religion | 1562–98 | Catholics vs. Huguenots | Edict of Nantes (1598) — limited tolerance |
| Eighty Years' War | 1568–1648 | Dutch Revolt vs. Spain | Dutch Republic independent; Calvinist |
| Thirty Years' War | 1618–48 | Catholic vs. Protestant states (Holy Roman Empire) | Devastated central Europe; Peace of Westphalia — *cuius regio, eius religio* (the ruler's religion is the region's religion) |

## Part II: Islamic Early Modernity — The Gunpowder Empires

While Europe was fragmenting, the Islamic world was centralising under three great empires:

### The Ottoman Empire (c. 1300–1922)

- **Capital**: Constantinople (Istanbul, after 1453)
- **Religion**: Sunni Islam, with the Sultan as Caliph. The *millet* system: non‑Muslim communities (Orthodox, Jewish, Armenian) had their own courts and leaders
- **Relationship with Europe**: Military rival, trade partner, and cultural exchange — the Ottomans were part of the European balance of power
- **Intellectual life**: A shift from the earlier philosophical openness toward legalistic conservation; the *ulema* (religious scholars) gained power over the state

### The Safavid Empire (1501–1736)

- **Capital**: Isfahan (Persia / Iran)
- **Religion**: Twelver Shī'a Islam as the state religion — a deliberate choice to mark off Safavid territory from the Sunni Ottomans
- **Achievements**: Beautiful architecture (Isfahan's Naqsh‑e Jahan Square); flourishing philosophy (*ḥikmat*, especially Mullā Ṣadrā, 1571–1636); carpet weaving

### The Mughal Empire (1526–1857)

- **Capital**: Agra / Delhi (India)
- **Religion**: Sunni Islamic rule over a majority‑Hindu population
- **Akbar** (r. 1556–1605): The most remarkable religious experiment of the early modern period. He abolished the *jizya* (tax on non‑Muslims), invited Hindus, Christians, Jains, and Zoroastrians to theological debates, and founded his own syncretic cult — the *Dīn‑i Ilāhī* ("Divine Faith") — which died with him
- **Aurangzeb** (r. 1658–1707): Reversed Akbar's tolerance; re‑imposed the *jizya*, destroyed temples, alienated Hindu subjects — contributing to the empire's decline

## Part III: Colonial Encounters and the New World

### The Americas

- **1492**: Columbus's arrival begins the most dramatic religious transformation of the early modern period — the forced Christianisation of the Americas
- **Spanish missions**: Franciscans, Dominicans, and Jesuits converted millions — often by coercion, occasionally by genuine persuasion. Indigenous temples were destroyed; sacred objects were burned
- **Syncretism**: The Virgin of Guadalupe (Mexico, 1531) — a dark‑skinned Virgin Mary who appeared to an indigenous convert, speaking Nahuatl. She became the symbol of Mexican Catholicism, blending the Mother of God with the indigenous earth goddess Tonantzin
- **Protestant North America**: The Pilgrims (1620) and Puritans (1630) sought religious freedom — then often denied it to others

### Asia

- **Jesuits in China**: Matteo Ricci (1552–1610) — a Jesuit who mastered Chinese language and Confucian classics. He dressed as a Confucian scholar and argued that Christianity *fulfilled* Confucianism, not replaced it. The **Rites Controversy** (whether ancestor worship and Confucian rituals were compatible with Christianity) eventually led the Pope to ban the Jesuit approach (1704, 1742) — a catastrophic Lost Opportunity
- **India**: The Portuguese (Goa, 1510) and later the British (East India Company, 1600, 1757) brought Christianity — but conversions were limited. Hindu‑Muslim coexistence was the norm under Mughal rule

### Africa

- **Slavery**: The trans‑Atlantic slave trade (15th–19th c.) transported millions of Africans to the Americas — their religious traditions (Yoruba, Igbo, Akan, Kongo) mixed with Christianity to produce new Afro‑Atlantic religions: **Santería** (Cuba), **Candomblé** (Brazil), **Vodou** (Haiti)
- **Islam in West Africa**: Continued to expand through trade and Sufi brotherhoods

## Part IV: The Scientific Revolution and the Challenge to Religion

The early modern period also saw the birth of modern science:

| Figure | Discovery / Idea | Challenge to Religion |
|--------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Copernicus** (1473–1543) | Heliocentric model | Earth is not the centre of creation |
| **Galileo** (1564–1642) | Telescope evidence for Copernicus | Forced to recant (1633); symbol of science vs. Church |
| **Newton** (1643–1727) | Universal gravitation; mechanical universe | The "clockmaker" God — deism |
| **Descartes** (1596–1650) | Rationalist epistemology | "I think, therefore I am" — knowledge founded on reason, not authority |

The **Scientific Revolution** did not destroy religion — most early scientists were devout — but it shifted the ground: the universe was now a *mechanical system* governed by laws, not a living drama of sin and salvation.

---

## Part V: Key Legacy of the Period

1. **The fragmentation of Western Christianity** — permanent, irreversible, and the precondition for the modern secular state
2. **The nation‑state** replaces the Church as the supreme political authority — the Peace of Westphalia (1648) is the symbolic birth of the modern state system
3. **Global Christianity**: From a European religion to a truly global one — through colonialism, missions, and the slave trade
4. **Pluralism begins**: Not yet the pluralism of equal rights, but the end of the assumption that one religion can enforce uniformity
5. **Science and religion begin to separate** — a relationship that will sharpen in the 19th century
