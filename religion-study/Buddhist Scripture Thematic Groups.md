# Buddhist Scripture Thematic Groups

A reference guide to the major thematic groups that run through the vast corpus of Buddhist scripture — from the Pāli Canon (Tipiṭaka, c. 1st c. BCE) through the Mahāyāna sūtras (c. 1st c. BCE–5th c. CE) to the Vajrayāna tantras (c. 6th–12th c. CE). Each group links texts that share a common subject, doctrinal theme, or narrative pattern. Use this note to navigate Buddhist scripture by theme and to see how different texts — separated by centuries and schools — engage the same questions.

> [!note] How the groups were built
> The major scriptures of the Buddhist tradition (the Pāli Tipiṭaka, Mahāyāna sūtras, and Vajrayāna tantras) were grouped by their dominant doctrinal themes, narrative function, and literary genre. Many texts belong to multiple groups — for example, the Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta (SN 56.11) is simultaneously a Four Noble Truths text, a biography of the Buddha's first teaching, a Middle Way text, and the foundation of the Noble Eightfold Path. The groups below consolidate these into navigable themes. Cross-references to the [[Buddhist Stories MOC|Buddhist Stories MOC]] and [[Buddhist Figures MOC|Buddhist Figures MOC]] link thematic groups to the narrative and figure traditions.

## 1. The Life of the Buddha

The biographical narrative of Siddhattha Gotama — from his celestial vow under Dīpaṅkara Buddha, through his final birth as Prince Siddhattha, his renunciation, awakening, forty-five-year ministry, and final parinirvāṇa. No single text tells the complete story; it is pieced together from suttas, Vinaya narratives, and later chronicles.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Mahāpadāna Sutta** (DN 14) | The template: Vipassī Buddha's life as a blueprint for Gotama's — the identical pattern of birth, renunciation, awakening, and teaching |
| **Acchariyabbhutadhamma Sutta** (MN 123) | Ānanda describes the miraculous events surrounding the Bodhisatta's birth — the last birth |
| **Ariyapariyesanā Sutta** (MN 26) | The most complete autobiographical account: the Four Sights, the Great Renunciation, the search with Āḷāra Kālāma and Uddaka Rāmaputta |
| **Mahāsaccaka Sutta** (MN 36) | The ascetic years, the extremes of self-mortification, the discovery of the Middle Way, the Enlightenment |
| **Mahāparinibbāna Sutta** (DN 16) | The final year — the Buddha's last journey, illness, passing, and funeral; the longest single sutta in the Pāli Canon |
| **Mahāvagga** (Vinaya) | The first sixty days after awakening: the first teaching, conversions of Yasa and the three Kassapa brothers, the Saṅgha established |
| **Nidānakathā** (Jātaka commentary) | The late biographical tradition: the twenty-four past Buddhas, the vow under Dīpaṅkara, the descent from Tusita, the full life narrative |
| **Buddhavaṃsa** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | The lives of the 24 previous Buddhas and our Buddha Gotama — the lineage of awakening |
| **Lalitavistara Sūtra** (Mahāyāna) | The expanded, miraculous Mahāyāna biography: the Bodhisattva teaching the gods in Tusita, entering the womb as a white elephant, the birth, the youthful sports |

**Core texts:** MN 26 (autobiographical), DN 16 (the final year), Mahāvagga (the ministry), Nidānakathā (the complete late biography)

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → The Birth of the Bodhisatta, The Four Sights, The Great Renunciation, The Enlightenment, The Buddha's Parinirvāṇa; [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → The Buddha

## 2. The Four Noble Truths & The Noble Eightfold Path

The foundational teaching of Buddhism — the *ariya-sacca* (noble truths) that diagnose the human condition and prescribe the path to its cure. This is the single most important doctrinal framework in all Buddhist schools.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta** (SN 56.11) | The first sermon — the Buddha's premiere teaching: the Middle Way, the Four Noble Truths, the Noble Eightfold Path. "The Eye of Dhamma arose" |
| **Sammādiṭṭhi Sutta** (MN 9) | Sāriputta's detailed exposition of Right View: what each noble truth means, with all its implications for karma, rebirth, and the āsavas (taints) |
| **Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna Sutta** (DN 22) | The most complete practical exposition: each noble truth is linked to the practice of mindfulness — suffering is to be understood, its origin abandoned, its cessation realised, the path developed |
| **Saccavibhaṅga Sutta** (MN 141) | Sāriputta's analytical breakdown: each factor of the path defined, each truth identified with its specific function |
| **Abhidhamma analysis** (Dhammasaṅgaṇī) | The Four Noble Truths mapped to ultimate reality: all conditioned dhammas are classified under the first two truths; nibbāna is the third; magga (path) is the fourth |
| **Visuddhimagga** (Buddhaghosa, ch. 16–22) | The definitive commentarial treatment: each truth linked to a task (pariññā, pahāna, sacchikiriyā, bhāvanā), each path factor detailed |

**Core teaching:** The First Sermon — SN 56.11

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → The First Sermon (Dhammacakkappavattana)

## 3. Dependent Origination (Paṭiccasamuppāda)

The Buddha's most profound discovery: the twelve-link chain of causation that explains how suffering arises and how it ceases. "He who sees dependent origination sees the Dhamma; he who sees the Dhamma sees dependent origination."

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Mahānidāna Sutta** (DN 15) | The longest and most detailed sutta on dependent origination — ten links (the standard twelve minus avijjā and saṅkhārā), with Ānanda's statement "It is clear, Lord" criticised as shallow understanding |
| **Paṭiccasamuppāda Vibhaṅga Sutta** (SN 12.2) | The twelve links defined — the standard formula with each factor explained |
| **Mahāpadāna Sutta** (DN 14) | Vipassī Buddha's discovery of dependent origination, the template for all Buddhas |
| **Kaccānagotta Sutta** (SN 12.15) | Avoiding eternalism and annihilationism — "Everything exists" is one extreme, "Nothing exists" is the other; dependent origination is the Middle Way |
| **Abhidhamma** (Paṭṭhāna) | The twenty-four conditional relations (*paccaya*) — the complete taxonomy of causal interdependence, far beyond the twelve-link chain |
| **Mūlamadhyamakakārikā** (Nāgārjuna) | The Mahāyāna re-reading: all dhammas are empty of intrinsic existence *because* they arise dependently — "Whatever arises dependently is emptiness" |

**Core text:** DN 15 (Mahānidāna Sutta) — the definitive sutta

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → The Enlightenment Under the Bodhi Tree

## 4. Karma & Rebirth

The law of moral causation — intentional actions (*kamma*) ripen in this life or a future one, determining the realm and quality of rebirth. Unlike theistic traditions, karma operates impersonally, without a divine judge.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Cūḷakammavibhaṅga Sutta** (MN 135) | The Buddha's answer to a Brahmin on why humans are born short-lived or long-lived, healthy or sick, rich or poor, high or low caste — "Beings are the owners of their actions" |
| **Mahākammavibhaṅga Sutta** (MN 136) | The advanced analysis: the complexity of karmic fruition across multiple lifetimes, the impossibility of simplistic one-to-one mapping |
| **Devadūta Sutta** (MN 130) | The "divine messengers": old age, sickness, and death — the torments of the hells described in vivid detail as consequences of evil deeds |
| **Lakkhaṇa Sutta** (DN 30) | The thirty-two marks of a Great Man explained as the *kammic* result of specific good deeds in past lives |
| **Petavatthu** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | Stories of hungry ghosts (*petas*) — the fate of those who died with greed, miserliness, or wrong views; the most graphic karmic retribution literature in the Pāli Canon |
| **Vimānavatthu** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | The celestial mansions of the reborn — stories of those who were reborn in heavenly realms through generosity and faith |
| **Abhidhamma** | Kamma as a *rūpa* (material quality): kamma-born matter, the classification of kamma by function (productive, supportive, obstructive, destructive) |

**Core text:** MN 135 (Cūḷakammavibhaṅga Sutta) — the simplest introduction

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → Kisā Gotamī and the Mustard Seed; [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → The Buddha

## 5. The Three Marks of Existence (Tilakkhaṇa)

The three characteristics that mark all conditioned phenomena: *anicca* (impermanence), *dukkha* (suffering/unsatisfactoriness), *anattā* (not-self). To see these directly is to become enlightened.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Anattalakkhaṇa Sutta** (SN 22.59) | The second sermon — the five aggregates (*khandha*) are not-self: "Form is not-self… feeling is not-self… Therefore, any kind of form whatever… should be seen as it actually is with right wisdom: 'This is not mine, this I am not, this is not my self'" — the five ascetics become arahants upon hearing it |
| **Ādittapariyāya Sutta** (SN 35.28) | "The Fire Sermon" — all the senses and their objects are burning with the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion — three marks made visceral |
| **Phena Sutta** (SN 22.95) | The aggregates as foam, a bubble, a mirage, a hollow log, a magic show — the most poetic expression of the three marks |
| **Salla Sutta** (SN 36.6) | The two darts: physical pain (inevitable) and mental anguish (avoidable) — the three marks applied to the experience of feeling |
| **Vipallāsa Sutta** (AN 4.49) | The four distortions of perception: taking what is impermanent as permanent, what is suffering as happiness, what is not-self as self, what is impure as pure |
| **Dhammapada** (ch. 20, 21) | "All conditioned things are impermanent" (20.277); "All conditioned things are unsatisfactory" (20.278); "All dhammas are not-self" (20.279) — the three most-quoted verses in Buddhism |
| **Visuddhimagga** (ch. 20–21) | The classic commentarial expositions: the *kalāpa* analysis of radical impermanence, the seventeen types of insight knowledge leading to nibbāna |

**Core verses:** Dhammapada 277, 278, 279

**See also:** [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → The Buddha (the second sermon at the Deer Park)

## 6. Nibbāna — The Unconditioned

The third noble truth — the cessation of suffering, the unconditioned (*asaṅkhata*). Nibbāna is described in the suttas by paradox (what it is *not*) and by simile (what it is *like*).

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Nibbāna Suttas** (AN 6.55, AN 9.33–36) | Various suttas on nibbāna: "There is, monks, an unborn, unbecome, unmade, unconditioned…" — the unconditioned as the beyond of all conditioned existence |
| **Āneñja Sutta** (MN 106) | The path to the imperturbable — the meditative progression from the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception to the cessation of perception and feeling |
| **Milindapañha** (Questions of King Milinda) | "Is nibbāna a place? Is it a state? Can it be described?" — Nāgasena's famous similes: the immovable, the island, the shelter, the refuge |
| **Kevaddha Sutta** (DN 11) | The Buddha's famous refusal to answer where the four great elements cease — "Consciousness, not manifesting, infinite, luminous all round" — a rare positive hint at nibbāna |
| **Māgandiya Sutta** (MN 75) | The Buddha describes nibbāna to the hedonist Māgandiya: "When the eye sees a form and there is no desire, no passion… that is peace" |
| **Sāriputta's Aṭṭhakathā** (commentarial) | Nibbāna as *dhamma* — a reality that is not mind nor matter but is knowable through the supramundane path consciousness |
| **Aṅgulimāla Sutta** (MN 86) | The transformation of a serial killer into a peaceful arahant — nibbāna as the power of forgiveness and transformation |

**Core description:** AN 6.55 — "There is, monks, an unborn…"

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → Aṅgulimāla's Conversion, The Buddha's Parinirvāṇa

## 7. The Vinaya — Monastic Law & the Saṅgha

The rule of life for the monastic community — the oldest surviving monastic code in the world, still used today. The Vinaya Piṭaka preserves not just rules, but the narratives of why each rule was laid down, giving a vivid picture of early Buddhist social life.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Pāṭimokkha** (Sutta Vibhaṅga) | The 227 rules for bhikkhus (311 for bhikkhunīs), classified by gravity: *pārājika* (defeat, expulsion), *saṅghādisesa* (formal meeting), *pācittiya* (confession), etc. |
| **Mahāvagga** (Khandhaka) | The history of the early Saṅgha: the first teaching, the first ordinations, the major monastic procedures (*uposatha*, *vassa*, *kaṭhina*) |
| **Cullavagga** (Khandhaka) | The later monastic regulations: the first and second councils, the nuns' ordination, the settlement of disputes |
| **Bhikkhunī Vibhaṅga** | The rules specific to nuns: the eight *garudhammas* (heavy rules), the dual Saṅgha ordination requirement |
| **Khandhaka** (various) | The *nissaya* (requisites): robes, almsfood, lodging, medicine — the material culture of early Buddhism |
| **Vinīta Vatthu** | Case law — precedents from the Buddha's own decisions: "Monks, I allow you to do thus" |
| **Tibetan Vinaya** (Mūlasarvāstivāda) | The 253 rules of the Tibetan monastic tradition, with a different arrangement and additional narrative traditions |

**Core text:** Mahāvagga — the living constitution of the Saṅgha

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → Mahāprajāpatī's Ordination, Devadatta's Schism and Fall, The First Buddhist Council; [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → Upāli (foremost in Vinaya), Mahākassapa

## 8. Jātaka Tales — Previous Lives of the Buddha

The 550+ stories of the Bodhisatta's past lives — as a king, a merchant, a monkey, a rabbit, an elephant, a tree spirit, a god — perfecting the ten *pāramīs* (perfections) over countless lifetimes. The most popular Buddhist genre across all traditions.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Jātaka** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | 547 birth stories with verses and prose commentary. Each story has three layers: the past life (the Bodhisatta), the present life (the Buddha gives the story), and the identification (who was who in the past) |
| **Cariyāpiṭaka** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | 35 verse summaries of past lives organised by the ten perfections — a condensed anthology of the Jātaka tradition |
| **Jātaka-mālā** (Āryaśūra, Mahāyāna) | 34 Jātaka stories in elegant Sanskrit verse — the literary masterpiece of the genre, emphasising the Bodhisattva's compassion |
| **Mahāvastu** (Mahāsāṅghika-Lokottaravāda) | A massive collection of Jātakas and Avadānas from the *Mahāvastu* tradition — the Bodhisatta's career from his vow under Dīpaṅkara |
| **Jātaka Aṭṭhakathā** | The Pāli commentary that preserves the full narrative of each story — the most widely transmitted version in Theravāda traditions |
| **Vessantara Jātaka** (Jātaka 547) | The last and most important birth: Prince Vessantara who perfected giving (*dāna*) by giving away everything, including his wife and children — the most popular story in Theravāda Buddhism |

**Core story:** The Vessantara Jātaka — the paradigm of the perfect generosity (*dāna pāramī*)

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] (Jātaka section); [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → The Buddha (as the Bodhisatta in past lives)

## 9. Meditation & Mindfulness (Bhāvanā)

The systematic training of the mind — from the foundational practices of mindfulness of breath and body to the deep states of *jhāna* (absorption) and the insight knowledges leading to enlightenment. This is the heart of Buddhist practice.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta** (MN 10) | The four foundations of mindfulness: body, feelings, mind, mind-objects — "The one-way path for the purification of beings… for the realisation of nibbāna" |
| **Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna Sutta** (DN 22) | The expanded version of MN 10, with the Four Noble Truths appended — the most complete single meditation text in the Pāli Canon |
| **Ānāpānasati Sutta** (MN 118) | Mindfulness of breathing — sixteen steps from simple breath awareness to the full awakening: "When mindfulness of breathing is developed and cultivated, it fulfills the four foundations of mindfulness" |
| **Kāyagatāsati Sutta** (MN 119) | Mindfulness of the body — from breath awareness through the thirty-two parts of the body to the contemplations of the charnel ground |
| **Sāmaññaphala Sutta** (DN 2) | The progressive training of a monk — from virtue (*sīla*) through sense-restraint, mindfulness, the jhānas, to the three knowledges. The best single overview of the Buddhist path |
| **Girimānanda Sutta** (AN 10.60) | Ten meditation subjects: perception of impermanence, not-self, foulness, danger, abandoning, dispassion, cessation, all-world dissatisfaction, the impermanence of all formations, and mindfulness of breathing |
| **Visuddhimagga** (Buddhaghosa, ch. 3–11) | The encyclopedia of meditation: 40 subjects of meditation (*kammaṭṭhāna*), detailed instructions for each jhāna, the five hindrances, the ten kasinas, the divine abidings |
| **Yoga Sūtras parallel** | Notable: the Patañjali Yoga Sūtras' eight limbs share deep structural parallels with the Buddhist path (sīla → yama/niyama, samādhi → dhāraṇā/dhyāna/samādhi) |

**Core text:** MN 10 (Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta) — the foundational meditation discourse

**See also:** [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → The Buddha, Sāriputta, Moggallāna (arising from meditation)

## 10. The Bodhisatta Path & Mahāyāna

The Mahāyāna turn: the ideal of the Bodhisattva — one who postpones personal liberation to save all beings. The perfections (*pāramitās*), the emptiness (*śūnyatā*) of all phenomena, the skill-in-means (*upāya*) of the bodhisattva.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Aṣṭasāhasrikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra** (c. 1st c. BCE) | The earliest Mahāyāna sūtra: "Form is emptiness, emptiness is form." The perfection of wisdom, the ten stages (*bhūmis*), the bodhisattva's non-attachment to all views |
| **Śatasāhasrikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra** | The expanded version (100,000 lines) — the definitive Mahāyāna statement on śūnyatā |
| **Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra** ("Diamond Sūtra") | "Thus shall you think of all this fleeting world: A star at dawn, a bubble in a stream… A flash of lightning in a summer cloud." The most famous short prajñāpāramitā text |
| **Hṛdaya Sūtra ("Heart Sūtra")** | The shortest and most chanted: "Form is empty; emptiness is form. No eye, no ear, no nose… no path, no wisdom, no attainment." Buddhism's most famous single page |
| **Saddharmapuṇḍarīka Sūtra (Lotus Sūtra)** | The definitive Mahāyāna text: the one vehicle (*ekayāna*), all beings destined for Buddhahood, the Bodhisattva as the true ideal, the parable of the burning house, the skill-in-means of all Buddhas |
| **Gaṇḍavyūha Sūtra** (Avataṃsaka collection) | The youth Sudhana's pilgrimage to fifty-three teachers — the final teaching: "Mind is the painter; it paints all worlds." The Mahāyāna vision of interpenetration |
| **Bodhicaryāvatāra** (Śāntideva, 8th c.) | The classic Mahāyāna manual: "For as long as space endures, for as long as beings remain, may I too remain to dispel the suffering of all beings" — the prayer of the bodhisattva |
| **Mūlamadhyamakakārikā** (Nāgārjuna) | The philosophical foundation of Mahāyāna: all things are empty, all things are conventional, nibbāna is saṃsāra seen correctly |
| **Ten Stages Sūtra (Daśabhūmika Sūtra)** | The ten bhūmis of the Bodhisattva path — from the joyful (pramuditā) to the cloud of dharma (dharmameghā) |

**Core text:** Hṛdaya Sūtra (the most chanted); Bodhicaryāvatāra (the most loved)

**See also:** [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → The Buddha (as the primordial Bodhisattva)

## 11. Supernatural Powers (Iddhi) & Celestial Beings

Psychic powers, celestial realms, Māra the tempter, and the cosmology of invisible beings — the "supernatural" dimension of the Buddhist universe, which the Buddha treated as real but warned was a distraction from the path.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Sāmaññaphala Sutta** (DN 2) | The standard list of psychic powers: one becomes many, walks through walls, flies through the sky, hears sounds far away, reads minds, remembers past lives, sees beings passing away and being reborn |
| **Iddhipāda Saṃyutta** (SN 51) | The four bases of psychic power: concentration on intention, energy, mind, and investigation — the actual meditative basis for iddhi |
| **Mahāmoggallāna stories** (various suttas) | The Buddha's disciple foremost in psychic powers travels to the heavens and hells, visits the hungry ghosts, battles Māra, shakes the palace of the gods |
| **Mahāsamaya Sutta** (DN 20) | The great gathering of the devas — tens of thousands of deities assemble to hear the Buddha teach. "The gods are not shy; they come gladly" |
| **Āṭānāṭiya Sutta** (DN 32) | A protection sutta: the four Great Kings (Cātummahārājikā devas) teach the Buddha a chant for protection against malevolent beings |
| **Devatāsaṃyutta** (SN 2) | Conversations between the Buddha and various devas — some reverent, some questioning, some challenging. The devas as the Buddha's interlocutors |
| **Māra Saṃyutta** (SN 4) | Māra's repeated attempts to distract and tempt the Buddha — from the Enlightenment onward. Māra as the personification of death, desire, and delusion |
| **Mahāyāna celestial Buddhas** (Sukhāvatīvyūha) | Amitābha's Pure Land, Akṣobhya's Abhirati, Bhaiṣajyaguru's Vaiḍūryanirbhāsa — the entire cosmos becomes a field of awakened beings and pure lands |

**Core text:** Sāmaññaphala Sutta — the classic catalogue; Māra Saṃyutta — the narrative

**See also:** [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → Moggallāna, Māra; [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → The Enlightenment Under the Bodhi Tree

## 12. Women in the Early Saṅgha

The voices of the first Buddhist women: the *therīs* (elder nuns) who achieved awakening against the patriarchal currents of ancient India. The Therīgāthā is the oldest collection of women's religious poetry in any tradition.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Therīgāthā** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | 73 poems by 100 nuns — the earliest known Buddhist women's literature. "I was a wanderer, homeless, childless, friendless… Now I am free!" — Subhā's song of liberation |
| **Therī Apadāna** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | The legends of the nun arahants — their past-life aspirations and their final births as awakened women |
| **Gotamī Sutta** (AN 8.51 / Cullavagga X) | Mahāprajāpatī Gotamī's request for ordination: Ānanda's intervention, the eight *garudhammas*, and the establishment of the Bhikkhunī Saṅgha — the foundational narrative for Buddhist nuns |
| **Mahāprajāpatī's verses** (Therīgāthā 157–162) | The first nun's verses after her awakening — "Buddha, hero, homage to you! You have freed me from all bonds" |
| **Kisā Gotamī's story** (Dhammapada Aṭṭhakathā) | The mustard seed parable — the grieving mother who lost her child, learned impermanence, and became an arahant |
| **Paṭācārā's story** (Therīgāthā 112–116) | The woman who lost her husband, children, and family in a single day — utterly broken, she was healed by the Buddha and became foremost in Vinaya among the nuns |
| **Khemā Sutta** (SN 44.1) | Khemā the nun, foremost in wisdom, confounds King Pasenadi with her teaching on the Tathāgata after death — a woman as the Buddha's equal in debate |
| **Uppalavaṇṇā's story** (Therīgāthā 69–72) | The beautiful nun who was assaulted in her hut — the Buddha's instruction on impermanence and the protection of the Bhikkhunī Saṅgha |
| **Soma Sutta** (SN 5.2) | Māra taunts Soma: "That state (arahantship) is hard to achieve — women with two-finger-wisdom cannot do it." Soma replies: "What difference does gender make when the mind is concentrated?" |

**Core text:** Therīgāthā — the first and greatest collection of women's spiritual poetry

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → Mahāprajāpatī's Ordination, Kisā Gotamī and the Mustard Seed; [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → Mahāprajāpatī Gotamī, Kisā Gotamī, Khemā, Uppalavaṇṇā, Paṭācārā

## 13. Ethics & Lay Practice

The Buddha's teaching for householders — how to live an ethical life without renouncing the world. The Five Precepts (*pañcasīla*), the layperson's duties, and the social ethics of the Dhamma.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Sigālovāda Sutta** (DN 31) | The "householder's Vinaya" — the Buddha's most comprehensive discourse on lay ethics: the six directions (parents, teachers, spouse, friends, servants, ascetics), the six causes of waste, the four types of good friend |
| **Maṅgala Sutta** (Sn 2.4, Khuddakapāṭha) | The 38 highest blessings — from the simple (avoiding fools, respecting elders) to the profound (supporting parents, right livelihood, the path to nibbāna). The most popular lay Buddhist chant |
| **Parābhava Sutta** (Sn 1.6) | "The Downfall" — the things that lead to a layperson's ruin: loving sleep, laziness, debt, bad company, gambling, drinking, adultery |
| **Vyagghapajja Sutta** (AN 8.54) | The Buddha's advice to the householders Dīghajānu and Vyagghapajja: four sources of happiness — ownership, enjoyment, debtlessness, blamelessness |
| **Dhammika Sutta** (Sn 2.14) | The Buddha distinguishes the layperson's precepts from the monk's: "Do not kill, do not steal, do not lie, avoid alcohol, avoid sexual misconduct — this is the layperson's path" |
| **Ādīya Sutta** (AN 5.41) | Five benefits of wealth: make yourself happy, support your family, share with friends, prepare for emergencies, support ascetics — wealth as a basis for the good life |
| **Vāseṭṭha Sutta** (MN 98) | Not birth but conduct makes one a Brahmin — the Buddha's revolutionary rejection of caste: "By birth one is not an outcast; by deeds one is an outcast" |
| **Karaṇīyamettā Sutta** (Sn 1.8) | The fifteen qualities of a truly ethical person: "Let them be able and upright, gentle and not proud, content and easy to support" — loving-kindness as the layperson's way |

**Core text:** DN 31 (Sigālovāda Sutta) — the householder's Vinaya

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → Kisā Gotamī and the Mustard Seed; [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → Anāthapiṇḍika, Visākhā, King Bimbisāra

## 14. Abhidhamma — Ultimate Reality

The "higher teaching" — the philosophical analysis of ultimate reality (*paramattha dhamma*) into irreducible mental and material phenomena (citta, cetasika, rūpa, nibbāna). The Abhidhamma is Buddhist scholasticism at its most rigorous.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Dhammasaṅgaṇī** (Abhidhamma Piṭaka) | The enumeration of all ultimate dhammas: 52 mental factors (*cetasikas*), 89 (later 121) types of consciousness (*cittas*), 28 types of material phenomena (*rūpas*), and nibbāna |
| **Vibhaṅga** (Abhidhamma Piṭaka) | The analysis — 18 chapters analysing the major sutta teachings (khandhas, āyatanas, dhātu, sacca, indriya, etc.) through the Abhidhamma method |
| **Dhātukathā** (Abhidhamma Piṭaka) | The discourse on elements — how the khandhas, āyatanas, and dhātus interrelate. A matrix of classification |
| **Paṭṭhāna** (Abhidhamma Piṭaka) | The "Great Treatise" — 24 conditional relations (*paccaya*): the most massive text in the Pāli Canon, a complete theory of causal interdependence. "The Paṭṭhāna is the Buddha's omniscience" |
| **Kathāvatthu** (Abhidhamma Piṭaka) | 217 points of controversy debated and resolved — the record of the Third Council (c. 250 BCE). "A person exists only conventionally" — the refutation of personhood |
| **Visuddhimagga** (Buddhaghosa, 5th c.) | Not canonical Abhidhamma, but the encyclopaedic synthesis: the entire Buddhist path from sīla through samādhi to paññā — the single most important non-canonical text in Theravāda |
| **Abhidhammattha-saṅgaha** (Anuruddha, 11th c.) | A concise summary of the entire Abhidhamma — a 50-page handbook used as the introductory text in all Theravāda Abhidhamma studies |

**Core text:** Dhammasaṅgaṇī (the foundation); Paṭṭhāna (the culmination)

**See also:** [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → Sāriputta (traditionally the originator of the Abhidhamma method)

## 15. Pure Land & Devotional Buddhism

The Mahāyāna path of faith: devotion to the Buddha Amitābha (Infinite Light) and rebirth in his Pure Land (Sukhāvatī) — a "pure land" where all conditions are favourable for enlightenment. In East Asia, Pure Land Buddhism became the most widely practised form of Buddhism.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtra (Longer)** (c. 2nd c. CE) | Dharmākara Bodhisattva's 48 vows, the creation of Sukhāvatī, Amitābha's immeasurable life and light — "If I attain Buddhahood and beings in the ten directions sincerely desire to be reborn in my land… may they be reborn" — the foundational Pure Land text |
| **Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtra (Shorter)** | A condensed version: Amitābha's light, the glories of the Pure Land, the simple practice of recollection (*nien-fo / nembutsu*) |
| **Amitāyurdhyāna Sūtra** (c. 5th c.) | Queen Vaidehī's vision: sixteen visualisations of the Pure Land, from the setting sun to the lotus seat — the intermediate-length meditation sūtra |
| **Pratyutpanna Samādhi Sūtra** | The earliest known Pure Land text (1st c. BCE): through sustained meditation on Amitābha, one sees him directly "as if in a mirror" — the practice of "Buddha-recollection" (*buddhānusmṛti*) |
| **Vimalakīrti Nirdeśa Sūtra** | The layman Vimalakīrti — "When the mind is pure, the land is pure." The Pure Land is not a distant place but the present reality of the awakened mind |
| **Shinran's Kyōgyōshinshō** | The Jōdo Shinshū (True Pure Land) reformation: other-power (*tariki*), the single practice of *nembutsu*, the assurance of rebirth through faith alone |

**Core text:** Longer Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtra (the 48 vows)

**See also:** [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → The Buddha (as the primordial awakened being)

## 16. Buddhist Cosmology

The structure of the universe — the 31 planes of existence, the three worlds (*kāma-loka*, *rūpa-loka*, *arūpa-loka*), the cycles of cosmic expansion and contraction (*kappa*). The Buddhist cosmos is vast, multiple, and without a creator.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Abhidhamma Piṭaka** (especially Vibhaṅga) | The complete classification of the 31 planes: four woeful states (hell, animals, hungry ghosts, asuras), seven sense-sphere heavens, the sixteen fine-material heavens, the four immaterial heavens |
| **Mahāsamaya Sutta** (DN 20) | The assembly of the gods from the lowest devas to the highest Brahmās — a divine geography of the cosmos |
| **Sakkapañha Sutta** (DN 21) | Sakka, king of the Tāvatiṃsa heaven, questions the Buddha — the heavenly realm of the Thirty-Three described in detail |
| **Kevaddha Sutta** (DN 11) | A monk wants to know where the elements cease: the Buddha's answer — "Consciousness, without sign, infinite, luminous all round" |
| **Aggi-Vacchagotta Sutta** (MN 72) | The Buddha's silence on cosmology: "Does the world exist forever? Does it not? Is the Tathāgata after death?" — the famous unanswered questions |
| **Brahmajāla Sutta** (DN 1) | 62 philosophical views on the origin of the cosmos and the self — the Buddha's comprehensive taxonomy of wrong views, including eternalism, annihilationism, and partial-eternalism |
| **Loka Sutta** (SN 12.44) | "The world (*loka*) arises, monks, when the six senses and their objects come together in consciousness" — the Buddha redefines "the world" in phenomenological terms |
| **Abhidhammattha-saṅgaha** (ch. 5) | The complete cosmography: kamma as the architect of the planes, the duration of realms, the beings born in each |

**Core text:** Abhidhamma classification; DN 1 (Brahmajāla Sutta) for the philosophical context

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → The First Buddhist Council (set in the human realm); [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → Māra (lord of the highest sense-sphere heaven)

## 17. Conversion & The Growth of the Saṅgha

The stories of how the Dhamma spread — from the first five ascetics at the Deer Park, through the mass conversions of the three Kassapa brothers and their 1,000 followers, to the expansion across the Gangetic plain. Buddhism began as a small community and became a mass movement within the Buddha's lifetime.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Mahāvagga** (Vinaya, Khandhaka 1) | The first year: the conversion of Yasa and his 54 friends, the three Kassapa brothers (1,000 followers), the teaching to King Bimbisāra, the great fire sacrifice stopped |
| **Sāriputta & Moggallāna conversion** (Mahāvagga) | The most famous conversion: Assaji's verse "Of those things that arise from a cause, the Tathāgata has told the cause" — Sāriputta hears it and becomes a Stream-enterer on the spot |
| **Uruvela Kassapa story** (Mahāvagga, Vinaya) | The Buddha tames the Nāga, performs 3,500 miracles, converts the fire-worshipping Kassapa brothers with their 1,000 disciples — the turning point of the early mission |
| **Pabbajjā Sutta** (Sn 3.1) | King Bimbisāra sees the newly ordained monks and exclaims: "These sons of noble families have gone forth — there is nothing small about them" |
| **Bāhiya Sutta** (Ud 1.10) | Bāhiya Dārucīriya, an old ascetic, asks the Buddha to teach him — the Buddha says: "In the seen, only the seen… Then for you, Bāhiya, there will be no 'here,' no 'beyond,' no 'in-between'" — instant arahantship |
| **Subhadda Sutta** (DN 16) | The last conversion: Subhadda the wanderer hears the Buddha's final teaching and becomes the last disciple ordained directly by the Buddha |
| **Milindapañha** | 1st c. BCE — King Milinda (Menander) converts to Buddhism after the debate with Nāgasena; the classic model of Buddhist intellectual conversion |

**Core text:** Mahāvagga — the earliest history of the Saṅgha

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → Aṅgulimāla's Conversion, Mahāprajāpatī's Ordination, The First Buddhist Council; [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → Sāriputta, Moggallāna, Ānanda, Bāhiya

## 18. The Discourses (Sutta Piṭaka)

The five Nikāyas — the collections of the Buddha's discourses, arranged by length, subject, and number. Together they form the largest and oldest collection of Buddhist teachings.

| Collection | Content | Key Features |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Dīgha Nikāya** (34 long discourses) | Major doctrinal and narrative suttas — the Mahāparinibbāna, the Brahmajāla, the Sāmaññaphala, the Sigālovāda, the Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna | The richest narrative content; most philosophical scope; the longest single suttas |
| **Majjhima Nikāya** (152 middle-length discourses) | The most widely studied collection — the Ariyapariyesanā (Going Forth), the Ānāpānasati, the Satipaṭṭhāna, the Cūḷakammavibhaṅga, the Sammādiṭṭhi | The personal, autobiographical Buddha; the sharpest debates with Brahmins and ascetics |
| **Saṃyutta Nikāya** (56 connected collections, 7,762 suttas) | Thematically grouped: karma, causation, the five aggregates, the six senses, dependent origination, the seven factors of enlightenment | The most systematic collection: each Saṃyutta is a doctrinal unit; SN 56 is the Four Noble Truths Saṃyutta |
| **Aṅguttara Nikāya** (9,557 suttas, numbered 1s–11s) | Suttas arranged by number of items — from "one thing" (the mind) to "eleven things" (factors for stream-entry) | The best *reference* collection: find everything the Buddha said on any topic by its number |
| **Khuddaka Nikāya** (15 books, minor collection) | The Dhammapada, Udāna, Itivuttaka, Sutta Nipāta, Theragāthā, Therīgāthā, Jātaka, Buddhavaṃsa, etc. | The most *varied* collection: poetry, songs, birth stories, past Buddhas, the most lyrical voice of the Buddha |

**Core texts:** Majjhima Nikāya (the "middle-length" heart of Buddhism); Dhammapada (the soul of Buddhism)

**See also:** All Buddhist stories and figures in this vault

## 19. Philosophical Debates & Interfaith Dialogue

Buddhism was born in a marketplace of ideas — Brahminical orthodoxy, Jain asceticism, Ājīvika fatalism, and materialist schools all contested with the Buddha and his followers. The suttas preserve these debates as living dialogues.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Brahmajāla Sutta** (DN 1) | 62 philosophical views taxonomised — the Buddha's comprehensive map of all possible wrong views: eternalism, annihilationism, agnosticism, origination-view, and their combinations |
| **Milindapañha** (1st c. BCE) | The debate between King Milinda (Menander, a Greek king) and the elder Nāgasena — the classic defence of Buddhism: "Is the chariot the wheels? The axle? The frame? No — 'chariot' is a mere convention." The most famous Buddhist philosophical work outside the suttas |
| **Pāṭika Sutta** (DN 24) | The debate with the naked ascetic Pāṭikaputta — the Buddha demonstrates his psychic powers and defeats the claim that he has no special knowledge |
| **Mahāli Sutta** (DN 6) | The debate with the Licchavi Mahāli: the Buddha proves the attainments of the jhānas to sceptics |
| **Poṭṭhapāda Sutta** (DN 9) | The debate with the wanderer Poṭṭhapāda on the nature of consciousness and the self — the Buddha's most subtle analysis of perception |
| **Vāseṭṭha Sutta** (MN 98) | The debate on caste: Vāseṭṭha and Bhāradvāja, two Brahmins, ask the Buddha "What makes a Brahmin?" — the Buddha's famous reply: "Not by birth, but by deeds" |
| **Samaṇamuṇḍika Sutta** (MN 78) | The Buddha defines what makes a true ascetic — versus the Jains who defined it by external practices |
| **Cūḷamāluṅkya Sutta** (MN 63) | Māluṅkyaputta asks the unanswered questions (is the world eternal, etc.) — the Buddha's parable of the poisoned arrow: "If you refuse treatment until you know the archer, you die" |
| **Sāmaññaphala Sutta** (DN 2) | King Ajātasattu asks each of the six contemporary teachers for the fruit of the ascetic life — and finds only the Buddha's answer satisfactory. The best survey of the six heterodox schools |

**Core text:** Milindapañha (the masterpiece of Buddhist debate); MN 63 (the poisoned arrow)

**See also:** [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → The Buddha (as the supreme debater), Sāriputta (as the sub-commentator of the Buddha's teachings)

## 20. Protective Texts & Paritta

The chanting protection (*paritta*) tradition — suttas believed to ward off danger, illness, and spirits. These are the most frequently chanted texts in living Theravāda Buddhism.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Karaṇīyamettā Sutta** (Sn 1.8) | Loving-kindness: "May all beings be happy and secure" — the most chanted protection sutta. The Buddha taught it to monks troubled by forest spirits |
| **Ratana Sutta** (Sn 2.1) | The "Jewel Discourse" — chanted to ward off plague and famine: "Whatever treasures exist in this world or the next, nothing compares to the Buddha, the Dhamma, the Saṅgha" |
| **Maṅgala Sutta** (Sn 2.4) | The 38 blessings — chanted at weddings, house-warmings, and new beginnings. The most popular paritta in Theravāda countries |
| **Mora Sutta** (Sn 1.8, partial) | The peacock's protection — the Buddha's former life as a golden peacock. Chanted for journeys and protection from harm |
| **Khandha Paritta** (AN 4.67) | Protection from snakes and venomous creatures — the Buddha's teaching to a monk bitten by a snake |
| **Āṭānāṭiya Sutta** (DN 32) | The great protection sutta against malevolent spirits, taught by the four Great Kings. Long and elaborate |
| **Bojjhaṅga Paritta** (SN 46) | Chanting the seven factors of enlightenment as a protection for the sick — cure through Dhamma |
| **Vaṭṭa Sutta** (Sn 1.10) | The practice of loving-kindness as protection against all harm |

**Core text:** Maṅgala Sutta, Ratana Sutta, Karaṇīyamettā Sutta — the "three great parittas"

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → all stories (the paritta tradition envelopes the narrative tradition)

## 21. Hymns, Verses & Poetry

The poetic voice of Buddhism — the inspired utterances, the songs of the monks and nuns, the condensed wisdom of the Dhammapada. Buddhism's most quoted texts are almost all verses.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Dhammapada** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | 423 verses in 26 chapters — the single most translated Buddhist text. "Hatred never ends by hatred; by love alone it is healed" (1.5). "Better than a thousand hollow words is one word that brings peace" (8.100) |
| **Udāna** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | 80 "inspired utterances" of the Buddha — emotional, poetic responses to events: "Happy indeed are the arahants… Their last body is laid down" |
| **Itivuttaka** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | 112 short discourses, each introduced by "Thus it was said by the Buddha" — pithy teachings in mixed prose and verse: "Give up anger, abandon pride, transcend all fetters" |
| **Sutta Nipāta** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | 71 suttas in verse — the most archaic layer of the Pāli Canon. Contains the Aṭṭhaka Vagga (the "octet chapter"), believed to be among the very oldest Buddhist texts: "One against whom there is nothing false among all things seen, heard, or thought — how could anyone here describe such a sage?" |
| **Theragāthā** (Khuddaka Nikāya) | 264 poems by 264 elder monks — the songs of the first arahants. "I burnt down my house — standing on the mountain, my heart is free" — Mahākassapa. "The rain seeps through a badly roofed hut, but passion seeps through a poorly trained mind" — the Buddha's first verse |
| **Therīgāthā** (see also group 12) | The nuns' songs — the oldest women's liberation poetry: "Free! I am free! Free from the three crooked things: the mortar, the pestle, and my crooked husband!" — Muttā's song |
| **Buddhavaṃsa** | 24 verse-narratives of the past Buddhas — a poetic lineage of awakening |
| **Vimānavatthu** | Stories of celestial mansions in verse — beings recount the good deeds that led to their heavenly rebirth |
| **Petavatthu** | Ghost stories in verse — the torments of the hungry ghosts, the consequences of evil deeds |

**Core text:** Dhammapada — the most quoted and loved text in all Buddhism

**See also:** [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → The Buddha, Ānanda (who remembered every verse), Mahākassapa (Theragāthā poet)

## 22. The Commentarial Tradition (Aṭṭhakathā)

The vast commentarial literature that preserved, explained, and systematised the Buddha's teachings. Without the commentaries, much of the Pāli Canon would be unintelligible.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Visuddhimagga** (Buddhaghosa, 5th c. CE) | "The Path of Purification" — the single greatest work of Theravāda Buddhism. Three parts: sīla (virtue), samādhi (concentration), paññā (wisdom). Buddhaghosa's masterwork, the synthesis of the entire Sinhalese commentarial tradition |
| **Sumaṅgalavilāsinī** (Buddhaghosa) | Commentary on the Dīgha Nikāya |
| **Papañcasūdanī** (Buddhaghosa) | Commentary on the Majjhima Nikāya |
| **Sāratthappakāsinī** (Buddhaghosa) | Commentary on the Saṃyutta Nikāya |
| **Manorathapūraṇī** (Buddhaghosa) | Commentary on the Aṅguttara Nikāya |
| **Paramatthajotikā** (Buddhaghosa) | Commentary on the Khuddaka Nikāya (including Dhammapada, Jātaka, Sutta Nipāta) |
| **Dhammapada Aṭṭhakathā** | The story-commentary on each verse of the Dhammapada — the richest narrative work in Pāli literature. Each verse is introduced by a story of why the Buddha said it |
| **Jātaka Aṭṭhakathā** | The full prose narrative of each Jātaka tale — the Buddha's past lives with the present-life frame story |
| **Ṭīkā** (sub-commentaries) | Later commentaries on Buddhaghosa's commentaries — the ongoing scholastic tradition |

**Core text:** Visuddhimagga (the encyclopaedia of Theravāda Buddhism); Dhammapada Aṭṭhakathā (the narrative treasury)

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] — many story notes draw on the Dhammapada Aṭṭhakathā; [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → Mahākassapa (the patron of the commentarial tradition)

## 23. Council History & Sectarian Divisions

The historical councils that fixed the canon and defined the schools. Buddhism is not a single monolithic tradition but a family of schools that emerged from these early debates.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Cullavagga** (Vinaya XI) | The First Council (Rājagaha, c. 480 BCE): Mahākassapa convenes 500 arahants; Ānanda recites all the Suttas; Upāli recites the Vinaya. The foundation of the canonical tradition |
| **Cullavagga** (Vinaya XII) | The Second Council (Vesālī, c. 380 BCE): the dispute over the "ten indulgences" (money, eating after noon, etc.); the orthodox party declares them unlawful; the first major schism |
| **Kathāvatthu** (Abhidhamma Piṭaka) | The Third Council (Pāṭaliputta, c. 250 BCE): Moggaliputta-Tissa presides; 217 points of controversy resolved; the "Vibhajjavāda" (School of Analysis) emerges — the ancestor of Theravāda |
| **Mahāvaṃsa** (Dīpavaṃsa, 5th c. CE) | The chronicle of Sri Lanka: the Third Council, the mission of Mahinda to Sri Lanka, the establishment of the Mahāvihāra tradition |
| **Dīpavaṃsa** (4th c. CE) | The earliest Pāli historical chronicle: the council history, the missions of Aśoka's son, the arrival of Buddhism in Sri Lanka |
| **Mahāyāna retrospective** (Mahāparinirvāṇa Sūtra, Lotus Sūtra) | Mahāyāna's self-understanding: the Mahāyāna sūtras were taught secretly by the Buddha to advanced disciples and preserved in the Nāga realm until humanity was ready |
| **Schools of Buddhism** (sectarian catalogues) | The 18 (or 20) early schools: Sarvāstivāda, Mahāsāṅghika, Pudgalavāda, Sthaviravāda, etc. — their doctrines, regions, and fates |

**Core text:** Cullavagga (the only canonical account of the First Council)

**See also:** [[Buddhist Stories MOC]] → The First Buddhist Council; [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] → Mahākassapa, Ānanda, Upāli

## 24. Vajrayāna & Tantric Buddhism

The esoteric third turning of the Dharma: Buddhism as practiced in Tibet, Bhutan, Mongolia, Nepal, and Japan (Shingon). Tantric methods use the energies of desire and the body as the path to enlightenment — "by which one is bound, by that one is freed."

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Mahāvairocana Sūtra** (c. 7th c.) | The foundational Vajrayāna sūtra: Vairocana Buddha teaches the *garbhadhātu* (womb/realm) maṇḍala — the world as the expression of the Dharmakāya Buddha's wisdom |
| **Vajraśekhara Sūtra** (c. 8th c.) | The complementary *vajradhātu* (diamond-realm) maṇḍala: the five Dhyāni Buddhas, the five wisdoms, the identity of the practitioner and the Buddha |
| **Guhyasamāja Tantra** (c. 5th c.) | "The Secret Assembly" — the earliest Buddhist tantra: the practice of *utpatti-krama* (generation stage), the transformation of the three poisons into the three bodies of a Buddha |
| **Kālacakra Tantra** (c. 10th c.) | "The Wheel of Time" — the most elaborate Buddhist tantra: cosmology, astronomy, physiology, and the attainment of Buddhahood in a single lifetime through the control of the subtle body |
| **Hevajra Tantra** (c. 8th c.) | The union of wisdom and method (*upāya*), the *mahāmudrā* (great seal), the practitioner's identity as the deity Hevajra |
| **Cakrasaṃvara Tantra** (c. 8th c.) | The practice of Heruka Cakrasaṃvara — the most widely practiced yidam (meditation deity) in Tibetan Buddhism |
| **Bardo Thodol** ("Tibetan Book of the Dead", 8th c., revealed 14th c.) | The intermediate state (*bardo*) between death and rebirth: the visions of peaceful and wrathful deities, the instructions for liberation in the bardo |
| **Mañjuśrīmūlakalpa** (c. 6th c.) | An encyclopedic tantric text: maṇḍalas, mantras, initiation rites, the construction of images, and the powers of various Buddhas and bodhisattvas |
| **Gampopa's Jewel Ornament of Liberation** (12th c.) | The Kagyu classic — the stages of the path in a single volume: "The mind itself is the Buddha; do not seek Buddhahood elsewhere" |

**Core text:** Bardo Thodol (most widely known in the West); Guhyasamāja Tantra (the earliest systematic tantra)

**See also:** [[Buddhist Figures MOC]] — the Bodhisattvas of the Mahāyāna-Vajrayāna continuum: Mañjuśrī, Avalokiteśvara, Vajrapāṇi

## 25. Theravāda vs. Mahāyāna — Key Doctrinal Differences

A comparative group for cross-referencing — not a single text but a framework for understanding how the same Buddhist themes took different forms across traditions.

| Theme | Theravāda Position | Mahāyāna Re-interpretation |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| **Goal** | Arahantship (personal liberation) | Buddhahood (full awakening for all beings) |
| **Buddha** | Historical Gotama — a human who awakened | Trikāya: Dharmakāya, Sambhogakāya, Nirmāṇakāya — the Buddha as cosmic principle |
| **Bodhisattva** | The future Buddha before awakening (only Gotama's past lives) | All beings can take the Bodhisattva vow — the universal path |
| **Śūnyatā (Emptiness)** | Anattā — the self is not found in the aggregates | All dhammas are empty — including nibbāna, including emptiness itself |
| **Nibbāna** | The unconditioned element (*asaṅkhata-dhātu*) | Nirvāṇa = Saṃsāra — seen correctly, the world is already liberation |
| **Upāya (Skill-in-means)** | The Buddha taught what was appropriate to the listener | Whole collections of sūtras are "expedient means" — including the entire Hīnayāna |
| **Buddha-nature** | Not affirmed | Tathāgatagarbha — all beings have the potential for Buddhahood within |
| **Pure Land** | Not a doctrine — kamma determines rebirth | Faith in Amitābha leads to rebirth in a buddha-field |
| **Canon** | Pāli Tipiṭaka (closed, fixed) | Infinite sūtras — the Mahāyāna canon is open: "The Tathāgata's teaching has no end" |

**See also:** All Thematic Groups above — each group includes both Theravāda and Mahāyāna references

## How to Use These Groups

**To find texts by theme:** Browse the group headings above and follow the wikilinks to the key suttas and sources. For example, group 2 (The Four Noble Truths) will take you from SN 56.11 through MN 9 to DN 22.

**For thematic study:** Read a group sequentially — for instance, trace the theme of karma (group 4) from the simple Cūḷakammavibhaṅga Sutta (MN 135), through the complex Mahākammavibhaṅga (MN 136), to the Abhidhamma's ultimate analysis.

**For cross-referencing notes:** When writing about a topic (e.g., "mindfulness in Buddhist thought"), consult group 9 (Meditation & Mindfulness) to find all relevant texts, then group 2 (Four Noble Truths) for the soteriological context.

**To understand individual texts:** Each text's cross-reference in the groups above explains *why* it belongs to that theme. Many texts appear in multiple groups — the Mahāparinibbāna Sutta (DN 16), for instance, appears in groups 1, 6, 11, and 23 — reflecting its role as biography, nibbāna discourse, supernatural narrative, and council source.

**Paired study:** Use alongside [[Quran Thematic Groups]], [[Bible Thematic Groups]], [[Hindu Scripture Thematic Groups]], and [[Hadith Thematic Groups]] to compare how the world's major scriptures treat shared themes like creation, prophecy/revelation, ethics, law, eschatology, and the nature of the ultimate.
