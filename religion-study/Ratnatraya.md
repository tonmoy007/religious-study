# Ratnatraya (The Three Jewels)

## Definition

**Ratnatraya** (रत्नत्रय, "three jewels") is the foundational path to liberation (*mokṣa*) in Jainism. It consists of three inseparable constituents: **samyak darśana** (right faith), **samyak jñāna** (right knowledge), and **samyak cāritra** (right conduct). The Tattvārtha Sūtra opens with the authoritative statement:

> *Samyagdarśanajñānacāritrāṇi mokṣamārgaḥ* — "Right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct together constitute the path to liberation." (Tattvārtha Sūtra 1.1)

## The Three Jewels in Practice

| Jewel | Meaning | Practical Essence |
|---|---|---|
| **Samyak Darśana** | Right Faith / Vision | Firm conviction in the nine *padārthas* (fundamental categories of truth) — the nature of soul, karma, bondage, and liberation. It is faith born from understanding, not blind belief. |
| **Samyak Jñāna** | Right Knowledge | Accurate comprehension of reality as revealed by the *āgamas* and *tattvas* — discerning the distinction between *jīva* (sentient) and *ajīva* (insentient). |
| **Samyak Cāritra** | Right Conduct | Ethical living in accordance with faith and knowledge — observance of the five *mahāvratas* (great vows: non-violence, truthfulness, non-stealing, celibacy, non-attachment). |

## Relationship: Simultaneous, Not Sequential

The three jewels are not steps in a linear progression; they are co-present and mutually reinforcing:

- **Faith without knowledge is blind** — conviction without correct understanding leads to superstition and error.
- **Knowledge without conduct is useless** — mere intellectual grasp of truth without action produces no spiritual fruit.
- **Conduct without faith is mechanical** — ethical action divorced from conviction lacks the inner transformation that leads to liberation.

True spiritual progress requires all three operating together, like the legs of a tripod — remove one and the whole structure collapses. The path is described as simultaneous arising (*yugapat*) rather than a graduated ladder.

## The Eight Limbs of Right Faith (Aṣṭa-aṅga Darśana)

Right faith (*samyak darśana*) is further elaborated through eight qualities or limbs (*vyākhyāna aṣṭaka*):

1. **Niḥśaṅkita** — Freedom from doubt regarding the Tīrthaṅkaras and their teachings
2. **Niḥkāṅkṣita** — Absence of desire for worldly pleasures over liberation
3. **Nirvicikitsā** — Absence of disgust toward ascetics or the impure
4. **Amūḍhadṛṣṭi** — Unshaken faith, rejecting false gods and deluded views
5. **Upagūhana** — Protecting and promoting the true doctrine
6. **Sthitīkaraṇa** — Steadfastness in maintaining faith when it wavers
7. **Vātsalya** — Affectionate regard for spiritually advanced souls
8. **Prabhāvanā** — Glorifying the faith through conduct and teaching

These eight ensure that right faith is not a passive acceptance but an active, cultivated orientation of the soul.

## Key Sources

- **Tattvārtha Sūtra**, ch. 1 (especially 1.1–1.4) — the foundational canonical formulation.
- **Puruṣārtha Siddhyupāya** of Amṛtacandra — extensive commentary on the interdependence of the three jewels.
- **Ratnakaraṇḍa Śrāvakācāra** — on the householder's practice of the three jewels.

## Comparison with Buddhist Tri-ratna

Jain and Buddhist traditions both employ the "Three Jewels" (tri-ratna) motif, but with entirely different referents:

| Aspect | Jain Ratnatraya | Buddhist Tri-ratna* |
|---|---|---|
| Content | Faith, Knowledge, Conduct | Buddha, Dharma, Saṅgha |
| Orientation | Internal qualities of the aspirant | External objects of refuge |
| Goal | Liberation via individual purification | Liberation via refuge in the three supports |
| Nature | Path itself (mārga) | Objects of veneration and refuge |
| *Source: common to all Buddhist schools (e.g., Pali Suttas, Three Refuges formula) | | |

While the Buddhist Three Jewels are *refuge objects* one turns toward, the Jain Ratnatraya is the *path itself* — the internal disposition and practice that constitutes the way to liberation. Both systems agree that no single jewel suffices alone, but they conceive the interdependence differently.
