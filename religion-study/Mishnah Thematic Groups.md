# 📜 Mishnah Thematic Groups

A reference guide to the major thematic groups running through the 63 tractates of the Mishnah (מִשְׁנָה) — the foundational text of rabbinic Judaism, redacted c. 200 CE by [[Rabbi Yehudah haNasi]]. Unlike the order-based arrangement of the Six Orders (Sedarim), this index groups tractates by **cross-cutting themes** — showing how a single idea (prayer, purity, justice, family) weaves across the entire corpus. Use this note to navigate the Mishnah by subject and to see thematic connections between tractates that span different Orders.

> [!note] How the groups were built
> The 63 tractates were grouped by their dominant subject matter, following classical Jewish understanding (the *sugyot* of the Talmud) and modern scholarship. Many tractates belong to multiple groups — for example, Hagigah is simultaneously about the pilgrimage festivals (Shabbat & Holy Days) and purity rules (Ritual Purity). Cross-references to [[Jewish Figures MOC]], [[Jewish Stories MOC]], and [[Jewish Concepts MOC]] connect the legal content to the people, narratives, and core ideas that animate Mishnaic study.

---

## 1. Prayer, Blessings & Daily Worship (תְּפִלָּה וּבְרָכוֹת)

The laws governing the rhythm of daily Jewish prayer — the Shema, the Amidah (Shemoneh Esreh), blessings over food and experiences, the synagogue liturgy, and the sanctification of the calendar. Prayer in the Mishnah replaces the Temple service as the primary mode of connecting with God.

| Tractate | Order | Key Contribution |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| [[Mishnah Berakhot]] | Zera'im | The Shema (times, posture, intent); the Amidah; blessings over food, phenomena, and mitzvot; grace after meals |
| [[Mishnah Rosh Hashanah]] | Mo'ed | The sanctification of the new moon; the shofar; the judgement of the world on Rosh Hashanah |
| [[Mishnah Ta'anit]] | Mo'ed | Communal fasts for drought; the prayer for rain; the role of the leader in public prayer |
| [[Mishnah Megillah]] | Mo'ed | Reading the Megillah on Purim; synagogue readings; laws of the Torah scroll |
| [[Mishnah Shekalim]] | Mo'ed | The half-shekel Temple tax — the community's collective participation in worship |

**Core text:** Mishnah Berakhot 1:1 — "From when does one recite Shema in the evening?... until the end of the first watch." The Mishnah opens with the Shema, establishing that the acceptance of the yoke of heaven is the foundation of all Jewish life.

## 2. Shabbat, Festivals & Sacred Time (שַׁבָּת וּמוֹעֲדִים)

The laws of sacred time — the weekly Shabbat, the pilgrimage festivals (Pesah, Shavuot, Sukkot), the High Holy Days (Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur), and the intermediate days. These tractates preserve the Temple-era rhythms and adapt them for a post-Temple world.

| Tractate | Order | Key Contribution |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| [[Mishnah Shabbat]] | Mo'ed | The 39 categories of forbidden labour (avot melakhah); carrying, cooking, lighting — the foundation of Shabbat observance |
| [[Mishnah Eruvin]] | Mo'ed | Creating eruv boundaries to permit carrying; eruv tavshilin (cooking on festival eve); Shabbat domains |
| [[Mishnah Pesahim]] | Mo'ed | Removing leaven; the Passover sacrifice; the Seder — the most detailed Temple-era festival tractate |
| [[Mishnah Yoma]] | Mo'ed | The Yom Kippur Temple service (Avodah); the High Priest's preparation; the scapegoat; confession |
| [[Mishnah Sukkah]] | Mo'ed | The sukkah construction; the four species (lulav, etrog, hadas, aravah); water-drawing ceremony |
| [[Mishnah Beitzah]] | Mo'ed | The laws of Yom Tov (festival days); cooking and carrying on holy days |
| [[Mishnah Hagigah]] | Mo'ed | The pilgrimage offerings; the purity levels required for festival ascent to Jerusalem |

**Core text:** Mishnah Shabbat 7:2 — the list of the 39 categories of forbidden labour (avot melakhah), derived from the work of constructing the Tabernacle. Shabbat is the most elaborated tractate in Mo'ed (24 chapters).

## 3. Agriculture, Tithing & Provision for the Poor (זְרָעִים — מִצְווֹת הַתְּלוּיוֹת בָּאָרֶץ)

The agricultural laws that bind the people to the land and to each other — the tithes for priests, Levites, and the poor; the sabbatical year; the dietary boundaries between permitted and forbidden mixtures. These are the commandments dependent on the Land of Israel (mitzvot ha-teluyot ba'aretz).

| Tractate | Order | Key Contribution |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| [[Mishnah Pe'ah]] | Zera'im | The corner of the field left for the poor (Lev 19:9–10); gleanings, forgotten sheaves; defines the minimum for charity |
| [[Mishnah Demai]] | Zera'im | Produce of uncertain tithing status — balancing trust and legal obligation |
| [[Mishnah Kil'ayim]] | Zera'im | Prohibited mixtures: seeds, fabrics (shatnez), animals — the theological boundary against chaos |
| [[Mishnah Shevi'it]] | Zera'im | The sabbatical year (shemittah) — letting the land rest; debt remission; the produce of the seventh year |
| [[Mishnah Terumot]] | Zera'im | The heave-offering for the priests; its sanctity and rules of separation |
| [[Mishnah Ma'aserot]] | Zera'im | The first tithe (ma'aser rishon) given to the Levites |
| [[Mishnah Ma'aser Sheni]] | Zera'im | The second tithe — eaten in Jerusalem; the redemption of coins |
| [[Mishnah Hallah]] | Zera'im | The dough offering — a portion given to the priest (Num 15:17–21) |
| [[Mishnah Orlah]] | Zera'im | The fruit of trees in the first three years — forbidden as orlah (Lev 19:23–25) |
| [[Mishnah Bikkurim]] | Zera'im | First fruits brought to the Temple — the declaration (Deut 26:1–11) |

**Core text:** Mishnah Pe'ah 1:1 — "These are the things that have no measure: the corner of the field (pe'ah), the first fruits (bikkurim), the festival pilgrimage offering (re'iyah), acts of loving-kindness (gemilut hasadim), and the study of Torah." The Mishnah's opening halakhic statement places social ethics alongside Temple worship and Torah study.

## 4. Marriage, Divorce & Family Law (נָשִׁים — דִּינֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה)

The tractates that govern the creation and dissolution of the Jewish family — betrothal, marriage contracts, divorce, levirate marriage, vows, and the ordeal of the suspected adulteress. These laws centre on the status of women in the covenantal community and the legal framework that protects and defines their rights.

| Tractate | Order | Key Contribution |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| [[Mishnah Yevamot]] | Nashim | Levirate marriage (yibbum/halitzah); prohibited degrees of marriage; the duty to perpetuate a brother's name |
| [[Mishnah Ketubot]] | Nashim | The marriage contract (ketubah) — a woman's financial rights; virginity claims; spousal duties |
| [[Mishnah Nedarim]] | Nashim | Vows and their annulment; the power of words; the husband's and father's power to annul |
| [[Mishnah Nazir]] | Nashim | The Nazirite vow (Num 6) — abstinence from wine, hair-cutting, and corpse contact |
| [[Mishnah Sotah]] | Nashim | The ordeal of the suspected adulteress (Num 5:11–31); the bitter waters; the decline of the ordeal |
| [[Mishnah Gittin]] | Nashim | Divorce procedure; the writ of divorce (get); agency in divorce; laws of bills |
| [[Mishnah Kiddushin]] | Nashim | Betrothal (erusin/kiddushin); modes of acquisition; the lineage of Jews; forbidden marriages |

**Core text:** Mishnah Kiddushin 1:1 — "A woman is acquired in three ways and acquires herself in two ways." The tractate opens with the legal framework of marriage and then immediately pivots to the broader principle that all mitzvot, social positions, and relationships have a legal foundation.

## 5. Civil Law, Torts & Social Justice (נְזִיקִין — מִשְׁפָּט עִבְרִי)

The comprehensive civil and criminal law of the Mishnah — torts, property, labour, bailments, inheritance, courts, evidence, capital punishment, and the prohibition of judicial corruption. This is the foundation of Jewish civil law (mishpat ivri) and remains the basis of halakhic commercial law today.

| Tractate | Order | Key Contribution |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| [[Mishnah Bava Kamma]] | Nezikin | Torts and damages: the five categories of damage (shor, bor, maveh, hev'er, adam); compensation for bodily injury |
| [[Mishnah Bava Metzia]] | Nezikin | Found property; bailments (guardianship); labour law; lending and interest; lost objects; the law of the finder |
| [[Mishnah Bava Batra]] | Nezikin | Real estate law; property boundaries; inheritance; partnership; sales and conveyances |
| [[Mishnah Sanhedrin]] | Nezikin | The court system (Sanhedrin); criminal procedure; capital punishment (four modes); the rebellious elder |
| [[Mishnah Makkot]] | Nezikin | False witnesses (edim zomemim); cities of refuge; flogging (malkot) |
| [[Mishnah Shevu'ot]] | Nezikin | Oaths — their types, their violations, their judicial contexts |
| [[Mishnah Eduyyot]] | Nezikin | A collection of legal testimonies and precedents — a cross-section of the chain of tradition |
| [[Mishnah Horayot]] | Nezikin | Erroneous rulings by the Sanhedrin or the High Priest; the sin-offering for communal error |

**Core text:** Mishnah Bava Metzia 1:1 — "Two hold a garment. One says: 'I found it,' and the other says: 'I found it.' The division is by oath." The Mishnaic method of legal reasoning is immediately visible: concrete cases, competing claims, and procedural resolution.

## 6. Idolatry, Pagan Interaction & Jewish Distinctiveness (עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה)

The boundary between Jewish and non-Jewish society in the Greco-Roman world — the prohibition of idolatry, commercial interactions with pagans, and the demarcation of Jewish identity through law.

| Tractate | Order | Key Contribution |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| [[Mishnah Avodah Zarah]] | Nezikin | Prohibition of idolatry; trade with pagans before their festivals; nullification of idols; Jewish wine and gentile handling; laws of images and statues |

**Core text:** Mishnah Avodah Zarah 1:1 — "Three days before the festivals of the pagans, it is forbidden to do business with them." The Mishnah maps the social boundaries that preserve Jewish distinctiveness in a pagan world.

---

## 7. Ethics, Wisdom & the Good Life (אָבוֹת — מוּסָר)

The only non-halakhic tractate of the Mishnah — Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers) — a collection of ethical maxims from the sages spanning the chain of tradition from Sinai through the Tannaitic period. This is the Mishnah's voice of aggadah (narrative theology) and musar (ethical instruction).

| Tractate | Order | Key Contribution |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| [[Pirkei Avot]] | Nezikin | The chain of transmission (Moses → Joshua → Elders → Prophets → Men of the Great Assembly); Hillel's Golden Rule; Akiva's "Love your neighbour as yourself, this is a great principle of the Torah"; Yohanan ben Zakkai's disciples; the world stands on Torah, Avodah, and Gemilut Chasadim |

**Core text:** Avot 1:1 — "Moses received the Torah at Sinai and transmitted it to Joshua, Joshua to the elders, the elders to the prophets, and the prophets to the Men of the Great Assembly." The chain of tradition that grounds all of rabbinic authority.

---

## 8. Temple & Sacrificial System (קָדָשִׁים — עֲבוֹדַת בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ)

The laws of animal and grain offerings in the Temple; the rules of consecration and disqualification; the architecture of the Temple itself; and the daily rhythm of sacrificial worship. Despite the Temple's destruction in 70 CE, these laws are studied as a form of worship and in anticipation of its future rebuilding.

| Tractate | Order | Key Contribution |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| [[Mishnah Zevahim]] | Kodashim | The laws of animal offerings: types of sacrifice (olah, shelamim, hatat, asham); where and how they are slaughtered and offered |
| [[Mishnah Menahot]] | Kodashim | Grain and meal offerings; the showbread; the incense; the omer offering |
| [[Mishnah Hullin]] | Kodashim | Ordinary (non-sacred) slaughter — kosher slaughter (shehitah); dietary laws; the prohibition of the sciatic nerve |
| [[Mishnah Bekhorot]] | Kodashim | Firstborn animals and humans (pidyon haben); blemishes that disqualify a priest or an animal |
| [[Mishnah Arakhin]] | Kodashim | Vows of valuation (arakhin) — dedicating a person's value to the Temple |
| [[Mishnah Temurah]] | Kodashim | Substituting a consecrated animal — the prohibition of exchanging (Lev 27:10) |
| [[Mishnah Keritot]] | Kodashim | Sins punishable by karet (excision) — the 36 categories; the sliding-scale sin-offering |
| [[Mishnah Me'ilah]] | Kodashim | Misusing consecrated property (me'ilah) — trespass from sacred to secular |
| [[Mishnah Tamid]] | Kodashim | A narrative description of the daily morning and evening Temple service |
| [[Mishnah Middot]] | Kodashim | The measurements and architecture of the Second Temple — doors, courtyards, gates, the Sanctuary |
| [[Mishnah Kinnim]] | Kodashim | The complex laws of bird offerings — the most difficult tractate in the Mishnah |

**Core text:** Mishnah Tamid — the only tractate that reads as continuous narrative rather than legal debate, describing the daily Temple service from the priests' waking to the offering of the incense.

## 9. Ritual Purity & Impurity (טָהֳרוֹת — דִּינֵי טֻמְאָה וְטָהֳרָה)

The most voluminous section of the Mishnah (12 tractates, largest by number of chapters at 120 total) — the intricate system of ritual purity and impurity (tum'ah ve-taharah) that governed access to the Temple, consumption of sacred food, and the boundary between the sacred and the ordinary.

| Tractate | Order | Key Contribution |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| [[Mishnah Keilim]] | Tohorot | The impurity of vessels — the largest tractate (30 chapters); a taxonomy of all manufactured objects and their susceptibility to impurity |
| [[Mishnah Oholot]] | Tohorot | Corpse impurity (tumat met) and its transmission through tents/overshadowing (Num 19:14–16) |
| [[Mishnah Nega'im]] | Tohorot | The laws of tzara'at (leprosy) — diagnosis, quarantine, purification (Lev 13–14) |
| [[Mishnah Parah]] | Tohorot | The Red Heifer (parah adumah) ritual (Num 19) — the mystery of purification from corpse impurity |
| [[Mishnah Tohorot]] | Tohorot | Minor impurities — the lesser degrees of uncleanness and their remedies |
| [[Mishnah Mikva'ot]] | Tohorot | The laws of ritual immersion pools (mikveh) — valid water, minimum measures, valid immersion |
| [[Mishnah Niddah]] | Tohorot | Menstrual impurity (niddah); the laws of family purity (taharat ha-mishpachah); examining bloodstains |
| [[Mishnah Makhshirin]] | Tohorot | Liquids that make produce susceptible to impurity (Lev 11:34, 38) — the role of water, oil, wine |
| [[Mishnah Zavim]] | Tohorot | Unclean discharges (zav/zavah) — Leviticus 15; the counting of clean days |
| [[Mishnah Tevul Yom]] | Tohorot | The status of one who has immersed but awaits sunset to complete purification |
| [[Mishnah Yadayim]] | Tohorot | The impurity of hands — hand-washing (netilat yadayim); the status of the sacred scriptures |
| [[Mishnah Oklashin]] | Tohorot | How food contracts impurity — the mechanisms by which food becomes susceptible |

**Core text:** Mishnah Keilim 1:1 — "The Fathers of Impurity" (avot ha-tum'ot). The tractate opens with the categories that generate impurity, establishing the taxonomy that underlies 12 tractates of legal intricacy.

---

## 10. The Oral Law, Authority & Tradition (תּוֹרָה שֶׁבְּעַל־פֶּה)

The meta-halakhic tractates — the ones that reflect on the nature of the Oral Law itself, the authority of the sages, the chain of transmission, and the principles of legal interpretation. These tractates make explicit what the rest of the Mishnah presupposes.

| Tractate | Order | Key Contribution |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| [[Pirkei Avot]] | Nezikin | The chain of transmission from Sinai; the authority of each generation of sages; ethical qualities of the Torah scholar |
| [[Mishnah Eduyyot]] | Nezikin | Testimonies about precedents — sometimes called "Mishnat Hasidim" (the Mishnah of the Pious); a collection of rulings that establish the law |
| [[Mishnah Horayot]] | Nezikin | The consequences of erroneous rulings; the authority of the Sanhedrin; the principle that communal error requires a different sacrifice |

**Core text:** Pirkei Avot 1:1 — "Moses received the Torah at Sinai and transmitted it to Joshua, Joshua to the elders..." This single sentence is the founding claim of rabbinic Judaism: that the Oral Law is as divine as the Written Law.
