# Ahiṃsā (Non-Violence)

## Definition and Centrality

**Ahiṃsā** (Skt. *a*-"non" + *hiṃsā*-"harm/violence") is the foundational ethical principle of Jainism, often encapsulated in the maxim **"ahiṃsā paramo dharmaḥ"** — non-violence is the supreme dharma (*Tattvārtha Sūtra* 1.1, *Ācārāṅga Sūtra* 1.4.1). For Jains, ahiṃsā is not merely a negative abstention from harm but a positive commitment to the welfare of all living beings, rooted in the recognition that all souls (*jīvas*) are intrinsically equal in their potential for liberation.

## The Five Great Vows (*Mahāvratas*)

Ahiṃsā stands as the first and most important of the five great vows taken by Jain ascetics:

| Vow | Meaning |
|-----|---------|
| **Ahiṃsā** | Non-violence in thought, word, and deed |
| **Satya** | Truthfulness |
| **Asteya** | Non-stealing |
| **Brahmacarya** | Chastity / Celibacy |
| **Aparigraha** | Non-attachment / Non-possession |

For laypeople (*śrāvakas*), these are observed as the *aṇuvratas* (lesser vows), with ahiṃsā guiding the degree of restraint appropriate to householder life.

## The Gradation of Life (*Saṃjñī* and *Asaṃjñī*)

Violence is classified according to the number of senses possessed by the being harmed:

| Senses | Beings | Examples |
|--------|--------|----------|
| 1 (touch) | *Ekendriya* | Earth-bodied, water-bodied, fire-bodied, air-bodied, plants |
| 2 (touch + taste) | *Dvīndriya* | Worms, leeches |
| 3 (+ smell) | *Trīndriya* | Ants, bugs, moths |
| 4 (+ sight) | *Caturendriya* | Bees, flies, butterflies |
| 5 (+ hearing) | *Pañcendriya* | Animals, birds, humans, gods, infernal beings |

The more senses a being possesses, the more severe the karmic consequence (*karma-bandha*) of harming it. However, *any* unnecessary harm binds karma, and even injury to a single-sensed being (e.g., uprooting a plant) is to be avoided where possible.

## Practical Implications

The commitment to ahiṃsā profoundly shapes Jain daily life:

- **Vegetarianism / Veganism**: A strict lacto-vegetarian diet, avoiding roots and tubers (which host countless microbes and whose harvest kills the plant). Many Jains are vegan or avoid certain vegetables (onion, garlic) for their tamasic and life-destroying qualities.
- **Filtering Water**: Water is strained through a cloth before drinking to avoid swallowing tiny organisms.
- **Sweeping the Ground**: Ascetics sweep the ground before sitting or walking to avoid crushing insects.
- **Mouth-covering (*muhpattī*)**: A cloth worn over the mouth to prevent inhaling or harming airborne beings.
- **Mindful Speech**: Harsh, angry, or deceitful words are forms of *bhāva hiṃsā* (mental violence) and are equally proscribed.
- **Occupational Limits**: Lay Jains avoid professions involving killing (farming, fishing, butchering, even some forms of construction).

## Hiṃsā, Ahiṃsā, and Intention (*Bhāva*)

Jain philosophy distinguishes between:

- **Hiṃsā** (violence): The act of causing harm to a living being.
- **Ahiṃsā** (non-violence): The deliberate refraining from harming.
- **Bhāva hiṃsā**: Violence rooted in intention, anger, pride, deceit, or greed — this is the most serious form, because karma is bound primarily by the mental state of the agent.
- **Dravya hiṃsā**: Physical violence as an act, which may be unintentional (and thus less karmically binding).

A key Jain insight: even if no physical harm occurs, an intention to harm constitutes *bhāva hiṃsā*. Conversely, unavoidable harm caused while acting with pure intention (e.g., a mendicant inadvertently stepping on an ant while walking mindfully) binds minimal or no karma, provided proper precautions were taken.

## Comparison with Buddhist and Hindu Ahiṃsā

| Tradition | Scope | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| **Jain** | Absolute — all beings including plants and microorganisms | Every soul is equal; violence binds karma and delays liberation |
| **Buddhist** | Strong but contextual — avoids harm, but allows meat-eating under certain conditions (e.g., alms food) | Rooted in compassion (*karuṇā*) and the First Precept, but practice adjusted for monastics |
| **Hindu** | Variable — strong in ascetic/yogic traditions, qualified in householder life (e.g., animal sacrifice in Vedic contexts, though later reinterpreted) | Grounded in *ahiṃsā paramo dharma* (Mahābhārata), but often subordinated to caste and ritual duty (*varṇāśrama dharma*) |

## Key Scriptural Sources

- **Ācārāṅga Sūtra 1.4.1**: "All beings love life, all beings desire happiness, all beings fear pain, all beings shrink from suffering, all beings are averse to death." — the classic grounding of ahiṃsā in universal empathy.
- **Daśavaikālika Sūtra 6.1–10**: A detailed exposition of the five great vows, with ahiṃsā at the head; warns that even a moment of negligence can bind immense karma.
- **Tattvārtha Sūtra 7.1**: "Hiṃsā is the binding of karma through negligence in the protection of life" — a definitive formulation linking violence to karmic influx (*āsrava*).
- **Sūtrakṛtāṅga Sūtra 1.1.1**: Distinguishes between the violence of the ignorant (who harm through passion) and the non-violence of the wise (who act with equanimity).
- **Uttarādhyayana Sūtra 32**: Contains a detailed analysis of the fourteen *guṇasthānas* (stages of spiritual development), showing how ahiṃsā deepens as the soul progresses toward omniscience.

## Ahiṃsā in the Path to Liberation

Ahiṃsā is not an isolated ethical rule but the very fabric of the Jain path. By minimizing harm, the aspirant stops the influx of new karma (*saṃvara*), sheds existing karma (*nirjarā*), and ultimately purifies the soul to the state of *kevala* (omniscience) and *mokṣa* (liberation). As the *Ācārāṅga* declares: "Knowing this, one should not harm any living being — not the great ones, nor the tiny ones, not those that move, nor those that are still — for all are dear to themselves."
