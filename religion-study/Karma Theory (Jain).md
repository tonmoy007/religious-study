# Jain Karma Theory

Jain karma theory differs radically from Hindu or Buddhist conceptions: karma is not a metaphysical principle of action-reaction but a **physical substance** — subtle matter (*karma-puñja* or karmic matter, also called *karma-varganā*) that literally **bonds with the soul** (*jīva*), weighing it down and obscuring its innate qualities of infinite knowledge, perception, bliss, and energy.

## The Soul–Karma Relationship

The soul in its pure state possesses four infinities (*ananta catuṣṭaya*): infinite knowledge, infinite perception, infinite energy, and infinite bliss. Karmic matter, having bonded with the soul, obscures these qualities. Liberation (*mokṣa*) is achieved when all karmic matter is fully shed — not through divine grace, but through disciplined self-effort.

This framework operates as a **natural law** (like gravity), requiring no creator-god or cosmic judge. The universe itself enforces the moral order through the mechanical bonding and fruition of karmic particles.

## The 8 Types of Karma (*Aṣṭa Karma*)

Jain scriptures classify karma into eight principal types, grouped into two categories:

### Ghātiyā Karma — Destructive (Obscuring) Karmas
These directly obstruct the soul's intrinsic qualities:

| # | Sanskrit | Meaning | What It Obscures |
|---|----------|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | *Jñānāvaraṇīya* | Knowledge-obscuring | The soul's capacity for omniscience |
| 2 | *Darśanāvaraṇīya* | Perception-obscuring | The soul's faculty of direct intuition |
| 3 | *Mohanīya* | Deluding | Right faith and right conduct (further subdivided into 28 sub-types) |
| 4 | *Antarāya* | Obstacle-creating | The soul's inherent energy and power |

### Aghātiyā Karma — Non-destructive (Body-related) Karmas
These determine the material circumstances of embodied existence:

| # | Sanskrit | Meaning | What It Determines |
|---|----------|---------|--------------------|
| 5 | *Vedanīya* | Feeling-producing | Pleasure and pain experienced |
| 6 | *Āyuṣka* | Lifespan-determining | Duration of the current birth |
| 7 | *Nāma* | Body-producing | Physical form, species, and bodily features |
| 8 | *Gotra* | Status-determining | Family, social standing, and environment |

> **Note:** The first four (*ghātiyā*) are the primary obstacles to liberation; once destroyed, the soul attains *kevalajñāna* (omniscience). The last four (*aghātiyā*) persist even after omniscience is attained, continuing until the final moment before liberation.

## Sub-types and Complexity

The eight types are further subdivided into 148 sub-species (e.g., *mohanīya* alone has 28 sub-types, including the 16 *kaṣāyas* or passions such as anger, pride, deceit, and greed). This granular taxonomy allows Jain theorists to map every conceivable mental state and life circumstance back to its karmic cause.

## The Four Processes of Karmic Mechanics

These constitute the core dynamic of Jain karma theory:

1. **Āśrava (Inflow)** — The influx of karmic matter into the soul, caused by activities of body, speech, and mind. The "channels" through which karma flows in are activated by the presence of passions (*kaṣāya*).

2. **Bandha (Bondage)** — The actual bonding of karmic particles to the soul. Bondage has four dimensions:
   - *Prakṛti-bandha* — type of karma bound
   - *Sthiti-bandha* — duration of the bond
   - *Anubhāga-bandha* — intensity of fruition
   - *Pradeśa-bandha* — quantity of karmic matter

3. **Saṃvara (Stoppage)** — The prevention of new karmic inflow. Achieved through:
   - *Gupti* — control of body, speech, and mind (three restraints)
   - *Samiti* — carefulness in five activities (walking, speaking, eating, placing objects, and disposing of waste)
   - *Dharma* — observance of ten cardinal virtues
   - *Anuprekṣā* — twelve reflections (*bhāvanā*)
   - *Parīṣaha-jaya* — endurance of twenty-two afflictions
   - *Cāritra* — conduct following the five great vows

4. **Nirjarā (Shedding)** — The partial or complete elimination of already-bonded karma. Two types:
   - *Savipāka nirjarā* — passive shedding through natural fruition
   - *Avipāka nirjarā* — active shedding through ascetic practices (austerities, penance, fasting), which is the preferred path because it accelerates liberation

## Karma and the Doctrine of *Anekāntavāda*

Jain karma theory is intimately linked to *anekāntavāda* (non-absolutism): because karmic conditioning limits each soul's perspective, no single viewpoint can claim absolute truth. The gradual removal of karmic veils allows increasingly comprehensive understanding.

## Key Differences from Hindu/Buddhist Karma

| Aspect | Jainism | Hinduism (Vedānta) | Buddhism |
|--------|---------|--------------------|----------|
| Nature of karma | **Physical substance** (matter) | Metaphysical *saṃskāra* / *vāsanā* | Mental intention (*cetanā*) |
| Agency | Self-effort alone | Grace + effort | Effort + practice |
| Soul | Eternal, unchanging substance | Eternal but modified by karma | No permanent self (*anātman*) |
| Liberation path | Shedding of all karmic matter | Realization of *Brahman* / non-duality | Cessation of craving / *nirvāṇa* |
| Cosmic judge | None (mechanical law) | Īśvara / God dispenses results | None (conditioned arising) |

## Key Sources

- **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (ch. 8) — The foundational text on karma theory; Umāsvāti's systematic presentation of the 8 karmas and 4 processes.
- **Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama** — The oldest Digambara canonical text on karma; detailed treatise on the nature of karmic bondage.
- **Gommaṭasāra Karmakāṇḍa** — Nemicandra's comprehensive manual on the 148 sub-types, duration, intensity, and computational frameworks of karma.
- **Pañcāstikāya** — Kunda-Kunda's work on the five substances, including the nature of karmic matter.
- **Dhavalā** (commentary on Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama) — Virasena's 9th-century commentary providing exhaustive mathematical analysis of karma.

## Significance

Jain karma theory is one of the most detailed and materialist cosmological systems in Indian philosophy. Its contribution to Indian thought includes:
- A rigorous causal framework linking ethics to physics
- A non-theistic moral order that places full responsibility on the individual
- A detailed psychology of passions (*kaṣāya*) as the root cause of bondage
- A practical path of graduated asceticism to systematically eliminate karmic defilement
