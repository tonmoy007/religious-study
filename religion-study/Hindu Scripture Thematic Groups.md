# Hindu Scripture Thematic Groups

A reference guide to the major thematic groups that run through the vast corpus of Hindu scripture — from the Vedas (c. 1500 BCE) through the Purāṇas (c. 500–1500 CE). Each group links texts that share a common subject, theological theme, or narrative arc. Use this note to navigate Hindu scripture by theme and to see how different texts — separated by centuries — engage the same questions.

> [!note] How the groups were built
> The major scriptures of the Hindu tradition (Śruti and Smṛti) were grouped by their dominant theological themes, narrative function, and literary genre. Many texts belong to multiple groups — for example, the Bhagavad Gītā is simultaneously a yoga text, a mokṣa text, a bhakti text, and a dialogue on dharma. The groups below consolidate these into navigable themes. Cross-references to the [[Hindu Stories MOC]] and [[Hindu Figures MOC]] link thematic groups to the narrative and figure traditions.

## 1. Creation & Cosmogony

The beginning of all things — from the cosmic enigma of the Nāsadīya Sūkta to the elaborate galactic cycles of the Purāṇas. Hindu creation texts are among the most philosophically daring in world scripture.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Rig Veda 10.129** (Nāsadīya Sūkta) | "Neither being nor non-being was then… That One breathed by itself without breath" — creation as a mystery, not a doctrine |
| **Rig Veda 10.90** (Puruṣa Sūkta) | The cosmic man (*puruṣa*) is sacrificed; from his body come the Vedas, the elements, and the four varṇas |
| **Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad** 1.2, 1.4 | In the beginning there was only the Self (*Ātman*); it split itself into male and female; from that union all beings arose |
| **Chāndogya Upaniṣad** 3.19 | The world was just Being (*Sat*) — one only without a second; it willed to become many |
| **Aitareya Upaniṣad** 1 | The Self created the worlds, the cosmic person, and the elements by thought alone |
| **Manusmṛti** 1.5–57 | The cosmic egg (*Hiraṇyagarbha*) emerges; Brahmā creates the worlds, time, beings, and caste from the egg |
| **Viṣṇu Purāṇa** 1.2–5 | Viṣṇu as the source; the lotus from his navel births Brahmā who creates the universe |
| **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** 2.5–6, 3.6–20 | The most elaborate cosmology: the puruṣa incarnates as Kāraṇodakaśāyī Viṣṇu; creation unfolds in stages — mahat, ahaṅkāra, tanmātras, senses, worlds |
| **Śiva Purāṇa** 1.7–9 | Śiva as the supreme cause; the liṅga emerges as the mark of the unmanifest |

**Core text:** Rig Veda 10.129 (Nāsadīya Sūkta) — the most quoted creation hymn in Hindu philosophy

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → The Churning of the Ocean, The Great Flood and Manu

## 2. Brahman & Ātman (Ultimate Reality & the Self)

The central philosophical theme of the Upaniṣads: the identity of the individual self (*ātman*) with ultimate reality (*Brahman*). This is the backbone of Vedānta.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad** 3.7 (Antaryāmī Brāhmaṇa) | "He who dwells within all beings, whom all beings do not know… He is the Inner Controller, your own immortal Self" |
| **Chāndogya Upaniṣad** 6.8–16 (Śvetaketu) | *Tat tvam asi* — "That thou art." The great mahāvākya: the essence of the individual is the essence of the cosmos |
| **Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad** 2.2 | Brahman is the self-luminous, the formless, the one who knows all; from Him the universe is born, sustained, and dissolved |
| **Kaṭha Upaniṣad** 1.3.3–15 | The Self is the lord of the chariot; the body is the chariot; the intellect is the charioteer |
| **Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad** 3.8–21 | Brahman as Śiva — "higher than the high, without form, without parts, pure, the light of lights" |
| **Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad** | The syllable *Oṁ* as Brahman — the four states of consciousness (waking, dream, deep sleep, *turīya*) |
| **Taittirīya Upaniṣad** 2 (Ānandavallī) | Brahman as bliss (*ānanda*): five sheaths (*kośas*) covering the Self — from the physical to the blissful |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 7–8, 13–15 | Kṛṣṇa declares Himself as the supreme Brahman; the field (*kṣetra*) and the knower of the field (*kṣetrajña*) |
| **Yoga Vāsiṣṭha** | Consciousness (*caitanya*) alone is real; the world is a long dream from which one awakens |

**Core texts:** Chāndogya 6 (tat tvam asi), Bṛhadāraṇyaka 3 (neti neti), Māṇḍūkya (Oṁ)

**See also:** [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Yājñavalkya, Śaṅkara, Rāmānuja, Madhva

## 3. Dharma & the Order of Life

The concept of *dharma* — duty, law, righteousness, the order that sustains the world — is the organising principle of Hindu ethics.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Rig Veda** — *ṛta* | The cosmic order that upholds truth and law; the gods themselves act in accordance with *ṛta* |
| **Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad** 1.4.14 | "Dharma is the king of kings… there is nothing higher than dharma" |
| **Manusmṛti** (2–12) | The definitive legal code: *varṇāśrama dharma* — the duties of caste and stage of life (student, householder, forest-dweller, renunciant) |
| **Rāmāyaṇa** | Rāma as *maryādā puruṣottama* (the ideal man who upholds boundaries) — his entire life is an enactment of dharma under impossible circumstances |
| **Mahābhārata** — Śānti Parva | Bhīṣma's discourses on *rājadharma* (the duty of kings) — the longest legal-political section of any ancient text |
| **Mahābhārata** — Yakṣa Praśna | The *yakṣa* tests Yudhiṣṭhira on dharma: "What is the highest dharma? Non-injury (*ahiṃsā*)" |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 3, 18 | Kṛṣṇa redefines dharma: do your duty (*svadharma*), but without attachment to the results |
| **Vasiṣṭha Dharma Sūtra** | One of the oldest Dharmaśāstras; natural law grounded in the Vedas |

**Core texts:** Manusmṛti (legal), Bhagavad Gītā 3 (ācāra), Mahābhārata Śānti Parva (rājadharma)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → The Dice Game, Yudhiṣṭhira's Dharma Dilemmas; [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Yudhiṣṭhira, Rāma, Bhīṣma

## 4. Mokṣa (Liberation from Saṃsāra)

The ultimate goal (*puruṣārtha*) — release from the cycle of birth and death. Different schools propose different paths: knowledge (*jñāna*), devotion (*bhakti*), action (*karma*), and yoga.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad** 4.4 | "As a caterpillar, having reached the tip of a blade of grass, draws itself over to a new one, so the self, having cast off this body, draws itself over to a new body" — the mechanics of *saṃsāra* |
| **Kaṭha Upaniṣad** 2.3 | The immortal Self, free from death, is the goal; "when all desires that dwell in the heart are cast off, then the mortal becomes immortal" |
| **Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad** 3.2 | The knower of Brahman becomes Brahman; "he crosses over sorrow" |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 2, 5, 8, 12, 18 | The three yogas (jñāna, karma, bhakti) are three paths to one goal; "fix your mind on Me, you shall reach Me" |
| **Muktika Upaniṣad** | The only Upaniṣad that lists 108 Upaniṣads and teaches the path to liberation through *Oṁ* meditation |
| **Yoga Vāsiṣṭha** (Nirvāṇa Prakaraṇa) | A vast philosophical dialogue on liberation while living (*jīvanmukti*) — the awakened sage who lives free while still breathing |
| **Vivekacūḍāmaṇi** (Śaṅkara) | "Who am I? What is bondage? How is liberation attained?" — a systematic Advaita guide to mokṣa |
| **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** 11 (Uddhava Gītā) | Kṛṣṇa's final instructions to Uddhava — bhakti as the supreme path to liberation in the Kali Yuga |

**Core texts:** Bhagavad Gītā (the three paths), Kaṭha Upaniṣad (the chariot metaphor), Vivekacūḍāmaṇi (systematic Advaita)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → The Bhagavad Gītā; [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Arjuna, Yudhiṣṭhira, Śaṅkara

## 5. Yoga & the Disciplining of Mind

The systematic science of spiritual practice — from the seminal Yoga Sūtras to the integration of yoga in the Gītā and the Upaniṣads.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali** (c. 350 CE) | The eight limbs (*aṣṭāṅga*): yama (ethical restraints), niyama (observances), āsana (posture), prāṇāyāma (breath), pratyāhāra (sense withdrawal), dhāraṇā (concentration), dhyāna (meditation), samādhi (absorption) |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 6 | "Yoga is the equilibrium of the mind" — the yogī who sits like a lamp in a windless place |
| **Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad** 2.8–15 | The earliest systematic description of yoga: "holding the body in three erect postures, drawing the senses into the heart, the wise one should cross the fearful river of saṃsāra on the raft of Brahman" |
| **Kaṭha Upaniṣad** 2.3.10–11 | "When the five senses and the mind are stilled… that is the highest state" — the foundation of *rāja yoga* |
| **Maitrāyaṇīya Upaniṣad** 6.18–30 | The six-limbed yoga — prāṇāyāma, pratyāhāra, dhyāna, dhāraṇā, tarka, samādhi |
| **Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā** (c. 1400 CE) | The classic manual of physical yoga — 84 āsanas, ṣaṭkarma (cleansings), prāṇāyāma, kuṇḍalinī awakening |
| **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** 3.28 | The yogī's progression from āsana to samādhi; the vision of the Universal Form within |
| **Yoga Vāsiṣṭha** (Upāśama Prakaraṇa) | The supreme yoga of knowledge (*jñāna yoga*) — "cessation of mental conditioning is liberation" |

**Core text:** Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali (the canonical authority); Bhagavad Gītā 6 (the Gītā's integration of yoga)

**See also:** [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Patañjali, Kṛṣṇa (as yogīśvara)

## 6. Bhakti (Devotion to a Personal God)

The Bhakti current — devotion to a personal God (Viṣṇu, Śiva, Devī, Kṛṣṇa) — is the most widespread form of Hindu religious practice. These texts articulate the theology, emotion, and practice of loving God.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 12 | Bhakti yoga defined: "Those who fix their mind on Me, who are ever devoted to Me… to them I bring full security" |
| **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** | The bible of bhakti — 18,000 verses of devotion to Kṛṣṇa; the story of Prahlāda, the gopīs' love, the Vṛndāvana līlā |
| **Nārada Bhakti Sūtras** (c. 1000 CE) | 84 aphorisms on the nature of supreme devotion (*parā bhakti*): "Bhakti is intense love for God" — it is beyond knowledge and action |
| **Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad** 6.18–23 | "To the one who has supreme devotion (*bhakti*) for God, and for the teacher as for God… the truths shine forth" |
| **Rāmāyaṇa of Tulsīdās** (Rāmacaritamānasa, 1574 CE) | Hindi vernacular epic — Rāma as the accessible personal God; the most beloved text in North Indian bhakti |
| **Divya Prabandham** (Āḻvār hymns, 6th–9th c.) | 4,000 Tamil hymns of passionate love for Viṣṇu/Kṛṣṇa — "When will I see You? In my heart You dwell" |
| **Tēvāram** (Nāyaṉār hymns, 6th–9th c.) | Tamil Śaiva bhakti — "Dance, O Lord, in my heart" |
| **Gīta Govinda** (Jayadeva, 12th c.) | Sanskrit erotic-spiritual poetry: Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa's love as the soul's longing for God |
| **Sūr Sāgar** (Sūrdās, 16th c.) | Braj Bhasha songs of Kṛṣṇa's childhood and the gopīs' love |
| **Abhaṅgas of Tukārām** (17th c.) | Marathi devotional poetry — ecstatic, intimate, rejecting all pride: "I have no lineage, no virtue, I am low… You are my only refuge" |

**Core text:** Bhāgavata Purāṇa (the most influential bhakti scripture); Bhagavad Gītā 12 (the shortest theological definition)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → Prahlāda and Narasiṁha, Krishna's Birth and Childhood; [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Kṛṣṇa, Rāma, Mīrābāī, Kabīr

## 7. Avatāra Theology (Descent of God)

The doctrine that the Supreme descends into the world in embodied form to restore dharma. Most associated with Viṣṇu (the Daśāvatāra), but also with Śiva and Devī.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 4.7–8 | The classic statement: "Whenever dharma declines and adharma rises, I manifest Myself. For the protection of the good and the destruction of evil, I am born age after age" |
| **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** 1.3, 10–11 | The full list of 22 avatāras (ch. 1.3) — then 10 books focused on Kṛṣṇa's Vṛndāvana līlā as the *pūrṇāvatāra* (full descent) |
| **Viṣṇu Purāṇa** 4–6 | The avatāras as cosmic cycles — each *manvantara* has a different set of divine manifestations |
| **Matsya Purāṇa** | The Daśāvatāra list in its classic tenfold form: Matsya, Kūrma, Varāha, Narasiṁha, Vāmana, Paraśurāma, Rāma, Kṛṣṇa, Buddha, Kalki |
| **Rāmāyaṇa** | Rāma as *avatāra* of Viṣṇu — the story that defines the concept for most Hindus. His humanity is the point: he suffers, doubts, weeps |
| **Śiva Purāṇa** | Śiva's avatāras (veṣas — "disguises"): Bhairava, Vīrabhadra, and 28 others |
| **Devī Bhāgavata Purāṇa** | The Goddess takes avatāras: Durgā, Kālī, Satī, Umā, etc. |

**Core text:** Bhagavad Gītā 4.7–8 (the doctrinal kernel); Bhāgavata Purāṇa (the richest narrative)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → Prahlāda and Narasiṁha, Vāmana and King Bali; [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Viṣṇu, Rāma, Kṛṣṇa, Kalki

## 8. The Goddess (Devī / Śakti)

The feminine divine — as supreme creator, warrior, mother, and destroyer. The Goddess tradition (Śāktism) gives Devī equal or supreme status.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Devī Māhātmya** (Mārkaṇḍeya Purāṇa, c. 5th–6th c.) | The foundational Goddess text: Durgā slays Mahiṣāsura (the buffalo demon); Kālī drinks the blood of Raktabīja; the Devi is praised as the supreme reality |
| **Devī Bhāgavata Purāṇa** (c. 11th c.) | The Goddess as the Supreme — she creates Brahmā, Viṣṇu, and Śiva; the universe is her play |
| **Lalitā Sahasranāma** (Brahmāṇḍa Purāṇa) | A thousand names of Lalitā (the Goddess as beautiful, playful sovereign of the universe) — one of the most chanted of all Hindu hymns |
| **Rig Veda 10.125** (Devī Sūkta) | "I am the Queen, the gatherer of treasures, the first of those who are to be worshipped… I move with the Rudras, with the Vasus, with the Ādityas" — the Goddess speaks in the Vedas |
| **Śrīmad Bhāgavata** 10.2–3 | The Goddess as Yoganidrā: she enables Viṣṇu's sleep during pralaya and is worshipped as Durgā |
| **Chāṇḍī Pāṭha** (daily reading of Devī Māhātmya) | She is the one by whom the universe is upheld — "venerate the Goddess who is all-pervading consciousness" |
| **Tantras** (Kālīkulārṇava, Rudrayāmala, etc.) | The Goddess as *parā devī* — the ultimate reality; *dīkṣā* (initiation) through the Goddess |

**Core text:** Devī Māhātmya (the scriptural foundation of Śāktism)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → The Churning of the Ocean (Lakṣmī), The Descent of the Ganges (Gaṅgā); [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Sarasvatī, Lakṣmī, Pārvatī (the Tridevī forms)

## 9. Śiva — The Lord of Yogi̱s

Śiva as the destroyer-transformer, the archetypal yogī, the lord of the liṅga, the dancer whose *tāṇḍava* creates and dissolves universes.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Śiva Purāṇa** (7 Saṃhitās) | Śiva's glory, the liṅga as the mark of the unconditioned; the story of Bhasmāsura, Dakṣa's sacrifice, the burning of Kāma |
| **Liṅga Purāṇa** | The liṅga as the cosmic pillar — Brahmā and Viṣṇu unable to find its top or bottom; 28 avatāras of Śiva |
| **Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad** | "By knowing Śiva, the Eternal, the one who is hidden in all beings… the wise overcome sorrow" — Śiva as the supreme Brahman |
| **Tirumantiram** (Tirumūlar, c. 6th–8th c.) | Tamil Śaiva scripture: "He is the beginning, the middle, the end — He is the One who stands as all" — 3,000 verses of Śaiva Siddhānta |
| **Śiva Sūtras** (Kashmir Śaivism, c. 9th c.) | "Consciousness is the Self" — the recognition (*pratyabhijñā*) that one's own consciousness *is* Śiva |
| **Śiva Tāṇḍava Stotra** (Rāvaṇa's hymn) | A powerful Sanskrit hymn describing Śiva's cosmic dance — "from the striking of the three drums, the universe dances" |
| **Mahābhārata** 13 (Anuśāsana Parva) | Bhīṣma's thousand names of Śiva — the earliest prototype of the Śiva Sahasranāma |
| **Dakṣiṇāmūrti Stotra** (Śaṅkara) | Śiva as the silent teacher under the banyan tree who awakens the disciple through wordless transmission |

**Core text:** Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad (the earliest Śaiva scripture); Śiva Purāṇa (the narrative corpus)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → The Marriage of Śiva and Pārvatī, The Destruction of Tripura; [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Śiva, Pārvatī, Gaṇeśa

## 10. Karma & Rebirth (Saṃsāra)

The causal law that binds the self to the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth. Hindu texts explore it as both a cosmic mechanism and a spiritual problem to transcend.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad** 4.4.1–7 | The earliest clear articulation of karma: "As a man acts, as he behaves, so does he become. The doer of good becomes good, the doer of evil becomes evil" |
| **Chāndogya Upaniṣad** 5.10 | The two paths after death — the path of the gods (*devayāna*) for the knower, the path of the fathers (*pitṛyāna*) for the ritualist, and rebirth as low creatures for the wicked |
| **Kaṭha Upaniṣad** 1.2.5–6 | The soul is not born and does not die; "one who is born surely dies, and one who dies is surely born" |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 2.22, 4.5, 8.23–27 | "As a person casts off worn-out garments and puts on new ones, so the embodied self casts off worn-out bodies" — Kṛṣṇa remembers his many past births |
| **Yoga Sūtras** 2.12–14 | The karmic store — *karmāśaya*: the accumulated impressions that ripen in future births as class, lifespan, and experience |
| **Mārkaṇḍeya Purāṇa** (Yayāti's story) | The myth of King Yayāti who begs his sons to lend him their youth — a parable of unfulfilled desire and repeated rebirth |
| **Mahābhārata** — Draupadī's curse | Each of the 12 civil years in exile is directly linked to her past karmic choices |
| **Yoga Vāsiṣṭha** | The most systematic treatment: karma is real but it can be dissolved through knowledge; the jīvanmukta is not bound by past karma |

**Core text:** Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad 4.4 (the original formulation); Bhagavad Gītā 2.22 (the most quoted verse)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → The Dice Game, Karna's Fate; [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Yayāti, Karna (the tragic karma figure)

## 11. The Mahābhārata as Dharma-Scripture

The entire epic (100,000 verses) is itself a single vast text on dharma — but certain sections function as independent doctrinal or philosophical works.

| Section | Key Contribution |
|---------|----------------|
| **Bhagavad Gītā** (Bhīṣma Parva, 6.23–40) | Kṛṣṇa's discourse to Arjuna — the most famous Hindu scripture after the Vedas |
| **Śānti Parva** (Book 12) | Bhīṣma teaches Yudhiṣṭhira on kingship (rājadharma), crisis dharma (āpaddharma), and liberation (mokṣa dharma) — the longest philosophical book in world literature |
| **Anuśāsana Parva** (Book 13) | Bhīṣma's final teachings: charity (*dāna*), dharma of women, types of sin, the thousand names of Śiva |
| **Yakṣa Praśna** (Vana Parva, Book 3) | A forest spirit tests Yudhiṣṭhira with riddles on dharma — "What is the wonder of the world? Every day beings go to death, yet the rest think themselves immortal" |
| **Nārāyaṇīya** (Śānti Parva, Book 12) | A Bhāgavata-like section within the epic: the story of Nārada, the avatāras, bhakti to Nārāyaṇa |
| **Vidura Nīti** (Udyoga Parva, Book 5) | Vidura's practical wisdom on statecraft, ethics, and human nature — addressed to the blind king Dhṛtarāṣṭra |

**Core text:** Bhagavad Gītā (philosophical heart); Śānti Parva (political dharma); Yakṣa Praśna (the crown of wisdom literature)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → Mahābhārata stories; [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Vyāsa (the compiler)

## 12. The Rāmāyaṇa as Ideal Life

Vālmīki's Rāmāyaṇa (c. 500–100 BCE) and Tulsīdās's Rāmacaritamānasa (1574 CE) are not just stories — they are scriptural models of the four puruṣārthas: dharma, artha, kāma, and mokṣa embodied in the life of Rāma.

| Book (Kāṇḍa) | Theme | Narrative |
|-----|--------|-----------|
| **Bāla Kāṇḍa** | Childhood & dharma | Rāma's birth, education, the breaking of Śiva's bow, marriage to Sītā |
| **Ayodhyā Kāṇḍa** | Duty & sacrifice | Rāma's exile to honour his father's vow — "I would give up Sītā, Ayodhyā, and life, but I would never break my promise" |
| **Araṇya Kāṇḍa** | Ascetic life & trial | The forest exile, meetings with sages, the kidnapping of Sītā |
| **Kiṣkindhā Kāṇḍa** | Alliance & artha | Rāma's friendship with Sugrīva and Hanumān — the practical politics of reclaiming Sītā |
| **Sundara Kāṇḍa** | Devotion & courage | Hanumān leaps to Laṅkā, finds Sītā, burns the city — the most beloved book |
| **Yuddha Kāṇḍa** | War & restoration | The bridge to Laṅkā, the battle, Rāvaṇa slain, Sītā's trial by fire |
| **Uttara Kāṇḍa** | Tragedy & mokṣa | Sītā's exile, the birth of Lava and Kuśa, Sītā's return to the earth, Rāma's ascent — the ambiguous ending |

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → Ramayana Arc (6 story notes); [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Rāma, Sītā, Lakṣmaṇa, Hanumān, Rāvaṇa

## 13. Purāṇic Cosmology — Time & Ages (Yugas)

The Purāṇas contain a vast, mathematically detailed cosmology: the universe passes through four yugas in cycles of 4.32 million years, over 1,000 such cycles form a single day of Brahmā, and the life of Brahmā lasts 100 Brahmā-years — after which the cosmos is dissolved.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Manusmṛti** 1.64–80 | The four yugas defined: Kṛta (1.728M yrs), Tretā (1.296M), Dvāpara (864K), Kali (432K); dharma declines by one quarter each age |
| **Viṣṇu Purāṇa** 1.3, 6.1–3 | The full time-scale: the *kalpa* (Brahmā's day), the *manvantara* (a period ruled by a Manu), and the dissolution (*pralaya*) at the end of each kalpa |
| **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** 3.11 | The most detailed: the divisions of time from the atom (*paramāṇu*) to the lifespan of Brahmā |
| **Mahābhārata** (Vana Parva) | The kali yuga described: "Brahmins will become greedy… the earth will be ruled by barbarian kings… dharma will be abandoned" |
| **Śiva Purāṇa** (Śatarudra Saṃhitā) | Time as Śiva's dance — the cosmic dancer creates and destroys through the rhythm of the ages |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 8.17–19 | "Those who know Brahmā's day and night know that the universe comes into being at the beginning of Brahmā's day and dissolves at his night" |

**Core text:** Bhāgavata Purāṇa 3.11 (most systematic); Viṣṇu Purāṇa 1.3 (accessible)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → Prahlāda and Narasiṁha, Vāmana and King Bali; [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Kalki

## 14. The Guru (Spiritual Teacher)

The guru is central to Hindu spiritual practice — the human conduit through whom the tradition lives. Scripture, it is said, is only the milk; the guru is the udder through which the milk flows.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad** 1.2.12–13 | "To a student who has approached a teacher properly… the teacher imparts the knowledge of Brahman" |
| **Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad** 6.23 | "To one who has supreme devotion for God and for the teacher as for God, these truths shine forth" |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 4.34 | "Know that by prostrating yourself, by inquiry, and by service, the wise who have seen the truth will instruct you in that knowledge" |
| **Kaṭha Upaniṣad** 1.1–2 | Naciketas approaches Yama (Death) as a teacher: "Please teach me, sir, about what happens after death" |
| **Advaya Taraka Upaniṣad** | Lists the six signs of a true guru — one who can teach the crossing between the worlds |
| **Gurugītā** (Skanda Purāṇa) | A 200-verse hymn on the guru: "The guru is Brahmā, the guru is Viṣṇu, the guru is the supreme Lord Śiva — to that guru I bow" |
| **Saundarya Laharī** (Śaṅkara) | The guru's grace alone awakens the disciple to the reality of the Goddess |
| **Chāndogya Upaniṣad** (Śvetaketu) | Śvetaketu returns home after 12 years of Vedic study, arrogant; his father teaches him the highest knowledge through the famous "tat tvam asi" — the paradigm of guru-śiṣya transmission |

**Core text:** Bhagavad Gītā 4.34 (the classic formulation); Gurugītā (the devotional celebration)

**See also:** [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Vedavyāsa, Yājñavalkya, Śaṅkara

## 15. Non-Violence & Ecological Ethics (Ahiṃsā)

*Ahiṃsā* (non-injury) — to beings, to the earth, to oneself — is one of Hinduism's greatest contributions to global ethics. Its roots are in the Upaniṣads and its fullest expression is in the Mahābhārata and later bhakti traditions.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Chāndogya Upaniṣad** 3.17 | Five abstentions listed: "austerity, charity, uprightness, non-violence, truthfulness" — ahiṃsā as one of the core virtues |
| **Mahābhārata** — Yakṣa Praśna | Yudhiṣṭhira: "What is the highest dharma?" Yakṣa: "Non-injury (*ahiṃsā*)" |
| **Mahābhārata** (Śānti Parva) | "One should never do to another what one considers harmful to oneself. This, in brief, is the essence of dharma." — the golden rule in Indian form |
| **Yoga Sūtras** 2.30 | Ahiṃsā is the first of the five yamas — "when ahiṃsā is perfected, all hostility ceases in one's presence" |
| **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** 7.15 | Prahlāda teaches that the truly wise see all beings as equal — "a dog, an outcast, a cow, and the Lord are seen with the same eye" |
| **Manusmṛti** 4.162, 4.245 | "Non-injury, truth, non-anger, sharing, forgiveness, procreation of children through one's own wife, purity, tranquility — this is the condensed law" |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 16.1–3 | "Fearlessness, purity of heart, non-violence, truthfulness… these are the endowment of one born to a divine destiny" |
| **Taittirīya Upaniṣad** 1.11 | The teacher's final instruction: "Speak the truth. Live by dharma. Do not neglect the welfare of the creatures" |

**Core text:** Mahābhārata — Yakṣa Praśna (the highest dharma); Yoga Sūtras 2.30 (the yogic foundation)

**See also:** [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Yudhiṣṭhira, Buddha (as avatāra of ahiṃsā)

## 16. Dialogue & Philosophical Disputation

Some of the most profound Hindu texts are structured as dialogues — teacher and student, husband and wife, king and sage, God and devotee — where truth emerges through questioning.

| Dialogue | Text | Setting | Significance |
|----------|------|---------|-------------|
| **Kṛṣṇa & Arjuna** | Bhagavad Gītā | The battlefield at Kurukṣetra | God and man debate duty, yoga, and the meaning of life — with an army waiting for Arjuna's choice |
| **Yājñavalkya & Maitreyī** | Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad 2.4, 4.5 | The sage's household | The husband's last teaching: "Not for the sake of the husband is the husband dear, but for the sake of the Self" |
| **Yājñavalkya & Gārgī** | Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad 3.6, 3.8 | King Janaka's court | Gārgī, the woman philosopher, interrogates Yājñavalkya on the warp and woof of the universe |
| **Naciketas & Yama** | Kaṭha Upaniṣad | The house of Death | A boy questions the god of death on what happens after death — the most gripping dialogue in Hindu scripture |
| **Śvetaketu & Uddālaka** | Chāndogya Upaniṣad 6 | The Āruṇi household | The father's patient teaching: "Bring a fig… cut it. What do you see? In that subtle essence, all existence has its self" |
| **Prahlāda & Hiraṇyakaśipu** | Bhāgavata Purāṇa 7 | The demon's palace | Father and son debate faith: "Where does God dwell?" "He is in every atom, in every thought" |
| **King Janaka & Various Sages** | Mahābhārata (Śānti Parva), Yoga Vāsiṣṭha | Janaka's court | A king who attained liberation while ruling tests and is tested by sages |

**Core text:** Kaṭha Upaniṣad (the most dramatic); Bṛhadāraṇyaka 2.4, 3.6, 3.8 (the most profound)

**See also:** [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Yājñavalkya, Gārgī, Arjuna, Prahlāda

## 17. Māyā & the Illusory World

The doctrine that the empirical world is not ultimately real — it is appearance (*māyā*), a name-and-form superimposition on the single reality of Brahman. Most associated with Advaita Vedānta.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad** with Gauḍapāda's Kārikā (c. 6th c. CE) | "The world is like a dream, an illusion, a city in the sky" — Gauḍapāda's *ajāti vāda* (the doctrine of no-origination): nothing was ever born |
| **Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad** 2.1.20 | The Self is like a great tree standing in the world, but it is the inner ruler who cannot be seen |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 7.14–15 | "My *māyā* is divine, hard to cross; but those who take refuge in Me cross beyond it" — māyā is Kṛṣṇa's power |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 13.1–3 | The field (*kṣetra*) and the knower of the field; the body is the field, the Knower in the body is the Supreme |
| **Vivekacūḍāmaṇi** (Śaṅkara) | The world is like a mirage; Brahman alone is real; "Brahman is truth, the world is false, the jīva is none other than Brahman" — the classic *śānti* verse (attributed) |
| **Yoga Vāsiṣṭha** | The world is a long dream (*dīrgha svapna*); waking up to reality is the goal |
| **Śiva Purāṇa** (Vāyavīya Saṃhitā) | Śiva as the supreme reality; the world is his play (*līlā*) — not an illusion in the unreal sense, but a manifestation of his freedom |

**Core text:** Māṇḍūkya Kārikā (the most radical non-dualism); Bhagavad Gītā 7.14 (the theological version)

**See also:** [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Śaṅkara (the systematizer of māyā)

## 18. Renunciation & the Ascetic Life (Sannyāsa)

The fourth stage of life (*āśrama*) according to classical dharma — and the path taken by the Buddha, Mahāvīra, and countless Hindu sages: leaving family, society, and ritual behind to seek liberation.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Saṃnyāsa Upaniṣads** (16 minor Upaniṣads) | Manuals for renunciation: the ochre robe, the begging bowl, the staff, the fire-less life; "One who has abandoned all desires and possessions, who contemplates the Self alone — he is a sannyāsī" |
| **Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad** 3.5 | Yājñavalkya prepares to leave for the forest — "the state of a renunciant is the highest end" |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 5 | "The true renunciant (*sannyāsī*) is neither one who merely abandons rituals nor one who abandons action, but one who renounces the fruits of action" — Kṛṣṇa redefines sannyāsa as inner renunciation |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 18.1–12 | The final chapter distinguishes *sannyāsa* (renunciation of action) from *tyāga* (renunciation of the fruits of action) — the latter is superior |
| **Mahābhārata** (Śānti Parva — Mokaṣadharma section) | Bhīṣma's instructions on the life of the wandering ascetic — sleeping under trees, eating by begging, fixing the mind on the Self |
| **Manusmṛti** 6.33–86 | The rules for the forest-dweller (*vānaprastha*) and the renunciant (*sannyāsī*) — the last two āśramas |
| **Jābāla Upaniṣad** | The shortest Upaniṣad: "What is sannyāsa?" — a direct answer from the Creator; one who has realised the Self may renounce even without the formal rite |

**Core text:** Bhagavad Gītā 5, 18 (the philosophical redefinition); Jābāla Upaniṣad (the practical completion)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → The Great Renunciation (Buddha); [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Śaṅkara, Vyāsa

## 19. Sacred Geography & Pilgrimage (Tīrtha)

The land of India is itself a scripture in the Hindu imagination — every river, mountain, forest, and city has a Purāṇic story and a pilgrimage circuit. The *tīrthas* (fords, crossing-places) are where the divine meets the terrestrial.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Mahābhārata** (Vana Parva — Tīrthayātrā section) | The earliest and longest pilgrimage manual: hundreds of sacred sites across India described, with the merit of each |
| **Varāha Purāṇa** | The Tīrtha section: "By bathing in this river, one obtains the fruit of all pilgrimages" — an encyclopaedia of sacred places |
| **Padma Purāṇa** (Pātāla Khaṇḍa) | Lists of major pilgrimages: the 12 *jyotirliṅgas* (Śiva's self-manifested light-pillars), the 4 *dhāmas* (cardinal abodes: Badrinath, Dwarka, Puri, Rameswaram), the 7 sacred cities |
| **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** 5.16–19 | The geography of Bhārata-varṣa (India) and Mount Meru — the cosmic mountain at the centre of the universe, around which the sun, moon, and stars revolve |
| **Śiva Purāṇa** (Kedāra Khaṇḍa) | The Kailāśa pilgrimage circuit — the mountain where Śiva sits in eternal meditation |
| **Skanda Purāṇa** | The largest Purāṇa — contains the most detailed accounts of pilgrimage circuits for Śiva, Durgā, Viṣṇu, and Gaṇeśa temples |
| **Kūrma Purāṇa** | The Kāśī (Varanasi) section — "The city of Śiva cannot be described; it is liberation itself in the form of a city" |

**Core text:** Mahābhārata Vana Parva (the oldest); Varāha Purāṇa (the most comprehensive)

## 20. Ritual & Sacrifice (Yajña)

The fire sacrifice (*yajña*) is the central ritual of the Vedic religion, and its reinterpretation in post-Vedic texts — as inner discipline, as any selfless action, as the universe itself — is a key theme in Hindu theology.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Rig Veda** (the entire Saṃhitā) | The hymns are composed *for* the sacrifice; Agni is the priest, the messenger, the mouth of the gods. The Soma sacrifice is the archetype |
| **Brāhmaṇas** (Śatapatha, Aitareya, Taittirīya, etc.) | The prose manuals for sacrifice: the building of the fire-altar, the horse sacrifice (*aśvamedha*), the *agniṣṭoma* — the universe is a sacrifice |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 3.9–16 | Kṛṣṇa grounds all action in sacrifice: "The Creator created sacrifice together with humanity" — but redefines sacrifice as any work done without attachment |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 4.23–24 | The highest sacrifice: "Brahman is the offering, Brahman is the oblation poured by Brahman into the fire of Brahman" — internalised, universalised sacrifice |
| **Chāndogya Upaniṣad** 8 | Reinterprets the *agnihotra* (fire ritual) as the offering of one's life-breath into the fire of digestion and meditation — the "life-fire" teaching |
| **Manusmṛti** 2–4 | The householder's daily *pañca mahāyajña* (five great sacrifices): to the gods (homa), to the ancestors (śrāddha), to the sages (Vedic study), to humans (hospitality), to creatures (bali) |
| **Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa** 1.2.5 | The story of how Prajāpati exhausted himself creating the cosmos and restored himself through sacrifice — the paradigm for all ritual |

**Core text:** Bhagavad Gītā 3, 4 (the internalisation of sacrifice); Rig Veda (the original hymnody for sacrifice)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → The Churning of the Ocean (cosmic sacrifice metaphor)

## 21. Divine Love & Erotic Mysticism

The boldest current in Hindu spirituality: the metaphors of human love — longing, union, separation, devotion — are pressed into the service of theology. The soul loves God as the lover loves the beloved.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Gīta Govinda** (Jayadeva, 12th c.) | The most famous: Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa's love in twelve cantos — separation, jealousy, the tryst, the night of love. Sung across India as both poetry and liturgy |
| **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** 10.32–33 | The *rāsa līlā* circle dance: Kṛṣṇa multiplies himself to dance with every gopī, each believing he is wholly hers. "They came to the river Yamunā… they sang, they danced, they became Kṛṣṇa" |
| **Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad** 4.3 | The embrace of the Self: "As a man embraced by his beloved wife knows nothing within or without, so the Self embraced by the Supreme Self knows nothing within or without" |
| **Divya Prabandham** (Āḻvār hymns) | "You are my father, my mother, my lover, my lord… You are everything" — Tamil viraha bhakti (love-in-separation) |
| **Naivedya** of Rabindranath Tagore | "My desires are many and my cry is pitiful, but You saved me with hard refusals" — the modern Bengali poet who revived bhakti as universal poetry |
| **Songs of Mīrābāī** | "I have been pierced by Kṛṣṇa's arrow. When will I see him? The night is dark, and he does not come" — the queen who abandoned her palace for the love of Kṛṣṇa |
| **Sūr Sāgar** (Sūrdās) | Kṛṣṇa's childhood and youth sung simultaneously as divine play and as the intimacy of the devotee's heart |

**Core text:** Gīta Govinda (the artistic peak); Bhāgavata Purāṇa 10 (the theological foundation)

## 22. The Universal Form (Viśvarūpa)

The vision of God as the cosmos — a single form containing all beings, all worlds, all time. The most spectacular theophany in world scripture.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 11 | The supreme theophany: Kṛṣṇa reveals his viśvarūpa to Arjuna — "If the light of a thousand suns were to rise in the sky, that might resemble the splendour of that great Being." Arjuna: "I see You without beginning, middle, or end… All the sons of Dhṛtarāṣṭra, all the warriors on both sides — they rush into Your gaping mouths" |
| **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** 2.1, 3.6 | The cosmic form of Nārāyaṇa — the *virāṭ puruṣa*: the universe is his body; his skin the sky, his eyes the sun and moon, his breath the wind |
| **Viṣṇu Purāṇa** 1.22 | Viṣṇu as the universal form — the earth is his feet, the mountains his bones, the rivers his veins |
| **Rig Veda 10.90** (Puruṣa Sūkta) | The original prototype: the cosmic man whose sacrifice becomes the universe |
| **Śiva Purāṇa** 3.5 | The liṅga as the viśvarūpa: Śiva manifests the entire universe in the form of the fiery liṅga at the beginning of time |
| **Mahābhārata** 5.129 (Sanatsujātīya) | The inconceivable form of the Supreme: "He is smaller than an atom, larger than the largest. He is far, He is near. He is within all, He is outside all" |

**Core text:** Bhagavad Gītā 11 (the narrative+poetic peak); Rig Veda 10.90 (the archetype)

## 23. Women in Hindu Scripture

The portrayal of women across the Hindu canon is deeply ambivalent — from philosopher-queens (Gārgī, Maitreyī) to silent heroines (Sītā), from terrifying goddesses to legal codes that subordinate women. This group collects texts that define, celebrate, or critique women's roles.

| Figure / Text | Key Contribution |
|---------------|----------------|
| **Gārgī** (Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad 3.6, 3.8) | A woman philosopher in King Janaka's court who interrogates Yājñavalkya on the nature of reality — "You are indeed the greatest scholar among Brahmins!" |
| **Maitreyī** (Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad 2.4, 4.5) | "Sir, if I had all the wealth in the world, would I become immortal through it?" — chooses knowledge over inheritance |
| **Sītā** (Rāmāyaṇa) | The ideal woman and the tragic victim: she is abducted, rescued, doubted, exiled, and swallowed by the earth. Her final words: "If I have been true to Rāma in thought, word, and deed, may the Earth receive me" |
| **Draupadī** (Mahābhārata) | The most complex woman in Hindu epic: polyandrous wife, fiercely proud, vengeful after her humiliation — "I have no fathers, no sons, no brothers… I am only Draupadī" |
| **Manusmṛti** 5.147–165 | The most quoted (and most criticised) passage on women: "A woman should never be independent" — in childhood she is under her father, in youth under her husband, in widowhood under her sons |
| **Devī Māhātmya** | The Goddess as supreme, independent, and the source of all gods — the theological counterbalance to legal patriarchy |
| **Sabhā Parva** (Mahābhārata) | The disrobing of Draupadī — the most searing indictment of patriarchy in Hindu scripture: "Whom did you lose first — yourself or me?" |

## 24. The Bhagavad Gītā as Independent Scripture

While structurally part of the Mahābhārata, the Gītā functions as an independent scripture — the most translated, quoted, and commented-upon text in Hindu tradition. It synthesises the philosophical and devotional currents of the earlier tradition and lays them out as a unified path.

| Chapter | Theme | Key Verse(s) |
|---------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Arjuna's despair (*viṣāda*) | "I see no good in killing my own kinsmen" — the entire Gītā arises from this crisis |
| 2 | Sāṅkhya & discernment | "The soul is not born, nor does it ever die" (2.20); "You have a right to action, never to its fruits" (2.47) |
| 3 | Karma yoga | "Action is better than inaction" (3.8); "Do your duty without attachment" (3.19) |
| 4 | Transcendental knowledge | "Whenever dharma declines, I manifest Myself" (4.7); "Even action is a sacrifice" (4.23) |
| 5 | True renunciation | "The true renunciant is balanced, free from duality" (5.3) |
| 6 | Dhyāna yoga | "The yogī should sit alone, with mind concentrated… like a lamp in a windless place" (6.10, 6.19) |
| 7 | Jñāna-vijñāna yoga | "I am the source of all; all proceeds from Me" (7.6); "My māyā is hard to cross" (7.14) |
| 8 | The imperishable Brahman | "At the time of death, remember Me and you shall reach Me" (8.5) |
| 9 | Rāja-vidyā (the royal secret) | "With devotion I respond; offer Me a leaf, a flower… with love, I accept it" (9.26) |
| 10 | Divine manifestations | "I am the Self in all beings; I am the beginning, middle, and end" (10.20) |
| 11 | The universal form | "I see You without beginning or end; the universe is Your body" (11.16) |
| 12 | Bhakti yoga | "Fix your mind on Me — that is the highest way" (12.8) |
| 13–15 | Field & knower; the three guṇas; the world tree | "Know the Supreme as the knower in the field" (13.2) |
| 16–17 | Divine vs. demonic nature; threefold faith | "Fearlessness, purity, non-violence, truth…" (16.1) |
| 18 | Mokṣa-sannyāsa (final summation) | "Abandon all dharmas and take refuge in Me alone" (18.66) |

**Core verse:** 18.66 (the *carama śloka* — the final, essential teaching)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → The Bhagavad Gītā; [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Kṛṣṇa, Arjuna

## 25. Law & Statecraft (Rājadharma)

The duties of the king — the model for all political authority in Hindu tradition. The king is not above dharma; he is its first servant and most accountable guardian.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Mahābhārata** — Śānti Parva (Book 12) | The world's longest political treatise embedded in an epic: *rājadharma* — "the king must be like a father; his subjects are his children" |
| **Mahābhārata** — Rajadharma section | The *daṇḍanīti* (science of punishment): "If the rod of punishment were not wielded, the strong would devour the weak" |
| **Manusmṛti** 7–9 | The duties of the king: taxation, justice, war, espionage, and the seven elements of the state (svāmin, amātya, durga, koṣa, daṇḍa, janapada, mitra) |
| **Kauṭilya's Arthaśāstra** (c. 300 BCE) | Not a religious text but the most influential work on statecraft — the realpolitik tradition: "The enemy's enemy is a friend; the neighbour is the natural enemy" |
| **Viṣṇu Purāṇa** 3.1–7 | The ideal of the *cakravartin* (universal emperor): he rules by dharma, not conquest |
| **Śukranītisāra** (Śukra's polity) | A later (medieval) manual: the king as the embodied protector of the *varṇāśrama* order |
| **Rāmāyaṇa** | Rāma as the ideal king (*rāmarājya*): the king who rules for his subjects, not for himself — "What use is kingship if the subjects are unhappy?" |

**Core text:** Śānti Parva (longest); Manusmṛti 7–9 (most systematic)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → Yudhiṣṭhira's Exile, The Dice Game; [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Yudhiṣṭhira, Rāma, Bhīṣma

## 26. Death, Dying & Afterlife

Hindu scripture offers a remarkably varied picture of what happens after death — from the Vedic heaven of the fathers (*pitṛloka*) to the Upaniṣadic dissolution into Brahman to the Purāṇic geography of heavens and hells.

| Text | Key Contribution |
|------|----------------|
| **Rig Veda** 10.14–15 (Yama hymns) | Yama, the first mortal, became the king of the dead. The hymns send the deceased on the path of the fathers: "Go forth, go forth on the ancient path where our fathers have gone before" |
| **Kaṭha Upaniṣad** (entire) | Naciketas asks Death: "What happens after death? Some say it is, some say it is not. Teach me!" Yama's teaching on the imperishable Self is the answer |
| **Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad** 4.3–4 | The stages of dying: speech merges into mind, mind into breath, breath into fire, fire into the Highest — "the Self, having become a mass of consciousness, descends into a new body" |
| **Chāndogya Upaniṣad** 5.10 | The two paths: the *devayāna* (path of the gods, to Brahman) for the knower; the *pitṛyāna* (path of the fathers, to rebirth) for the ritualist |
| **Bhagavad Gītā** 8.23–27 | "At the time of death, one who departs during the bright half of the moon… attains Brahman; during the dark half… returns to mortal birth" |
| **Bhāgavata Purāṇa** 3.30 | The agony of death: "The dying being, his voice choked, his throat parched, cries out but nobody hears… he is dragged away by Yama's messengers" |
| **Yama Saṃhitā** (various Purāṇas) | The detailed geography of hell: 28 *narakas* for specific sins; the judgments of Yama's court |
| **Garuḍa Purāṇa** | The most famous Hindu text on death: the rites after death, the journey of the soul, the 28 hells, and the means by which the living can help the dead |
| **Śrīmad Bhāgavata** 4.22 | "The self is not born and does not die. Fire cannot burn it, water cannot wet it, wind cannot dry it" — a passage almost identical to Bhagavad Gītā 2 |

**Core text:** Kaṭha Upaniṣad (the definitive dialogue); Garuḍa Purāṇa (the practical manual); Bhagavad Gītā 8 (the synthesis)

**See also:** [[Hindu Stories MOC]] → The Dice Game; [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Yama (the god of death), Yudhiṣṭhira

## 27. Sūtra Literature — The Condensed Tradition

The *sūtras* (aphorisms) are the most condensed form of Hindu scripture — a single thread that strings together an entire system. Patañjali's Yoga Sūtras and the Brahma Sūtras (Vedānta Sūtras) are the two most important.

| Work | Author / Date | Content | Significance |
|------|--------------|---------|-------------|
| **Brahma Sūtras** / Vedānta Sūtras | Bādarāyaṇa (c. 400 BCE) | 555 sūtras in 4 chapters: the nature of Brahman (1), the unchanging Self (2), the means of liberation (3), the fruit of knowledge (4) | The definitive systematisation of Upaniṣadic thought; commented on by Śaṅkara, Rāmānuja, Madhva — each finding their own school in the same text |
| **Yoga Sūtras** | Patañjali (c. 350 CE) | 196 sūtras in 4 pādas: samādhi (1), sādhana (2), vibhūti (powers) (3), kaivalya (liberation) (4) | The canonical text of Rāja Yoga; "yogaś citta vṛtti nirodhaḥ" — yoga is the cessation of mental modifications |
| **Mīmāṃsā Sūtras** | Jaimini (c. 400 BCE) | 12 chapters on Vedic ritual exegesis: the science of interpreting the Vedas correctly | The foundation of Hindu hermeneutics; established rules of textual interpretation adopted by other schools |
| **Nyāya Sūtras** | Gautama (c. 2nd c. BCE) | 16 categories of logic: perception, inference, analogy, testimony — the four pramāṇas | The founding text of Indian logic |
| **Vaiśeṣika Sūtras** | Kaṇāda (c. 3rd c. BCE) | Categories of reality: substance, quality, action, generality, particularity, inherence, non-existence | The atomic theory of the universe |
| **Nārada Bhakti Sūtras** | Nārada (c. 1000 CE) | 84 sūtras on the nature of bhakti: "It is intense love for God" | The classic text of bhakti theology |
| **Kāma Sūtras** | Vātsyāyana (c. 3rd c. CE) | 7 books on pleasure (*kāma*) as a legitimate human end | A reminder that Hindu tradition includes *kāma* (pleasure) as one of the four puruṣārthas |

**Core text:** Brahma Sūtras (the foundation of Vedānta); Yoga Sūtras (the foundation of Yoga)

**See also:** [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Patañjali, Vyāsa (the compiler of the Vedas)

## 28. Hymns of Praise & Liturgy (Stotras)

The *stotras* — hymns of praise — are the most recited texts in daily Hindu worship. Most are in Sanskrit, many are anonymous, and they range from the simplest praise of a deity to the most philosophically dense theology.

| Stotra | Deity | Key Verse / Significance |
|--------|-------|-------------------------|
| **Śrī Rudram** (Kṛṣṇa Yajur Veda) | Śiva | The most ancient Śaiva hymn: "To the Rudra who dwells in fire, in water, in plants… salutations" — chanted daily in Śaiva temples |
| **Puruṣa Sūkta** (Rig Veda 10.90) | Viṣṇu / Cosmic Being | "Thousands of heads, thousands of eyes, thousands of feet" — the original theophany |
| **Śrī Sūkta** (Rig Veda, khila) | Lakṣmī | "She who is seated on a lotus, holding lotuses, garlanded with lotuses… bring me wealth, prosperity, and good fortune" |
| **Viṣṇu Sahasranāma** (Mahābhārata, Anuśāsana Parva) | Viṣṇu | 1,008 names of Viṣṇu — the most popular stotra in Vaiṣṇavism. Bhīṣma chants it on his deathbed; "He who recites this every day attains peace" |
| **Śiva Sahasranāma** | Śiva | 1,008 names of Śiva — "śivāya, śāntāya, sadāśivāya" |
| **Lalitā Sahasranāma** (Brahmāṇḍa Purāṇa) | Devī | 1,000 names of the Goddess as Lalitā Tripurasundarī |
| **Śiva Tāṇḍava Stotra** | Śiva | "From the beating of the drums, the rhythm of the cosmic dance — He dances, the universe dances with Him" |
| **Bhaja Govindam** (Śaṅkara) | Kṛṣṇa | A philosophical lullaby: "Worship Govinda, worship Govinda. When your life's end approaches, grammar rules won't save you" |
| **Nārāyaṇa Sūkta** | Nārāyaṇa | "Everything in the universe — whatever we see, moving and unmoving — is pervaded by Nārāyaṇa" |

**Core texts:** Viṣṇu Sahasranāma (most chanted); Lalitā Sahasranāma (Śākta); Śrī Rudram (Vedic Śaiva)

**See also:** [[Hindu Figures MOC]] → Bhīṣma (who recited the Viṣṇu Sahasranāma), Śaṅkara (author of multiple stotras)

## How to Use These Groups

**To find texts by theme:** Browse the group headings above and follow the references to the major scriptures. For example, group 4 (Mokṣa) will take you from the Kaṭha Upaniṣad to the Bhagavad Gītā to the Vivekacūḍāmaṇi.

**For thematic study:** Read a group sequentially — for instance, trace the theme of creation (group 1) from the Rig Veda's enigma (10.129) through the Upaniṣads' deep philosophy to the Purāṇas' elaborate cosmology.

**For cross-referencing notes:** When writing about a topic (e.g., "karma in Hindu thought"), consult group 10 (Karma & Rebirth) and group 3 (Dharma) to find all relevant texts.

**To understand individual texts:** Each text's cross-reference in the groups above explains *why* it belongs to that theme. Many texts appear in multiple groups — the Bhagavad Gītā, for instance, appears in groups 4, 6, 7, 10, 18, 22, 24 — reflecting its encyclopaedic scope.

**Paired study:** Use alongside [[Quran Thematic Groups]], [[Bible Thematic Groups]], and [[Hadith Thematic Groups]] to compare how the world's major scriptures treat shared themes like creation, prophecy/revelation, ethics, law, eschatology, and the nature of God.
