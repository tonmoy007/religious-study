# The Great Famine and the Digambara Migration

## Overview

The Great Famine of Magadha (c. 300 BCE) stands as one of the most consequential events in Jain history. It precipitated the first major geographical expansion of Jainism beyond its heartland and sowed the seeds of the tradition's most enduring division — the split between the Digambara ("sky-clad") and Śvetāmbara ("white-clad") sects. At the center of this story is Bhadrabāhu, the last Śruta Kevalin (possessor of perfect scriptural knowledge), whose decision to lead a mass exodus southward reshaped Jainism forever.

## Bhadrabāhu's Prophecy

According to Jain tradition, Bhadrabāhu — the eighth Śruta Kevalin and the last person in Jain history to possess complete knowledge of the Jain canon — foresaw a devastating twelve-year famine approaching the Magadha region (modern-day Bihar). He was regarded as the foremost spiritual authority of his time, a master who had attained the highest levels of scriptural comprehension.

The famine was said to be so severe that people would resort to eating seeds meant for planting, and the social order would break down. Monks dependent on alms would find it impossible to sustain themselves in the stricken region.

> **Śruta Kevalin** — A title meaning "possessor of all revealed knowledge." In Jain tradition, the Śruta Kevalins were the fourteen successors to Mahāvīra who preserved the entire canon through memory before it began to be lost. Bhadrabāhu was the last.

## The Decision to Migrate

Bhadrabāhu gathered the monastic community and announced his intention to lead a large portion of the Saṅgha southward, where the famine's effects were expected to be less severe. The proposal created a momentous decision point for the Jain community:

- **The Exodus Group (~12,000 followers)**: Led by Bhadrabāhu, they would travel south to the Deccan plateau and the Kannada-speaking regions (modern Karnataka).
- **The Stay-Behind Group**: Led by Sthūlabhadra (a senior disciple of Bhadrabāhu), they would remain in Magadha to preserve the tradition through the famine.

This was not a hostile split — both groups respected each other's decisions — but geography, isolation, and drastically different circumstances would gradually drive them apart.

## The Migration Route

The journey south followed established trade routes:

1. **Magadha (Bihar)** — Departure point, heartland of the Mauryan Empire
2. **Pāṭaliputra (modern Patna)** — The capital, where the group organized
3. **Ujjayinī (modern Ujjain)** — A major crossroads and cultural center where the group likely stopped and may have established a community
4. **Through the Vindhya ranges** — Crossing into the Deccan plateau
5. **Śrāvaṇabelagoḷa (Karnataka)** — The final destination, which became the spiritual heart of Digambara Jainism

Śrāvaṇabelagoḷa, with its hills and natural caves, provided an ideal setting for a monastic community committed to rigorous ascetic practices. The Chandragiri hill, named after Chandragupta Maurya in Digambara tradition, became a center of meditation and study.

## Life in the South: The Emergence of Digambara Tradition

The southern migrants, living in relative isolation and facing a new environment, developed distinct practices and interpretations:

### The Commitment to Nudity (Digambara)

In the harsh climate of Magadha, the practice of total nudity (acelakatva) — considered the highest form of detachment by Mahāvīra himself — was already demanding. In the more temperate south, the migrant monks intensified this commitment. They argued that a true ascetic should own nothing, not even a garment, maintaining the ideal of complete non-attachment. The term **Digambara** literally means "sky-clad" (dig = direction/space, ambara = garment).

### Preserving the Canon Differently

The southern community preserved the scriptures they had brought with them through rigorous oral transmission. However, without Bhadrabāhu's complete knowledge (he had passed away by the time the canon was formally compiled), they eventually developed textual traditions that diverged from those who stayed behind.

### Monastic Lineages

The southern migration established a continuous lineage of teachers (ācāryas) in the Deccan and Karnataka regions that persists to this day, with figures like:
- **Bhadrabāhu** (the leader)
- **Viśākhācārya** (a successor)
- The **Mūla Saṅgha** and **Kāṣṭhā Saṅgha** lineages that emerged later

## Those Who Stayed: The Preservation Struggle

The monks who remained in Magadha under Sthūlabhadra's leadership faced extraordinary hardship:

- **Alms were scarce**: The famine made daily begging rounds nearly impossible
- **Loss of life**: Many monks and nuns perished from starvation
- **Scriptural erosion**: Without the guidance of a Śruta Kevalin, the canonical knowledge began to fragment

When the famine finally ended, the surviving community in the north found itself in a precarious position. They had preserved what they could of the canon, but gaps had formed. To address this, they convened the **Council of Pāṭaliputra** (c. 300 BCE) under Sthūlabhadra's leadership, where the existing scriptures were compiled into eleven Aṅgas (limbs). This council consciously did **not** include the southern monks, who had not participated in the compilation — a decision that would prove fateful.

> **The Council of Pāṭaliputra** was the first major attempt to codify the Jain canon. The southern community, still recovering and geographically distant, was not represented. The resulting compilation became the basis for the Śvetāmbara canon, while the Digambaras later claimed that the true, complete canon had been lost.

## The Emergence of Differences

The centuries following the famine saw the slow solidification of differences between the two communities:

| Aspect | Northern / Śvetāmbara | Southern / Digambara |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Clothing** | White robes for monks (śveta-ambara = white-clad) | Total nudity for monks (dig-ambara = sky-clad) |
| **Canon** | Accepts the 11 Aṅgas compiled at Pāṭaliputra | Considers the original canon lost; holds later texts by ācāryas as authoritative |
| **Women's liberation** | Women can attain mokṣa directly | Women must first be reborn as men to attain mokṣa |
| **Idol worship** | Both practice, with minor differences in ritual | Distinct ritual traditions centered at Śrāvaṇabelagoḷa and later sites |
| **Key figures** | Sthūlabhadra, the Valabhī council compilers | Bhadrabāhu, later ācāryas like Kundakunda |

It is important to note that these differences did not emerge overnight. For several centuries after the famine, the two communities maintained some contact and mutual recognition. The hardening of the division was a gradual process that accelerated after the **Council of Valabhī** (5th century CE), where the Śvetāmbara canon was finally written down.

## The Chandragupta Connection

A particularly rich — and controversial — element of this story involves **Chandragupta Maurya** (r. c. 321–297 BCE), the founder of the Mauryan Empire.

**Digambara tradition** (recorded in texts like the *Bhadrabāhu Carita* and later inscriptions at Śrāvaṇabelagoḷa) holds that:
- Chandragupta, late in his life, became a disciple of Bhadrabāhu
- He renounced his throne and accompanied Bhadrabāhu on the southern migration
- He ended his days at Śrāvaṇabelagoḷa, practicing rigorous austerities (sallekhanā — the ritual fast unto death)
- The hill at Śrāvaṇabelagoḷa where this is said to have occurred is named **Chandragiri** in his honor

**Śvetāmbara tradition** generally disputes this account, suggesting Chandragupta remained a lay follower and did not become a monk or travel south. The historical evidence is ambiguous:
- The Jain text *Parisiṣṭaparvan* (12th c. CE) by Hemacandra connects Chandragupta to Jainism but does not mention him traveling south
- Inscriptions at Śrāvaṇabelagoḷa (c. 600–900 CE) affirm the tradition
- Greco-Roman sources (Megasthenes, who visited Chandragupta's court) describe a vibrant ascetic culture but do not confirm this specific narrative

Whether historically accurate or not, the Chandragupta connection powerfully symbolizes the prestige and royal patronage that Jainism enjoyed — and the Digambara tradition's claim to deep roots in the south.

## Comparison to Other Famine-Migration Stories in Religious History

The Great Famine of Magadha follows a pattern seen in many religious traditions:

- **The Buddhist Councils** (c. 483 BCE, 383 BCE): After the Buddha's death, monastic councils were convened to settle doctrinal disputes and codify the canon, leading to the Mahāsaṅghika / Sthavira split — a parallel to the Jain schism.
- **The Flight of the Brāhmaṇas to the South**: Hindu Purāṇic traditions describe brāhmaṇas migrating south during periods of persecution or famine, carrying Vedic knowledge to new regions.
- **The Exodus (Hebrew Bible)**: A famine-driven migration that became a foundational narrative for a religious people, though with far more dramatic divine intervention.
- **The Islamic Hegira (622 CE)**: Not famine-driven, but a migration (from Mecca to Medina) that re-established a religious community in a new location, creating a geographical pivot point for the faith.
- **The Mormon Pioneer Migration (1846–1847)**: Persecution-driven migration to the Salt Lake Valley, where isolation allowed the development of distinct theological and institutional practices.

Common threads across these stories:
1. **Crisis as catalyst**: A natural disaster or persecution forces a community to relocate
2. **Geographical separation → doctrinal divergence**: Physical distance allows practices to evolve independently
3. **Canonical anxiety**: The fear of losing sacred knowledge drives compilation efforts (often contested)
4. **Founder-hero figure**: The migration is led by a charismatic authority (Bhadrabāhu, Moses, Muhammad, Brigham Young)
5. **New center, old center**: The original homeland and the new settlement both claim legitimacy

## Legacy and Significance

The Great Famine and the Digambara Migration fundamentally shaped the Jain tradition:

1. **Geographical expansion**: Jainism moved from being a Magadha-centric tradition to one with deep roots in Karnataka, Maharashtra, and later Tamil Nadu.
2. **The emergence of two Jain traditions**: While both Digambaras and Śvetāmbaras trace their lineage to Mahāvīra through the same early teachers, the famine created the conditions for divergence.
3. **Monastic vs. lay emphasis**: The southern tradition, with its rigorous environment, developed a particularly strong emphasis on extreme asceticism; the northern tradition, while still ascetic, accommodated more lay-oriented practices.
4. **The problem of the canon**: The debate over which texts are authentic scripture — and whether the original canon is truly lost — remains unresolved between the sects.

The Chandragiri hill at Śrāvaṇabelagoḷa still bears the footprint of this migration. Every twelve years, when the Mahāmaştakābhiṣeka (great head-anointing ceremony) is performed on the 18-meter statue of Bāhubalī, Jain pilgrims from both traditions gather — a reminder that the river that divided still flows from the same source.

## Key Takeaways

- **Date**: c. 300 BCE (traditional dating varies, some place it during Chandragupta Maurya's reign)
- **Key figures**: Bhadrabāhu (last Śruta Kevalin), Sthūlabhadra (leader of the stay-behind group)
- **Cause**: 12-year famine in Magadha foretold by Bhadrabāhu
- **Route**: Magadha → Ujjayinī → Śrāvaṇabelagoḷa (Karnataka)
- **Consequence**: Geographical, textual, and ritual divergence leading to the Digambara / Śvetāmbara split
- **Debated element**: The Chandragupta Maurya connection (Digambara tradition affirms; uncertain historicity)
- **Historical note**: The division hardened gradually over centuries, not as a single event
