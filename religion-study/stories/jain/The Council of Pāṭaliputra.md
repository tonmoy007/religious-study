# The Council of Pāṭaliputra

> *"We have lost the thread. The words of the Jina are scattered like stars in the sky — and we, without a telescope, cannot gather them again."*
> — Attributed to Sthūlabhadra, opening the Council of Pāṭaliputra

The **Council of Pāṭaliputra** (c. 300 BCE) was the first great attempt to compile and codify the Jain scriptures — the Āgamas — after a century of crisis, famine, and monastic dispersion. Convened by the elder **Sthūlabhadra** in the Mauryan capital of Pāṭaliputra (modern Patna, Bihar), this council marks the hinge-point between the age of oral memory and the age of written scripture, and it established the **Śvetāmbara** textual tradition as a distinct entity.

It is not a clean story of triumphant preservation. It is a story of **loss**, **debate**, **partial recovery**, and the first major crack between the **Śvetāmbara** and **Digambara** communities — a crack that would widen over the centuries until it became a permanent division in the Jain tradition.

---

## The Context: After the Great Famine

The Council of Pāṭaliputra cannot be understood without the catastrophe that preceded it.

### The Twelve-Year Famine

Around **350–300 BCE** (traditional dates vary), a terrible famine struck the Magadha region of eastern India — the heartland of the Jain monastic community. Known in Jain sources as the **Durbhikṣa** (the Great Famine), it lasted **twelve years** and devastated the population. It is sometimes linked to the collapse of the **Nanda dynasty** and the rise of **Chandragupta Maurya** (c. 321–297 BCE), though the chronology is uncertain.

For the Jain saṅgha, the famine was catastrophic — not because monks and nuns starved (though many did), but because the **oral transmission** of scripture was broken.

### The Oral Tradition at Risk

In the earliest Jain tradition, the Āgamas — the teachings of Mahāvīra — were preserved **entirely through memorisation**. The eleven **Gaṇadharas** (chief disciples) had each learned a portion of the teaching directly from Mahāvīra. After Mahāvīra's nirvāṇa (traditionally 527 BCE), this knowledge was passed down through a lineage of **Śruta Kevalins** — "embodiments of scripture" — monks who had memorised the entire canon.

The lineage, as recorded in the Nandī Sūtra and the Sthavirāvalī:

| # | Name | Notes |
|---|------|-------|
| 1 | **Gautama Indrabhūti** | Mahāvīra's chief Gaṇadhara |
| 2 | **Sudharmā** | The Gaṇadhara who transmitted the Āgamas |
| 3 | **Jambūsvāmī** | First post-Mahāvīra head of the order |
| 4 | **Prabhava** | |
| 5 | **Śayyambhava** | |
| 6 | **Yaśobhadra** | |
| 7 | **Sambhūtivijaya** | |
| 8 | **Bhadrabāhu (I)** | The last Śruta Kevalin — see below |

### Bhadrabāhu's Departure

As the famine worsened, the elder **Bhadrabāhu** — the eighth in the line of succession from Mahāvīra and the **last Śruta Kevalin** (the last monk who knew the entire 12 Aṅgas by heart) — made a fateful decision.

According to Śvetāmbara tradition, Bhadrabāhu foresaw the famine and led a large group of monks and laity southward, into the **Deccan** (the Karnataka region), where food was more plentiful. This migration is the origin story of the **Digambara** tradition in the south.

But the critical point for our story is this: **Bhadrabāhu took his knowledge south with him**. The monks who remained in the north — including **Sthūlabhadra**, who was Bhadrabāhu's disciple — were cut off from the master. And the master carried the last complete memory of the Āgamas.

> For the full story of the famine and the migration, see **[[The Great Famine and the Digambara Migration]]**.

### What Was Lost

By the time the famine ended and the northern saṅgha had recovered, **many teachings had been forgotten**. The exact scale of the loss is debated in the sources, but all agree on this:

- The **12th Aṅga** — the **Dṛṣṭivāda** ("Disputation of Views") — was **irretrievably lost**. This text, by all accounts, was the most philosophically dense of the Aṅgas, containing the complete Jain theory of knowledge, logic, and the history of philosophical disputes.
- Portions of the other 11 Aṅgas had been corrupted, fragmented, or simply forgotten by the surviving monks.
- No single monk — with Bhadrabāhu gone — could any longer recite the entire canon.

Something had to be done.

---

## The Convening: Sthūlabhadra's Initiative

### The Man: Sthūlabhadra

**Sthūlabhadra** (literally "the one with a great (stūla) arm (bhadra)") was a complex figure. He was:

- A **disciple of Bhadrabāhu**, trained before the famine
- A monk of immense learning, but **not** a Śruta Kevalin — he had not mastered all 12 Aṅgas
- A pragmatist who understood that the oral tradition was dying and that new measures were necessary

The traditional accounts (Nandī Sūtra, Sthavirāvalī, and later Śvetāmbara chronicles) paint him as a man of authority, resolve, and political skill. He had the respect of the saṅgha, and — crucially — he had the ear of the Mauryan court.

### The Location: Pāṭaliputra

Pāṭaliputra (Greek: **Palibothra**) was the capital of the **Maurya Empire** — the largest and most powerful kingdom in the Indian subcontinent at the time. Under **Chandragupta Maurya** (r. c. 321–297 BCE) and his son **Bindusāra** (r. c. 297–273 BCE), the city had become a centre of administration, commerce, and intellectual life.

Why Pāṭaliputra? Several reasons:

1. **Royal patronage**: The Mauryan kings — particularly Chandragupta — are said to have been sympathetic to Jainism. Jain tradition claims that Chandragupta himself became a Jain monk and ended his life by **sallekhanā** (ritual fasting unto death) at **Śravaṇa Beḷgoḷa** in Karnataka.
2. **Central location**: Pāṭaliputra was at the confluence of Jain monastic communities from across the Gangetic plain.
3. **Stability**: After the chaos of the famine, the Mauryan peace provided a secure environment for a major scholarly convocation.

### Who Attended

The council was not a mass gathering. It was an assembly of **elders** (sthaviras) — senior monks who had survived the famine and who represented the major branches of the northern saṅgha.

The sources do not give a precise list, but the following figures are named or implied:

| Name | Role |
|------|------|
| **Sthūlabhadra** | Convenor and presiding elder |
| **Saṅghadāsa** | A disciple of Bhadrabāhu's lineage |
| **Nāgahastin** | Elder from the Vajji region |
| **Sarvadeva** | Elder from the Magadha region |
| **Mārija** | Elder; possibly a representative of the Āryā Suhastin lineage |

Notably absent: **Bhadrabāhu**, who was still in the south and did not attend (or was not invited). His absence would shape everything.

## The Task: Recollecting the 11 Aṅgas

### Method

The council's method was simple in concept, agonising in practice:

> *The assembled monks would **recite what they remembered** of each Aṅga — passage by passage, verse by verse, story by story — and the council would compare versions, reconcile differences, and produce an authorised text.*

This was a process of **communal recollection** — a Jain version of the Buddhist *saṅgīti* (communal chanting). But unlike the First Buddhist Council (where 500 arhats reportedly recited the entire Dhamma and Vinaya from memory), the Jain council had to contend with **incomplete and conflicting memories**.

### What Was Recollected

The council managed to reconstruct **11 of the 12 Aṅgas** — the core canonical texts attributed to Mahāvīra's direct teaching. They are, in the traditional Śvetāmbara order:

| # | Aṅga | Content |
|---|------|---------|
| 1 | **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** | Monastic discipline, conduct, Mahāvīra's ascetic biography |
| 2 | **Sūtrakṛtāṅga** | Doctrinal exposition, refutation of rival views |
| 3 | **Sthānāṅga Sūtra** | Doctrinal lists grouped by number (1, 2, 3…) |
| 4 | **Samavāyāṅga Sūtra** | Extended lists, numerical groupings |
| 5 | **Bhagavatī Sūtra (Viyāhapannatti)** | The largest Aṅga; dialogues on karma, cosmology, ethics; includes the Mahāvīra-Gosāla story |
| 6 | **Jñātṛdharmakathāḥ** | Parables and stories illustrating Jain ethics |
| 7 | **Upāsakadaśāḥ** | Stories of ten exemplary lay followers |
| 8 | **Antakṛtdaśāḥ** | Stories of those who conquered death and attained liberation |
| 9 | **Anuttaraupapātikadaśāḥ** | Accounts of rebirth in the highest heavens |
| 10 | **Praśnavyākaraṇāni** | Questions and answers on karma and ethics |
| 11 | **Vipākaśruta** | Stories illustrating karmic retribution |
| 12 | **Dṛṣṭivāda** | **LOST** — the 12th Aṅga, on philosophical disputation |

### The Problem of the Lost Aṅga

The loss of the **Dṛṣṭivāda** was not a minor gap. According to later commentaries, this text contained:

- **The complete Jain theory of knowledge (pramāṇa-śāstra)** — the epistemology that underlies all Jain philosophy
- **The history of philosophical disputation** — a survey of the views of rival schools (Buddhists, Ājīvikas, Materialists, Vedists) and the Jain responses
- **The "seven methodologies"** — the *saṭa-bhaṅgī-naya* (the seven-fold schema of conditional predication) in its most developed form
- **The anuyogas** — the complete exposition of the Jain system across four branches: history, cosmology, doctrine, and practice

For Śvetāmbaras, the loss is acknowledged as a tragedy of history. For Digambaras, the loss is understood differently — see the controversy below.

### The Decision to Write

The most momentous decision of the Council of Pāṭaliputra was this: **the recovered Āgamas would be committed to writing**.

This was revolutionary. The Jain tradition — like the Vedic and early Buddhist traditions — was fundamentally **oral**. Scriptures were not books to be read; they were texts to be memorised, recited, and transmitted from teacher to student in an unbroken chain of living voices.

But the council saw that the chain had been broken — or was breaking. Monks died in the famine. Memories faded. The next famine, or the next war, could erase everything.

> *"The voice is fragile. The mind is unreliable. Only what is written remains."*

So the 11 Aṅgas were **written down** — first on **palm-leaf manuscripts** (tāla-patra), the standard writing material of ancient India. This was not the final codification (that would come later, at the **Council of Valabhī** in the 5th or 6th century CE), but it was the **first time** the Jain scriptures existed as physical objects.

## The Controversy: Did They Get It Right?

The Council of Pāṭaliputra is a foundation story for **Śvetāmbara** Jainism — but it is a story of **disputed foundations**.

### The Digambara Rejection

The **Digambara** tradition rejects the Pāṭaliputra council outright. The Digambara position, articulated in texts like the **Ādi Purāṇa** of Jinasena (9th century CE) and the commentary of **Vīrasena** (6th century CE), runs as follows:

1. **The original Āgamas were completely lost**: After Bhadrabāhu's departure, the northern saṅgha lost all authentic memory of Mahāvīra's teaching. The 11 Aṅgas "recollected" at Pāṭaliputra were **fabrications** — well-meaning but unreliable reconstructions.

2. **The Dṛṣṭivāda was not the only loss**: All 12 Aṅgas were lost. The texts that the Śvetāmbaras call "Āgamas" are later compositions, not the authentic words of the Jina.

3. **The true Āgamas are not in books**: For Digambaras, the authentic teaching of Mahāvīra was preserved not in written texts but through an **unbroken lineage of Ācāryas** (teachers) in the south — beginning with Bhadrabāhu and continuing through the medieval Digambara commentators. These teachers transmitted the **essence** (not the letter) of Mahāvīra's teaching, and it is from their writings — the **Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama** (6th-century commentary), the **Kaṣāya Prāhṛta**, and later digests — that the true Jain doctrine can be known.

4. **The written text is secondary**: The Digambara tradition produced its own massive commentarial literature in **Sanskrit** and **Prākrit**, which it considers more reliable than the Śvetāmbara Aṅgas.

### The Śvetāmbara Defence

The Śvetāmbara response, articulated by later commentators like **Abhayadeva Sūri** (11th century) and **Śīlaṅka** (9th century), is:

1. **Partial preservation is not total loss**: The monks who remained in the north had memorised large portions of the canon. The council did not fabricate — it **recollected**, comparing multiple witnesses to arrive at the most authentic version.

2. **Bhadrabāhu himself acknowledged Sthūlabhadra**: According to Śvetāmbara sources, Bhadrabāhu, before departing south, entrusted the continuity of the saṅgha to Sthūlabhadra. The council had his implicit or explicit blessing.

3. **The written text preserves what would otherwise be lost**: Writing is not a corruption — it is a **preservation**. The Council of Valabhī (c. 453–467 CE), which produced the final recension, was simply a continuation of the same principle.

4. **The Digambara "Āgamas" are later compositions**: The Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama dates to the 6th century CE — nearly a millennium after Mahāvīra. If the Digambaras accuse the Śvetāmbaras of late fabrication, the charge can be returned.

### The Unresolved Schism

This disagreement is not academic. It is the **central textual dispute** between the two major Jain traditions:

| Aspect | Śvetāmbara Position | Digambara Position |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Status of Pāṭaliputra Āgamas** | Authentic, though incomplete | Fabrications; the original canon is entirely lost |
| **Number of surviving Aṅgas** | 11 (the 12th lost) | 0 (all 12 lost) |
| **Authoritative texts** | The 11 Aṅgas + later 34 texts = 45 Āgamas in the Śvetāmbara canon | The Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama, Kaṣāya Prāhṛta, and later commentarial works |
| **Role of writing** | Necessary preservation; the written text is authoritative | Writing is secondary; the oral teaching of the Ācārya lineage is authoritative |
| **Implication** | The Śvetāmbara canon is a reliable guide to Mahāvīra's teaching | Only the Digambara Ācāryas preserve the authentic tradition |

Both sides cannot be right. Both sides are, from their own perspective, preserving what is genuinely true. The Council of Pāṭaliputra is the moment this divergence became visible.

## The Significance: What the Council Achieved

### For Śvetāmbara Identity

The Council of Pāṭaliputra is the **first act of Śvetāmbara self-definition**. Before the council, there was simply "the Jain saṅgha" — an undivided community of monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen following Mahāvīra's teaching. After the council, there were **two paths**:

- The **northern path** (later Śvetāmbara), which had the 11 Aṅgas in written form and accepted the council's authority
- The **southern path** (later Digambara), which rejected the council's work and maintained that the true tradition had been preserved elsewhere

The council did not intend to create a schism — but it **made the schism inevitable**. Once the northern saṅgha had a written canon and the southern saṅgha rejected it, the two communities already had different scriptures. Everything else — differences in practice, doctrine, monastic rules, and the status of women — would flow from that foundational divergence.

### The Transition from Oral to Written

The Council of Pāṭaliputra marks a watershed in the **history of Indian scripture**. It is one of the earliest known attempts in India to **write down** a previously oral religious canon.

Compare with other traditions:

| Tradition | Canonisation Event | Date | Form |
|-----------|-------------------|------|------|
| **Jain (Śvetāmbara)** | Council of Pāṭaliputra | c. 300 BCE | First written version of 11 Aṅgas |
| **Jain (Śvetāmbara)** | Council of Valabhī | 5th–6th c. CE | Final redaction of the 45-text canon |
| **Buddhist (Theravāda)** | First Buddhist Council (Rājagṛha) | c. 480 BCE | Oral recitation (written down c. 80 BCE in Sri Lanka) |
| **Buddhist (Mahāyāna)** | Fourth Buddhist Council (Kashmir) | c. 100 CE | Written canon |
| **Jewish** | Council of Jamnia (Yavneh) | c. 90 CE | Finalisation of the Hebrew Bible canon |
| **Christian** | Council of Nicaea | 325 CE | Creedal definition; canon lists later |
| **Islamic** | ʿUthmānic recension | c. 650 CE | Standardised written text of the Qur'an |

What makes Pāṭaliputra distinctive is **the crisis that prompted it**. Unlike the Buddhist councils (which were called soon after the founder's death to settle disputes and ensure continuity), the Jain council was called **after a disaster** — a famine that had already destroyed living memory. It was an act of salvage, not of routine transmission.

### The Preservation of Narrative

One of the most important legacies of the council is the **survival of the Jain story-telling tradition**. The Aṅgas — particularly Aṅgas 6–11 — are filled with **parables, conversion stories, and exemplary narratives**: the layman who conquered his temper, the courtesan who became a nun, the merchant who chose death over dishonesty, the king who renounced his throne.

Without the council, these stories would have been lost. And without these stories, we would have little sense of what it **felt like** to be a Jain householder or monk in the centuries after Mahāvīra. The council preserved not just doctrines but **lives**.

## The Aftermath: From Pāṭaliputra to Valabhī

The council did not solve the problem of scripture — it **deferred** it.

### The Limitations of the First Effort

The texts produced at Pāṭaliputra were:

- **Incomplete** — one Aṅga was missing entirely
- **Unstable** — different recensions existed in different regions
- **Unedited** — the council had collected and written, but not systematically redacted

Over the next several centuries, the Āgamas continued to be transmitted orally alongside the written versions. The written manuscripts were fragile, copied by hand, and subject to scribal error and regional variation.

### The Road to Valabhī

By the 4th–5th century CE, it was clear that a **second council** was needed. The **Council of Valabhī** (in modern Gujarat), convened by the Ācārya **Devarddhi Gaṇin Kṣamāśramaṇa** around 453–467 CE, would undertake the systematic redaction, editing, and codification of the entire Śvetāmbara canon — producing the definitive collection of **45 Āgamas** (11 Aṅgas + 12 Upāṅgas + 10 Prakīrṇakas + 6 Cheda Sūtras + 4 Mūla Sūtras + 2 additional texts).

If Pāṭaliputra was the **first draft** of the canon, Valabhī was the **final edition**.

## Parallels with Other Great Councils

The Council of Pāṭaliputra bears a striking resemblance to other tradition-defining councils in world religious history:

### The First Buddhist Council (c. 480 BCE)

| Aspect | Jain: Pāṭaliputra | Buddhist: First Council (Rājagṛha) |
|--------|--------------------|------------------------------------|
| **Context** | After a famine that broke oral transmission | Immediately after the Buddha's death |
| **Convenor** | Sthūlabhadra | Mahākassapa (presiding); Ānanda (reciting Suttas); Upāli (reciting Vinaya) |
| **Crisis** | Loss of scripture due to death of Śruta Kevalins | Potential for doctrinal deviation after the founder's death |
| **Method** | Communal recollection from multiple sources | Recitation by designated experts (Ānanda, Upāli) with communal confirmation |
| **Result** | 11 Aṅgas written down | Dhamma and Vinaya recited, then transmitted orally for centuries |
| **Controversy** | Digambaras rejected the council's authority | Purāṇa (a later council) — some elders dissented; the Mahāsaṅghika schism emerged |

Key difference: The Buddhist council was held **immediately** after the Buddha's death, when living memory was fresh. The Jain council was held roughly **200 years** after Mahāvīra's nirvāṇa, after the famine had already done its damage. This explains why the Jain council was more improvisational — and more controversial.

### The Council of Jamnia / Yavneh (c. 90 CE)

The Jewish rabbinical council at **Yavneh** (Jamnia), after the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE, faced a similar crisis: with the Temple gone and the priesthood dispersed, how was Jewish scripture and practice to be preserved?

| Aspect | Jain: Pāṭaliputra | Jewish: Yavneh |
|--------|--------------------|----------------|
| **Crisis** | Famine destroyed monastic continuity | Temple destroyed; priestly authority collapsed |
| **Leader** | Sthūlabhadra (elder monk) | Yoḥanan ben Zakkai (Pharisee rabbi) |
| **Task** | Recover and canonise scripture | Canonise the Hebrew Bible (Tanakh) |
| **Loss** | The 12th Aṅga (Dṛṣṭivāda) | Some books disputed (Ezekiel, Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Song of Songs) |
| **Result** | A written canon; identity of the Śvetāmbara community | A defined canon; identity of Rabbinic Judaism |
| **Controversy** | Digambaras reject the canon | Sadducees and Hellenised Jews reject the Pharisaic canon |

Both councils were **crisis responses** that **defined a community's boundaries** by defining its **scripture**. Both produced canons that were accepted by one branch of the tradition and rejected by another.

### The Council of Nicaea (325 CE)

The First Council of Nicaea, convened by Emperor Constantine, addressed a different kind of crisis: doctrinal dispute within a community that already had a scripture.

| Aspect | Jain: Pāṭaliputra | Christian: Nicaea |
|--------|--------------------|-------------------|
| **Crisis** | Loss of scripture | Doctrinal division (Arian controversy) |
| **Task** | Recover the text of the Āgamas | Define the orthodoxy of the teaching |
| **Method** | Recitation and comparison of memories | Theological debate and creedal formulation |
| **Result** | A written canon (first draft) | The Nicene Creed; no single canon of scripture settled |
| **Role of state** | Mauryan capital hosted, but no direct imperial control | Constantine convened and enforced the council; imperial authority directly involved |

Nicaea was about **interpretation** of an existing canon (the Christian scriptures were already written). Pāṭaliputra was about **recovery** of a canon that was almost lost. Nicaea settled a doctrinal dispute. Pāṭaliputra settled a textual crisis.

## Key Figures

### Sthūlabhadra

The central figure of the council. Sthūlabhadra is remembered as:

- A **pragmatist** who understood that oral transmission was no longer sufficient
- A **leader** who could command the respect of the divided northern saṅgha
- A **bridge** between the generation of Bhadrabāhu and the post-famine generation

He is also a controversial figure. Some Digambara sources portray him as well-meaning but mistaken — a man who tried to reconstruct what he could not remember, and whose work therefore introduced error into the tradition.

In Śvetāmbara hagiology, Sthūlabhadra is a saint — a **Sthavira** (elder) whose wisdom preserved the Dhamma when it was at its most fragile.

### Bhadrabāhu (the Last Śruta Kevalin)

Though absent from the council, Bhadrabāhu is its **shadow figure**. His departure south is the reason the council was necessary. His knowledge — the last complete memory of the 12 Aṅgas — was unavailable.

In Digambara tradition, Bhadrabāhu is the saviour of the true Jain tradition — the Ācārya who preserved the authentic teaching by migrating south and establishing a lineage that would produce the Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama.

In Śvetāmbara tradition, Bhadrabāhu is also revered, but his departure is seen as unfortunate — a choice that, however justified, left the northern saṅgha in a difficult position.

### Saṅghadāsa and the Other Elders

The supporting elders — Saṅghadāsa, Nāgahastin, Sarvadeva, Mārija — are less famous but no less important. They represent the **regional diversity** of the early Jain community: monks from Magadha, Vajji, Aṅga, and other regions, each bringing fragments of a shared tradition.

## The Meaning of the Story

The Council of Pāṭaliputra is a story about **what happens when memory fails**.

It is not a triumphalist narrative. No one at the council claimed that they had perfectly recovered everything. The 12th Aṅga was gone, and everyone knew it. The best they could do was to gather the fragments, write them down, and hope that what remained was enough.

And in a sense, it was enough. The 11 Aṅgas — even incomplete — contain the core of Jain teaching: the nature of the soul, the mechanics of karma, the path of the three jewels, the biographies of the Tīrthaṅkaras, the parables of lay and monastic life, the cosmology of the universe, and the ethical disciplines that lead to liberation.

The council also tells us something about **how traditions work**. A tradition is not a fixed deposit of truth that passes unchanged from generation to generation. It is a **living transmission** — and a living thing can be wounded, can lose parts of itself, can recover and adapt. The council was the Jain tradition's first great act of **self-preservation under pressure**.

Finally, the council is a story about **the power of writing**. When the oral tradition broke, writing saved what remained. The palm-leaf manuscripts of Pāṭaliputra have long since crumbled to dust — but the tradition they preserved, the texts they fixed, the community they defined — those survive.
