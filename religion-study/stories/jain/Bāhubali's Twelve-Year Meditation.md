# Bāhubali's Twelve-Year Meditation

## The Prince of Takkasilā

Bāhubali was the second son of Ṛṣabhanātha, the first Tīrthaṅkara of this descending half of the cosmic time-cycle. His mother was Sunandā, one of Ṛṣabha's two principal queens. From childhood, Bāhubali displayed extraordinary strength, pride, and ambition — qualities befitting a warrior-prince of the Ikshvāku dynasty.

When Ṛṣabha grew old and resolved to renounce the world, he divided his vast kingdom between his two eldest sons. To Bharata, the eldest, he gave the capital city of Ayodhyā and the lion's share of the realm. To Bāhubali, he granted the northwestern territories centred on the splendid city of Takkasilā (Taxila) — a great seat of learning and power in its own right. For many years, the brothers ruled their respective domains in peace, each attended by the finest scholars, warriors, and ministers of the age.

## The Cakraratna Stops

Bharata, however, was not content with half a kingdom. He aspired to become a *cakravartin* — a universal emperor — whose authority would extend over all seven continents of the Jambūdvīpa world-system. By the grace of his past merit, the wheel-jewel (*cakraratna*) appeared before him, the first sign that universal dominion was within reach. This divine war-wheel, radiant and invincible, would roll unimpeded across any territory that rightfully belonged to a cakravartin, and the armies of those lands would submit without a fight.

Bharata followed the wheel-jewel as it rolled eastward, conquering kingdom after kingdom. But when the wheel reached the border of Bāhubali's realm, it stopped. It spun in place, humming, but would advance no further. The message was unmistakable: Bāhubali was no mere chieftain to be subdued. He was an equal — a sovereign whose power matched Bharata's own.

Bharata's pride would not allow him to accept this. He sent an ultimatum to his brother: acknowledge Bharata's supremacy, or face war. Bāhubali, young and proud, refused. "I bow to no man," he is said to have replied. "Not even my elder brother."

## The War of the Brothers

And so the unthinkable happened — brother marched against brother. The two great armies met on a vast plain, the banners of Ayodhyā fluttering against those of Takkasilā. But the ministers and sages on both sides saw the catastrophe that was about to unfold: thousands would die because of the rivalry of two princes. They proposed a solution — a duel between the brothers themselves, three contests of skill and strength, with the outcome deciding the war. No armies need be slaughtered. No cities need burn.

Bharata and Bāhubali agreed.

### The First Contest: The Gaze

The brothers stood at a distance of one hundred bows' length, facing each other. They locked eyes. The contest was simple: the first to blink, to look away, to break the stare, would lose.

For hours they stood, motionless as statues, their gazes locked in silent combat. The sun climbed to its zenith and began its descent. Sweat beaded on their brows. Their servants brought them water and food, but neither would touch it, lest they lose concentration. Bharata's eyes grew bloodshot; Bāhubali's did not waver. At last, as the afternoon shadows lengthened, Bharata blinked — a single, involuntary flutter. The first contest went to Bāhubali.

### The Second Contest: The Water Battle

Now they entered a lake, waist-deep in cool water. The object was to force one's opponent out of the lake, using only the power of water — splashing, wave-making, the churning of limbs. No physical blows were permitted.

Bharata struck the water with his massive arms, sending walls of foam crashing toward his younger brother. Bāhubali answered in kind. The lake churned like a stormy sea. Fish fled to the depths. The watchers on the shore could barely distinguish the two figures through the spray. For the better part of a day they wrestled with the element itself, but in the end Bāhubali's superior stamina prevailed. Bharata, exhausted, was driven to the shallows and forced to concede. The second contest, too, was Bāhubali's.

### The Third Contest: The Wrestling Match

The final contest was primal: a wrestle to the death. The brothers shed their ornaments and upper garments, binding their loincloths tight. They circled each other on the dry riverbank, their bodies gleaming with oil, their breath coming deep and even.

They closed.

For a time, they seemed evenly matched — elder strength against younger vigour, seasoned skill against raw power. They grappled and strained, each trying to lift, throw, or pin the other. The earth shook beneath their feet. But Bāhubali was stronger. He lifted Bharata clean off the ground, spun him, and slammed him onto the hard-packed earth. Before Bharata could rise, Bāhubali seized him by the leg and dragged him along the ground, meaning to dash him against a nearby rock and end his life then and there.

## The Buddha-dṛṣṭi and the Renunciation

But in that moment of triumph, something broke open within Bāhubali. As he raised his brother above his head, his eyes met Bharata's — and he saw not an enemy, but his own blood. He saw the kingdom they had fought over, the pride that had driven them, the violence that had filled their hearts. A profound revulsion arose in him — not at his brother, but at the whole machinery of conquest and possession.

He saw, with sudden clarity, that the very thing he had been fighting for — dominion, power, the acclaim of men — was the very source of suffering. He had won. And winning felt hollow. Empty. Worse than empty — it felt like poison.

Releasing his brother gently, Bāhubali stepped back. He did not help Bharata to his feet; he did not speak. He simply turned and walked away from the battlefield, from the armies, from Takkasilā, from everything he had ever known.

He went to a forest grove and, stripping off his royal garments, pulled out his hair in a gesture of renunciation — the *keśa-loca* still practised by Jain ascetics today. He stood in the *kāyotsarga* posture — arms hanging straight at his sides, legs straight, body utterly still, the posture of complete abandonment of the body. And there he remained, without moving, without eating, without drinking, lost in the deepest meditation the world had ever seen.

## The Twelve-Year Vigil

The seasons turned. The rains came and went. The leaves of the trees around him fell and regrew twelve times. And all the while, Bāhubali stood — a statue carved from living flesh.

In time, the natural world began to reclaim him. Creepers and vines crept up his legs, twining around his calves and thighs. Ants built their hills against his feet, the earth rising slowly around his ankles, then his knees. Serpents coiled in the hollows of his arms, mistaking his motionless body for an ancient tree. His hair grew long and wild, matted with dust and dew. Birds perched upon his shoulders. His skin grew tough as bark, weathered by sun and wind and rain.

His sister, the wise and learned Brāhmī, had heard of her brother's renunciation and came searching for him through the forests. For days she wandered, calling his name, until at last she came upon a strange figure half-buried in the earth, overgrown with vegetation, indistinguishable from the surrounding wilderness. Only when she drew close did she see the faint rise and fall of breath, the slight gleam of living eyes through the tangle of hair and vines.

She wept at the sight. Gently, with infinite care, she began to pull away the creepers and vines that had grown into his flesh. She brushed away the anthills. She coaxed the snakes to depart. And as each vine was removed, each obstruction cleared, the meditation of Bāhubali deepened toward its final goal.

The moment the last vine was pulled from his leg, the last obstruction removed, Bāhubali's meditation reached its culmination. All karmic bonds shattered. The four types of knowledge — sensory, scriptural, clairvoyance, and mind-reading — unfolded in their perfect fullness, and with them the final, absolute knowledge of all things in all times and all places: *Kevala Jñāna*.

Bāhubali became a *Kevalin* — a liberated soul while still embodied, an omniscient being.

## The Legacy

The story of Bāhubali's twelve-year meditation and his attainment of omniscience has inspired Jains for millennia. The great statue of Bāhubali (also known as Gommateshvara) at Shravanabelagola in Karnataka stands 57 feet tall, carved from a single block of granite — the tallest monolithic statue in the world. It depicts the prince precisely as Brāhmī found him: standing in *kāyotsarga*, utterly still, the stillness of absolute peace.

Every twelve years, the *Mahamastakabhisheka* festival is held there, during which the statue is anointed from head to toe with water, milk, sugarcane juice, saffron paste, and hundreds of other sacred substances — a ritual re-enactment of Brāhmī's loving removal of the vines, a celebration of the moment when stillness gave birth to omniscience.

Bāhubali's story teaches that victory over oneself is infinitely greater than victory over others — and that the longest war, the deepest meditation, and the most profound peace all begin the same way: with the courage to let go.
