# Ṛṣabha and the Civilising of Humanity

> *"There was a time when the kalpavṛkṣas yielded all that humans desired — honey, fruit, clothing, ornaments. But when those trees ceased to give, darkness fell upon the world. Then the first Tīrthaṅkara was born, not to escape the world, but to build it."*
> — Ādīpurāṇa (adapted)

---

## The Age of the Wish-Fulfilling Trees

Before Ṛṣabha, in the distant past of the present **avaspiṇī** (descending half of the time-cycle), humanity lived in a golden age. The earth was covered with **kalpavṛkṣas** — "wish-fulfilling trees" that bore whatever the heart desired. No one planted, sowed, or harvested. The trees provided sweet honey, ripe fruit, fine cloth, and ornaments — all without labour. There was no hunger, no thirst, no cold.

There was no need for **kings** — no laws, no armies, no prisons. There was no **violence** — for what reason could there be to harm another when everything was freely given? There was no **writing** — for what needed to be recorded when memory sufficed? There was no **counting** — for what needed to be measured when there was no scarcity? Humans lived like gods, drifting through endless summers in a world without want.

> *"In that age, the kalpavṛkṣas were the mothers and fathers of humanity. They gave food to the hungry, clothes to the naked, shelter to the homeless. No being took what was not given. No hand was raised in anger. The earth was a garden of infinite delight."*
> — Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra, Book I

This was the **Sukumāla** (Happy) age — the first of six eras in the descending cycle. It was a paradise, but like all paradises in Jain cosmology, it was **impermanent**. The universe moves in vast, impersonal cycles: the utsarpiṇī (ascending half) and the avaspiṇī (descending half), each divided into six spokes of time. The golden age was already beginning to fade.

---

## The Decline of the Trees

As the ages passed, the kalpavṛkṣas began to **yield less**. The trees still bore fruit, but the fruit was smaller, less sweet. The honey grew thin. The cloth grew coarse. The gems and ornaments no longer appeared.

Humanity was **not prepared**. For eons they had never planted a seed, built a shelter, or stored a harvest. They did not know how to cook, weave, count, or write. They had no concept of property, leadership, or law. They stood before the fading trees like children whose mother had fallen silent — bewildered and afraid.

Arguments broke out. Hunger appeared for the first time, and with hunger came **greed**. With greed came **conflict**. The paradise of the kalpavṛkṣas had been possible only because there was no scarcity; now scarcity had entered the world, and with it, the seeds of violence, theft, and suffering.

> *"When the kalpavṛkṣas ceased to yield, the people wept. They gathered in despair and cried out: 'Who will save us? Who will teach us what to do?' Their cries reached the heavens."*

The gods themselves looked down and saw that the age of the trees was over. A new kind of being was needed — one who could **show humanity how to live** in a world of limits, work, and consequence. The first Tīrthaṅkara was about to be born.

---

## The Birth of Ṛṣabha

**Ṛṣabha** — whose name means "the Bull" or "the Foremost" — was born in the city of **Vinītā** (later Ayodhyā) to King **Nābhi** and Queen **Mārudevī** of the Ikshvāku dynasty. His birth was no ordinary event. The gods themselves descended from their celestial realms to celebrate it. **Indra**, the king of the gods, performed the customary **abhiṣeka** (consecration) on the infant, anointing him on **Mount Meru** — the cosmic mountain at the centre of the Jain universe.

The child was marked with the **śrīvatsa** (auspicious mark) on his chest and bore the sign of the **bull** on his thigh — the emblem that would identify him as the first Tīrthaṅkara of this age. His mother Mārudevī had fourteen **auspicious dreams** before his birth — the same fourteen dreams that would later appear to Triśalā, the mother of Mahāvīra: a white elephant, a lion, the goddess Śrī, a garland, the moon, the sun, a pair of fish, a full vase, a lotus lake, the ocean, a celestial palace, a heap of jewels, and a smokeless fire.

He grew to be magnificent — a hundred and twenty metres tall (in the Jain tradition of the time), radiant as the sun, strong as a bull. He married **Yaśasvatī** (also called **Sunandā**) and had ninety-nine sons and two daughters. His eldest son was **Bharata**; his second was **Bāhubali**. His daughters were **Brāhmī** and **Sundarī**.

But Ṛṣabha was born for more than kingship. He was born because the age of the wish-fulfilling trees had ended, and humanity needed **a teacher who could civilise**.

---

## The Civilising of Humanity

When Ṛṣabha saw the suffering of the people — their hunger, their confusion, their conflict — he did not retreat into meditation. He did not preach renunciation to starving people. He **first taught them how to live**.

This is what makes Ṛṣabha unique among Tīrthaṅkaras. Later ford-makers would teach the path to liberation — the great vows, the fourfold refuge, the nature of karma and the soul. But Ṛṣabha had to teach **everything** first: the arts of survival, organisation, and society. He is not only the first spiritual teacher of this age but its **first king, first farmer, first artisan, and first lawgiver**.

### Agriculture and the Gift of Food

The people were hungry. The kalpavṛkṣas had stopped giving. Ṛṣabha taught them to **plough the earth** — to break the soil, sow seeds, irrigate the fields, and harvest grain. He taught them to distinguish edible plants from poisonous ones, to store grain for lean seasons, and to cook food over fire.

> *"He took the plough in his own hands and showed them how to cut the earth. He sowed the first seeds, watered them, and waited. When the grain sprouted, the people marvelled — they had never seen food grow from the ground. Ṛṣabha said: 'The earth is your mother. Treat her with care, and she will feed you.'"*
> — Ādīpurāṇa 17

He taught them to domesticate animals — cows for milk, oxen for ploughing, buffalo for labour. He showed them how to churn butter, how to prepare ghee, how to make yoghurt. He taught the **sixty-four arts of food preparation**.

### Counting, Writing, and the Preservation of Knowledge

When people began to produce surplus grain, they needed to **count** it, to measure it, to record debts and exchanges. Ṛṣabha taught them **saṅkhyā** — the science of numbers. He taught them writing.

His daughters **Brāhmī** and **Sundarī** became the first scribes. According to Jain tradition, Brāhmī gave her name to the **Brāhmī script** — the ancestor of most writing systems in South and Southeast Asia. Sundarī mastered a second script. Ṛṣabha declared that knowledge must be preserved beyond memory, that the word must be made visible and lasting.

> *"Ṛṣabha said: 'What is spoken is lost on the wind. What is written remains for the wise to read.' And he gave them the art of letters — the ā, the ā, the i, the ī — the sounds of the world made permanent."*
> — Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra I.3

### Law, Kingship, and the Social Order

With property came **disputes**. With stored grain came **theft**. With surplus came **inequality**. Ṛṣabha saw that humanity, without the protection of the kalpavṛkṣas, needed structure. He established the first system of **law** — defining property rights, punishments for theft, rules for marriage, and the duties of each social class.

He did not invent the four *varṇas* (castes) in the rigid Brahmanical sense, but he **divided society by function** — those who rule (kṣatriyas), those who trade (vaiśyas), those who serve (śūdras). The brāhmaṇas, in his system, were not a priestly class born to privilege but those who had **renounced the world** to seek liberation — a spiritual not a social category.

He taught the principles of **daṇḍa-nīti** — the rule of law enforced by just punishment. He said: *"Where there is no king, the strong devour the weak. Where there is no law, the wise are silent and the wicked thrive."*

### Crafts, Arts, and City-Building

He taught them to build cities — to cut stone, burn bricks, raise walls, and dig moats. He showed them how to weave cloth from cotton and silk, how to tan leather, how to forge metal tools and weapons. He taught them the arts of painting, music, dance, and theatre — the sixty-four kalās (classical arts) that would later define Indian civilisation.

He himself became the first **cakravartin** (universal monarch) — not through conquest but through the sheer natural authority of his wisdom and compassion. His rule was just, his kingdom prosperous, and his people flourished.

### The Division of the Kingdom

When Ṛṣabha decided to renounce the world — to become the first **Jain monk** of this age — he divided his vast kingdom among his **one hundred children** (ninety-nine sons and one further descendant counted in some traditions). To his eldest son **Bharata** he gave the capital city **Ayodhyā** and the heart of the kingdom. Bharata would later become the first **cakravartin** (wheel-turning emperor) of this age — conquering the six continents and ruling the entire world.

To **Bāhubali**, the second son, he gave a region to the south — the prosperous city of **Podanapur** (or Takshaśilā, according to some accounts). The other sons received smaller territories.

Ṛṣabha divided his kingdom not as a reward but as a **test**. He knew that Bharata's ambition would drive him to seek universal dominion, and that Bāhubali's pride would resist. The division was the seed of a future conflict — a story of renunciation that would follow the story of civilisation.

This division is recorded in the **Ādīpurāṇa** and the **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (the "Lives of the Sixty-Three Illustrious Men") by Ācārya Hemacandra. The stories of Bharata's conquest and Bāhubali's epic meditation form their own independent narratives.

## The Renunciation

Having given humanity everything it needed to survive and thrive — agriculture, writing, law, arts, cities, and governance — Ṛṣabha looked upon his work and saw that it was **complete**. The people no longer needed him to manage their material lives. Now they needed something else.

He called his sons and his people together. He told them that he had fulfilled his duty as a king, a father, and a teacher of the worldly arts. But there was another teaching — a **deeper one** — that he had not yet given. The soul, he said, does not live by grain and law alone. The soul needs **liberation**.

> *"I have taught you how to live. Now I must teach you how to die — or rather, how not to be born again. The kingdom I leave to Bharata. The path I leave to all of you."*

He removed his royal garments, tore out his hair in five handfuls (the **keśa-loca** — the traditional Jain renunciation), and became the first **nirgrantha** ("one without ties") of this age — a **Jain monk**. In the Jain tradition, Ṛṣabha is **Ādinātha** — the First Lord, the primordial teacher.

He spent the remainder of his life in complete **asceticism** — wandering, meditating, fasting, and teaching. He attained **kevala-jñāna** (omniscience) — the direct, perfect knowledge of all reality — and finally **mokṣa** (liberation) on Mount **Aṣṭāpada** (now identified with Mount Kailash in Tibetan tradition), becoming a **siddha** — a fully liberated soul.

---

## Ṛṣabha as Culture-Hero

Ṛṣabha is not merely a Tīrthaṅkara — a ford-maker who shows the way across the river of saṃsāra. He is also a **culture-hero** — a figure who brings the foundational arts of civilisation to humanity at the dawn of history. This role distinguishes him from later Tīrthaṅkaras and aligns him with similar figures across world traditions:

| Tradition | Figure | Domain | Comparison |
|-----------|--------|--------|------------|
| **Greek** | **Prometheus** | Fire, craft, civilisation | Prometheus stole fire from the gods and gave humans the arts of civilisation — metalworking, writing, agriculture, medicine. He was punished for it. Ṛṣabha, by contrast, acted not in defiance of the gods but in harmony with cosmic order. |
| **Hindu** | **Manu** | Law, social order, progenitor | Manu is the first man, the lawgiver (Manusmṛti), and the survivor of the flood who repopulates the earth. Ṛṣabha shares the lawgiving role but is not a survivor of a catastrophe — he is a teacher who responds to gradual decline. |
| **Mesopotamian** | **Oannes** / Uan | Writing, law, arts | Oannes, the fish-bodied sage, emerged from the sea each day to teach humans writing, law, and the building of cities. Like Ṛṣabha, he appeared at the dawn of civilisation to bring the tools of culture. |
| **Chinese** | **Shennong** | Agriculture, herbal medicine | Shennong, the "Divine Farmer," taught agriculture, identified medicinal herbs, and invented the plough. Like Ṛṣabha, his first gift to humanity was the ability to grow food. |
| **Egyptian** | **Osiris** | Law, agriculture, civilisation | Osiris taught the Egyptians agriculture, law, and the worship of the gods before being killed by his brother Set. Both Ṛṣabha and Osiris are civilising kings who teach before passing into another realm. |
| **Abrahamic** | **Adam** | Naming, dominion | Adam names the animals and is given dominion over the earth. The naming parallels Ṛṣabha's role as the one who classifies and orders the world, but Adam is a first *human* made by God, whereas Ṛṣabha is a divine soul who chooses to incarnate as a teacher. |

### What Makes Ṛṣabha Distinct

Despite these parallels, Ṛṣabha's civilising role is framed in a uniquely Jain cosmology:

1. **No rebellion, no punishment.** Unlike Prometheus, Ṛṣabha does not steal from the gods. He acts within the natural order of the descending time-cycle. The gods themselves honour and support him.

2. **Civilisation as a temporary necessity.** Ṛṣabha teaches worldly arts *first* so that he can teach spiritual ones *later*. The material world is not an enemy — it must be built before it can be transcended. Agriculture, writing, and law are not ultimate goods but necessary foundations.

3. **The teacher renounces what he builds.** Having given humanity its entire culture, Ṛṣabha walks away from it. He becomes a naked ascetic, homeless and possessionless. The king who built cities lives in the forest. The teacher of crafts owns nothing. This embodies the Jain paradox: build civilisation with compassion, then renounce it with detachment.

4. **No divine revelation, only human effort.** Ṛṣabha does not receive his teaching from a god or a voice in the sky. His knowledge comes from his **omniscience** — the direct perception of reality that all liberated souls can attain. He teaches not because an external being commanded it, but because he sees what needs to be done and does it.

> *"Ṛṣabha is the first link in the chain of ford-makers. He did not receive the teaching from a teacher — he rediscovered it himself, in the darkness of the fallen age, and passed it on. All later Tīrthaṅkaras are his heirs."*
> — Tattvārtha Sūtra (commentary)

---

## Legacy

Ṛṣabha's civilising mission shapes the entire Jain understanding of history. The world is not a fallen place from which we must escape — it is a place that a compassionate teacher **built for us**, knowing it would not last, knowing we would need to leave it.

The Jain tradition holds that the **hundred sons of Ṛṣabha** became the founders of the hundred clans of humanity. **Bharata** gave his name to **Bhārata-varṣa** (India). **Bāhubali** became the model of the ascetic who renounces even the victory his brother craves. **Brāhmī** gave her name to the script that recorded the Vedas, the Buddhist suttas, and the Jain Āgamas.

The story of Ṛṣabha is preserved in its fullest form in two great works:

- **Ādīpurāṇa** ("The First Purāṇa") by **Jinasena** (9th century CE) — a Digambara epic of 36,000 verses that tells Ṛṣabha's life as the archetypal story of the soul's journey from worldly engagement to liberation.
- **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** ("The Lives of the Sixty-Three Illustrious Men") by **Hemacandra** (12th century CE) — a Śvetāmbara compendium that places Ṛṣabha's story within the larger framework of Jain world history.
