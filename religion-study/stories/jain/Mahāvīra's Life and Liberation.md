# Mahāvīra's Life and Liberation

> *"I have left the world. I have pulled out my hair. I have thrown away the body. I have no attachment. I have no aversion. I have no home. I am what I have always been: pure knowledge, pure seeing, pure bliss."*
> — Ācārāṅga Sūtra (adapted)

The story of **Mahāvīra** (Mahāvīramāṇa), the 24th and final Tīrthaṅkara of the present age, is the master narrative of Jainism — the culmination of the teaching of the eternal Dhamma for this half of the time-cycle. His life is not simply a biography; it is a template for liberation itself, a drama played out across the realms of gods, humans, demons, and animals, in which a soul of infinite purity burns away the last veil of karma and shows the world the path across the ocean of saṃsāra.

Mahāvīra means "Great Hero" — not the hero of battles and conquests, but the hero who conquered the inner enemies: attachment, aversion, ignorance, pride, and deceit. Before his renunciation he was called **Vardhamāna** ("the Increasing One"), because from the moment of his conception, the prosperity and spiritual radiance of his family and kingdom increased.

What follows is the great story — told with its two great tellings, the Śvetāmbara and Digambara versions, woven together.

---

## The Embryo Transfer: A Controversial Beginning

The story of Mahāvīra begins not at birth, but at **conception** — and immediately we encounter the most famous disagreement between the two main Jain traditions.

### The Embryo Transfer

According to the **Śvetāmbara** tradition (recorded in the Kalpa Sūtra and the Āvaśyaka Sūtra), Mahāvīra was first conceived in the womb of **Devānandā**, a **Brāhmaṇa** woman, wife of a priest named Ṛṣabhadatta of the Kauśika clan, living in the town of Kuṇḍapurī. The conception was marked by celestial signs: the gods rained flowers, the earth trembled, and the ten directions blazed with light.

But the gods — led by **Indra (Śakra)**, king of the celestial realm — were troubled. A Tīrthaṅkara, they knew, cannot be born into a **low-caste Brāhmaṇa** family. The eternal law of Tīrthaṅkara birth requires that a ford-maker be born into either the **kṣatriya** (warrior-ruler) caste or, failing that, a high Brāhmaṇa family — but never a Brāhmaṇa family of low status, because the teaching of the Tīrthaṅkara must command universal respect and cannot be associated with a caste that might be looked down upon.

So Indra did what Indra must do. He commanded the divine beings:

> *"The soul of the Tīrthaṅkara cannot remain in a low Brāhmaṇa womb. It must be transferred to a kṣatriya queen of noble lineage, where it can be born with the full dignity of a future ford-maker."*

Indra dispatched the **Nakṣatra-devatās** (constellation deities) and the **Vyantaras** (attendant gods) to accomplish the transfer. By divine power, the embryo — the soul itself — was gently lifted from Devānandā's womb while she slept, carried across the sky as a lotus of light, and placed into the womb of **Triśalā**, queen of **King Siddhārtha** of the **Jñātṛka** clan, a kṣatriya lineage of the Ikṣvāku dynasty, ruling from **Kṣatriya Kuṇḍagrāma** (a suburb of Vaiśālī, in modern Bihar).

When Devānandā awoke, she felt a pang of inexplicable loss — a sense that something infinitely precious had been taken from her. But she could not know what. And when Triśalā awoke, she felt a surge of radiant joy, a warmth spreading from her womb.

> The gods, it is said, wept for Devānandā — but the law of the ford-maker is older than compassion. Even a mother must give way to the destiny of the Tīrthaṅkara.

### The Digambara Rejection

The **Digambara** tradition rejects the embryo transfer outright. In Digambara accounts, there is no Devānandā — no transfer at all. Mahāvīra was conceived and born directly to **Triśalā** (also called **Priyakarīṇī**) and King Siddhārtha. There was no Brāhmaṇa womb, no divine intervention, no rearrangement of the cosmic order.

| Aspect | Śvetāmbara | Digambara |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| **First conception** | Devānandā (Brāhmaṇa woman) | None |
| **Transferred to** | Triśalā (Kṣatriya queen) | N/A |
| **Reason for transfer** | Tīrthaṅkara cannot be born in a low Brāhmaṇa family | No transfer needed — birth was always kṣatriya |
| **Motive** | The gods intervene to ensure dignity of the ford-maker | The embryo transfer is a later legend; the original truth is direct kṣatriya birth |

The Śvetāmbara account explains the transfer theologically: the gods are the guardians of the Tīrthaṅkara tradition, and they act to preserve the purity of the lineage. The Digambara account prioritises narrative simplicity — Mahāvīra's mother was always Triśalā, always kṣatriya, always royal.

For scholars, the disagreement is fascinating. The embryo-transfer story may reflect a historical memory of adoption, a theological dispute about caste and purity, or a symbolic narrative about the Tīrthaṅkara belonging not to any one family but to the cosmos itself. For the faithful of either tradition, it simply is — each reading its own truth in the silence between the two tellings.

## Triśalā's 14 Auspicious Dreams

Whichever tradition one follows, all agree on this: when Mahāvīra was in Triśalā's womb, she experienced **14 auspicious dreams** — each carrying a precise symbolic meaning for the child she carried. (Some traditions say the dreams came to Devānandā before the transfer, and then again to Triśalā after; others say only Triśalā received them. All agree on the number 14.)

The dreams, as recorded in the Kalpa Sūtra:

| # | Dream | Symbol | Meaning |
|---|-------|--------|---------|
| 1 | A **white elephant** (airāvata) | The vehicle of Indra; royal power | The child will be a lord among beings |
| 2 | A **white bull** (bṛṣabha) | Strength, virility, the emblem of Ṛṣabha (the first Tīrthaṅkara) | The child will restore the Dhamma as Ṛṣabha first gave it |
| 3 | A **lion** (siṃha) | Fearlessness, kingship | He will be fearless in asceticism and teaching |
| 4 | The **goddess Śrī** (Lakṣmī) | Auspiciousness, prosperity | He will be a source of blessing for all beings |
| 5 | A **garland** (mālā) | Honour, celebration | He will be honoured by gods and humans |
| 6 | A **full moon** (pūrṇa-candra) | Peace, radiance, completion | His mind will be calm and luminous |
| 7 | The **rising sun** (sūryodaya) | Light dispelling darkness | He will remove the darkness of ignorance |
| 8 | A **great flag** (dhvaja) | Victory, the Jain banner | He will conquer the inner enemies |
| 9 | A **full pot** (pūrṇa-kalaśa) | Abundance, fertility | He will fill the world with teaching and merit |
| 10 | A **jewel-encrusted throne** (ratna-siṃhāsana) | Sovereignty, meditation seat | He will sit in meditation and teach from the seat of omniscience |
| 11 | A **pair of fish** (matsya) | Fearlessness in water — the fish moves through the current without obstruction | He will move through saṃsāra without being entangled |
| 12 | A **celestial lake** (puṣkara) | Purity, liberation | He will purify all who come to him |
| 13 | The **ocean of milk** (kṣīra-sāgara) | Boundlessness, the source of all | His compassion will be as vast as the cosmic ocean |
| 14 | A **celestial palace** (vimāna) | The abode of the gods; the siddha-śilā | He will attain liberation and dwell in the eternal abode |

Triśalā woke each morning with the dream fresh in her mind — each one more vivid than the last. When she told King Siddhārtha, he sent for the royal dream-interpreters and the Brāhmaṇa sages. All gave the same answer:

> *"O Queen, you carry no ordinary child. The one in your womb is a Tīrthaṅkara — a ford-maker — who will establish the path to liberation for countless beings. He will be a Jina, a Victor, a Mahāvīra."*

---

## Birth at Kṣatriya Kuṇḍagrāma

On the thirteenth day of the bright half of the month of **Caitra** (March–April), in the year traditionally given as **599 BCE** (or 540 BCE by some calculations), Queen Triśalā gave birth in the city of **Kṣatriya Kuṇḍagrāma** (identified with modern Basarh-Bakhira in Vaiśālī district, Bihar).

The birth was not a quiet affair. The entire cosmos took note.

**Indra** and the gods of the **Saudharma Kalpa** (the first celestial realm) descended in their jewel-laden vimānas. They performed the **birth-rite of the Tīrthaṅkara** — the same rite that was performed for Ṛṣabha and Pārśva and all the ford-makers before. Indra himself took the newborn, wrapped him in a divine cloth, and placed him on the lap of the mother with great ceremony.

> *"The gods danced in the sky. The earth grew calm. The trees bloomed out of season. The rivers flowed with sweet water. The blind saw, the deaf heard, the lame danced. For a moment, the universe remembered its original perfection."*
> — Kalpa Sūtra (adapted)

The child was given the name **Vardhamāna** — "the Increasing One" — because from the moment of his birth, the kingdom's prosperity grew. The rains came on time. The harvests were abundant. The cattle flourished. The people were happy. Even the animals seemed more peaceful, more content.

He was also called **Mahāvīra** from birth in some texts — but that title would be earned in full only after his enlightenment.

---

## Early Life as Prince Vardhamāna

Vardhamāna grew up in the luxury of a kṣatriya court. His father, **King Siddhārtha**, was a chieftain of the **Jñātṛka** clan (also called **Nāya** in Pāli, related to the **Licchavis** of Vaiśālī). His mother, **Triśalā**, was **Princess Priyakarīṇī** of Vaiśālī — a woman of immense spiritual refinement.

The boy was extraordinary from the start.

### The Dragon and the Serpent (Childhood Stories)

A story from the **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** records that as a young boy, Vardhamāna was playing in the palace gardens with his friends when a **serpent** was discovered in the grove. The other children screamed and ran. But Vardhamāna walked calmly toward the serpent, looked at it with perfect equanimity, and said:

> *"Brother, you have no reason to fear me. I have no reason to fear you. Let us both be at peace."*

The serpent — which had been poised to strike — lowered its head and slithered away.

On another occasion, a **dragon** (a great lizard or crocodile) living in the palace reservoir terrorised the servants. The king ordered archers to kill it. Vardhamāna intervened:

> *"Father, do not kill it. It is as much a part of this world as we are. Give me three days."*

For three days, Vardhamāna sat by the reservoir, speaking gentle words to the dragon. On the third day, the dragon emerged, bowed its head before the boy, and swam away into the river, never to trouble the palace again.

These stories — whether historical or legendary — establish the central quality of Mahāvīra's character from childhood: **ahimsa** (non-violence) so total that even wild animals recognised it and were tamed by it.

### Education and Temperament

Vardhamāna was an exceptional student. He mastered the Vedas, the scriptures, the arts of government, the martial disciplines, and the philosophical systems of his day. But his heart was never in them. He was quiet where other princes were loud, contemplative where they were active, detached where they were ambitious.

His teachers noted that he seemed to be **waiting** — as if he already knew everything they could teach him, and was merely passing the time until the moment of his true calling arrived.

---

## Marriage to Yaśodā and Daughter Priyadarśanā

The question of whether Mahāvīra married is one of the great dividing lines between the Śvetāmbara and Digambara traditions.

### Śvetāmbara Account

According to the **Śvetāmbara** tradition (the Kalpa Sūtra and the Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra), Vardhamāna did marry. His wife was **Yaśodā** (also called **Priyadarśanā** or **Candanā** in some texts), a princess of the **Vaiśālī** royal family. She was beautiful, virtuous, and deeply devoted to him.

The marriage was arranged by their families according to the customs of the kṣatriya. Vardhamāna, respecting his parents' wishes, entered into the householder's life.

They had one child — a daughter named **Priyadarśanā** (meaning "the Lovely-to-Behōld," also called **Aṇojā** or **Prabhāvatī** in some accounts). Priyadarśanā would later marry the chieftain **Jāmāli**, who would become a monk under Mahāvīra and — in a dramatic twist — would later lead a schism, becoming the founder of the **Jāmāliya** sect, the first recorded split in the Jain monastic order.

Vardhamāna lived as a householder for **his first 30 years** — governing, managing estates, fulfilling his duties as a prince, husband, and father. But even in the midst of palace life, his mind was not of this world. He was, the texts say, like a lotus in a muddy pond: rooted in the filth of the world but untouched by it.

### Digambara Account

The **Digambara** tradition emphatically denies that Mahāvīra ever married. In the Digambara telling, Vardhamāna remained **celibate from birth** (a **dīkṣā-brahmacārī** — one who is celibate by nature, not by vow taken at renunciation). He had no wife, no daughter, no householder life at all.

The Digambara texts (the **Ādi Purāṇa** of Jinasena) argue that a Tīrthaṅkara, who is the embodiment of perfect renunciation, cannot have been entangled in marriage and family life. The Śvetāmbara stories of Yaśodā and Priyadarśanā are dismissed as later interpolations — attempts to make the Tīrthaṅkara's life conform to the expectations of householder society.

| Aspect | Śvetāmbara | Digambara |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| **Marital status** | Married to Yaśodā | Never married; celibate from birth |
| **Children** | One daughter: Priyadarśanā (married Jāmāli) | No children |
| **Reason** | Tīrthaṅkara fulfils householder duties before renouncing; shows the path for laypeople | Tīrthaṅkara must be perfectly detached from birth; marriage would compromise this |
| **Textual basis** | Kalpa Sūtra, Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra | Ādi Purāṇa, Mahāpurāṇa |
| **Theological implication** | The path is accessible to householders; a Tīrthaṅkara lived through all stages | The path is absolute renunciation; a Tīrthaṅkara is already liberated in essence from birth |

For the community, the choice between these accounts is not trivial. It shapes the relationship between the monastic ideal and the lay life, between the radical otherworldliness of the Jina and the everyday world of marriage and family. Both traditions claim their version preserves the authentic teaching.

---

## Renunciation at Age 30

Whether or not Vardhamāna had a wife and daughter, all traditions agree that **at age 30** he made the decision that would define his life: he renounced the world.

### The Catalyst

The gods — who watch over the spiritual progress of beings — saw that the time for the Tīrthaṅkara's departure was near. They appeared to Vardhamāna in a vision:

> *"Prince, look at this body. It is a wound that never heals. Look at this household. It is a cage of obligations. Look at this world. It is a burning house. The time has come for the Great Departure."*

Vardhamāna needed no convincing. He had known his destiny since childhood. The vision merely gave him the outer permission his inner certainty had long awaited.

### The Great Departure

Vardhamāna went to his father, King Siddhārtha, and asked for permission to renounce. The king — knowing what kind of son he had raised — wept but gave his blessing.

What happened next is one of the most powerful images in Jain ascetic literature.

Vardhamāna **gave away all his possessions** — his royal robes, his jewels, his weapons, his lands, his gold. He gave his elephants to the temple. He gave his horses to the army. He gave his grains to the poor. He stood before his family, his friends, his servants — and owned nothing.

Then he performed the **pañca-muṣṭi-loca** — the **plucking of the hair in five handfuls**. This is the ritual of absolute renunciation. With his own hands, Vardhamāna tore out his hair, fistful by fistful, in five motions. The pain was severe, but he did not flinch. His face showed no sign of suffering.

> *"Pain is a sensation. Sensation is karma. Karma is bondage. I am finished with bondage."*
> — Ācārāṅga Sūtra (adapted)

His head was now bare. His body was marked by the act of renunciation. He wore **a single garment** (in the Śvetāmbara tradition) — a simple cloth that hung from his shoulder — or **nothing at all** (in the Digambara tradition — **digambara** means "sky-clad," naked as the sky).

### Śvetāmbara vs Digambara: The Clothing Question

Once again, the two traditions differ:

| Aspect | Śvetāmbara ("White-Clad") | Digambara ("Sky-Clad") |
|--------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| **At renunciation** | Mahāvīra wore a single cloth (the **devadūṣya** garment given by the gods) | Mahāvīra renounced naked immediately; the gods themselves could not make him wear cloth |
| **During asceticism** | He wore the cloth for about 13 months, then it tore and he discarded it; after that he was naked | He was always naked; the idea of him ever wearing cloth is a corruption |
| **Theological meaning** | The gradual shedding of possessions and shame; shows that nudity is not a requirement but a natural consequence of detachment | Nudity is essential; the Tīrthaṅkara's absolute non-attachment must be visible from the moment of renunciation |

The name **Śvetāmbara** ("white-clad") and **Digambara** ("sky-clad") derive directly from their positions on this question.

After the renunciation ceremony — his **dīkṣā** — the former prince walked out of Kṣatriya Kuṇḍagrāma, a homeless wanderer. Behind him were thirty years of palace life, family, name, and status. Ahead of him was twelve years of the most extreme asceticism the world has ever known.

## Twelve Years of Extreme Asceticism

Mahāvīra's twelve-year search for enlightenment (which he called **Kevala Jñāna**, "absolute knowledge") is the heart of his story. It is not a gentle journey. It is a war waged against the body, against the elements, against the society that mocked and abused him, and — most of all — against the karmic residues accumulated over infinite lifetimes.

### The Ascetic's Life

Mahāvīra wandered as a **nirgrantha** — "one without knots" (without attachments, without possessions, without ties). His practice was extreme by any standard:

- **Fasting**: He would fast for days, sometimes for months, on end. His longest recorded fast was **six months**. When he did eat, he would accept only the simplest food, no matter how spoiled or meagre.
- **Silence (mauna)**: He observed long periods of total silence, walking through villages without speaking, refusing to answer even the simplest question.
- **Meditation (dhyāna)**: He stood or sat in the **kāyotsarga** posture for hours, days — motionless, arms hanging at his sides, utterly indifferent to heat, cold, rain, insects, and pain.
- **Wandering**: He never stayed in one place for more than a day or two (except during the rainy season, when wandering was suspended). He walked through forests, fields, and villages, always moving, never settling.
- **Nudity**: In both traditions, by the end of his ascetic period, he was naked — a sky-clad sage, his body exposed to the elements, his dignity resting entirely on his inner state.

### The Abuse of the World

Mahāvīra's asceticism was not a retreat from the world — it was a confrontation with it. The world did not treat him kindly.

**Villagers** mocked him as a naked madman. They threw stones at him. They spat on him. They set dogs on him. They shouted insults:

> *"Look at the madman! Look at the naked ascetic! Where is your palace now, prince? Where is your kingdom?"*

Mahāvīra never responded. He never argued. He never defended himself. He simply walked on, his mind fixed on a truth that the mockers could not see.

On one occasion, a man drove a **nail into his ear** (or in some accounts, pinned his ear with a peg to a fence). Blood streamed down his face. He did not move. He did not cry out. He continued meditating as if nothing had happened.

When the man was later rebuked by the village elder, Mahāvīra said:

> *"Do not blame him. He is the instrument of my karma. I have done the same to others in past lives. The debt is paid. Let it go."*

> *This is the Jain response to suffering — not resentment, but karmic accounting. Every blow is a debt being repaid. Every insult is a weight being lifted.*

### Encounters Along the Way

#### The Farmer and the Earthworm

One of the most poignant stories from Mahāvīra's ascetic period is the **farmer and the earthworm**.

Mahāvīra was passing through a field when a farmer was ploughing the earth for the spring planting. The plough cut through the soil, turning up worms, insects, and small creatures — slicing some in half, exposing others to birds waiting behind the plough.

Mahāvīra stopped. He watched. The blood of the worms seeped into the furrows. The farmer — a good man, not cruel, just doing what farmers do — noticed the naked ascetic staring at the plough and scratched his head.

> *"Sage, why are you staring? The field must be ploughed. The worms… well, the worms are part of farming."*

Mahāvīra trembled. Not with anger — with the pain of **universal compassion**.

> *"Every one of these worms — every single one — has been my mother in some past life. Every one has been my father, my sister, my brother. And today, I am standing here, unable to save them."*

He did not blame the farmer. He did not preach. He simply stood there, feeling the suffering of every tiny being whose life had been cut short by the plow, and he wept.

Then he walked on — more determined than ever to find the path that would put an end to all such suffering.

#### The Serpent Caṇḍaka

Another encounter: Mahāvīra was meditating in a forest when a huge serpent named **Caṇḍaka** (the "Fierce One") approached. The serpent was coiled, hood spread, ready to strike. Villagers who had followed Mahāvīra screamed and ran.

Mahāvīra opened his eyes. He looked at the serpent with complete equanimity.

> *"Brother, you are angry because you are afraid. I am not afraid. If you must strike, strike. It will not change where I am going."*

The serpent hesitated. It had never met a human who did not flinch. It lowered its hood, coiled around Mahāvīra's body — not to constrict, but to curl — and lay still, its head resting on the ascetic's lap. Mahāvīra placed his hand on the serpent's head and continued meditating.

When the villagers returned, they found the two of them — the naked sage and the great serpent — in perfect stillness, like a sculpture of peace.

> *The Caṇḍaka story echoes the earlier childhood serpent story and reinforces the same theme: ahimsa is not just the absence of violence; it is the presence of a peace so powerful that even venomous beings feel safe in it.*

#### The Encounter with Gosāla

Mahāvīra's most significant human encounter during his ascetic period was with **Gosāla Maskarīputra**, the leader of the **Ājīvika** sect — a rival šramaṇa tradition that taught radical determinism (niyati).

The two met by chance: Gosāla was a naked ascetic as well, wandering the same territories. They recognised in each other a kindred spirit — and a rival. For **six years** (years 6 to 12 of Mahāvīra's asceticism), they wandered together, practising austerities, debating philosophy, and sharing the hardships of the homeless life.

This relationship is explored in full in the companion story **[[Mahāvīra and Gosāla]]**. Here it is enough to know that the partnership ended bitterly: Gosāla claimed that Mahāvīra had given him a prediction about a wealthy householder that proved false (or that Mahāvīra had withheld secret knowledge), and the two split. Gosāla would later claim to have attained full omniscience himself, founding the Ājīvika community, and would attempt to kill Mahāvīra by magical means — a story of friendship, jealousy, and the tragic turning of a fellow seeker into an adversary.

## Attainment of Kevala Jñāna

After **twelve years** of asceticism — the full span of the great spiritual search — Mahāvīra came to the end of his journey.

It happened on the tenth day of the bright half of the month of **Vaiśākha** (April–May), under the **Sāla tree** (Shorea robusta) on the banks of the **Ṛjupālika River** (also called **Nirvāṇa-Kuṇḍiya** or **Pālika**), near the village of **Jṛmbhikāgrāma**, outside the city of **Vaiśālī**.

He had been fasting — for **two days**, according to the Ācārāṅga Sūtra. The sun was rising. The Sāla tree was in bloom. The river was flowing gently. Mahāvīra was sitting in the **kāyotsarga** posture, legs apart, arms hanging at his sides, eyes open, fixed on the tip of his nose — the classic Jain meditation posture.

The veil of karma that had obscured his soul's natural omniscience — accumulated over infinite lifetimes — suddenly dissolved.

It was not a gradual process. It was not a learning. It was a **shedding**.

> As a snake sheds its skin and the old body is left behind, Mahāvīra shed the last four karmic veils — the **ghātiyā karmas** — that obscure knowledge, perception, bliss, and power.

In that moment, he became **Kevalin** — the Possessor of Absolute Knowledge.

He saw:
- **All substances** in the universe — their past, present, and future forms
- **All souls** — their karmic states, their births, their deaths, their trajectories
- **All places** — from the lowest hell (the seventh naraka) to the highest heaven (the siddha-śilā at the summit of the universe)
- **All time** — the beginningless cycles of the universe, the endless turning of the wheel

He knew everything simultaneously — without effort, without limitation, without error. He was omniscient. He was a **Jina** — a Victor over the inner enemies.

> *"I have wandered through countless births, seeking the builder of this house. Painful is repeated birth. But now, O builder of the house, you are seen. You shall build no house again. All your rafters are broken. Your ridgepole is shattered. My mind has attained the unconditioned. The end of craving has been reached."*
> — Dhammapada (Buddhist) — but the same liberation is described in Jain terms: *"The knots are untied. The veil is lifted. The soul stands in its own light."*

The earth shook. The gods rejoiced. The trees burst into flower. The Sāla tree under which he sat became a sacred site. Mahāvīra was 42 years old. He had completed the hardest journey any being can undertake.

## The First Sermon on Mount Vipula

After his enlightenment, Mahāvīra remained in silence for some time. He knew that the truth he had realised was beyond words — that the Dhamma could be pointed to but never fully expressed. But he also knew that he was a Tīrthaṅkara, and a Tīrthaṅkara does not keep the path to himself.

The request to teach came — as it always does — from the **gods**.

**Indra** and the **devas** of the Saudharma realm assembled before Mahāvīra, bowed, and entreated him:

> *"Lord, you have crossed the ocean. Now build the ford. You have attained the light. Now light the lamps. The beings of the world are drowning in saṃsāra. They have no guide. They have no refuge. Speak."*

Mahāvīra consented. He chose as the site for his first sermon the slopes of **Mount Vipula** (also called **Vipulācala**), one of the five hills of **Rājagṛha** (modern Rajgir, Bihar) — a place rich in spiritual associations, where the Buddha would also preach.

### The Audience

The audience for the first sermon consisted of:

- **Celestial beings (devas, asuras, nāgas)** — Indra and his retinue, who had come to witness the establishment of the ford
- **Human followers** — including many who had been followers of **Pārśva's cāturyāma** (fourfold) tradition, now hearing the full fivefold teaching
- **Animals** — according to the texts, even the animals gathered to listen: deer, elephants, peacocks, and serpents, all drawn by the power of the Jina's presence

The most important human listeners were **eleven Brāhmaṇa scholars**, each a master of the Vedas and the philosophical systems of the time. They had been followers of Pārśva's tradition and were now seeking the highest truth. The chief among them was **Indrabhūti Gautama** — a man of immense intelligence and fierce pride.

### The Teaching

Mahāvīra's first sermon is not recorded verbatim (the earliest Jain scriptures are recollections), but its core is preserved:

> *"Souls are eternal. Karma is matter that clings to the soul. The cycle of birth and death is beginningless but not endless. By right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct — the three jewels — the soul can burn away karma and attain liberation.*

> *The five great vows — non-violence, truthfulness, non-stealing, celibacy, and non-attachment — are the path. The restraint of the mind, speech, and body is the practice. The destruction of karma is the goal."*

This was the teaching of the **pañca-mahāvrata** — the five great vows — which expanded and made explicit the fourfold code of Pārśva. The addition of **brahmacarya** (complete celibacy) as a separate vow was Mahāvīra's adaptation for a spiritually weaker age.

### The Conversion of the Gaṇadharas

The eleven Brāhmaṇa scholars — led by Indrabhūti Gautama — debated Mahāvīra. They challenged him on the nature of the self, the mechanism of karma, and the validity of the Vedas. Mahāvīra answered each objection with precision, patience, and a depth of insight that none of them could match.

One by one, they fell silent. One by one, they bowed. They cut off their topknots (the sign of the Brāhmaṇa householder), tore off their sacred threads, and became his disciples.

Each of the eleven became a **Gaṇadhara** — a chief disciple — the leader of a **gaṇa** (sub-order) of monks. Together, they would form the backbone of Mahāvīra's community.

The story of this conversion is told in detail in **[[The Conversion of the Ganadharas]]**.

---

## The Formation of the Fourfold Saṅgha

With the Gaṇadharas as his foundation, Mahāvīra established the **fourfold saṅgha** (caturvidha saṅgha) — the community of followers in four categories:

| Category | Order | Description |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| **Monks (Sādhus)** | Male renunciants | Naked (Digambara) or white-clad (Śvetāmbara); observe the five great vows; wander, meditate, study, teach |
| **Nuns (Sādhvīs)** | Female renunciants | White-clad in both traditions (Digambara nuns wear white, rejecting full nudity due to social constraints); observe the same five vows but with additional rules for interaction with male ascetics |
| **Laymen (Śrāvakas)** | Male householders | Observe the **aṇuvratas** (smaller vows) — a modified version of the five great vows tailored for domestic life |
| **Laywomen (Śrāvikās)** | Female householders | Same aṇuvratas as laymen; the backbone of Jain lay practice, often more numerous and more devout than the men |

Mahāvīra's order was revolutionary in **including women** as renunciants. While the nuns were subject to additional regulations (they could never travel alone, they had to defer to monks in seniority even if they were more spiritually advanced), the very existence of the **sādhvī-saṅgha** was a radical departure from the Brāhmaṇa tradition, which had no parallel order of female ascetics.

## Thirty Years of Teaching

For **thirty years** — from his enlightenment at age 42 to his liberation at age 72 — Mahāvīra wandered and taught.

### The Wandering

Mahāvīra's territory was the **greater Magadha region** — the Gangetic plain stretching from **Aṅga** (eastern Bihar) to **Malla** (modern Uttar Pradesh), from **Vajji** (northern Bihar) to **Magadha** (south Bihar). He walked from village to village, city to city, always moving except during the **four-month rainy season** (cāturmāsa), when he would settle in one place to teach intensively.

The Ācārāṅga Sūtra preserves a detailed record of his wanderings — the villages he visited, the rivers he crossed, the forests he passed through. It is one of the oldest travelogues in Indian literature.

### The Teachings

Mahāvīra's teachings covered every dimension of spiritual life:

- **Doctrine**: The six substances (dravyas), the nine categories (padārthas), the theory of karma, the nature of the soul, the process of liberation
- **Ethics**: The five great vows, the three guptis (restraints of mind, speech, body), the five samitis (carefulness in walking, speaking, eating, handling objects, and eliminating waste)
- **Meditation**: The techniques of dhyāna, the stages of spiritual progress (guṇasthānas), the cultivation of equanimity
- **Cosmology**: The structure of the universe, the realms of existence, the cycles of time
- **Narrative**: The lives of previous Tīrthaṅkaras, the history of the world, the exemplary tales of saints and sinners

He taught in **Prākrit** (the language of the people), not in Sanskrit (the language of the Brāhmaṇas). His words were memorised by his disciples and eventually codified into the **Jain Āgamas** — the scriptural corpus that, in its Śvetāmbara recension, comprises 45 texts.

### The Community He Built

By the time of his liberation, Mahāvīra's community had grown to extraordinary numbers:

| Category | Śvetāmbara Numbers |
|----------|---------------------|
| Gaṇadharas (chief disciples) | 11 |
| Monks (Sādhus) | 14,000 |
| Nuns (Sādhvīs) | 36,000 |
| Laymen (Śrāvakas) | 159,000 |
| Laywomen (Śrāvikās) | 318,000 |

The community was not homogeneous. It included:
- **Kings and paupers** — from the Emperor Śreṇika (Bimbisāra) of Magadha to the poorest outcaste
- **Scholars and fools** — the learned Brāhmaṇas who became Gaṇadharas and the simple villagers who understood nothing but devotion
- **Saints and sinners** — former bandits, courtesans, and murderers who transformed their lives through the Dhamma

Mahāvīra had no hierarchy beyond the Gaṇadharas. Each Gaṇadhara led his own gaṇa (sub-community), and within each gaṇa, seniority in renunciation determined rank — not birth, not learning, not social status.

> *"In my order, there is no Brāhmaṇa, no Kṣatriya, no Vaiśya, no Śūdra. All have renounced their caste as they renounced their homes. The only distinction is between those who have just arrived and those who have been walking longer."*

## Nirvāṇa at Pāvāpurī

At age **72** — after 30 years of teaching, after 42 years of struggling, after 72 years of living in the world — Mahāvīra's work was complete.

He walked to the town of **Pāvāpurī** (or **Pāvā**), near modern **Bihar Sharif** in Bihar. There, at dawn on the **new moon** (amāvasyā) of the month of **Kārtika** (October–November), he began his final meditation.

### The Final Fast

Mahāvīra undertook the **sallekhanā** — the Jaina ritual of fasting unto death. This is not suicide in the Jain understanding; it is the final act of spiritual purification, in which the ascetic, knowing that death is near and that the body can no longer serve the purpose of liberation, voluntarily relinquishes the physical form with full awareness, equanimity, and detachment.

He sat in the **kāyotsarga** posture under a tree, surrounded by his disciples, including **Indrabhūti Gautama** and **Sudharmā** (the Gaṇadhara who would become the most important transmitter of the Āgamas for the Śvetāmbara tradition).

He spoke his final words:

> *"Beings, listen! The Dhamma is taught. The ford is built. The path is open. Those who walk it with faith, knowledge, and conduct will reach the same state I have reached. Do not despair. Do not delay. The time is now."*

### The Shedding of the Body

As the sun rose over Pāvāpurī, Mahāvīra's meditation deepened. The last remaining karma — the **a-ghātiyā** (non-obscuring) karmas that determine the body, the lifespan, and the social status — were exhausted.

His soul separated from his body.

There was no death — not in the ordinary sense. Death implies a loss, a defeat, a passage into something unknown. This was a **release** — a soul of infinite knowledge, perception, bliss, and energy, freed forever from the cycle of birth, aging, disease, and death.

His body remained seated in the meditation posture, but it was empty — a discarded shell, like a snake's shed skin, like a house whose inhabitants have left forever.

The gods performed the final rites. Indra and the devas carried Mahāvīra's soul — now a **siddha** (a liberated being) — to the **siddha-śilā**, the eternal abode at the summit of the universe, where pure souls dwell forever in perfect knowledge, perfect bliss, and perfect rest.

> *"He was born. He lived. He renounced. He struggled. He conquered. He taught. He was liberated. He will never be born again."*
> — Kalpa Sūtra (adapted)

### The Date

The traditional date of Mahāvīra's nirvāṇa is the new moon of Kārtika (October–November) in the year **527 BCE** (or **510 BCE** in some Digambara calculations). This date — **Dīpāvalī** (the festival of lights) — is celebrated by Jains as **Mahāvīra Nirvāṇa Divasa**, the day when the light of the 24th Tīrthaṅkara was extinguished in this world and relit in the eternal abode.

On the same night, according to Jain tradition, **Gautama (Indrabhūti Gautama)** — Mahāvīra's chief disciple — attained Kevala Jñāna, completing the transmission of the teaching from the Tīrthaṅkara to the first human link in the chain of tradition.

## The Two Traditions: Summary of Differences

The following table summarises the major differences between the Śvetāmbara and Digambara tellings of Mahāvīra's life:

| Aspect | Śvetāmbara | Digambara |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| **Embryo** | Transferred from Devānandā (Brāhmaṇa) to Triśalā (Kṣatriya) | No transfer; always in Triśalā's womb |
| **Marriage** | Married to Yaśodā; fathered Priyadarśanā | Never married; permanently celibate |
| **Clothing** | Wore a garment for ~13 months after renunciation, then naked | Naked from the moment of renunciation (digambara — "sky-clad") |
| **Women's liberation** | Women can attain liberation; female Tīrthaṅkaras possible (though 24th was male) | Women must be reborn as men to attain liberation (the 24th was male) |
| **Basis** | Kalpa Sūtra, Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra, Āgamas | Ādi Purāṇa, Mahāpurāṇa, later commentaries |
| **Key figure** | Sudharmā (recited Āgamas as Gautama attained kevala) | Gautama attained kevala on the night of Mahāvīra's nirvāṇa; Sudharmā is less prominent |

Both traditions, despite these differences, revere **the same Mahāvīra** — the same 24th Tīrthaṅkara, the same path of the three jewels and the five vows, the same conviction that the soul can liberate itself through its own effort.

## The Meaning of the Story

Mahāvīra's story is not merely a biography — it is a **map of liberation**.

Each stage of his life corresponds to a stage on the path:

| Stage of Life | Spiritual Parallel |
|---------------|-------------------|
| **Birth** | The soul's entry into saṃsāra, conditioned by karma |
| **Embryo transfer** | The divine protection of the Tīrthaṅkara tradition; karma's role in determining birth |
| **Early life & marriage** | The worldly state from which liberation begins |
| **Renunciation** | The turning point — the choice to seek liberation |
| **12 years of asceticism** | The long, hard path of purifying the soul |
| **Kevala Jñāna** | The goal — omniscience, liberation-in-life (jīvan-mukti) |
| **30 years of teaching** | The compassion of the enlightened one, building the ford |
| **Nirvāṇa** | Final liberation — complete, permanent, irreversible |

Mahāvīra is the **template**. Every Jain — monk, nun, layman, laywoman — walks the same path, in miniature and in part. The monk who plucks his hair and renounces the world is walking Mahāvīra's path. The layperson who observes the aṇuvratas and fasts on the holy days is walking Mahāvīra's path. The soul that purifies itself, life after life, birth after birth, is walking the same path that Mahāvīra walked to completion.

The story is told and retold — in Kalpa Sūtra recitations, in temple sermons, in household pūjās, in the quiet meditation of a solitary monk — because it is not a story about the past. It is a story about **now**. About the path that is still open. About the liberation that is still possible.

> *"Mahāvīra walked the path. The path is still here. The ford is still open. What are you waiting for?"*

## Sacred Sites

- **Kṣatriya Kuṇḍagrāma** (Basarh-Bakhira, Vaiśālī, Bihar) — Birthplace of Mahāvīra; temple dedicated to the Tīrthaṅkara's birth
- **Vaiśālī** — The ancient city of the Licchavis; Mahāvīra's maternal home; location of the Buddha's last sermon as well
- **Ṛjupālika River** (near Vaiśālī) — The site of Mahāvīra's enlightenment; a sacred bathing ghat
- **Mount Vipula** (Rajgir, Bihar) — The site of the first sermon; seat of the Gaṇadharas' conversion
- **Pāvāpurī** (Bihar Sharif, Bihar) — The site of Mahāvīra's nirvāṇa; the most sacred Jain pilgrimage site; the **Jal Mandir** (temple in a lake) marks the spot of his final liberation
- **Sammeta Śikharajī** (Pārasnāth Hill, Jharkhand) — Though associated primarily with Pārśva and the other 20 Tīrthaṅkaras, also the place where Mahāvīra's first Gaṇadhara, Gautama, is said to have attained nirvāṇa
