# Pārśva — The Lord Protected

> *"Though the storm raged, the sage stood still. Though the demon hurled mountains, the serpent's hood held firm. This is the victory of equanimity."*

The story of **Pārśva** (Pārśvanātha), the 23rd Tīrthaṅkara of Jainism, is built around a single luminous image: a meditating sage, utterly still, while a cosmic storm of hail and rock crashes around him, and a great serpent-king spreads his hood above the sage's head as a living canopy. It is a story of enmity that spans multiple lifetimes, of compassion that tames the savage, and of protection that comes not from weapons but from the power of unbroken meditation.

---

## The Enmity Across Births

Before Pārśva became the 23rd Tīrthaṅkara, the enmity between him and his adversary **Kamatha** stretched across **84 previous births** — life after life of conflict, murder, and unfinished karma.

In one critical earlier life, Pārśva was born as **Marubhūti**, a merchant of noble character. His brother **Kamatha** was consumed by greed and jealousy. When Marubhūti refused to share an inheritance Kamatha felt entitled to, Kamatha murdered him in cold blood. A **Nāga** (serpent) living in a tree near the merchant's house witnessed the killing — and was filled with compassion for the victim and revulsion at the killer.

The karmic pattern was set:

| Being | Rebirth 1 | Rebirth 2 | Rebirth 3 (The Climax) |
|-------|-----------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Marubhūti** (victim) | Ascetic with great virtue | Continues spiritual ascent | **Pārśva** — the 23rd Tīrthaṅkara |
| **Kamatha** (murderer) | Hunter, kills again | Wild elephant, tramples ascetics | **Meghamālin** / **Samvara** — a powerful demon of the storm |
| **Witnessing Nāga** | Takes human birth, becomes a śrāvaka (lay Jain) | Continues devotion | **Dharaṇendra** — king of the Nāgas, protector of Pārśva |

Across these lives, Kamatha never stopped trying to destroy his former brother, and Marubhūti never responded with anger — only with patience, forgiveness, and the quiet accumulation of spiritual power. The witness-serpent, whose compassion began as a simple feeling, grew into the devotion of a celestial protector.

This is the Jain law of karma in narrative form: every action creates a trajectory. Hatred, unbroken, deepens the chain of violence. Compassion, unbroken, becomes protection.

---

## Birth in Vārāṇasī

Pārśva was born as a prince of **Vārāṇasī** (modern Varanasi, Uttar Pradesh), one of the oldest living cities in the world, to **King Aśvasena** of the Ikṣvāku dynasty and **Queen Vāmādevī** (also called Brāhmī).

As with all Tīrthaṅkaras, the queen's pregnancy was announced by **14 auspicious dreams** — including dreams of a serpent, a jewelled throne, a full moon, and a celestial discus. When the child was born, the gods themselves celebrated: **Indra** and the **Nāga king Dharaṇendra** descended from their realms to honour the future ford-maker.

The child was named **Pārśva** — "He at Whose Side a Serpent Was Found" — because a serpent was discovered coiled beside the newborn, or, as some traditions say, because the serpent-god Dharaṇendra himself guarded the infant from the moment of birth.

| Detail | Tradition |
|--------|-----------|
| **Father** | King Aśvasena of Vārāṇasī (Ikṣvāku dynasty) |
| **Mother** | Queen Vāmādevī (also called Brāhmī) |
| **Birthplace** | Vārāṇasī (Varanasi, Uttar Pradesh) |
| **Date (traditional)** | c. 877–872 BCE |
| **Emblem** | Serpent (cobra hood) |
| **Colour** | Dark blue / black / green (śyāma varṇa) |

### Early Life

Raised in luxury, Pārśva was from childhood marked by a profound compassion that set him apart from other princes. He refused to harm even insects. He gave aid to the sick and the poor. He showed no interest in the hunt or in martial display. His heart was already turned inward.

The Śvetāmbara tradition holds that he married a woman named **Prabhāvatī**; the Digambara tradition maintains he remained celibate throughout his life. Both agree that the world could not hold him.

---

## Renunciation

At age **30**, the turning point came. The gods — who know when a Tīrthaṅkara is to arise — appeared to Pārśva and reminded him of his destiny:

> *"The body is impermanent. Wealth is a mirage. Youth passes like a flash of lightning. Now is the time for the Great Departure."*

Pārśva looked at the life ahead of him — kingdom, family, old age, death — and saw no lasting satisfaction. With perfect clarity, he made his choice.

He distributed his wealth to the poor. He performed the **pañca-muṣṭi-loca** — the ritual plucking of his hair in five handfuls, a sign of absolute renunciation that remains one of Jainism's most powerful ascetic symbols. He assumed a simple garment (or none, in the Digambara tradition) and walked out of Vārāṇasī as a homeless ascetic.

The city that had been his home became another stop on the long road of spiritual striving.

---

## The Ascetic Period and the Storm

For **83 days**, Pārśva wandered and meditated, fasting and standing in the **kāyotsarga** posture — standing motionless, arms hanging at his sides, utterly indifferent to the body. He sought a spot under a **Dhātakī tree** (Woodfordia fruticosa) — a flowering shrub — and there he stood, day and night, without moving.

It was then that the old enmity caught up with him.

Kamatha, after his many lives of violence, had been reborn as **Meghamālin** (also called **Samvara**) — a demon of immense power, lord of clouds and storms. From his celestial realm, Meghamālin looked down and saw Pārśva meditating. The memory of lifetimes of hatred surged up in him.

> *"There he is — the one who escaped me life after life. He thinks he will attain liberation? I will break him."*

### The Attack (Vivid)

Meghamālin descended with the full force of his demonic power. He conjured a storm unlike any the world had seen:

**First came the wind** — a gale that uprooted trees, flattened grass, and ripped leaves from every branch. The Dhātakī tree bent almost to the ground. But Pārśva stood unmoved.

**Then came the rain** — not gentle drops but torrential sheets, a flood from the sky that would have drowned any ordinary man. It lashed Pārśva's face, streamed down his body, soaked the ground at his feet. He did not flinch.

**Then came the hail** — stones the size of fists, then boulders, then what seemed like entire mountains hurled from the sky. The ground shook. The air roared. Hail shattered against Pārśva's shoulders, his head, his arms. Cuts appeared on his skin. Blood mixed with rain. Still he did not move.

**Then came the mud** — a thick, suffocating torrent of earth and rock, as if the earth itself had turned against him. It rose around his ankles, his knees, his thighs — attempting to swallow him into the ground itself.

Meghamālin raged from the sky, throwing everything he had. He screamed with frustration: *"Move! Fall! Die! Give up!"*

Pārśva remained in kāyotsarga. His mind was as still as a lake in a cave — untouched by the storm outside. He had no anger toward the demon, no fear, no wish for revenge. He simply stood, in perfect equanimity, while the cosmos tried to break him.

---

## The Serpent's Protection

It was then that the third being from the original story — the witness — entered the scene.

**Dharaṇendra**, king of the Nāgas, was meditating in his jewel-encrusted palace beneath the earth when he felt a disturbance in the spiritual atmosphere. His mind — purified by lifetimes of devotion — recognised the crisis above. The merchant's killer was attacking the merchant's final incarnation.

Dharaṇendra rose from the underworld with **Padmāvatī** (also called Suṣīmā), his queen. They burst through the earth's crust at the base of the Dhātakī tree.

What happened next is the defining image of Pārśva's hagiography:

Dharaṇendra reared his massive serpentine body behind Pārśva and spread his **multi-headed cobra hood** — seven heads, each with a jewel on its crown — above the sage's head, forming a perfect canopy. The hailstones that would have crushed Pārśva's skull bounced harmlessly off Dharaṇendra's scales. The rain slid down the hood's surface like water off a lotus leaf.

Padmāvatī coiled her body around Pārśva's lower limbs, shielding his legs from the rising mud and the biting cold. Her body was warm, smooth, and steady.

Together, the serpent king and queen formed a living fortress around the meditating sage — heads above, bodies below, unbreakable devotion on all sides.

For **three days and three nights**, the storm raged. Meghamālin exhausted every weapon in his arsenal — hailstorms, floods, lightning, rocks, mudslides. Nothing worked. Every attack was absorbed by Dharaṇendra's hood or deflected by Padmāvatī's coils.

Pārśva never opened his eyes. He never acknowledged the storm, the demon, or the serpents. He simply meditated.

Finally, on the third day, Meghamālin collapsed in exhaustion. All his power had been spent against a man who would not fight back — and that, more than any counterattack, was what defeated him. He fell to the ground, prostrate, and recognised the spiritual mastery of the one he had tried to destroy.

> *"I have hated you for 84 lives,"* the demon wept. *"And you have not hated me even once. I surrender."*

Pārśva opened his eyes. He looked at the fallen demon — his brother from so many lives ago — with nothing but compassion. He spoke no word of accusation. He simply accepted the demon's surrender.

Dharaṇendra and Padmāvatī, seeing the danger past, withdrew their hoods and coils, bowed to Pārśva, and returned to their subterranean kingdom — their devotion now immortalised in Jain memory.

---

## Kevala Jñāna

On the **84th day** of his asceticism — immediately after the storm had passed — Pārśva, standing under the Dhātakī tree, attained **Kevala Jñāna**: perfect, infinite, omniscient knowledge.

All the veils of karma that had obscured his soul's natural omniscience fell away. He saw, simultaneously, all substances in the universe — past, present, and future — their modes, their transformations, their relationships. There was nothing he did not know. There was nothing he did not see.

He had become a **Jina** — a Victor — one who has conquered the inner enemies of attachment, aversion, and ignorance. And because he was a Tīrthaṅkara, he would now teach others how to cross the ocean of saṃsāra to the same liberation.

---

## The Fourfold Vow (Cāturyāma Dharma)

Pārśva's teaching is known as the **cāturyāma** — the fourfold restraint. This was the ethical code he established for his community of monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen.

| Vow | Meaning | Key Distinction |
|-----|---------|-----------------|
| **Ahiṃsā** | Non-violence | Absolute non-harm to all living beings — the primary, non-negotiable foundation |
| **Satya** | Truthfulness | Speaking only what is true, beneficial, and pleasant |
| **Asteya** | Non-stealing | Taking nothing that is not freely given |
| **Aparigraha** | Non-attachment | Renunciation of possessions, sensual pleasures, and worldly attachments — **including celibacy**, which was not a separate vow because Pārśva taught that chastity is naturally contained within non-attachment |

### Why Only Four? The Question of Brahmacarya

The most notable difference between Pārśva's code and Mahāvīra's later **pañca-mahāvrata** (five great vows) is the absence of **brahmacarya** (complete celibacy) as an independently enumerated vow.

Pārśva taught that **aparigraha** — non-attachment to all things — implicitly includes celibacy. If one has truly renounced attachment to possessions, relationships, and sensory pleasures, then sexual restraint follows naturally. It does not need to be named separately. To enumerate it as a distinct vow would be, in Pārśva's framework, to give it an artificial prominence that might suggest it could be practised in isolation from the broader discipline of non-attachment.

Mahāvīra, living in a more spiritually weakened age, found it necessary to make brahmacarya explicit — to ensure that ascetics understood that celibacy was not optional. The evolution from four vows to five reflects a central Jain understanding: the same eternal Dhamma is taught in different forms according to the capacity of the age.

> *"The Dhamma is one. The teaching adapts. What was implicit for Pārśva became explicit for Mahāvīra — not because the truth changed, but because the hearers had grown dull."*

---

## Ministry and Community

For **70 years** after his enlightenment — until he was 100 years old — Pārśva wandered the Gangetic plain, teaching the path of liberation. He gathered a vast community:

| Category | Number (Śvetāmbara tradition) |
|----------|-------------------------------|
| **Chief disciples (Gaṇadharas)** | 87 (led by Ārya Śubhadatta / Śubha) |
| **Monks** | 36,000 |
| **Nuns** | 46,000 |
| **Laymen** | 164,000 |
| **Laywomen** | 327,000 |

His order — known as the **Pārśvāpatyas** — was a fully organised religious community with its own rules, lineages, and scriptures. It was this community, already flourishing for roughly two centuries, that Mahāvīra would encounter when he began his own ministry.

---

## Nirvāṇa on Mount Sammeta

At age **100**, Pārśva knew that his work was complete. He had shown the path, established the order, and exhausted the karma that had kept him bound to the cycle of birth and death.

He walked to **Mount Sammeta** (Sammeta Śikharajī, now called **Pārasnāth Hill** in Giridih district, Jharkhand) — a place of such spiritual potency that 20 of the 24 Tīrthaṅkaras would attain liberation there. On the eighth day of the dark half of the month of Pauṣa (December–January), he entered a deep meditation and shed the final vestiges of karma.

His soul — pure, omniscient, blissful — ascended to the **siddha-śilā**, the eternal abode of liberated beings at the summit of the universe. It would never be reborn again.

---

## The Connection to Mahāvīra

Perhaps the most remarkable aspect of Pārśva's story is not what happened during his life — but what happened after. When Pārśva attained nirvāṇa, his community did not dissolve. It continued as a living tradition, still teaching the fourfold vow, still following the monastic discipline, still waiting — perhaps unconsciously — for the next Tīrthaṅkara.

Two centuries later, **Mahāvīra** attained kevala jñāna and began teaching. What happened next changed Jain history forever:

**All 11 Gaṇadharas of Mahāvīra — his eleven chief disciples — came from Pārśva's tradition.**

| Gaṇadhara | Background |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Indrabhūti Gautama** | A Brahmin scholar and former follower of Pārśva; he was the first to be converted by Mahāvīra after a famous debate in which he asked about the self and karma |
| **Agnibhūti** | Indrabhūti's brother; also a Brahmin and Pārśva follower |
| **Vāyubhūti** | Third brother; same background |
| **Vyākta / Vyaktasvabhāva** | From Pārśva's monastic lineage |
| **Sudharmā** | A former Pārśva śrāvaka (lay follower) who became Mahāvīra's disciple and later the most important Gaṇadhara for the Śvetāmbara canon |
| (6 others) | Each from Pārśva's tradition |

This means that the entire chain of Jain scriptural transmission — the Āgamas — passes through disciples who first learned the Dhamma from Pārśva's tradition and then received its final form from Mahāvīra. The two traditions were not rivals; they were a continuous flow.

The fourfold vow and the fivefold vow coexisted in the early Jain community. The *Uttarādhyayana Sūtra* records a dialogue between followers of Pārśva and Mahāvīra, where the fourfold and fivefold formulations were compared and the difference explained as an adaptation to the age — not a contradiction of the eternal Dhamma.

## Comparison: The Protected Sage

The motif of a **sage protected from a storm by a serpent or divine being** recurs across Indian religious traditions. Here is how Pārśva's story compares:

| Tradition | Figure | Protector | Threat | Meaning |
|-----------|--------|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Jainism** | Pārśva (23rd Tīrthaṅkara) | Dharaṇendra (serpent-king) + Padmāvatī (serpent-queen) | Meghamālin (demon of storm) — hail, rocks, flood, mud | Equanimity conquers aggression; protection comes to those who do not fight back |
| **Hinduism** | Śiva (at Gaṅgā's descent) | Śiva's matted hair (or the serpent Vāsuki) | Gaṅgā's cosmic torrent (would have shattered the earth) | Divine power absorbs destructive force; the god channels rather than blocks |
| **Hinduism** | Śeṣa / Ananta (serpent) | His coiled body and hood | Cosmic dissolution (pralaya) | The serpent supports the universe through cycles of destruction and creation |
| **Buddhism** | The Buddha (meditating under the Bodhi tree) | The Nāga king Mucalinda | Storm at the end of the rainy season (seven days of rain, wind, and cold) | Mucalinda coiled around the Buddha's body and hooded his head — the same motif as Pārśva's protection |
| **Buddhism** | The Buddha (after enlightenment) | No storm story, but the Māra-āvāha (Māra's assault) | Māra's army, daughters, doubts | Temptation and fear are overcome by mindfulness and touching the earth |
| **Jainism (variation)** | Mahāvīra (24th Tīrthaṅkara) | No serpent protector, but celestial beings | Demon Saṅgamaka, attacks from villagers, animals | Ascetic endures alone; no external protector needed — his own austerity is his shield |

### The Striking Parallel: Pārśva and the Buddha (Mucalinda)

The most direct parallel to Pārśva's serpent protection is the story of **Mucalinda** in the Buddhist tradition. After the Buddha's enlightenment, a storm raged for seven days as he meditated. The Nāga king Mucalinda emerged, coiled seven times around the Buddha's body, and spread his hood above the Buddha's head — exactly as Dharaṇendra did for Pārśva.

| Element | Pārśva (Jain) | The Buddha (Buddhist) |
|---------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Time of protection** | During asceticism, *before* enlightenment (day 83 of 84) | Immediately *after* enlightenment (the seventh week) |
| **Duration** | 3 days and nights | 7 days |
| **Protector** | Dharaṇendra + Padmāvatī (king and queen) | Mucalinda (king alone) |
| **Posture of sage** | Kāyotsarga (standing) | Seated in meditation |
| **Nature of threat** | Demonic attack (Meghamālin's storm) | Natural storm (rain, wind, cold) |
| **Outcome** | Demon surrenders; sage attains omniscience the next day | The Buddha completes his post-enlightenment contemplation |
| **Theological meaning** | Protection earned by compassion in past lives; karmic enmity resolved | Protection as natural reverence of the devas for a fully awakened one |

Scholars have noted that the Mucalinda story may have been influenced by the older Pārśva tradition — or both may draw on a common Indian folk motif of the Nāga as protector of the meditating sage. In either case, the image of the serpent-hood canopy is one of the most powerful and cross-culturally resonant symbols in Indian religious iconography.

### What Makes Pārśva's Story Unique

While the protected-sage motif appears in multiple traditions, Pārśva's version is distinctive in three ways:

1. **The protection is karmic.** Dharaṇendra does not protect Pārśva because Pārśva is a Tīrthaṅkara, but because of a seed of compassion planted in a past life — the merchant Marubhūti's virtue was witnessed, remembered, and repaid. The story is embedded in a larger narrative of karma across lifetimes, not just a single moment of divine intervention.

2. **The protector is a couple.** Dharaṇendra and Padmāvatī together form the protective canopy — the king's hood above and the queen's coils below. This dual protection (masculine and feminine, sky and earth) is unique to Pārśva and adds a layer of relational, nurturing power to the protection motif.

3. **The attacker is redeemed.** Meghamālin is not destroyed or banished — he surrenders and becomes a devotee. The story does not end with the demon's defeat but with his conversion. Unlike the Buddhist Māra, who remains a tempter, or the Hindu asuras who are often slain, Meghamālin's hatred is burnt away by Pārśva's non-retaliation. This is a profoundly Jain resolution: violence ends not when one side wins, but when hatred exhausts itself against equanimity.

## Iconography: The Serpent Hood

The serpent hood of Dharaṇendra is the single most identifying feature of Pārśva in Jain art. Every image of him — whether painted, carved in marble, or cast in bronze — includes the cobra canopy.

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Hood** | Multi-headed cobra (usually 7 heads, sometimes 5 or 9), spread like an umbrella above Pārśva's head |
| **Colour** | Dark blue, black, or green — reflecting the serpent's hue |
| **Posture of Pārśva** | Seated in padmāsana (lotus pose) or standing in kāyotsarga |
| **Throne emblem** | A serpent coiled at the base |
| **Attendants** | Yakṣa Dharaṇendra (serpent-king) and Yakṣī Padmāvatī (serpent-queen) |
| **Clothing** | Śvetāmbara: cloth below the navel; Digambara: naked, seated on a lotus |

The image carries a dual meaning: the serpent is both a **literal protector** (shielding Pārśva from the storm) and a **symbol** (the coiled, earthly energy of the Nāga transformed into a guardian of spiritual awakening).

## Sacred Sites

- **Sammeta Śikharajī (Pārasnāth Hill)**, Jharkhand — The most sacred Pārśva site; 20 of 24 Tīrthaṅkaras attained nirvāṇa here. Pārśva's temple stands at the highest peak.
- **Pārśvanātha Temple, Khajuraho** — One of the best-preserved Jain temples at Khajuraho, with exquisite serpent-hood carvings.
- **Dilwara Temples, Mount Abu, Rajasthan** — Marble temples with intricate Pārśva images.
- **Pārśvanātha Temple, Girnar, Gujarat** — Major Śvetāmbara pilgrimage destination.

## The Meaning of the Story

Pārśva's story is, at its heart, a meditation on the **power of non-retaliation**. The demon Meghamālin attacks with the full fury of a being who has nursed hatred for 84 lifetimes — and all of that fury breaks against a man who will not fight back. Not because Pārśva is weak, but because he has transcended the very framework of fighting.

> *The storm could not move him. The hail could not break him. The mud could not swallow him. The demon's rage could not provoke him. This is what it means to be a Jina — a Victor.*

Dharaṇendra's protection is the universe's response to that non-violence. Because Pārśva never retaliated, the cosmos itself sent protectors. Because he never returned hatred, hatred finally exhausted itself.

It is a story that Jains return to again and again — a reminder that the ultimate victory is not over others but over the self, and that the most powerful protection in the universe comes to those who need it least.
