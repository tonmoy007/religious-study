# The Council of Valabhī

> *"The words of the Jina were scattered across a hundred monasteries, across a thousand memories, across three centuries of copying and forgetting. If we do not gather them now, our children will inherit only silence."*
> — Attributed to **Devarddhi Gaṇin Kṣamāśramaṇa**, convening the Council of Valabhī

The **Council of Valabhī** (c. 453–467 CE) was the second great convocation of the Śvetāmbara Jain tradition — and its **final act of scriptural codification**. Convened by the Ācārya **Devarddhi Gaṇin Kṣamāśramaṇa** in the bustling port city of **Valabhī** (modern Vala, Gujarat), capital of the **Maitraka dynasty**, this council undertook a monumental task: to gather every surviving fragment of the Jain Āgamas, compare every recension, resolve every discrepancy, and produce **a single, authoritative, written canon** — the 45-Āgama structure that defines Śvetāmbara Jainism to this day.

If the Council of Pāṭaliputra (c. 300 BCE) was the **first draft** — the emergency salvage of scripture after the Great Famine — then the Council of Valabhī was the **final edition**: the text fixed, the boundaries drawn, the canon closed.

> For the story of the first council, see **[[The Council of Pāṭaliputra]]**.

---

## The Context: Seven Centuries of Decay

### The Weight of Time

By the 5th century CE, more than **seven centuries** had passed since the Council of Pāṭaliputra. In those seven centuries, the Śvetāmbara community had grown, spread, and diversified — but its scriptures had **not** remained stable.

The 11 Aṅgas written on palm-leaf at Pāṭaliputra were not a finished canon. They were a **salvage collection** — the best the elders could do after the famine had broken the oral lineage. And in the centuries that followed, the texts began to drift:

- **Regional variations** emerged. Monks in **Saurashtra** recited one version of the **Bhagavatī Sūtra**; monks in **Magadha** recited another. The differences were not always large, but they accumulated.
- **Palm-leaf manuscripts** decayed. In India's monsoon climate, palm-leaf had a lifespan of perhaps 200–300 years. By the 5th century CE, the original Pāṭaliputra manuscripts had long since crumbled to dust, replaced by copies of copies — each generation of copyists introducing its own errors, interpolations, and omissions.
- **New texts had appeared**. Over the centuries, the monks had composed commentaries *(niryuktis, bhāṣyas, cūrṇis)*, supplementary texts *(prakīrṇakas)*, and codes of monastic discipline *(cheda sūtras)*. These texts were widely used but not formally canonised. Were they scripture? Commentary? Something in between? No one had decided.
- **The oral tradition was fading again**. The age of the **Śruta Kevalins** — monks who knew the entire canon by heart — was long past. Even the **Sthaviras** (elders) who knew large portions of the Āgamas were growing fewer. The memory that the Council of Pāṭaliputra had tried to fix was, once again, slipping away.

### The State of the Canon

What existed by the early 5th century CE was a **chaos of texts**:

| Category | Status |
|----------|--------|
| **The 11 Aṅgas** | Known and recited, but with regional variants and uncertain readings |
| **The 12th Aṅga (Dṛṣṭivāda)** | **LOST** — no surviving text, only quotations in commentaries |
| **The Upāṅgas** | A collection of 12 subsidiary texts; their status as "canonical" was debated |
| **The Prakīrṇakas** | A loose set of 10 independent treatises on various topics; widely varying manuscripts |
| **The Cheda Sūtras** | 6 texts on monastic discipline; essential for the functioning of the saṅgha but not formally integrated into the canon |
| **The Mūla Sūtras** | 4 foundational texts for monastic training; some considered them Āgamas, others did not |
| **Two "Extra" Texts** | The **Anuyogadvāra Sūtra** and the **Nandī Sūtra** — late compilations on the methodology of scriptural analysis; controversial as to whether they should be included |

The situation was urgent. Without a formal decision on what belonged to the canon — and in what form — the tradition risked **fragmentation into regional sects**, each with its own scripture, each claiming authenticity.

### The Patron: King Dhruvasena I of the Maitrakas

The council found its patron in an unexpected place: the **Maitraka dynasty** of Gujarat.

The Maitrakas rose to power after the collapse of the **Gupta Empire** in the late 5th century. Valabhī, their capital, was a wealthy port city on the Arabian Sea — a hub of maritime trade connecting India to the Persian Gulf, Africa, and the Mediterranean. The city's prosperity supported a vibrant religious culture: Buddhist monasteries, Hindu temples, and Jain upāśrayas (monastic dwellings) coexisted within its walls.

**King Dhruvasena I** (r. c. 450–470 CE) was a patron of learning. While the Maitrakas were generally **Śaiva** (devotees of Śiva), they followed the classical Indian policy of supporting all religious traditions within their realm. Dhruvasena saw the value of a fixed, authoritative scripture for the Jain community — both as a religious good in itself and as a means of consolidating his kingdom's cultural identity.

According to Jain sources, Dhruvasena placed the resources of the court at the disposal of the council: scribes, palm-leaf supplies, buildings for the assembly, and — perhaps most importantly — his **political authority** to enforce the council's decisions.

## The Convenor: Devarddhi Gaṇin Kṣamāśramaṇa

### The Man

**Devarddhi Gaṇin Kṣamāśramaṇa** (also spelled Devarddhigaṇi or Devarddhi Gaṇi Kṣamāśramaṇa) is the central figure of the Valabhī story — and one of the most important figures in Śvetāmbara history whose name is **not** widely known outside Jain studies.

His name tells us something about him:

- **Devarddhi**: "Given by the gods" — suggesting his birth was considered a divine gift
- **Gaṇin**: A title meaning "head of a gaṇa" (a monastic group) — indicating his seniority and authority
- **Kṣamāśramaṇa**: "The ascetic of patience" *(kṣamā = patience, forgiveness)* — an honorific that speaks to his character as a compassionate and patient scholar

He was, by all accounts, a **prodigious scholar**, a **skilled editor**, and a **diplomat** capable of holding together a fractious assembly of elders from different monastic lineages, each with their own cherished manuscripts and entrenched opinions.

### His Vision

Devarddhi understood that the problem was not simply **loss** — it was also **uncertainty**. The community needed not just a text, but a **decision**: a definitive list of what counted as "the word of the Jina" (Jina-vacana) and what did not.

His vision was:

1. **To gather every recension** of every text that any monastic lineage considered canonical
2. **To compare, collate, and correct** them against one another
3. **To produce a single master text** for each Āgama
4. **To organise the Āgamas into a permanent structure** — a numbered canon that could not be added to or subtracted from
5. **To write it all down** — not just as a pragmatic measure, but as the **authoritative form** of the scripture

The result would be a **closed canon** — something the Jain tradition had never had before.

## The Council: Sessions and Methods

### The Gathering

The council met in Valabhī over a period of several years — the traditional dating spans from **980 to 993 years after Mahāvīra's nirvāṇa** (roughly 453–467 CE, depending on the date used for Mahāvīra's nirvāṇa).

It was an assembly of **Sthaviras** (elders) representing all the major monastic lineages of the Śvetāmbara community:

| Monastic Lineage | Region | Key Representative |
|------------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Kātyā** lineage | Saurashtra (Gujarat) | Devarddhi Gaṇin himself |
| **Vācaka** lineage | Mālwa | Uncertain |
| **Kṣamāśramaṇa** lineage | Western India | Multiple elders |
| **Takkha** lineage | Sindh (modern Pakistan) | Named in some sources |
| **Nāgendra** lineage | Gujarat | Uncertain |

The council was not merely an editorial committee — it was a **convocation of the entire Śvetāmbara scholarly world**, and its decisions carried the weight of that authority.

### The Method

The work proceeded systematically:

**1. Collection**: Monasteries across India were asked to send their manuscripts. Monks who had memorised portions of the Āgamas were summoned to recite them before the council. The council gathered **every known version** of each text.

**2. Comparison**: For each text, the council compared the available recensions side by side. When passages differed, the elders debated which reading was original and which was a later corruption or interpolation.

**3. Reconciliation**: Where multiple readings were plausible, the council adopted a principle of **harmonisation** — choosing the reading that best fit the overall structure and style of the Āgamas. This was not simply "majority rules"; it was an **editorial judgment** based on linguistic, doctrinal, and stylistic criteria.

**4. Organisation**: The texts were arranged into a coherent **structure of 45 Āgamas** — a numbered list that would become the definitive Śvetāmbara canon.

**5. Writing**: The final recension of each text was **copied onto fresh palm-leaf manuscripts**, stored in monastic libraries (bhāṇḍāras), and distributed to major monasteries.

### The Principle of Inclusivity

One of the most striking features of the Valabhī council was its **inclusivity**. Rather than pruning the canon down to a minimal core, Devarddhi's council chose to **include** texts whose status had been uncertain:

- The **12 Upāṅgas** — which had existed alongside the Aṅgas for centuries but without formal canonisation — were officially included
- The **10 Prakīrṇakas** — a diverse collection of treatises on karma, cosmology, and monastic practice — were adopted
- The **6 Cheda Sūtras** — the rules of monastic discipline — were incorporated
- The **4 Mūla Sūtras** — foundational texts for novices — were added
- The **Anuyogadvāra Sūtra** and the **Nandī Sūtra** — late but important texts on the hermeneutics of scripture — were admitted as the 44th and 45th Āgamas

This was a deliberate choice. Devarddhi understood that a canon that was **too narrow** would cause more schisms than it prevented. By including the texts that various communities already revered, the council made its authority acceptable to the widest possible constituency.

## The 45-Āgama Structure

The canon fixed at Valabhī consists of **45 texts** in six divisions:

### 1. The 11 Aṅgas (Core Scriptures)

These are the primary scriptures, inherited from the Council of Pāṭaliputra:

| # | Aṅga | Content |
|---|------|---------|
| 1 | **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** | Monastic conduct; Mahāvīra's biography as an ascetic |
| 2 | **Sūtrakṛtāṅga** | Doctrinal exposition; refutations of rival schools |
| 3 | **Sthānāṅga Sūtra** | Doctrinal lists arranged by number (1-fold, 2-fold, 3-fold…) |
| 4 | **Samavāyāṅga Sūtra** | Extended numerical lists |
| 5 | **Bhagavatī Sūtra** | The largest Aṅga; dialogues on karma, cosmology, ethics |
| 6 | **Jñātṛdharmakathāḥ** | Parables and moral stories |
| 7 | **Upāsakadaśāḥ** | Stories of ten exemplary lay followers |
| 8 | **Antakṛtdaśāḥ** | Stories of those who attained liberation |
| 9 | **Anuttaraupapātikadaśāḥ** | Accounts of rebirth in the highest heavens |
| 10 | **Praśnavyākaraṇāni** | Questions and answers on karma and ethics |
| 11 | **Vipākaśruta** | Stories of karmic retribution |

The **12th Aṅga (Dṛṣṭivāda)** remained **lost** — and was acknowledged as lost. The Valabhī council did not attempt to fabricate a replacement. The 11 Aṅgas would stand as the core, and the loss would be recorded as a permanent gap.

### 2. The 12 Upāṅgas (Subsidiary Scriptures)

The Upāṅgas expand on themes introduced in the Aṅgas, particularly cosmology, geography, and narrative. They include:

| # | Upāṅga | Content |
|---|--------|---------|
| 1 | **Aupapātika Sūtra** | Description of celestial rebirths |
| 2 | **Rājapraśnīya Sūtra** | Dialogue between King Pradeśī and the monk Keśī |
| 3 | **Jīvājīvābhigama Sūtra** | Classification of living and non-living substances |
| 4 | **Praśnavyākaraṇa Sūtra** | (Distinct from Aṅga #10) — doctrinal questions |
| 5 | **Sūryaprajñapti** | Astronomy and calendrics |
| 6 | **Candraprajñapti** | Lunar astronomy |
| 7 | **Jambūdvīpaprajñapti** | Geography of the central continent |
| 8 | **Nirayārvalī Sūtra** | Descriptions of hell |
| 9 | **Kālikācakṣuḥ Sūtra** | (Sometimes grouped differently) |
| 10 | **Puṣpikāḥ** | Stories of past lives |
| 11 | **Puṣpacūlikāḥ** | Continuation of Puṣpikāḥ |
| 12 | **Vṛṣṇidaśāḥ** | Stories of the Vṛṣṇi clan |

### 3. The 6 Cheda Sūtras (Monastic Discipline)

These texts govern the life of monks and nuns — the rules of the order:

| # | Cheda Sūtra | Content |
|---|-------------|---------|
| 1 | **Niśītha Sūtra** | Penances for monastic transgressions |
| 2 | **Mahāniśītha Sūtra** | Expanded penances |
| 3 | **Daśāśruta Skandha** | Rules for the ten classes of monks |
| 4 | **Bṛhat Kalpa Sūtra** | The great code of monastic discipline |
| 5 | **Vyavahāra Sūtra** | Procedures for resolving disputes in the saṅgha |
| 6 | **Daśā Kalpa Sūtra** | Supplementary monastic rules |

### 4. The 10 Prakīrṇakas (Miscellaneous Texts)

The most diverse division — independent treatises on specialised topics:

| # | Prakīrṇaka | Content |
|---|------------|---------|
| 1 | **Caityavandana** | Hymns of praise to the Tīrthaṅkaras |
| 2 | **Catuḥśaraṇa** | The four refuges (Jina, Siddha, Sādhu, Dharma) |
| 3 | **Guruvaṅdanā** | Reverence to teachers |
| 4 | **Sthavirāvalī** | Lineage of the elders (important for chronology) |
| 5 | **Āturapratyākhyāna** | The deathbed declaration of faith |
| 6 | **Mahāpratyākhyāna** | Great renunciation of karmic bonds |
| 7 | **Saṁlekhanā** | The practice of ritual fasting unto death |
| 8 | **Mahāśamana** | The great equanimity |
| 9 | **Loṅkāsa** | (Content uncertain in some traditions) |
| 10 | **Saṁvādavaṅdanā** | Praise of harmonious disputation |

### 5. The 4 Mūla Sūtras (Foundation Texts)

These are the texts used to train novice monks and nuns:

| # | Mūla Sūtra | Content |
|---|------------|---------|
| 1 | **Āvaśyaka Sūtra** | The six daily obligations of a monk |
| 2 | **Daśavaikālika Sūtra** | Essential teachings in 10 chapters |
| 3 | **Uttarādhyayana Sūtra** | 36 chapters of advanced teaching; one of the most beloved Jain texts |
| 4 | **Daśāśruta Skandha** | (Also counts as a Cheda Sūtra; sometimes listed separately) |

### 6. The 2 "Extra" Texts

| 44 | **Nandī Sūtra** | A treatise on the methodology of scriptural knowledge (jñāna) |
| 45 | **Anuyogadvāra Sūtra** | A treatise on the methods of exposition (anuyoga) — how to analyse and teach the Āgamas |

These two texts are often described as **late additions** — they were composed perhaps as late as the 4th–5th centuries CE, shortly before the council itself. Their inclusion was controversial, but Devarddhi argued that they provided the **hermeneutical key** to the entire canon — without them, the methods of scriptural interpretation would be lost.

## The Decision to Write in Ardhamāgadhī

One of the most consequential decisions of the Valabhī council was **not** about which texts to include — it was about **what language** those texts should be written in.

### The Options

By the 5th century CE, the intellectual landscape of India had changed dramatically since Mahāvīra's time:

- **Sanskrit** had become the dominant language of learning, philosophy, and scripture. The Buddhist Mahāyāna Sūtras were composed in Sanskrit. The Hindu Purāṇas were being written in Sanskrit. The great Brahmanical philosophical systems (Nyāya, Vedānta, Sāṃkhya) were articulated in Sanskrit. It was, by any measure, the language of prestige and authority.
- **Prākrit**, the vernacular language of Mahāvīra's time, had evolved. The old Ardhamāgadhī of the original teachings was no longer a living spoken language — it was preserved only in the monastic recitations, like Latin in the medieval Catholic Church.

The question before the council: **Should the canon be translated into Sanskrit** to make it accessible to the wider learned world, or **preserved in Ardhamāgadhī Prākrit**, the language of Mahāvīra?

### The Decision

The council chose **Ardhamāgadhī Prākrit**.

The reasons were both practical and theological:

- **Authenticity**: The Jina taught in Ardhamāgadhī — the language of the people, the language of his time. Translating the Āgamas into Sanskrit would distance them from their origin. The words themselves, in their original phonetic form, were considered **sacred** — the syllables carried power *(mantra-śakti)*.
- **Accessibility**: Sanskrit was the language of the elite — Brahmins, court scholars, philosophers. Ardhamāgadhī, even in its fossilised form, was closer to the vernacular of ordinary monks, nuns, and laypeople. To write in Sanskrit would be to **lock the scripture away** behind a wall of class and learning.
- **Continuity**: The oral tradition had preserved the Āgamas in Ardhamāgadhī for a thousand years. To change the language now would break the connection to the lineage of reciters that stretched back to Mahāvīra himself.

This decision is remarkable because it goes **against the trend of the age**. Everywhere else in India, scripture was moving into Sanskrit. The Jain council deliberately chose the opposite direction — a decision that would shape Śvetāmbara identity for centuries.

*(The Digambara tradition, facing the same question centuries earlier, chose differently — its voluminous commentaries were written in Sanskrit and in later forms of Prākrit.)*

## The Closing of the Canon

### The Principle of Closure

The most important decision of the Valabhī council was also the simplest: **the canon was closed**.

No further texts could be added to the 45 Āgamas. The list was final. From this point forward, the Āgamas were a fixed collection — a defined body of scripture that could be commented on, studied, and transmitted, but not expanded.

This was a radical step. For the first seven centuries of Jain history, the boundaries of scripture had been fluid. New texts were composed, old texts were revised, and the community had never formally declared: *"This is scripture, and nothing else is."*

The Valabhī council changed that. After Valabhī, a Śvetāmbara monk could point to a list of 45 texts and say: **"This is the word of the Jina."**

### What Was Excluded

The closure necessarily excluded texts that had circulated in Jain communities but were not deemed canonical. Among them:

- **The Ṛṣibhāṣita** ("Sayings of the Sages") — a collection of teachings attributed to various non-Jain sages, probably excluded because of their non-Jain origin
- **The Bṛhatkathākośa** and other story-collections — edifying tales, but not Āgamas
- **Early commentaries** (niryuktis, bhāṣyas) — these were authoritative but not scripture; they were the **voice of the elders**, not the voice of the Jina
- **Various regional texts** — works cherished by particular lineages but not accepted by the whole saṅgha

The exclusion of these texts did not mean they were suppressed — many continued to be copied, read, and used. But they were now **outside the canon** — commentary, not scripture.

### The Consequences of Closure

Closing the canon had profound consequences:

1. **Stability**: The Śvetāmbara textual tradition gained a firm foundation. No matter what happened to the community — invasions, famines, migrations — the canon was defined.
2. **Identity**: The 45 Āgamas became the defining marker of Śvetāmbara identity. A Jain was Śvetāmbara insofar as they accepted the Valabhī canon as authoritative.
3. **Scholarship**: With the canon fixed, scholarship turned from **textual discovery** to **textual commentary**. The great commentators — **Abhayadeva Sūri** (11th century), **Śīlaṅka** (9th century), **Haribhadra** (8th century), **Hemacandra** (12th century) — produced a vast literature of *ṭīkās* (commentaries) on the Āgamas, interpreting, explaining, and systematising the fixed canon.
4. **Sectarian division**: The closing of the canon deepened the division between Śvetāmbaras and Digambaras. The Digambaras, who had already rejected the Pāṭaliputra canon, now saw the Valabhī council as a further consolidation of a false tradition.

## Comparison with Other Canon Closures

The Council of Valabhī was not unique. Across the world, religious traditions have faced the same question: **When is scripture complete?** And they have answered it — always — by closing the canon, sometimes gently, sometimes violently.

### The Buddhist Pāli Canon (c. 80 BCE, Sri Lanka)

The closest parallel to Valabhī is the writing down of the **Theravāda Buddhist canon** in **Sri Lanka** around the 1st century BCE:

| Aspect | Jain: Valabhī | Buddhist: Sri Lanka |
|--------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Date** | c. 453–467 CE | c. 80 BCE |
| **Location** | Valabhī (Gujarat) | Aluvihāra (Sri Lanka) |
| **Convenor** | Devarddhi Gaṇin | King Vaṭṭagāmaṇī Abhaya |
| **Motive** | Deteriorating manuscripts, fading memory, doctrinal divergence | Famine, war, and the decline of oral recitation |
| **Language** | Ardhamāgadhī Prākrit (deliberate archaism) | Pāli (a Middle Indo-Aryan language, preserved as distinct from Sanskrit) |
| **Structure** | 45 Āgamas in 6 groups | Tipiṭaka (3 "baskets"): Vinaya, Sutta, Abhidhamma |
| **Canon status** | Closed; no additions permitted | Closed; the Tipiṭaka was considered final |
| **Rejection** | Rejected by Digambaras | Rejected in part by Mahāyāna traditions |

In both cases, the writing of the canon was a **crisis response** — the community recognised that oral transmission was no longer reliable and that writing was the only way to preserve the teaching. In both cases, the decision to write was controversial — some monks argued that oral transmission was the authentic method and that writing was a corruption. And in both cases, the writing **fixed** a canon that had previously been fluid.

### The Hebrew Bible Canon (c. 90 CE, Yavneh)

The Jewish canon was closed by rabbinical consensus at **Yavneh (Jamnia)** after the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE:

| Aspect | Jain: Valabhī | Jewish: Yavneh |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| **Crisis** | Fading memory, manuscript decay | Destruction of the Temple, loss of priesthood |
| **Task** | Compile and close the Āgama canon | Finalise the Tanakh (Law, Prophets, Writings) |
| **Approach** | Inclusive — 45 texts accepted | Selective — some books excluded (Apocrypha, some Wisdom books) |
| **Controversy** | Digambaras reject the canon | Sadducees and Samaritans reject the rabbinic canon |
| **Aftermath** | Canon stable for Śvetāmbaras | Tanakh fixed; subsequent literature is commentary |

In both traditions, the closure of the canon **redefined the community**. After Yavneh, Jewish identity was centred on the book — a portable, study-based religion that could survive without a Temple. After Valabhī, Śvetāmbara identity was centred on the 45 Āgamas — a fixed scripture that could survive the decline of monasteries and the rise of new kingdoms.

### The Christian New Testament Canon (4th–5th centuries CE)

The Christian canon was settled gradually. Key milestones include the **Festal Letter of Athanasius** (367 CE), the **Council of Hippo** (393 CE), and the **Council of Carthage** (397 CE), which produced lists of 27 books:

| Aspect | Jain: Valabhī | Christian: Late 4th c. |
|--------|---------------|------------------------|
| **Date** | c. 453–467 CE | c. 367–397 CE |
| **Scope** | 45 texts in 6 divisions | 27 books (Gospels, Acts, Epistles, Revelation) |
| **Selection criteria** | Lineage from Mahāvīra; established use in the saṅgha | Apostolic authorship; liturgical use; orthodoxy of content |
| **Controversies** | Digambara rejection | Some books disputed (Hebrews, Revelation, 2 Peter) |
| **Language** | Ardhamāgadhī (original teaching language) | Koine Greek (original apostolic language) |
| **Excluded texts** | Numerous early story-collections, commentaries | Gnostic gospels, Didache, Shepherd of Hermas |

The Christian councils were about **which books to include** from a large pool of Christian writings. The Jain council was about **which texts to include** from a slightly smaller pool — but also about **what form** those texts should take, since many existed only in divergent oral and manuscript versions.

## The Digambara Rejection

### Why Digambaras Reject the Valabhī Canon

The **Digambara** tradition rejects the Valabhī canon in its entirety — and this rejection follows logically from Digambara premises:

**1. The Original Canon Was Completely Lost**

For Digambaras, the Great Famine (c. 350–300 BCE) was not a partial disaster — it was a **total break**. All 12 Aṅgas, not just the Dṛṣṭivāda, were lost when the last Śruta Kevalin (Bhadrabāhu) died. The texts that Sthūlabhadra's council "recollected" at Pāṭaliputra were — in the Digambara view — **well-intentioned fabrications**: fragments of memory patched together with conjecture.

If the Pāṭaliputra Āgamas are unreliable, then the Valabhī canon — which is simply a **more polished version** of the same unreliable texts — is doubly unreliable.

**2. The Digambaras Have Their Own Āgamas**

The Digambara tradition does not accept that the authentic teaching of Mahāvīra is lost. Rather, it was preserved in a **different lineage** — the southern lineage established by Bhadrabāhu after his migration. This lineage produced:

- The **Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama** (The Scripture in Six Parts), composed by **Ācārya Puṣpadanta** and **Ācārya Bhūtabali** in the 2nd century CE
- The **Kaṣāya Prāhṛta** by **Ācārya Guṇadhara** (2nd century CE)
- The **Cauvisaṅga Sutra** and other later texts

These texts are not "replacements" for the lost Āgamas — they are the **authoritative exposition** of the teaching that was transmitted through the unbroken lineage of Ācāryas. For Digambaras, the transmission of **meaning** is more important than the transmission of **literal words**.

**3. Valabhī Was a Sectarian Consolidation**

From the Digambara perspective, the Valabhī council was not a neutral editorial project — it was an act of **Śvetāmbara sectarian consolidation**. By fixing a particular set of texts in a particular language (Ardhamāgadhī) and declaring them closed, the Śvetāmbaras were drawing a boundary that excluded the Digambara lineage.

The decision to write in Ardhamāgadhī is particularly significant. Digambara Ācāryas wrote in **Sanskrit** and in **Mahārāṣṭrī Prākrit** — not Ardhamāgadhī. By making Ardhamāgadhī the exclusive language of scripture, the Valabhī council implicitly delegitimised the Digambara textual tradition.

**4. The Valabhī Canon Contains Teachings That Digambaras Reject**

The most famous point of difference is the **status of women**. The Śvetāmbara canon includes texts that affirm the possibility of **women attaining liberation** (mokṣa). The Digambara tradition — based on its own Āgamic lineage — maintains that **a woman cannot attain liberation without first being reborn as a man**. This is not a minor difference: it touches the fundamental question of who can practise Jainism at its highest level.

If the Valabhī canon includes texts that say women can attain liberation, and the Digambara tradition maintains that this is false, then the Valabhī canon cannot be authoritative.

### The Depth of the Divide

The Digambara rejection of Valabhī is not an argument about a few books — it is a **fundamental disagreement about the nature of scripture itself**:

| Aspect | Śvetāmbara (Valabhī) View | Digambara View |
|--------|---------------------------|----------------|
| **Source of scripture** | The written Āgamas as fixed at Valabhī | The unbroken lineage of Ācāryas, especially the authors of the Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama |
| **Language of scripture** | Ardhamāgadhī (the language of Mahāvīra) | Sanskrit and Mahārāṣṭrī Prākrit |
| **Status of the Aṅgas** | 11 Aṅgas are authentic (the 12th is lost) | All 12 Aṅgas are lost; the Ācārya lineage preserves the true teaching |
| **Women's liberation** | Possible in the same birth | Impossible; a female birth requires a male rebirth before liberation |
| **Monastic practice** | Monks wear white robes; nuns exist in the order | Monks are naked (dig-ambara = "sky-clad"); the order of nuns is disputed |
| **Canon structure** | 45 Āgamas in 6 divisions | The Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama, Kaṣāya Prāhṛta, and later commentaries |

The two traditions share a common origin — Mahāvīra — but they diverge so fundamentally on the question of **what counts as scripture** that they have effectively become separate religions.

---

## The Significance: What Valabhī Achieved

### The Stabilisation of Scripture

The Council of Valabhī achieved what the Council of Pāṭaliputra could not: **a canon that could be transmitted without decay**. The 45 Āgamas, written in a standardised form, with a clear structure and fixed boundaries, became the stable foundation of Śvetāmbara textual tradition.

For over 1,500 years since the council, the canon has remained unchanged. No text has been added. No text has been removed. The manuscripts have been copied and recopied, the texts have been commented upon endlessly, but the **list of 45** has held firm.

### The Preservation of the Language

The decision to preserve the Āgamas in Ardhamāgadhī meant that the **words of Mahāvīra** — or something very close to them — would survive in their original phonetic form. This is not merely a matter of linguistic archaism. In Jain theology, the sounds of the Āgamas have a **transformative power** *(varṇa-siddhi)*. The syllables, when recited correctly, generate merit and purify karma. To change the language would be to change the sound — and thus to diminish the power.

The council's linguistic conservatism ensured that the Śvetāmbara tradition would forever be linked to the **living sound** of Ardhamāgadhī — a language that lives nowhere else, but survives in the chanting of the Āgamas.

### The Definition of Śvetāmbara Identity

Before Valabhī, "Śvetāmbara" was a loose term for the white-robed monks of the northern Jain tradition. After Valabhī, it became a **defined religious identity** — a community centred on a fixed scripture, with a clear canon, a clear lineage, and a clear boundary.

The canon did not create the community — but it gave the community **self-consciousness**. A Śvetāmbara monk knew who he was because he knew what he studied: these 45 texts, and no others.

### The Shift from Oral to Written Authority

The council completed a shift that had begun at Pāṭaliputra seven centuries earlier: the shift from **oral authority** to **written authority**.

In the early Jain tradition, authority resided in the **living voice** — the teacher who had memorised the Āgamas and could recite them. The text was not a book; it was a relationship between teacher and student. After Valabhī, authority increasingly resided in the **written manuscript** — the physical object that could be stored, copied, and consulted. Monasteries built libraries (bhāṇḍāras). Scribes became important. The book became the icon of the tradition.

This shift was not without loss. The oral tradition — the art of memorisation, the living connection to the lineage of reciters — gradually declined. But the written canon ensured that even if the oral tradition disappeared entirely, the words would survive.

## The Aftermath: What Came After

### The Dissemination of the Canon

After the council, copies of the 45 Āgamas were distributed to major Śvetāmbara monasteries across India. The manuscript tradition grew:

- **Palm-leaf manuscripts** remained the standard until the arrival of paper (c. 12th–13th centuries CE)
- Major libraries, such as those at **Pāṭaṇ**, **Cambay**, **Jaisalmer**, and **Māṇḍvī**, preserved the canon
- The **Jain manuscript culture** became one of the most sophisticated in India — with beautifully illustrated manuscripts, careful colophons, and detailed catalogues

### The Age of Commentary

With the canon fixed, the great age of Śvetāmbara commentary began:

| Century | Commentator | Major Work |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| **8th c.** | **Haribhadra Sūri** | Multiple commentaries; systematised Jain logic and ethics |
| **9th c.** | **Śīlaṅka Sūri** | Commentary on the Ācārāṅga Sūtra |
| **11th c.** | **Abhayadeva Sūri** | The Vṛtti (commentary) on several Aṅgas |
| **11th c.** | **Nemicandra Sūri** | Pravacanasāroddhāra (a digest of the Āgamas) |
| **12th c.** | **Hemacandra** | The Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra and many grammatical/lexical works that made the Āgamas accessible |

These commentators did not add to the canon — but they **unlocked** it. Their commentaries explained obscure passages, reconciled apparent contradictions, and connected the Āgamas to the philosophical and scientific knowledge of their time.

### The Challenge of the Yāpanīyas

Not every Jain community accepted the Valabhī canon. The **Yāpanīya** sect — a now-extinct Jain tradition that existed primarily in Karnataka and Andhra (c. 5th–14th centuries CE) — occupied a middle ground between Śvetāmbaras and Digambaras:

- They agreed with the Śvetāmbaras on the **authenticity of the 11 Aṅgas** (the Pāṭaliputra/Valabhī texts)
- But they agreed with the Digambaras on **monastic practice** (like the Digambaras, they held that a monk who owns property is not a true monk)
- They developed their own commentarial tradition, synthesising Śvetāmbara and Digambara elements

The Yāpanīyas are a reminder that the binary of "Śvetāmbara vs. Digambara" was not the whole story. There were **intermediate traditions** that tried to hold both canons together — and they existed for nearly a millennium before fading away.

---

## The Legacy: Valabhī in the Later Tradition

### The Transmission to the Present

The 45 Āgamas fixed at Valabhī are not merely historical artefacts. They remain the **living scripture** of the Śvetāmbara tradition today:

- The **Śvetāmbara Mūrtipūjaka** (image-worshipping) community — the largest Śvetāmbara sub-tradition — accepts the 45 Āgamas as authoritative
- The **Sthānakavāsī** (non-image-worshipping) and **Terāpanthī** reform movements, which emerged in the 15th–18th centuries, accept **most** of the 45 Āgamas but reject some as later additions or corruptions
- The 45 Āgamas have been printed, translated, and studied in modern times — with critical editions produced by Jain scholars and Western academics alike

### The Unfinished Business

The Council of Valabhī closed the canon — but it did not settle all questions.

The most persistent unresolved question concerns the **Dṛṣṭivāda** — the lost 12th Aṅga. Some Śvetāmbara traditions hold that the Dṛṣṭivāda was **not actually lost** but was **withheld** from the canon because its teachings were too advanced for the current age. Others hold that it was suppressed because it contained doctrines that were too close to Digambara positions. A few esoteric lineages claim to preserve fragments of it in secret manuscripts.

None of these claims are accepted by mainstream Śvetāmbara scholarship. The Dṛṣṭivāda remains lost — a permanent gap in the canon, a reminder that not everything can be recovered.

### The Meaning of the Story

The Council of Valabhī is a story about **the power of a decision**.

The Jain tradition had lived for a thousand years without a closed canon. The texts were fluid, the boundaries were porous, and the community was held together not by a fixed scripture but by a shared lineage of teachers and a shared way of life. The Valabhī council **decided** to change that. It drew a line and said: *"This is scripture. Everything else is commentary."*

That decision had costs. It deepened the schism with the Digambaras. It excluded texts that some communities cherished. It froze the canon in a particular language and a particular form, foreclosing future development.

But it also had benefits. It gave the Śvetāmbara tradition a **stable foundation** — a canon that could survive the collapse of kingdoms, the destruction of monasteries, and the migration of communities. The manuscripts copied at Valabhī are long gone, but the canon those manuscripts contained has survived to the present day.

In the end, the Council of Valabhī tells us something profound about **how scripture works**. Scripture is not simply "revealed" — it is **made**. It is the result of human decisions: decisions about what to include and what to exclude, what language to use and what form to preserve, when to open the canon and when to close it. The Council of Valabhī was one of those decisions — a decision made by fallible, learned, devout human beings who understood that the alternative to decision was disintegration.

They chose. And their choice has shaped Śvetāmbara Jainism for 1,500 years.

## Key Figures

### Devarddhi Gaṇin Kṣamāśramaṇa

The central figure. Devarddhi is remembered as:

- **The editor-in-chief** of the Śvetāmbara canon — the man who oversaw the comparison, reconciliation, and finalisation of every text
- **A diplomat** who held together a disparate assembly of elders with competing loyalties
- **A traditionalist** who chose Ardhamāgadhī over Sanskrit, inclusivity over minimalism, and closure over openness
- **A saint** in later Śvetāmbara hagiography — his title "Kṣamāśramaṇa" (the patient ascetic) reflects his reputation for forbearance and wisdom

His exact dates are uncertain, but his achievement is not. Every Śvetāmbara Āgama manuscript in existence descends, ultimately, from the recension he established.

### King Dhruvasena I of the Maitraka Dynasty

The patron. Dhruvasena provided:

- **Financial support** — funding the council's operations, scribes, and materials
- **Political authority** — lending the legitimacy of the Maitraka court to the council's decisions
- **A secure location** — Valabhī's prosperity and stability allowed the council to work for years without interruption

His motivations were probably mixed: genuine religious piety, political calculation (a unified Jain community was a more stable community), and cultural ambition (a fixed canon was a mark of civilisational achievement).

### The Elders of the Council

The other elders who participated are mostly anonymous in the historical record — their names lost, their voices silent. But their presence mattered. The council's authority depended on representation: the major monastic lineages of the Śvetāmbara world had to be present for the decisions to be binding.
