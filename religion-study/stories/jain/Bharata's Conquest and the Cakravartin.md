# Bharata's Conquest and the Cakravartin

> *"The wheel of empire turns but once. He who seizes it must also know when to let it go — for the wheel of dharma is greater than the wheel of the world."*
> — Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra, Book II (adapted)

The story of **Bharata** — the first **Cakravartin** (universal emperor) of the present descending age — is the Jain tradition's great meditation on **power, ambition, renunciation, and the limits of worldly dominion**. It is a story that begins with a coronation and ends with a brother standing motionless in the forest for twelve years. More than a conquest narrative, it is a parable about how even the greatest empire must yield to the greater law of dharma.

Bharata is the eldest son of **Ṛṣabha** (Ādinātha), the first Tīrthaṅkara, and the brother of **Bāhubali**. His story is preserved most fully in the **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (Hemacandra, 12th century CE) and the **Ādīpurāṇa** (Jinasena, 9th century CE), and it provides the Jain etymology for the name **Bhārata-varṣa** — the ancient name for the Indian subcontinent.

---

## The Son of Ṛṣabha

Bharata was the **eldest** of Ṛṣabha's ninety-nine sons and one of two daughters (Brāhmī and Sundarī). He was born to Queen **Yaśasvatī** (also called **Sunandā**) in the city of **Vinītā** (later Ayodhyā). From infancy, he showed the signs of future greatness: a broad chest, a lion's gait, a voice that commanded attention, and a mind that grasped strategy and statecraft before his tutors could teach it.

> *"When Bharata was born, the gods placed a radiant tilaka on his forehead — the mark of the future Cakravartin. His father Ṛṣabha looked at the child and said: 'He will rule what I have only built. Let him be named Bharata — the Sustainer.'"*
> — Ādīpurāṇa 22 (adapted)

As a youth, Bharata mastered the **sixty-four kalās** (classical arts) that Ṛṣabha had taught humanity — writing, arithmetic, martial arts, music, dance, governance, engineering, and philosophy. But his true genius lay in **administration and warfare**. Where his younger brother Bāhubali was contemplative and brooding, Bharata was decisive and commanding. Where Bāhubali sought meaning, Bharata sought order.

When Ṛṣabha decided to renounce the world, he divided his kingdom among his sons. To **Bharata**, as the eldest, he gave the capital city **Ayodhyā** and the heart of the realm — the most fertile and populous region. To **Bāhubali**, the second son, he gave the prosperous southern city of **Podanapur** (Takshaśilā / Takkasilā in some accounts). The other ninety-seven sons received smaller territories.

> *"Ṛṣabha divided his kingdom as a farmer divides a field — not knowing which seed would grow, but trusting that each would bear its own fruit. He gave Bharata the centre. He gave Bāhubali the south. He gave the rest to the others. And he walked away."*
> — Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra I.4

The division was at once a bequest and a test. Ṛṣabha knew his sons. He knew that Bharata would not be satisfied with a single kingdom, and that Bāhubali would not bow to any man. The soil was prepared for a conflict that would define the spiritual history of the age.

---

## The Coronation and the Appearance of the Cakraratna

Upon Ṛṣabha's renunciation, Bharata was crowned king of Ayodhyā. The coronation was performed with full Vedic and Jain rites: the **abhiṣeka** (consecration), the offerings to the gods, the anointing with the five nectars (pañcāmṛta), and the declaration of the new king's sovereignty.

But the coronation of a future Cakravartin is never an ordinary affair. As the final rites were completed, a sign appeared from the heavens.

### The Divine Wheel-Jewel

The **cakraratna** — the divine wheel-jewel — descended from the celestial realms and appeared before Bharata. It was a great, radiant disc of pure light, spinning at the edge of the sky, its rim sharp as a blade, its hub a perfect circle of sapphire and gold. It hung in the air above the palace, visible to all.

This is the defining sign of a **Cakravartin** — a "wheel-turner," one who rules by the universal authority that dharma itself bestows. The cakraratna does not appear for every king. It appears only for the one whose accumulated merit — from countless past lives of generosity, restraint, and compassion — has earned the right to rule the entire world.

> *"The cakraratna is not a weapon. It is a symbol. It does not cut through bodies — it cuts through resistance. Where it rolls, the hearts of enemies melt. Where it stops, even the gods must pay homage."*
> — Ādīpurāṇa 24

### The Meaning of the Cakraratna

In Jain cosmology, the cakraratna is one of the **fourteen ratnas** (jewels) that accompany a Cakravartin. It is the first and most important. Its function is both practical and symbolic:

- **Authority**: Its appearance signifies that the bearer has been recognised by the cosmic order as the rightful universal monarch. No election, no conquest, no inheritance confers this — only the accumulated karma of ages.
- **Navigation**: The cakraratna rolls ahead of the army, leading the Cakravartin on his conquest. It rolls through the air at the height of a palm tree, visible to all, and it moves **toward** the kingdoms that must be conquered, never stopping until all have submitted.
- **Irresistibility**: Where the wheel passes, no army can stand against it. Kings either submit peacefully or are defeated by the sheer force of dharma that the wheel embodies.
- **Boundary**: The wheel marks the limits of the Cakravartin's dominion. It traverses each of the six continents (khaṇḍas) of the middle world (madhyaloka), and when it reaches the edge of the seventh, the ocean that rings the world, it stops — for even a Cakravartin cannot rule what lies beyond the horizon of the human realm.

Bharata, seeing the cakraratna, understood his destiny. He was not merely a king. He was the **Cakravartin** — the embodiment of righteous rule over the entire inhabited world.

---

## The Fourteen Jewels of the Cakravartin

Along with the cakraratna, thirteen other jewels (ratnas) manifested themselves to Bharata, each a divine instrument of sovereignty. The full set of **fourteen ratnas** defines the perfect Cakravartin's court:

| # | Ratna (Jewel) | Description | Function |
|---|---------------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | **Cakraratna** (Wheel-jewel) | The spinning disc of light | Leads the army; signals divine authority |
| 2 | **Chatraratna** (Parasol-jewel) | A divine white parasol, always held above the king | Symbol of sovereignty; provides shade from the sun of worldly suffering |
| 3 | **Khaḍgaratna** (Sword-jewel) | A flawless sword of celestial metal | Cuts through all obstacles; never loses its edge |
| 4 | **Cāmararatna** (Whisk-jewel) | A yak-tail fan that waves itself | Honour and service; the natural devotion of subjects |
| 5 | **Maṇiratna** (Gem-jewel) | A luminous gem that illuminates the palace at night | Light in darkness; wisdom in confusion |
| 6 | **Mukuṭaratna** (Crown-jewel) | A crown that fits only the rightful Cakravartin | Legitimacy; the visible sign of authority |
| 7 | **Aṅgulīyakaratna** (Ring-jewel) | A signet ring that stamps the royal seal | Executive power; the ability to enact law |
| 8 | **Karaṇḍaratna** (Casket-jewel) | A casket containing earth from all kingdoms | Dominion over the land itself |
| 9 | **Śayyāratna** (Bed-jewel) | A bed that grants perfect rest and prophetic dreams | Divine guidance; the connection between the king and the gods |
| 10 | **Āsanaratna** (Throne-jewel) | A throne that adjusts itself to the king's height | Justice; the perfect fit between ruler and rule |
| 11 | **Pādukāratna** (Sandal-jewel) | Sandals that lift the wearer above the ground | Transcendence; the king who walks above the dust of the world |
| 12 | **Snānaratna** (Bath-jewel) | A bath that purifies the king on contact | Ritual purity; the cleansing of karma through righteous rule |
| 13 | **Bherīratna** (Drum-jewel) | A drum that sounds the call to dharma, not to war | Proclamation; the voice of the king that commands without violence |
| 14 | **Aśvaratna** (Horse-jewel) | A divine white horse that can fly across continents | Speed; the reach of the Cakravartin's authority across the world |

In addition to these ratnas, a Cakravartin possesses unparalleled **armies** — infantry, cavalry, chariots, and elephants — each numbering in the tens of thousands, each perfectly trained, each loyal unto death. The treasury of a Cakravartin is inexhaustible, the granaries overflowing, the cities walled with gemstones.

But the fourteen jewels are not merely possessions. They are **karmic rewards** — the visible fruit of countless past lives of generosity (dāna), moral discipline (śīla), and meditation (dhyāna). A Cakravartin does not earn the jewels through conquest. He earns them before birth, through the slow accumulation of merit over aeons. The conquest merely actualises what the jewels already represent.

## The Conquest of the Six Continents

With the cakraratna spinning before him, Bharata set out from Ayodhyā to conquer the **six khaṇḍas** (continents) of the middle world (madhyaloka). In Jain cosmology, the human realm is divided into a series of concentric continents separated by oceans:

| Continent | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Jambūdvīpa** (Rose-Apple Island) | The innermost continent; contains Bhārata-varṣa, Airavata-varṣa, and other regions. Ayodhyā lies here. |
| **Dhātakīkhaṇḍa** | The second continent, encircling the Lavaṇa Ocean |
| **Puṣkaravara** | The third continent, in which Jambūdvīpa is said to be "the flower" |
| **Vāruṇīvara** | The fourth continent |
| **Kṣīravarā** | The fifth continent |
| **Śarkarā-parā** | The farthest known division |

The Cakravartin's dominion extends across all six. Beyond the sixth lies the seventh ocean and the edge of the inhabitable world — the **antardvīpas** (intermediate islands) where no human can rule.

### The Method of Conquest

Bharata's conquest was not, in the Jain telling, a series of bloody wars. The cakraratna precedes the army, and its presence is so powerful that most kings submit without a fight.

The logic of the cakravartin-conquest is distinctive in Jain tradition:

> *"When the cakraratna enters a kingdom, the king sees the wheel and knows that resistance is futile. Fighting the Cakravartin is like fighting dharma itself. The wise king bows, offers tribute, and becomes a vassal. The foolish king fights and is destroyed — not by Bharata's army, but by the accumulated bad karma that led him to resist the wheel."*
> — Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra II.3

The conquest proceeds in stages:

1. **The cakraratna enters the kingdom** — it appears in the sky, spinning, radiating light.
2. **The local king is given a choice** — submit to the Cakravartin and retain his throne as a tributary ruler, or resist and be destroyed.
3. **Most submit** — the cakraratna is, after all, a sign from the gods. To refuse it is to refuse cosmic order.
4. **Those who resist are defeated swiftly** — the cakraratna itself leads the assault; the army follows behind to secure what the wheel has broken.

Bharata passed through all six continents in this manner. Kingdom after kingdom bowed. King after king offered tribute — gold, elephants, gems, grain, and soldiers for the imperial army. The treasury of Ayodhyā swelled beyond measure. The empire of Bharata stretched from the northern mountains to the southern seas, from the eastern sunrise to the western sunset.

### The Sovereignty of the Nine Divisions of Bhārata-varṣa

Within **Jambūdvīpa**, the continent in which Bharata's own kingdom lay, there is the region called **Bhārata-varṣa** — named after Bharata himself. According to Jain geography, Bhārata-varṣa is divided into **nine divisions** (khaṇḍas), each with its own distinct landscape and people:

| Division | Location / Description |
|----------|----------------------|
| **Indra-dvīpa** | The easternmost region |
| **Kāśyapa-dvīpa** | The western region associated with the Kāśyapa clan |
| **Tāmraparṇī** | The southern peninsula, rich in copper and spices |
| **Gandharva** | The central highlands, home to the finest horses |
| **Vara-dvīpa** | The northern plains |
| **Vārāha** | The forested foothills of the Himavān range |
| **Kumuda** | The eastern monsoon lands |
| **Mālyavat** | The western hills, famous for sandalwood and gems |
| **The Heartland (Madhyadeśa)** | The Gangetic plain — the seat of Ayodhyā and the imperial capital |

Bharata ruled all nine divisions with justice. He appointed governors, established law courts, built roads, dug wells, and ensured that dharma was upheld in every village. He did not merely conquer — he **civilised**, as his father had civilised before him.

> *"Bharata walked through his empire not as a conqueror but as a gardener. He watered what was dry. He pruned what was overgrown. He let grow what was natural. Under his rule, even the trees bore sweeter fruit."*
> — Ādīpurāṇa 28

## The Naming of Bhārata-varṣa

The most enduring legacy of Bharata's empire is the name it gave to the land. **Bhārata-varṣa** — "the land of Bharata" — is the traditional Sanskrit name for the Indian subcontinent, and it is Jain tradition (shared with Hindu and Buddhist sources) that the land was named after this first universal emperor.

The **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** records:

> *"Because Bharata ruled this land with righteousness, because he established dharma here, because he brought all its peoples under a single law — therefore this varṣa is called Bhārata-varṣa. It shall bear his name as long as the earth endures."*
> — Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra II.5

The name carries theological weight. It is not mere geography. **Bhārata-varṣa** is, in Jain cosmology, the **only region in the universe where liberation is possible**. In other continents and regions, beings live in automatic cycles of pleasure or pain, but only in Bhārata-varṣa (and its parallel regions Airavata-varṣa and Videha-varṣa) do beings have the free will and the opportunity to become Tīrthaṅkaras and attain mokṣa. By naming the land after Bharata, the tradition links the worldly power of the Cakravartin to the spiritual destiny of the land itself.

## The Crisis at Takkasilā: Bāhubali's Defiance

The conquest of the six continents was complete — but one territory remained **unconquered**. Not a foreign kingdom, but the city given by Ṛṣabha to his second son: **Podanapur** / **Takkasilā** (Takshaśilā).

### The Wheel Stops

When Bharata set out with the cakraratna to subdue the remaining regions of Jambūdvīpa, the wheel — which had never stopped before — **halted at the border of Bāhubali's territory**.

This was an unprecedented event. The cakraratna, which had rolled through every continent, through every kingdom, through every army — now hovered at the boundary of a single city, spinning but not advancing, as if it had hit an invisible wall.

> *"The wheel stopped. The generals looked at each other in confusion. The soldiers murmured. Bharata, from his chariot, stared at the motionless disc. He had never seen it pause. He had never known it could be resisted. And yet — there it hung, spinning in place, like a lion that has found an unexpected rival."*
> — Ādīpurāṇa 31

The cakraratna does not advance into a kingdom ruled by one whose merit equals or exceeds that of the Cakravartin himself. Bāhubali, born with the same karmic inheritance as Bharata — the same father, the same lineage, the same past lives of austerity — had accumulated merit that made him **immune** to the wheel's authority. The cakraratna could not compel him because he was, in the cosmic balance, **Bharata's equal**.

### Bharata's Demand

Bharata sent messengers to Takkasilā. The demand was simple: Bāhubali must acknowledge Bharata's sovereignty, pay tribute, and accept the status of a vassal king. In return, he would retain his throne and his dignity.

Bāhubali's response was equally simple:

> *"Tell my brother that I do not bow to any man. I was born of the same father. I was raised in the same palace. I possess the same strength. If he wants my kingdom, let him come and take it — not through the wheel of the gods, but through the strength of his own arms."*
> — Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra II.8 (adapted)

The messengers returned with this reply. Bharata's court fell silent. The empire had never faced defiance. The Cakravartin had never been challenged. And now, the challenger was his own brother.

## The War Against Bāhubali

Diplomacy failed. The cakraratna would not advance. There was only one way forward: **war**.

### The Armies

Bharata assembled the full might of his empire. The numbers, given in the traditional sources, are staggering:

| Force | Strength |
|-------|----------|
| **Infantry** | 840,000 |
| **Cavalry** | 60,000 |
| **Chariots** | 30,000 |
| **Elephants** | 12,000 |
| **Foot-soldiers (reserve)** | Numberless |

Bāhubali's army was smaller but fiercely loyal. His soldiers were not conscripts — they were volunteers who believed in their king. And Bāhubali himself was a warrior of almost superhuman strength, trained in the same arts as Bharata, blessed with the same physical prowess.

> *"Bāhubali stood at the gates of Takkasilā, and his shadow fell across the plain. He was taller than any man in the army — not a giant of legend, but a warrior whose presence made others feel small. He carried no weapon. He needed none."*
> — Ādīpurāṇa 33

### The First Phase: The Battle of Champions

The war began conventionally. Chariots clashed. Elephants thundered. Arrows darkened the sky. For days, the armies fought. The earth soaked up blood. The rivers ran red.

But as the casualties mounted on both sides, the generals began to realise that this war could not be won by armies alone. Bharata and Bāhubali were too evenly matched. Their soldiers were too loyal. The battle would grind on until both kingdoms were emptied of young men.

A truce was called. It was decided: the war would be settled by **single combat between the brothers**. The armies would watch, but they would not fight. The kings themselves would decide the fate of the empire.

### The Four Contests

The single combat was not one fight but **four** — each a different mode of contest. The brothers agreed to meet in:

| Contest | Form | Description |
|---------|------|-------------|
| **1. Wrestling (Mallayuddha)** | Hand-to-hand combat | The brothers grapple, each trying to throw the other to the ground |
| **2. The Staring Match (Netra-yuddha)** | A contest of will | The brothers stare into each other's eyes; the first to blink loses |
| **3. The Water Battle (Jala-yuddha)** | A contest in water | The brothers fight in a lake or river, each trying to submerge the other |
| **4. The Slap (Tala-yuddha)** | A single blow | Each brother takes a turn striking the other with an open palm; the one who moves or shows pain loses |

#### The Wrestling Match

The brothers clashed in the centre of the field. They were evenly matched in size, strength, and training. Bharata was quicker; Bāhubali was stronger. They wrestled for hours, locked in a deadly embrace, neither able to gain the advantage. The dust rose around them. The armies watched in silence.

Finally, Bāhubali lifted Bharata off the ground — not with a throw, but with sheer strength. He held his brother in the air, suspended, helpless.

He could have thrown him. He could have broken his spine. But Bāhubali paused.

> *"This is my brother. This is the son of my father. If I kill him, what have I won? A kingdom soaked in the blood of my own blood."*
> — Bāhubali's thought, Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra II.12

He set Bharata down gently and stepped back. The wrestling match was declared a draw — but everyone present knew that Bāhubali had won, and had chosen not to claim the victory.

#### The Staring Match

The brothers sat cross-legged on the ground, facing each other. They locked eyes. The rule: the first to blink, to look away, to show any sign of distraction or discomfort, loses.

Hours passed. The sun crossed the sky. The afternoon shadows lengthened. Bharata's eyes began to water. Bāhubali's gaze was steady as stone. The younger brother had always had the stronger will — the capacity for absolute stillness that would later define his legendary meditation.

Bharata blinked.

Bāhubali had won again.

#### The Water Battle

They entered the river that bordered the battlefield. The water was up to their chests. The contest: each brother must try to force the other underwater and hold him there.

In the water, Bāhubali's advantage of raw strength was even more pronounced. He seized Bharata, forced him under the surface, and held him there. Bharata's lungs burned. He thrashed. Bāhubali held firm.

Seconds stretched into minutes. Bharata's struggles weakened. Soldiers on the bank began to murmur. Was Bāhubali going to drown his own brother?

At the last moment — when Bharata's body had gone limp, when the soldiers were about to rush into the water — Bāhubali pulled his brother up. Bharata gasped, coughed, and clung to Bāhubali's shoulder.

Bāhubali had won the third contest.

#### The Slap

The final contest was the most brutal. Each brother would stand still and receive a single open-palm slap from the other. The one who moved — who stumbled, who showed pain, who flinched — would lose.

Bharata struck first. He put all his strength, all his frustration, all his imperial fury into the blow. The slap landed on Bāhubali's chest with a sound like thunder. Bāhubali did not move. Not a muscle. Not a breath. He stood there like a mountain struck by lightning — unmoved, unmovable.

Now it was Bāhubali's turn.

He raised his hand. The armies fell silent. Bharata braced himself.

The slap landed.

Bharata stumbled backward. His eyes went wide. Pain shot through his chest — but more than the pain, there was **humiliation**. Before the entire army, before the vassal kings who had come to watch, before the gods who had descended to witness the contest — Bharata had lost. Not once, but four times.

He had conquered the world. He could not conquer his brother.

### Bharata's Humiliation and the Escalation

The four contests were over. Bāhubali had won all four. By the terms of the truce, the war should have ended. Bāhubali should have retained his kingdom. Bharata should have accepted the loss and returned to Ayodhyā.

But Bharata's pride would not let him.

He retreated to his camp in a fury. He called his ministers, his generals, his engineers. He demanded a new weapon — something that Bāhubali could not match with his bare hands. The ministers, afraid of the emperor's wrath, proposed a war machine: the **sahasra-bāhu-yantra** (the "thousand-armed machine") — a device that could throw a volley of weapons simultaneously, overwhelming even the greatest warrior.

> *"Build it,"* Bharata said. *"And build it quickly."*

### Bāhubali's Response — A Bolder Provocation

When Bāhubali heard that Bharata was preparing a new weapon, he did not wait for the attack. He walked out of Takkasilā alone — without armour, without weapons, without an army. He walked to the field where the imperial army was camped.

The soldiers saw him coming. They parted before him like water before a stone. He walked directly to the great war machine and stood before it.

Then he did something extraordinary.

He picked up a massive stone — a boulder the size of a small elephant — and carried it to a hill overlooking the camp. He sat down on the hill, the boulder beside him. And he waited.

The message was clear: *I will not attack. But I will not be attacked. If you bring your machine against me, I will crush it with this stone. I do not wish to fight. But I will not lose.*

The army did not know what to do. The generals did not know what to do. Bharata, watching from his tent, saw his brother sitting on the hill like a god of the mountain — invincible not because of his weapons but because of his **absolute refusal to be moved**.

> *"Bāhubali sat on that hill, and the sun set behind him, and the stars came out, and the campfires of the imperial army flickered below him. And Bharata looked at his brother and understood, for the first time, that there are things the cakraratna cannot conquer."*
> — Ādīpurāṇa 34

## The Intervention of the Sages and the Turn to Renunciation

The war had reached a stalemate. Bharata could not defeat Bāhubali. Bāhubali would not attack Bharata. The armies remained frozen, waiting for a command that neither king would give.

Into this impasse came the **sages** — the Jain monks, the renunciants who followed the path that Ṛṣabha had shown. They had been watching the conflict from the forests, from the caves, from the places where the world of conquest seemed a distant dream. Now they came to the battlefield.

### The Teaching of the Sages

The sages did not take sides. They did not condemn Bharata or praise Bāhubali. They simply spoke the truth:

> *"Kings, look at what you are doing. You are brothers. You share the same father. You share the same blood. You are fighting over a piece of earth that will not remember your names in a hundred years. Is this worth the karma you are accumulating? Is this worth the lives you are sacrificing? Is this worth the bondage you are weaving around your own souls?"*

> *"Your father Ṛṣabha renounced the world. He gave away the very kingdom you are fighting over. He walked into the forest with nothing. And now he sits in the siddha-śilā, beyond all sorrow, beyond all birth, beyond all death. Is this not the greater victory?"*

The words struck both brothers — but they struck **differently**.

Bharata, hearing the sages, felt the weight of his empire settle on his shoulders. He had conquered the world. He owned everything. And yet the sages owned nothing, and they were happier than he had ever been. A seed of doubt was planted.

Bāhubali, hearing the sages, felt something else. He had always been contemplative. He had always felt that the world of conquest was not his true home. Now, standing on the hill above the army, he looked down at the camp, at his brother's tent, at the wheel-jewel that still hovered at the border of his kingdom — and he saw it all as **māyā** — illusion.

> *"Why am I proud of this victory? What have I won? I have defeated my brother. I have humiliated him. But has my soul gained anything? My brother will go back to his palace. I will go back to mine. And we will both die, and be reborn, and die again — unless we find the path."*
> — Bāhubali's realisation, Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra II.15

### Bāhubali's Renunciation

Bāhubali was the first to act.

He stood up from the hill. The boulder he had carried there remained where it was. He walked down toward the imperial camp — not to fight, but to **renounce**.

He entered Bharata's tent. The generals stepped aside. Bharata looked up — expecting a messenger, expecting a challenge, expecting war.

What he saw was his brother, standing before him, with tears in his eyes.

> *"Brother,"* Bāhubali said. *"Take the kingdom. It is yours. I do not want it. I want what our father found. I want what the sages have. I want liberation."*

And without another word, Bāhubali walked out of the tent, walked out of the camp, walked out of Takkasilā, and walked into the forest. He tore out his hair in five handfuls — the **keśa-loca** — and became a **nirgrantha**, a homeless ascetic.

He never spoke again — not to Bharata, not to anyone — for twelve years. He stood in a single spot, motionless, in the **kāyotsarga** posture, his arms hanging at his sides, his eyes fixed on a point in the distance, utterly absorbed in meditation. His story continues in **[[Bāhubali's Twelve-Year Meditation]]**.

### Bharata's Response

Bharata sat in his tent alone for a long time after Bāhubali left. The sages' words echoed in his mind. His brother's renunciation echoed louder.

He could have followed immediately. He could have renounced then and there, as his father and his brother had done. But Bharata was the Cakravartin. He had responsibilities. He had an empire to govern. He had the dharma of a king to fulfil.

> *"Bharata said to himself: 'My brother has renounced because he is ready. I am not ready. I still have work to do. I must rule as a righteous king. I must uphold dharma. I must ensure that the world I have conquered is a world worth living in. When my work is done, I too will follow the path.'"*
> — Ādīpurāṇa 36

He returned to Ayodhyā. The war was over. The empire was intact. The brother was gone. The cakraratna — now that Bāhubali was no longer blocking it — rolled across Bāhubali's former kingdom, marking it as part of the empire. But Bharata took no joy in it. The wheel that had once filled him with pride now seemed hollow.

## Bharata's Later Rule: The Establishment of Dharma

Having conquered the world and lost his brother, Bharata turned his attention to the **administration of righteousness**. His later reign is remembered not for its wars but for its laws.

### The Establishment of Law (Vyavahāra)

Bharata systematised the legal code that Ṛṣabha had first established. He appointed judges in every city, defined punishments for every crime, and ensured that justice was administered equally to all — regardless of caste, wealth, or royal connection.

His legal principles, as recorded in Jain sources:

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Daṇḍa-samitā** | Punishment must be proportionate to the crime, never excessive |
| **Sākṣī-vidhi** | No one can be convicted without the testimony of witnesses |
| **Uttara-daṇḍa** | The king is subject to the same laws as the lowest subject |
| **Kṣamā-daṇḍa** | Where possible, forgiveness should replace punishment |
| **Dāna-daṇḍa** | Fines should be given to the poor, not to the treasury |

### The Regulation of Caste (Varṇa-vyavasthā)

Following Ṛṣabha's model, Bharata regulated the **varṇa** (social classes) not as a rigid birth-based hierarchy but as a **functional division of labour**:

- **Kṣatriyas** — those who rule, protect, and govern (the warrior-administrator class)
- **Vaiśyas** — those who trade, farm, and produce (the merchant-agricultural class)
- **Śūdras** — those who serve (the labourer class)
- **Brāhmaṇas** — those who have renounced the world to seek liberation (the spiritual class — **not** a priestly caste by birth)

Bharata decreed that no one was born into a varṇa by birth alone. Varṇa was determined by **karma** — the quality of one's actions. A śūdra who lived virtuously could be reborn as a kṣatriya. A kṣatriya who ruled unjustly could fall to the level of a śūdra. And any being, from any varṇa, could renounce and become a brāhmaṇa (a true seeker).

> *"Bharata said: 'The varṇa is not a cage. It is a ladder. You climb it with your deeds. You descend it with your deeds. The only source of true nobility is the nobility of the soul.'"*
> — Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra II.19

### The Patronage of Ascetics and Temples

Bharata became a great patron of the Jain monastic order. He built monasteries, provided food and shelter for wandering monks, and funded the copying of scriptures. He himself observed the **aṇuvratas** (the lesser vows of the Jain layperson) — abstaining from violence, falsehood, theft, unchastity, and attachment — as much as his royal duties permitted.

He also established the tradition of the **Cakravartin's homage to the Tīrthaṅkara**. Each year, he would travel to Mount Aṣṭāpada (Mount Kailash), where his father Ṛṣabha had attained liberation, and offer worship there. This pilgrimage became a model for later Jain kings.

### The Protection of the Saṅgha

One of Bharata's most important acts was the establishment of the **rāja-śāsana** (royal decree) protecting the Jain saṅgha:

> *"Let no one in my empire harm a monk or a nun, whether naked or white-clad, whether wandering or settled. Let no one obstruct their meditation. Let no one prevent them from begging their food. The saṅgha is the treasure of the empire, and the emperor who protects the saṅgha protects his own soul."*
> — Ādīpurāṇa 38

## Bharata's Later Renunciation and Liberation

Despite his long and righteous reign, Bharata never forgot the lesson of Takkasilā. He knew that the crown would not save him from death, that the empire would not follow him into the next life, and that the cakraratna — which had once seemed the greatest gift — was merely a sign of worldly power, not of spiritual progress.

In his old age, after he had ruled for many thousands of years (in the Jain time-scale of the first age), Bharata **renounced the throne**. He installed his eldest son as the new king, performed the keśa-loca, and became a Jain monk.

> *"He gave away his crown, his robes, his treasures, his elephants, his horses, his throne. He walked out of Ayodhyā naked and homeless — the same way his father had walked out, the same way his brother had walked out. He owned nothing. And in that nothing, he found everything."*
> — Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra II.24

He attained **kevala-jñāna** (omniscience) as a monk and, after years of ascetic practice, achieved **mokṣa** (liberation), joining his father Ṛṣabha in the siddha-śilā, the eternal abode of liberated souls.

## Bharata as Cakravartin: The Three Traditions Compared

The figure of the **Cakravartin** (universal monarch) appears in all three major Indian traditions — Jain, Buddhist, and Hindu — but the concept is inflected differently in each. The table below shows how Bharata fits into each framework:

| Aspect | **Jain Tradition** | **Buddhist Tradition** | **Hindu Tradition** |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| **Who is the first Cakravartin?** | **Bharata**, son of Ṛṣabha — the first Cakravartin of the present descending age | **King Mahāsammata** (the "Great Elect") — first king of the present world-cycle; also **Māndhātṛ** and later **Aśoka** are exemplary cakravartins | **Bharata** (in the Purāṇas) — son of Duṣyanta and Śakuntalā, progenitor of the Bhārata dynasty; also **Yudhiṣṭhira** and **Kṛṣṇa** in some listings |
| **Source texts** | Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra, Ādīpurāṇa, Jambūdvīpaprajñapti | Mahāsudassana Sutta (DN 17), Cakkavatti Sīhanāda Sutta (DN 26), Aśokāvadāna | Mahābhārata (Ādi Parvan), Viṣṇu Purāṇa, Bhāgavata Purāṇa |
| **Means of becoming Cakravartin** | Accumulated merit (puṇya) from past lives; appearance of the cakraratna at coronation | Ethical rule and past merit; the appearance of the cakkaratana (wheel-jewel) | Divine right; conquest (digvijaya); lineage and sacrifice (aśvamedha) |
| **Role of the wheel-jewel** | The cakraratna leads the conquest, signalling dharma's support; appears only for those with sufficient past-life merit | The cakkavatti-ratana appears in the sky; the king must rule righteously or the wheel disappears | The discus (sudarśana-cakra) of Viṣṇu is sometimes associated with ideal kingship; not a standard ratna for human kings |
| **Extent of dominion** | The **six khaṇḍas** (continents) of madhyaloka; the entire inhabited world | The **four continents** of Buddhist cosmology (Jambudīpa, Pubbavideha, Aparagoyāna, Uttarakuru) | The whole of **Jambūdvīpa** (the Indian subcontinent and its legendary extensions); conquest of the "seven dvīpas" in Purāṇic geography |
| **Nature of conquest** | **Ahimsa-based**: most kings submit voluntarily before the cakraratna; war is avoided where possible; Bāhubali is a unique exception | **Dhamma-based**: the Cakravartin conquers by righteousness, not by force; the wheel follows dhamma | **Digvijaya**: conquest in all directions, often involving war; the aśvamedha (horse sacrifice) ritual declares sovereignty |
| **Relationship to Tīrthaṅkara / Buddha / Avatāra** | Subordinate to the Tīrthaṅkara: the Cakravartin rules the world, but the Tīrthaṅkara shows the way beyond the world. Bharata is son of Ṛṣabha | Subordinate to the Buddha: the Cakravartin rules righteously but cannot end suffering; only the Buddha can show the path to nirvāṇa | Sometimes conflated: Rāma and Kṛṣṇa are both kṣatriya kings and avatāras; the line between Cakravartin and divine ruler is blurred |
| **Does the Cakravartin renounce?** | **Yes**: Bharata renounces in old age, becomes a monk, and attains mokṣa — the ideal arc from world-ruler to world-renouncer | **Sometimes**: King Mahāsudassana renounces at death's approach; Aśoka becomes a "dharma-rāja" but does not become a monk in the Theravāda account | **Rarely**: Most Hindu cakravartins (e.g., Yudhiṣṭhira) rule until death; renunciation is not the expected culmination of a king's life |
| **Theological significance** | The Cakravartin demonstrates that worldly power is real (not illusory) but ultimately inferior to liberation. The story of Bharata shows even the greatest emperor must one day let go | The Cakravartin is a foreshadowing of the Buddha's spiritual sovereignty. The wheel of the dhamma is greater than the wheel of empire | The Cakravartin is the ideal king who maintains cosmic order (dharma). He is often a partial incarnation (aṃśa) of Viṣṇu |
| **Key virtue** | **Detachment** (vairāgya): The Cakravartin must learn to renounce what he has won | **Compassion** (karuṇā): The Cakravartin rules for the welfare of all beings | **Righteousness** (dharma): The Cakravartin upholds the social and cosmic order |
| **Fate after death** | Attains mokṣa (liberation) after renunciation and ascetic practice (Bharata, in Jain telling) | Reborn in a heavenly realm due to merit; may eventually attain nirvāṇa after further practice | Reborn according to karma; usually goes to heaven (svarga) but returns to saṃsāra; liberation is not automatic |

### Key Distinctions

**Jain Cakravartin**: The emphasis is on **karmic merit** and **eventual renunciation**. Bharata earns the cakraratna through past lives of virtue, and his story culminates not in empire but in leaving the empire behind. The Jain Cakravartin is a figure who illustrates the principle that even the most powerful worldly state is temporary and that liberation requires letting go of everything, including the crown.

**Buddhist Cakravartin**: The emphasis is on **ethical rule** and the **welfare of beings**. The Buddhist Cakravartin is a bodhisatta in the making — a being whose perfect righteousness prepares the ground for a Buddha to arise. The wheel-jewel is a symbol of dharma, and the Cakravartin's highest achievement is to create conditions in which beings can practise the path. Aśoka is the paradigmatic example.

**Hindu Cakravartin**: The emphasis is on **sacrificial sovereignty** and **cosmic maintenance**. The Hindu Cakravartin performs the aśvamedha, claims dominion over the earth, and upholds the varṇāśrama-dharma (caste-and-life-stage duties). The Cakravartin is less a renunciant than a stabiliser — one who ensures that the world continues to function according to divine order.

## Bharata in the List of Sixty-Three Illustrious Men

Bharata occupies a precise place in the Jain cosmic hierarchy. The **Triṣaṣṭiśalākāpuruṣacaritra** (The Lives of the Sixty-Three Illustrious Men) lists the great beings who appear in each half of the time-cycle:

### The Sixty-Three Illustrious Men (Śalākāpuruṣas)

| Group | Number | Role |
|-------|--------|------|
| **Tīrthaṅkaras** (Ford-makers) | 24 | Spiritual teachers who re-establish the Jain path |
| **Cakravartins** (Wheel-turners) | 12 | Universal emperors who rule all six continents |
| **Baladevas** (Rama-figures) | 9 | Brothers of the Vasudevas; righteous kings |
| **Vāsudevas** (Krishna-figures) | 9 | Warriors who defeat the enemies of dharma |
| **Prati-Vāsudevas** (Anti-Krishnas) | 9 | Adversaries of the Vasudevas; ultimately slain |

**Total: 63**

Bharata is the **first of the 12 Cakravartins**. The others follow in sequence across the descending and ascending halves of the time-cycle. No Cakravartin surpasses Bharata in the Jain telling — not because he conquered more, but because his story includes the **renunciation** that gives worldly conquest its ultimate meaning.

## Legacy and Significance

### The Naming of India

As noted, **Bhārata-varṣa** — the name for India in Sanskrit and in the Indian constitution (Bhārata) — derives from Bharata. The Jain tradition asserts this etymology explicitly. While Hindu sources (the Mahābhārata and Purāṇas) also derive the name from a Bharata (son of Duṣyanta and Śakuntalā), the Jain tradition's Bharata is older in cosmic terms — he belongs to the first age of the descending cycle, while the Mahābhārata's Bharata belongs to the third age (dvāpara).

### The Model of the Righteous King

Bharata became the archetype of the **rājarṣi** (royal sage) — a king who rules with wisdom and ultimately renounces. In later Jain literature, whenever a king is praised for justice, compassion, and detachment, it is Bharata who is invoked as the standard:

> *"Like Bharata, he ruled. Like Bharata, he renounced."*

### The Cakravartin as a Karmic Narrative

Bharata's story illustrates a core Jain teaching about karma and status. The Cakravartin is not born — he is **made** by his past lives. Every moment of generosity, every restraint from violence, every hour of meditation in countless previous existences contributed to the accumulation of the merit that produced the cakraratna. The wheel is not a gift from the gods — it is the product of the soul's own effort over infinite time.

This means that the Cakravartin is **not a static category**. Anyone, in principle, could become a Cakravartin by accumulating sufficient merit. And anyone who has become a Cakravartin — like Bharata — is not excused from the ultimate task: liberation. The crown is a stage, not a destination.

### The Tension Between Power and Spirituality

The story of Bharata and Bāhubali is the Jain tradition's most powerful exploration of the **tension between worldly power and spiritual liberation**. The two brothers represent two paths:

| Bharata | Bāhubali |
|---------|----------|
| Conquers the world | Conquers himself |
| Rules through law | Transcends through stillness |
| Wins the empire | Wins liberation |
| Renounces only after a long life | Renounces immediately |
| The path of the householder-king | The path of the monk-ascetic |

Both paths lead eventually to liberation — but Bāhubali's is the faster, harder, more radical path. Bharata's is the slower, more patient, more responsible path. Jainism honours both — but it never lets the reader forget which one is superior. The world may need a Cakravartin, but the soul needs a monk.
