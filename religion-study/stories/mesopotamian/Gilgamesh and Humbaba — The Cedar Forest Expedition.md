# Gilgamesh and Humbaba — The Cedar Forest Expedition

### Sumerian Origins
The Cedar Forest episode originates in five distinct Sumerian poems about [[Gilgamesh]] and Huwawa, the oldest dating to the Ur III period (c. 2100 BCE). In the Sumerian versions, Huwawa is not a monster but a divine being with a terrifying radiance (*melam*). The Standard Babylonian version (Tablets III–V) condenses and dramatizes the material, adding the dream sequences and the moral tension around the killing.

## Summary
Fresh from their friendship, [[Gilgamesh]] proposes an expedition to the Cedar Forest to slay Humbaba (Huwawa), the monstrous ogre appointed by [[Enlil]] to guard the sacred cedars. Despite warnings from [[Enkidu]] (who knows Humbaba's terror) and the Elders of Uruk, [[Gilgamesh]] is determined. They journey deep into the forest, confront the monster, and are aided by the sun-god Shamash. Humbaba begs for mercy, but [[Enkidu]] urges the kill. They behead him and fell the sacred cedars — an act that seals [[Enkidu]]'s fate.

### The Meaning of the Cedar Forest
The Cedar Forest represents the sacred boundary between the civilized world and the untamed divine realm. Humbaba is not merely a monster but a divinely appointed guardian — [[Enlil]] set him there to protect the cedars. The expedition thus combines heroic ambition, sacrilege, and ecological transgression. The felling of the trees mirrors the real deforestation of Lebanon's cedars by Mesopotamian kings.

## The Story

### Act 1: The Proposal (Tablet III)
"Why do you want to do this thing?" [[Enkidu]] asks. [[Gilgamesh]] replies with a hero's logic: "Because I can, and because my name will outlive me." The Elders of Uruk bless the expedition but weep, knowing the danger.
[[Gilgamesh]] tells [[Enkidu]]: "I will go to the Cedar Forest, where Humbaba lives. I will set up my name where the name of famous men is written." [[Enkidu]] warns him — he has seen Humbaba's roar like a flood, his breath like fire, his voice like the storm. The Elders of Uruk also urge caution. [[Gilgamesh]]'s reply: "If I fail, they will say 'Gilgamesh has fallen against fierce Humbaba.' Let me go."

### Act 2: The Journey (Tablets III–IV)
[[Gilgamesh]] and [[Enkidu]] travel 20 leagues per day, digging wells at each stage and making offerings to Shamash. The journey takes them through the wilderness. [[Gilgamesh]] has a series of ominous dreams — a mountain falls on him, a bull gores him, a thunderbird breathes fire — each interpreted by [[Enkidu]] as a favorable omen. Shamash himself encourages them: "Hasten to the forest."

### Act 3: The Confrontation (Tablet V)
They enter the Cedar Forest. The trees are immense, draped in shadow. Humbaba sees them and attacks. [[Gilgamesh]] is seized with terror — the first time we see him afraid. [[Enkidu]] steadies him. [[Gilgamesh]] calls on Shamash, who sends eight winds: North Wind, South Wind, East Wind, West Wind, Dust-Devil, Ice-Wind, Tempest, and Scorching Wind. The winds trap Humbaba, blinding and immobilizing him.

### Act 4: The Killing (Tablet V)
Humbaba begs for mercy: "I will be your servant! I will cut the cedars for you!" [[Gilgamesh]] hesitates. [[Enkidu]] urges the kill: "When you finish a contest, do not let your opponent go." They cut off Humbaba's head and desecrate the forest. They strip the sacred cedars, felling the great trees and hauling them to Uruk.

### The Moral Ambiguity
The poem does not present the killing of Humbaba as an unambiguous good. [[Gilgamesh]] hesitates. Humbaba pleads for his life and offers servitude. [[Enkidu]] — who had initially discouraged the expedition — now presses for murder. The gods are divided: Shamash helps, [[Enlil]] is outraged. The heroes return in triumph, but the expedition's success sows the seeds of [[Enkidu]]'s death and the epic's tragic second half.

## Key Themes
- The heroic drive for fame and legacy
- The tension between wisdom (Enkidu's warnings) and ambition (Gilgamesh's desire)
- The destruction of sacred nature as hubris
- The role of the gods (Shamash helps; Enlil is angered)
- The first sin that leads to Enkidu's death
