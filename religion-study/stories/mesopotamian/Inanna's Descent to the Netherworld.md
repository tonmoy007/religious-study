# Inanna's Descent to the Netherworld

## Summary
[[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Inanna]], goddess of love and war, descends to the underworld ruled by her sister [[Ereshkigal]] — the Land of No Return. She passes through seven gates, stripped at each of her garments and powers until she stands naked before [[Ereshkigal]] and the seven Anunnaki judges. They pass judgment; [[Ereshkigal]] kills [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Inanna]] and hangs her corpse on a hook. After three days, [[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|Enki]] creates two genderless beings who descend and revive her with the food of life. But the Anunnaki rule that someone must take her place. Demons escort her back; she must provide a substitute — finally choosing her lover Dumuzi, who spends half the year in the underworld while his sister Geshtinanna takes the other half.

### Textual History
The Sumerian version (ETCSL t.1.4.1, c. 1800 BCE) is the oldest surviving descent myth. An Akkadian adaptation (Ishtar's Descent, c. 1200 BCE) exists with significant variants — in the Akkadian version, Ishtar threatens to break down the gates of the underworld, and Ea creates Asushunamir (a single genderless figure) rather than two. The Sumerian version is longer and richer in ritual detail, including the enigmatic figure of the *kurgarra* and *galatura*.

## The Story

### Act 1: The Decision to Descend (lines 1–30)
[[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Inanna]] "set her mind toward the Great Below." She abandons her temples in heaven and earth. Before leaving, she instructs her messenger [[Ninshubur]]: if she does not return after three days, raise a lament, beat the drum, go to the gods — first to [[Enlil]], then to Nanna, then to [[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|Enki]].

### Act 2: The Seven Gates (lines 115–170)
Inanna approaches the underworld palace. The gatekeeper Neti admits her — one gate at a time. At each gate, a garment or piece of regalia is removed: the *shugurra* crown (gate 1), the lapis bead necklace (2), the double-string beads (3), the breastplate (4), the gold ring (5), the measuring rod (6), the royal robe (7). Naked and bowed low, she enters the throne room of [[Ereshkigal]].

### Act 3: Judgment and Death (lines 164–195)
The seven Anunnaki judges sit in judgment. They fix [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Inanna]] with the "eye of death." [[Ereshkigal]] strikes her — she becomes a corpse, hung on a hook.

### Act 4: Ninshubur's Plea and Enki's Intervention (lines 200–280)
Three days pass. Ninshubur laments at [[Enlil]]'s temple — refused. At Nanna's temple — refused. At [[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|Enki]]'s temple, [[Enki]] hears and acts. He scrapes dirt from under his fingernails and creates two beings: the *kurgarra* and *galatura* — genderless, who know no hunger or thirst. He gives them the "food of life" and "water of life."

### Act 5: Resurrection (lines 280–320)
The two beings descend. [[Ereshkigal]], in the agony of childbirth, offers them anything. They ask for [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Inanna]]'s corpse. They sprinkle her with the food and water of life — she revives.

### Act 6: The Return and the Substitution (lines 320–410)
The Anunnaki seize [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Inanna]] as she ascends: "Who has ever ascended from the underworld unscathed? If Inanna is to return, she must provide a substitute." Demons follow her. She finds Dumuzi lounging on her throne, not mourning. Enraged, she "fastens the eye of death" on him. Dumuzi flees but is caught. Geshtinanna volunteers to take half the year for him. The myth establishes the seasonal cycle.

### The Ritual Dimension
The myth likely accompanied the rite of the *hieros gamos* (sacred marriage) and the Dumuzi lamentation rituals. The *kurgarra* and *galatura* were temple functionaries who performed lamentations. The stripping of Inanna's garments corresponds to the removal of temple statues' regalia during rites of mourning. The story thus operates simultaneously as myth, ritual text, and explanation for the seasonal agricultural cycle.

## Key Themes
- Descent and stripping of identity (powerlessness)
- Death and resurrection through divine intervention
- The cycle of seasons (dying god motif)
- Divine justice: substitute required for release
- [[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|Enki]] as wisdom figure who restores order
