# Atrahasis — The Creation of Humans and the Flood

### Textual Witnesses
The Atrahasis Epic survives in multiple copies: Old Babylonian tablets from Sippar (c. 1700 BCE) and later Neo-Assyrian copies from Nineveh (c. 700 BCE). The epic was originally three tablets of approximately 1,245 lines total. The flood section (Tablet III) was adapted directly into the [[Epic of Gilgamesh]] Tablet XI, with the hero renamed Utnapishtim. Fragments found at Ugarit and Emar show it was part of the scribal curriculum across the Ancient Near East.

## Summary
The Atrahasis Epic ("When the gods instead of man did the work") is the oldest surviving account of human creation and the Great Flood (c. 1700 BCE). The great [[Anunnaki]] gods force the lesser Igigi gods to labour. After 3,600 years, the Igigi revolt. The gods create humanity as a substitute — formed from clay mixed with the flesh and blood of the slain god We-ilu. Humans bear the workload but multiply and become noisy, disturbing [[Enlil]]'s rest. [[Enlil]] sends plague, famine, and finally the Flood to destroy them. Each time, [[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|Enki]] thwarts the plan — by teaching Atrahasis to appease the gods, and by instructing him to build an ark. After the Flood, the gods institute birth control and mortality as permanent population controls.

## The Story

### Act 1: The Igigi Revolt (Tablet I, lines 1–60)
"When the gods instead of man did the work, bore the loads" — the epic's opening line sets up its central problem. The Igigi (lesser gods) dig canals, build cities, carry baskets for 3,600 years. The Anunnaki (great gods) supervise.
When the [[Anunnaki]] gods made the Igigi bear the load sevenfold, the Igigi dug the Tigris and Euphrates, built the divine dwellings — and after 3,600 years of labour, they burned their tools and surrounded the house of [[Enlil]].

### Act 2: Creation of Humans (Tablet I, lines 200–320)
[[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|Enki]] proposes: create a substitute. The god We-ilu is slain. The mother goddess Nintu (Mami, Belet-ili) mixes clay with his flesh and blood. She pinches off fourteen pieces: seven wombs bear seven males, seven females. Humans inherit the god's intelligence — and mortality.

### Act 3: Overpopulation and Plagues (Tablet I–II)
Humans multiply. Their noise robs [[Enlil]] of sleep. He sends plague — but [[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|Enki]] tells Atrahasis to give offerings to Namtar, and the plague lifts. Then [[Enlil]] sends famine: [[Enki]] instructs Atrahasis to redirect offerings to the rain-god Adad. Then drought: Atrahasis offers bread to [[Enlil]]'s gatekeepers. Nothing works. [[Enlil]] decrees the Flood.

### Act 4: The Flood (Tablet III)
[[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|Enki]] swears an oath not to warn humanity — but he speaks to Atrahasis through a reed wall: "Tear down your house, build a boat." Atrahasis builds a vessel with a roof, upper and lower decks, sealed with pitch. He boards his family and animals. The Flood rages for seven days and nights. The noise of the storm silences human noise forever — but also starves the gods, who "thirst like flies" for offerings.

### Act 5: Aftermath (Tablet III)
Atrahasis offers sacrifices. The gods smell the sweet savour and gather "like flies." [[Enlil]] is furious to discover survivors. [[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|Enki]] defends his action and proposes a permanent solution: create imperfect humans with limits — infant mortality, sterile women, celibate priestesses — to keep population in check.

### Dramatic Irony
The Atrahasis Epic frames human existence as a solution to a divine labour dispute — and human destruction as a response to noise. The gods are never unified: [[Enlil]] acts with harsh authority, [[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|Enki]] with cunning compassion. The flood does not solve the underlying problem; the gods must learn to manage their creation with limits rather than destruction. The poem's structure — revolt, creation, overpopulation, destruction, reset — is the earliest known example of a cycle-of-civilization narrative.

## Key Themes
- Human creation as a substitute for divine labour
- Overpopulation as cosmic disorder
- The "noise" of civilization as a threat to divine order
- [[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|Enki]] as trickster-saviour vs [[Enlil]] as authoritarian judge
- The fragility of the human-god relationship
