# Gilgamesh and Enkidu — The Friendship That Changed a King

### The Arc of the Epic
This story — the creation and friendship of [[Enkidu]] — is the emotional foundation of the entire Epic of [[Gilgamesh]]. Everything that follows (Humbaba, the Bull of Heaven, [[Enkidu]]'s death, the quest for immortality) derives meaning from the bond formed here. Without this friendship, the epic would be a series of adventures; with it, the epic becomes a meditation on love, loss, and what it means to be human.

## Summary
The people of Uruk pray to the gods for relief from [[Gilgamesh]]'s tyrannical rule — his arrogance, his claim to the first night with every bride, his relentless demands. The goddess Aruru creates [[Enkidu]], a feral wild man who lives among the animals. A temple harlot seduces and civilizes him, bringing him to Uruk. [[Enkidu]] challenges [[Gilgamesh]]'s right to the bridal chamber. They fight in the street — a titanic struggle — and [[Gilgamesh]] prevails. Instead of enmity, a profound friendship is born. This relationship transforms [[Gilgamesh]] from a tyrant into a leader, and [[Enkidu]] from a beast into a hero.

### Narrative Context
Tablets I–II of the Standard Babylonian Version (SBV) combine two Sumerian narrative strands: the creation of [[Enkidu]] as Gilgamesh's rival and the civilizing of the wild man through sexual initiation. The Old Babylonian version (c. 1700 BCE) opens directly with the confrontation. The story establishes the core themes of the entire epic: the relationship between nature and culture, the transformative power of friendship, and the search for meaning through a worthy companion.

## The Story

### Scene 1: Gilgamesh the Tyrant (Tablet I, lines 1–100)
"Surpassing all kings" — the prologue introduces [[Gilgamesh]] as the builder of Uruk's great walls, the one "who knew the world's depths." But the same strength that makes him great makes him unbearable. He exercises *right of the first night* (*ius primae noctis*) over every bride, works men beyond exhaustion, and leaves no son to his father.
[[Gilgamesh]], two-thirds divine, king of Uruk, is unmatched in strength — but he oppresses his people. He works the young men without rest and claims the right to sleep with every bride on her wedding night. The people cry out to the gods for relief.

### Scene 2: Aruru Creates Enkidu (Tablet I, lines 100–180)
[[Enki (Ea) — God of Wisdom|The goddess]] Aruru washes her hands, pinches off clay, and creates [[Enkidu]] — a warrior with the strength of [[Anu (An) — Sky God|Anu]], covered in shaggy hair, living with the wild animals. He runs with gazelles, eats grass, drinks from the waterholes.

### Scene 3: The Civilizing of Enkidu (Tablet I, lines 180–260)
A hunter sees [[Enkidu]] destroying his traps and reports to [[Gilgamesh]]. [[Gilgamesh]] sends the temple harlot Shamhat to the watering hole. She copulates with [[Enkidu]] for six days and seven nights. His bond with the animals breaks; they flee. She clothes him, teaches him to eat bread and drink beer: "He became aware, his understanding grew broad."

### Scene 4: The Confrontation in Uruk (Tablet II, lines 140–230)
Shamhat tells [[Enkidu]] of [[Gilgamesh]] — his strength, his arrogance. [[Enkidu]] declares he will challenge him. She brings him to Uruk. As [[Gilgamesh]] approaches the bridal chamber, [[Enkidu]] blocks the door. They grapple in the street — the walls shake, the doorposts splinter.

### Scene 5: The Embrace (Tablet II, lines 230–260)
[[Gilgamesh]] throws [[Enkidu]] to the ground — the rage drains from both. They kiss and clasp hands. [[Gilgamesh]] brings [[Enkidu]] to his mother Ninsun, who adopts him as a brother. [[Enkidu]] weeps that he has no purpose; [[Gilgamesh]] proposes the Cedar Forest expedition. The friendship gives both meaning.

### The Civilizing Process
The sequence — wild man → sexual encounter → shame + flight of animals → clothing → bread and beer → human speech → city → confrontation → friendship — follows a deliberate arc from nature to culture. Each step strips away the wild and adds a layer of civilization. [[Enkidu]]'s transformation mirrors the Mesopotamian myth of human origins: like Adam, he is formed from clay and passes from innocence to knowledge.

## Key Themes
- The civilizing power of love and friendship
- The foil: civilized strength meets primal strength
- Transformation through the equal other
- Nature vs culture
- Companionship as the solution to tyranny
