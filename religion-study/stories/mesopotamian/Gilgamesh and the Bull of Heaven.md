# Gilgamesh and the Bull of Heaven

### The Rhetorical Structure
Ishtar's proposition and Gilgamesh's rejection form a carefully constructed rhetorical episode: (1) Ishtar's promise of wealth and glory, (2) Gilgamesh's refusal with a catalogue of her destroyed lovers, (3) Ishtar's appeal to Anu, (4) the Bull's devastation, (5) the joint slaying, (6) Enkidu's insult, (7) the divine council's judgment. Each step escalates the stakes, moving from desire to rejection to rage to death.

## Summary
After the Humbaba victory, [[Gilgamesh]] returns to Uruk in glory. [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Ishtar]], the goddess of love and war, sees him and propositions him with promises of wealth and divine marriage. [[Gilgamesh]] rejects her savagely, reciting a catalogue of her disastrous relationships with mortal lovers. Enraged, [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Ishtar]] demands the Bull of Heaven from her father [[Anu (An) — Sky God|Anu]]. The Bull wreaks havoc upon Uruk, killing hundreds with its fiery breath. [[Enkidu]] wrestles the Bull by the horns while [[Gilgamesh]] slays it. They offer its heart to Shamash, and [[Enkidu]] hurls the Bull's thigh at [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Ishtar]] while she shrieks from the city walls. The gods decree [[Enkidu]] must die for this sacrilege.

### The Turning Point
Tablet VI marks the hinge of the epic. Before it, the story is a heroic adventure — friends conquering monsters, winning fame. After it, the story becomes a tragedy of grief and the quest for meaning in the face of death. [[Enkidu]]'s insult to [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Ishtar]] — hurling the Bull's thigh in her face — is the act of hubris that seals his fate. The Bull of Heaven episode parallels the Humbaba expedition: another divinely appointed guardian, another joint killing, another divine offence compounding.

## The Story

### Act 1: The Proposition (Tablet VI, lines 1–80)
[[Gilgamesh]] washes his hair, polishes his weapons, dons his finest robes, and places his crown. [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Ishtar]] sees him and is overcome with desire: "Come, Gilgamesh, be my bridegroom! Give me your fruit!" She offers him a chariot of lapis lazuli, rulers bowing to him, abundance beyond measure.

### Act 2: The Rejection (Tablet VI, lines 80–170)
[[Gilgamesh]] refuses, then recites a litany of [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Ishtar]]'s lovers: Tammuz (wept for yearly), the bird (whose wing she broke), the lion (trapped in pits), the stallion (driven to exhaustion), the shepherd (turned into a wolf), Ishullanu the gardener (struck with leprosy). "Which lover did you love forever? Which shepherd pleased you for all time?"

### Act 3: The Bull of Heaven (Tablet VI, lines 170–260)
Enraged, [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Ishtar]] storms to heaven. She threatens [[Anu (An) — Sky God|Anu]]: if he does not give her the Bull of Heaven, she will "raise up the dead to devour the living." [[Anu (An) — Sky God|Anu]] relents. The Bull descends. With its first snort, it kills 200 men; with its second, 200 more; with its third, it plunges the Euphrates.

### Act 4: The Slaying (Tablet VI, lines 260–300)
[[Enkidu]] leaps behind the Bull, grabs its tail and horns. [[Gilgamesh]] drives his sword between the Bull's shoulders. They cut out its heart and offer it to Shamash. [[Enkidu]] tears off the Bull's thigh and hurls it at [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Ishtar]]: "If I could get you, I would do the same to you!" [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Ishtar]] shrieks curses from the wall.

### Act 5: The Decree (Tablet VI–VII)
[[Gilgamesh]] dedicates the Bull's horns to his father [[Lugalbanda]]. The people of Uruk celebrate. But the gods convene: [[Anu (An) — Sky God|Anu]], [[Enlil]], and Shamash. [[Enlil]] declares: "One of them must die." [[Enkidu]] falls ill and dies twelve days later — the direct consequence of the Bull's slaying and Humbaba's killing.

### The Rejection Speech
[[Gilgamesh]]'s litany of Ishtar's lovers is one of the most rhetorically powerful passages in Akkadian literature. He lists five former lovers — Tammuz, the bird, the lion, the stallion, the shepherd — each of whom Ishtar loved, then destroyed. The speech is not merely a refusal but a character indictment: Ishtar's love is fatal. This echoes a broader Ancient Near Eastern motif of the dangerous goddess whose desire brings ruin.

## Key Themes
- Hubris and divine retribution
- The mortal danger of rejecting a deity's advances
- The cost of fame — one deed too many
- The friendship bond tested by fate
- The cycle: heroic victory → divine punishment → death
