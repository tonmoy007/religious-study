# Epic of Gilgamesh — Overview

## Summary
The Epic of [[Gilgamesh]] follows the two-thirds divine king of Uruk from tyranny to wisdom. The gods create [[Enkidu]], a wild man, to oppose him. After wrestling, they become devoted friends and embark on adventures: killing Humbaba the Cedar Forest guardian and slaying the Bull of Heaven. The gods decree [[Enkidu]] must die. Devastated, [[Gilgamesh]] journeys to the edge of the world seeking Utnapishtim, the flood survivor granted immortality. He hears the Flood story, fails a wakefulness test, and is given the Plant of Youth — only to have it stolen by a serpent. He returns to Uruk chastened and wise. The epic is the finest surviving work of ancient Mesopotamian literature.

## The Story

### Tablet I–II: The Creation of Enkidu
[[Gilgamesh]] is the two-thirds divine king of Uruk — mighty but tyrannical, claiming the right to every bride on her wedding night. The gods answer his people's prayers by creating [[Enkidu]], a wild man who lives with animals, as his equal and eventual companion.
The people of Uruk pray for relief from [[Gilgamesh]]'s tyranny. The goddess Aruru creates [[Enkidu]], a wild man living among animals. A temple harlot civilizes him, and he challenges [[Gilgamesh]]'s right to the first night with brides. After a titanic struggle, a profound friendship is born.

### Tablet III–V: The Cedar Forest Expedition
[[Gilgamesh]] proposes to slay Humbaba, the ogre appointed by [[Enlil]] to guard the sacred cedars. Despite warnings, they journey to the Cedar Forest. Shamash sends eight winds to trap Humbaba. Humbaba begs for mercy; [[Enkidu]] urges the kill. They behead him and fell the sacred cedars.

### Tablet VI: The Bull of Heaven
[[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Ishtar]] propositions [[Gilgamesh]]; he rejects her brutally. Enraged, she demands the Bull of Heaven from [[Anu (An) — Sky God|Anu]]. The Bull kills hundreds, but [[Enkidu]] wrestles it and [[Gilgamesh]] slays it. They offer its heart to Shamash. [[Enkidu]] hurls the Bull's thigh at [[Inanna (Ishtar) — Goddess of Love and War|Ishtar]].

### Tablet VII–VIII: The Death of Enkidu
The gods decree [[Enkidu]] must die. He falls ill, curses the harlot, then relents and blesses her. He dies after twelve days of agony. [[Gilgamesh]] mourns him bitterly, refusing to bury him until a maggot falls from his nose.

### Tablet IX–X: The Quest for Immortality
Haunted by mortality, [[Gilgamesh]] travels through the Mountains of Mashu guarded by scorpion-beings, passes through twelve leagues of darkness, meets the wine-maker Siduri, and crosses the Waters of Death with the boatman Ur-Shanabi to reach Utnapishtim.

### Tablet XI: The Flood Story
Utnapishtim tells [[Gilgamesh]] the story of the Great Flood — a close adaptation of the [[Atrahasis Epic]]. He tests [[Gilgamesh]] by challenging him to stay awake six days; he fails. Utnapishtim's wife persuades him to reveal the Plant of Youth. [[Gilgamesh]] retrieves it, but a serpent steals it while he bathes.

### Tablet XII: The Ghost of Enkidu
[[Gilgamesh]] raises [[Enkidu]]'s ghost from the underworld — a translation of the Sumerian "Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Netherworld." [[Enkidu]] describes the grim fate of the dead.

## Key Themes
- The civilizing power of friendship
- The acceptance of mortality
- The hero's journey from tyranny to wisdom
- The limits of human achievement
- The contrast between wildness and civilization
- What is gained and lost in the quest for eternal life
