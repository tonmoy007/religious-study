# Solomon and the Queen of Sheba

> A queen's pilgrimage to test the wisest king on earth — the Bible's quiet version of an encounter the Qur'an would dramatically expand.

---

## The Story (1 Kings 10 / 2 Chronicles 9)

King **Solomon** — son of David — is the wealthiest and wisest king in the world. His fame spreads to distant lands.

The **Queen of Sheba** (likely from Yemen) hears of Solomon's fame "concerning the name of the Lord" (1 Kgs 10:1). She travels to Jerusalem with a great caravan — camels bearing spices, gold, and precious stones — to test him with **hard questions** (riddles).

Solomon answers every question. She sees his palace, his table, the seating of his officials, the food, the burnt offerings — and she is **overwhelmed**:

> [!quote] 1 Kings 10:6–9
> "The report I heard in my own country is true — but I did not believe it until I came and saw it with my eyes. Not even half was told to me. Happy are your men, happy are these your servants who continually stand before you and hear your wisdom! Blessed be the Lord your God, who delighted in you!"

She gives Solomon 120 talents of gold, vast quantities of spices, and precious stones. Solomon gives her "all that she desired" in return. She returns to her own land.

## Comparison with the Qur'anic Account

| Element | Bible (1 Kings 10) | Qur'an (27:15–44) |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Why she comes** | She heard of Solomon's fame | Solomon's **hoopoe bird** reports her sun-worship |
| **The test** | Riddles / hard questions | A **letter** demanding submission to God |
| **Gifts** | She brings them — Solomon accepts | She sends gifts — **Solomon rejects them** |
| **The throne** | Not mentioned | Solomon's **jinn transport her throne** in an instant |
| **The palace** | General description | A **glass floor over water** — she lifts her skirt |
| **Her response** | She praises Solomon's God, returns | She **converts**: "I submit to Allah" |
| **Her name** | Unnamed | **Bilqis** (in tradition) |

> [!tip] The Qur'an transforms a diplomatic visit into a dramatic conversion narrative. See [[Solomon and Sheba — The Queen's Visit Compared]] for the full analysis.
