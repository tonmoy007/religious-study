# Solomon & Sheba — The Queen's Visit

> The same historical queen visiting the same king — but what happens in the encounter reveals the deepest concerns of each scripture. The Bible emphasizes Solomonic wealth, wisdom, and international prestige. The Qur'an focuses on monotheism, the queen's political acumen, and her personal transformation.

---

## The Two Accounts: Side by Side

| Element | **1 Kings 10 / 2 Chronicles 9** (Bible) | **Qur'an 27:15–44** |
|---------|----------------------------------------|---------------------|
| **Why she comes** | She heard of Solomon's fame "concerning the name of the Lord" (1 Kgs 10:1) | The hoopoe reports: "I found a woman ruling them, given everything — she has a mighty throne. She and her people prostrate to the sun" (27:23–24) |
| **The test** | She brings "hard questions" (*ḥīdōt*) — riddles, to test his wisdom | Solomon sends a letter: "Be not haughty with me — come to me in submission" (27:31) |
| **Gifts** | She gives Solomon 120 talents of gold, spices, precious stones | She sends **gifts**, testing whether Solomon is a worldly king (who can be bribed) or a prophet (who cannot) |
| **Solomon's response to gifts** | He accepts them; the gifts enrich his treasury | He **rejects** the gifts: "What Allah has given me is better than what He has given you" (27:36) |
| **The throne** | Not mentioned | Solomon's jinn bring her throne to him in the blink of an eye — a miracle proving his prophethood |
| **The palace** | He shows her his palace (described in general terms) | He builds a palace whose floor is made of **glass over water** — she lifts her skirts, thinking it's a pool |
| **Her declaration** | "The report I heard in my own country is true — blessed be the Lord your God" (1 Kgs 10:9) | "My Lord, I have wronged myself — I submit with Solomon to Allah, Lord of the worlds" (27:44) |
| **Her submission** | She admires Solomon's God — but stays his equal, a fellow monarch | She **converts** — submits to Allah, becoming a believer under (not beside) Solomon |
| **Her name** | Unnamed (called "Queen of Sheba") | **Bilqis** (named in Islamic tradition, not in the Qur'an itself) |

---

## What is Shared

1. A powerful queen from the south (Sheba/Yemen) visits Solomon
2. She comes with gifts and tests him
3. She is impressed by his wisdom, wealth, and kingdom
4. She acknowledges the God of Israel / Allah as supreme
5. Solomon gives her "all that she desired" — they part with mutual respect

---

## What is Changed

### 1. The Hoopoe: A Bird as Messenger

The Qur'an's **hoopoe** (*hudhud*) is a unique and charming detail absent from the Bible. This bird:
- Spies on the queen's kingdom
- Reports her sun-worship to Solomon
- Becomes the catalyst for the entire story

**Why the hoopoe matters:** The Qur'an consistently presents nature (birds, ants, mountains) as intelligent servants of God. The hoopoe is not a messenger chosen at random — he is part of **Solomon's army of birds** (27:17). The natural world participates in prophetic mission.

### 2. From Riddles to Revelation

The Bible's "hard questions" (riddles, wisdom challenges) are replaced in the Qur'an with:
- A **letter** from Solomon: clear, direct, demanding submission
- A **theological test**: is Solomon a worldly king or a prophet?
- A **miraculous sign**: the transported throne

This shift from **wisdom competition** to **faith test** reflects the Qur'an's central concern: not wisdom per se, but *submission to God*.

### 3. The Queen's Transformation

The biblical queen remains an independent ruler. She praises Solomon's God but does not convert — she returns to Sheba as she came.

The Qur'anic queen **submits**: "I submit with Solomon to Allah." This is the climax of the story — a powerful monarch bows to the one God. Her conversion is:
- Complete: from sun-worshipper to monotheist
- Personal: "I have wronged myself" — a statement of individual responsibility
- Political: her people presumably follow

### 4. Solomon's Character

| Aspect | Biblical Solomon | Qur'anic Sulayman |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Wealth** | Accumulates gold, horses, wives — ultimately criticized (1 Kgs 11) | Commands jinn, wind, and animals — all as gifts from God, not personal acquisition |
| **Foreign wives** | 700 wives and 300 concubines *lead him to idolatry* (1 Kgs 11:4) | Not mentioned — as a prophet, he cannot fall into shirk |
| **Role** | A human king with divine wisdom | A **prophet** (*nabī*) who is also a king |

This is consistent with the doctrinal pattern: where the Bible shows a human king's moral complexity, the Qur'an presents a sinless prophet.

---

## Type of Connection

**Genealogical with transformation.** Both accounts spring from the same Near Eastern tradition (the queen of Sheba's visit to Solomon is almost certainly a historical memory of a trade mission between Yemen and Israel). The Qur'an expands the narrative with:
- Miraculous elements (the throne transport, the glass palace)
- New characters (the hoopoe, the jinn)
- A theological reorientation (the queen's conversion as the climax)

## Stylistic Comparison

| Element | Bible (1 Kings) | Qur'an (Surah 27) |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Length** | 13 verses | 30 verses |
| **Genre** | Royal chronicle / annals | Prophetic narrative / story |
| **Dialogue** | Minimal — direct exchanges only | Extensive — the queen's council debates; Solomon rejects gifts; the queen's submission speech |
| **Miracles** | None (wisdom is the marvel) | The throne transport; the glass palace; Solomon's command over jinn and birds |
| **Narrator** | Anonymous historian | Divine voice |
