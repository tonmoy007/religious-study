# First Murder — Cain and Abel

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]]

The first human death in the Abrahamic story is a **murder between brothers**.
Both the Bible and the Qur'an tell it — and the Qur'an adds a vivid detail the
Bible never mentions. A **genealogical** connection with an instructive
divergence.

## Genesis: the bare, stark account
From [[scriptures/bible-kjv/01 Genesis|Genesis 4]]:

> [!quote] Genesis 4:8
> "And Cain talked with Abel his brother: and it came to pass, when they were in the field, that **Cain rose up against Abel his brother, and slew him**."

Genesis is terse: the deed, then God's question "Where is Abel thy brother?"
and the curse. No names are given to the brothers in the Qur'an, but the event
is unmistakably the same.

## The Qur'an: the raven that teaches burial
From [[005 Al-Maaida|Surah Al-Maaida]] — the two sons of Adam:

> [!quote] Al-Maaida 5:30–31
> **5:30** — "But (the other's) mind imposed on him **the killing of his brother**, so he slew him and became one of the losers."
> **5:31** — "Then Allah sent a **raven scratching up the ground, to show him how to hide his brother's naked corpse**. He said: Woe unto me! Am I not able to be as this raven and so hide my brother's naked corpse? And he became repentant."

## What the Qur'an adds
| | Genesis | Qur'an (Al-Maaida) |
|---|---|---|
| The killing | "rose up… and slew him" | "he slew him… one of the losers" |
| Names | Cain, Abel | unnamed "two sons of Adam" |
| Aftermath | curse, exile to Nod | **the raven teaches burial**; the killer **repents** |
| Framing | origin of human violence | a moral lesson on regret and the sanctity of life |

The **raven** is the signature Qur'anic addition — a teaching moment about
burial and remorse absent from Genesis. The Qur'an then draws an explicit
universal lesson (5:32): that to kill one soul unjustly is as if to kill all
mankind. Same event; the Qur'an foregrounds **repentance and the value of a
single life**.

## What kind of connection?
**Genealogical.** The Qur'an consciously retells a Genesis narrative (part of
the broader pattern in [[Shared Abrahamic Figures]]), preserving the core event
while reshaping its meaning and adding the raven motif — paralleling the
[[Flood Narratives Compared|flood retelling]], where the Qur'an likewise keeps
the skeleton and adds a moral filter.
