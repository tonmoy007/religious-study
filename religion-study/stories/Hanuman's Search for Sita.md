# Hanuman's Search for Sita

> The Sundara Kanda — the most beautiful book of the Ramayana — where Hanuman leaps across the ocean to find Sita, meets her in Ashoka Vatika, and sets Lanka ablaze.

---

## The Story (Ramayana — Kishkindha & Sundara Kanda)

### The Pact with Sugriva

Rama meets **Sugriva** and **Hanuman** at Rishyamuka Mountain. Sugriva was exiled by his brother **Vali** (Valin), the monkey-king of Kishkindha, who also took his wife. Rama agrees: if Sugriva helps find Sita, Rama will kill Vali.

Rama kills Vali — Sugriva becomes king. But Sugriva forgets his promise — he spends months in revelry. Lakshmana comes raging. Sugriva apologizes and sends the monkey-army in all four directions.

### The Search

Hanuman is sent **south** — with Angada (Vali's son) and others. They reach the southern ocean. Sita's trail goes cold. A vulture **Sampati** (Jatayu's brother) tells them she is in Lanka.

The army despairs — the ocean is 100 leagues wide. Who can cross it?

### Hanuman's Leap

Hanuman remembers his divine powers. He grows to a gigantic size, stands on **Mount Mahendra**, and **leaps across the ocean** — the monkeys watch in awe.

On the way, a mountain emerges from the water — Hanuman crushes it. The serpent **Surasa** (mother of the nagas) tests him — he enters her mouth and emerges. The demoness **Simhika** seizes his shadow — he kills her.

### In Lanka

Hanuman shrinks to the size of a cat and enters Lanka by night. He searches the city — Ravana's palace, the gardens — and finds Sita in **Ashoka Vatika**, guarded by rakshasis. She is emaciated, weeping.

He watches from a tree. Ravana arrives — he threatens Sita, but she refuses him. Ravana leaves. The rakshasis threaten to eat her.

When all are gone, Hanuman drops **Rama's ring** before Sita. She is overjoyed. Hanuman offers to take her back to Rama on his back — she refuses: "Rama must come himself to win me — it is his honor."

### The Burning of Lanka

Hanuman is discovered. He allows himself to be captured. The rakshasas set his tail on fire (wrapping it in oil-soaked cloth). He escapes, leaps from roof to roof, and **burns all of Lanka** — sparing only Sita's grove.

He plunges into the sea, extinguishes his tail, and flies back to Rama with the news.

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Devotion** | Hanuman is the perfect devotee — strength, intellect, and humility united |
| **Sita's faith** | She refuses rescue — only Rama's victory will vindicate her honor |
| **The leap of faith** | Hanuman's leap is the archetype of the devotee's trust in divine power |
| **Lanka burned** | Evil destroyed — but the test of virtue (Sita) left untouched |

---

## Comparison with Other Traditions

Hanuman's leap across the ocean has parallels in other traditions: the idea of a divine helper crossing impossible distances with unwavering devotion. In the Islamic tradition, the speed of the **Buraq** and the **Isra and Mi'raj** share the motif of divine acceleration for a sacred mission. In the Bible, the angels who visit Lot or Daniel perform similar superhuman feats of protection and travel.
