# Impermanence and Vanity

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]]

Two traditions with no historical contact — Hebrew wisdom literature and early
Buddhism — independently confront the same fact: **everything passes**. Where
they *diverge* is the response. This is an **independent convergence**, and the
contrast is the lesson.

## Ecclesiastes: "all is vanity"
From [[21 Ecclesiastes|Ecclesiastes]] (Hebrew *hevel* — vapor, breath, fleeting):

> [!quote] Ecclesiastes 1:2
> "**Vanity of vanities**, saith the Preacher, vanity of vanities; **all is vanity**."

The Preacher (Qoheleth) surveys toil, wealth, and wisdom and finds them
transient — "a chasing after wind." The response: accept life's limits, enjoy
simple goods, and **fear God** (Ecc 12:13).

## The Dhammapada: the three marks of existence
From [[The Dhammapada|Dhammapada]] 277–279 — the Buddhist *tilakkhaṇa*:

> [!quote] Dhammapada 277–279
> "**'All created things perish,'** he who knows and sees this becomes passive in pain…
> **'All created things are grief and pain,'** he who knows and sees this becomes passive in pain…
> **'All forms are unreal,'** he who knows and sees this becomes passive in pain; this is the way to purity."

These are *anicca* (impermanence), *dukkha* (suffering), and *anattā*
(non-self) — the diagnostic core of the Buddha's teaching.

## Same diagnosis, different cure
| | Ecclesiastes | Dhammapada |
|---|---|---|
| Observation | "all is vanity" — all is fleeting | "all created things perish" |
| Mood | weary realism | clear-eyed analysis |
| Response | enjoy the day, fear God | detach, end craving, seek *nirvāṇa* |
| Goal | live well *within* transience | be *released* from the cycle |

Both stare unflinchingly at impermanence. Ecclesiastes counsels **acceptance
within** a God-ordered world; the Dhammapada counsels **liberation from** the
wheel of becoming. The shared premise throws the differing destinations into
sharp relief.

## A Daoist echo
The [[Tao Te Ching]] adds a third stance: not lament, not escape, but
**flow** — align with the impermanent Tao rather than resist it. Three
traditions, three responses to one fact.

## What kind of connection?
**Independent / archetypal.** No plausible historical channel links Qoheleth
and the Pali tradition; both arrive at impermanence from honest observation.
The value of the comparison is not "they're the same" — it's that *the same
problem yields radically different solutions*.
