# The Temptation of Christ

> Immediately after his baptism, Jesus is led into the wilderness — and confronts the devil with nothing but scripture.

---

## The Story (Matthew 4:1–11 / Luke 4:1–13)

After his baptism, Jesus is led by the Spirit into the wilderness. He **fasts for 40 days and 40 nights** — at the end, he is hungry.

The **tempter** (Satan / the devil) approaches:

**First temptation:** *"If you are the Son of God, command these stones to become bread."* Jesus replies: *"Man shall not live by bread alone, but by every word that comes from the mouth of God"* (Deuteronomy 8:3).

**Second temptation:** The devil takes Jesus to the pinnacle of the Temple: *"If you are the Son of God, throw yourself down — for it is written, 'He will command his angels concerning you.'"* Jesus replies: *"You shall not put the Lord your God to the test"* (Deuteronomy 6:16).

**Third temptation:** The devil shows Jesus all the kingdoms of the world: *"All these I will give you — if you will fall down and worship me."* Jesus replies: *"You shall worship the Lord your God, and him only shall you serve"* (Deuteronomy 6:13).

The devil leaves; **angels come and minister to him**.

---

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **The new Adam** | Where Adam fell when tempted, Jesus prevails — reversing the Fall |
| **Scripture as weapon** | Jesus defeats Satan by quoting the Torah — the Word of God |
| **The 40 days** | Echoes Israel's 40 years in the wilderness — but where Israel failed, Jesus triumphs |
| **Threefold temptation** | Physical (bread), spiritual (testing God), political (worldly power) |

---

## Comparison with Islamic Tradition

The Qur'an does not narrate a temptation of Jesus by Iblis (Satan). However:
- The Qur'an affirms that Jesus was **protected from Satan** — "the accursed Satan did not touch him" (hadith — "Every child born is touched by Satan except Mary and her son")
- Some Islamic traditions mention that Jesus was tempted by Iblis, but successfully resisted
- The Christian emphasis on Jesus *not using his divine power* (refusing to turn stones to bread) is absent — Isa is a prophet who relies on God, not a divine being who humbles himself
