# David and Goliath

> The shepherd boy who killed a giant with a sling and five smooth stones — the archetypal story of the underdog who triumphs through faith.

---

## The Story (1 Samuel 17)

The Philistines gather for war against Israel. Their champion: **Goliath of Gath** — a giant over nine feet tall, covered in bronze armor, carrying a spear whose head weighed 15 pounds. He challenges Israel: *"Choose a man — if he can fight and kill me, we will be your servants"* (17:8–9). For 40 days, no one steps forward.

**David** — the youngest son of Jesse, a shepherd — brings food to his brothers in the army. He hears Goliath's taunts and asks: *"Who is this uncircumcised Philistine, that he should defy the armies of the living God?"* (17:26).

Saul offers David his armor — but it is too heavy. David takes only his **shepherd's staff, five smooth stones, and a sling**.

Goliath despises the boy. David runs toward him, puts a stone in his sling, and strikes the Philistine in the forehead. Goliath falls. David takes Goliath's own sword and cuts off his head. The Philistines flee; Israel wins a great victory.

---

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Faith over fear** | "The battle is the Lord's" (17:47) — trust in God outweighs military power |
| **The humble exalted** | The shepherd boy becomes the hero of Israel — foreshadowing the king |
| **God and Israel's enemies** | Goliath defied God — his defeat is God's vindication |
| **Typology** | Christian tradition: David prefigures Christ — the shepherd who slays the enemy |

---

## Comparison with Islamic Tradition

The Qur'an mentions David and Goliath (*Jālūt*) briefly in **Surah 2:249–251**:

"And when they went forth against Goliath and his soldiers, they said: 'Our Lord, pour upon us patience, make firm our feet, and give us victory over the disbelieving people.' So they defeated them by the permission of Allah, and **David killed Goliath**. And Allah gave him the kingship and wisdom."

The Qur'anic account:
- Is far shorter — a few verses compared to 1 Samuel's 58 verses
- Removes the elaborate armor and stone count
- Emphasizes David's kingship and prophethood *after* the victory
- Presents it as one event among many — not the dramatic centerpiece
