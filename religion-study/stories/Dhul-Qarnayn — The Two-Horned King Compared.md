# Dhul-Qarnayn — The Two-Horned King

> The Qur'an's mysterious king who travelled to the ends of the earth. His identity is one of the most debated questions in comparative scripture — Alexander the Great? Cyrus the Persian? An ancient Himyarite king? Whoever he was historically, the Qur'an transforms him into a model of righteous, God-guided kingship.

---

## The Traditions Compared

| Element | **Alexander Romance** (Greek/Syriac, c. 3rd c. CE) | **Cyrus in Isaiah** (Hebrew Bible, c. 6th c. BCE) | **Qur'an 18:83–98** |
|---------|-----------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------|----------------------|
| **Name** | Alexander the Great | **Cyrus** (Koresh) | **Dhul-Qarnayn** ("The Two-Horned") |
| **Travel west** | Alexander reaches the "end of the world" where the sun sets in the ocean | (Not described) | Reaches the setting-place of the sun — "a muddy spring" (18:86) |
| **Travel east** | Alexander reaches the rising-place of the sun | (Not described) | Reaches the rising-place of the sun (18:90) |
| **The wall** | Alexander builds a wall of iron and brass in the Caucasus to contain the "Unclean Nations" (Gog and Magog) | (Cyrus is depicted as a liberator, not a wall-builder) | Builds a wall of iron and copper between two mountains to imprison Gog and Magog (18:94–97) |
| **Divine favor** | Alexander is son of Zeus Ammon (pagan) | Cyrus is called **God's anointed** (Isaiah 45:1) — explicitly chosen by Yahweh | Dhul-Qarnayn is "established by Allah" (18:84) — a God-guided king |
| **Eschatological role** | The Unclean Nations will break out at the end of time | (Not eschatological) | Gog and Magog will break out at the end — "When My Lord's promise comes, He will level it to dust" (18:98) |

## The Three Major Theories

### Theory 1: Dhul-Qarnayn = Alexander the Great

| For | Against |
|-----|---------|
| Alexander was depicted on coins **with two horns** (the horns of Zeus-Ammon) | Alexander was a **pagan** — worshipped Zeus, claimed divine parentage — the Qur'an would not present a polytheist as a model of righteous kingship |
| The Alexander Romance (Greek/Syriac) has the **exact same structure**: journey west, journey east, the wall against Gog and Magog | Alexander's wall was a literary fiction — the Qur'an presents the story as historical truth |
| Alexander was widely known in Syriac Christian tradition as a **monotheist** (the "Christianized Alexander") | The "Christianized Alexander" was a late development (6th–7th c.); the Qur'an would have received this version |

**Verdict:** Strong circumstantial evidence for the Alexander connection, especially through Syriac Christian intermediaries. The Qur'an **monotheizes** the Alexander figure — stripping away all pagan elements and presenting him as a servant of God.

### Theory 2: Dhul-Qarnayn = Cyrus the Great

| For | Against |
|-----|---------|
| Cyrus is called God's **anointed** (*messiah*) in **Isaiah 45:1** — the only non-Jew given this title | No tradition associates Cyrus with the walls against Gog and Magog |
| Cyrus was a **monotheist** (Zoroastrian, but the Bible presents him as worshipping Yahweh) | No horns in Cyrus iconography |
| Cyrus ruled "from east to west" — the two horns could symbolize his universal empire | The Qur'anic story is much closer to the Alexander Romance than to anything in Jewish scripture |
| The Jewish tradition (Josephus, *Antiquities* 11) associates Gog/Magog with the distant north — Cyrus was associated with the region | |

**Verdict:** Plausible for the *character* (a righteous monotheist king ruling a universal empire) but weak for the *story* details (the wall, the journeys, the horns).

### Theory 3: An Ancient South Arabian King

| For | Against |
|-----|---------|
| The Sabaean kings of Yemen used the title **"Two-Horned"** (Dhū Qarnayn) | No South Arabian king built walls in the Caucasus |
| Some Himyarite kings were **monotheists** (Jewish converts) | The Gog/Magog wall is clearly set in the Caucasus/Armenia |
| The geography (Yemen is south; the Qur'an has east-west orientation) | |

**Verdict:** Least likely to be the primary source, but the title may have resonated with Arabian audiences who knew of "two-horned" kings in the south.

## The Biblical Gog and Magog

The Qur'an's Gog and Magog (*Yājūj wa Mājūj*) are directly connected to biblical tradition:

| Scripture | Gog/Magog |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Ezekiel 38–39** | Gog, prince of Magog, leads a coalition against Israel at the end of days — defeated by God |
| **Revelation 20:7–9** | Satan gathers Gog and Magog from the four corners of the earth for the final battle — consumed by fire |
| **Qur'an 18:94–98** | Two peoples, corrupting the earth, imprisoned by Dhul-Qarnayn's wall until the end of time — they break out and are destroyed |

The connection is **directly genealogical**: Gog/Magog is a biblical motif adopted by the Qur'an and integrated into the Dhul-Qarnayn narrative.

---

## What the Qur'an Changes

1. **Removes paganism:** If Alexander — his divine sonship, his Greek pantheon worship — is rejected. The king is a monotheist.
2. **Adds justice:** Dhul-Qarnayn judges fairly: "those who believe — We shall reward them; those who do wrong — We shall punish them" (18:86–87).
3. **Moralizes geography:** The sun's rising and setting are not astronomical observations but **locations of divine judgment** — each society is tested.
4. **Eschatological framing:** The wall is not just a barrier but a sign of the Hour — "when My Lord's promise comes, He will level it" (18:98).

---

## The "Two Horns" Symbolism

| Interpretation | Meaning |
|---------------|---------|
| **Geographic** | He ruled from east to west (the "two ends" of the earth) |
| **Iconographic** | His crown had two horns (ancient symbol of power — seen on Alexander's coins) |
| **Prophetic** | Two periods of rule / two covenants |
| **Solar** | He followed the sun from its rising to its setting |

The most likely: the **iconographic** interpretation, connecting to Alexander's horned coins, reinterpreted by the Qur'an as a symbol of divinely-granted authority.
