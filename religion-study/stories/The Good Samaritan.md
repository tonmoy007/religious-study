# The Good Samaritan

> The parable that redefines "neighbor" — and turns a despised outsider into the hero of the story.

---

## The Parable (Luke 10:25–37)

A lawyer tests Jesus: *"Teacher, what shall I do to inherit eternal life?"* Jesus asks what the Law says. The lawyer answers: *"Love the Lord your God with all your heart, soul, strength, and mind — and love your neighbor as yourself."*

But the lawyer, *"desiring to justify himself,"* asks: *"And who is my neighbor?"*

Jesus replies:

A man descends from Jerusalem to Jericho — and falls among robbers. They strip him, beat him, and leave him half dead.

- A **priest** passes by — on the other side.
- A **Levite** passes by — on the other side.
- A **Samaritan** (despised by Jews as a heretic) sees him — and **has compassion**. He bandages his wounds, pours on oil and wine, sets him on his donkey, brings him to an inn, and pays for his care. *"Take care of him — when I return, I will repay you."*

Jesus asks: *"Which of these three was a neighbor to the man?"* The lawyer: *"The one who showed mercy."* Jesus: *"Go and do likewise."*

---

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Redefining neighbor** | Not who is my neighbor — but *whose* neighbor am I? |
| **Mercy over status** | The religious leaders fail; the heretic shows true religion |
| **Love in action** | Compassion is not a feeling but costly intervention |
| **The inverted expectation** | Jesus' parables consistently shock — the "righteous" fall, the "sinner" is praised |

---

## Connection with Islamic Tradition

There is no direct parallel in the Qur'an or Hadith. However:

> *"The parable of those who spend their wealth in the way of Allah is like a grain of corn that grows seven ears — every ear contains a hundred grains"* (Qur'an 2:261)

The emphasis on **charity without expectation of return** echoes the Samaritan's generosity. The story of **the man who fed a hungry dog** and was forgiven (hadith) similarly emphasizes mercy across social boundaries.
