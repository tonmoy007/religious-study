## The Full Narrative

### Act I: The Golden Age and the Betrayal

Osiris rules Egypt as the fourth divine king (after [[Ra — Egyptian God|Ra]], [[Shu — Egyptian God|Shu]], and [[Geb — Egyptian God|Geb]]). Under his reign:

> *"He turned the Egyptians away from their squalid and brutish manner of living, taught them how to cultivate the earth, gave them laws, and instructed them to honour the gods."* — Plutarch

Osiris is wise, just, and beloved — and his brother **Set** burns with jealousy.

Set secretly measures Osiris's body and commissions a **beautiful chest** (a chest, not yet a coffin) perfectly sized for him. At a grand banquet:

1. Set brings out the chest and promises it as a gift to whomever fits perfectly inside
2. One by one the guests lie in it — none fit
3. Osiris lies down — it fits perfectly
4. **Set and his 72 co-conspirators slam the lid shut**, seal it with lead, and throw it into the Nile

### Act II: The Search

The chest floats down the Nile, through the Delta, and across the Mediterranean to **Byblos** (in modern-day Lebanon). There it lands in a tamarisk tree, which grows into a magnificent pillar — the tree encloses the chest entirely.

The king of Byblos, impressed by the tree, **cuts it down and makes it a pillar in his palace** — not knowing the god's body is inside.

Meanwhile in Egypt:

- [[Isis — Egyptian Goddess|Isis]] cuts off a lock of her hair and dons mourning garments
- She **searches the entire land** — "I will not rest until I find him"
- She learns from children (who have the gift of prophecy) that the chest went north
- She follows it to Byblos

### Act III: The Recovery

At Byblos, Isis sits **by a well, weeping**. The queen's handmaidens come to draw water; Isis braids their hair and breathes a divine fragrance upon them. The queen sends for the stranger.

Isis becomes the **nurse of the queen's child**:
- She feeds the child **her finger** (immortal food) instead of milk
- Each night, she **burns away the child's mortality in the fire**, while turning into a swallow and flying around the pillar
- The queen sees this one night, screams in terror — and the spell is broken
- Isis reveals herself, **asks for the pillar**, and the king happily gives it

Isis cuts the pillar open, recovers the chest, and **weeps so bitterly that one of the king's sons dies of shock**.

### Act IV: The Dismemberment

Isis returns to Egypt with the chest. She hides it in the Delta marshes and goes to fetch her son [[Horus — Egyptian God|Horus]] from the city of Buto.

But **Set is hunting by moonlight**. He finds the chest, recognizes it, opens it. In a fury:

> *He took the body of Osiris and cut it into fourteen pieces, and scattered them throughout all the land of Egypt.* — Plutarch

Set leaves the empty chest behind. When Isis returns, she finds it empty.

### Act V: The Gathering

Isis and [[Nephthys — Egyptian Goddess|Nephthys]] (Set's own wife, who sides with Isis) begin the **longest search in Egyptian mythology**:

- They travel the Nile in a **papyrus skiff** (the reason crocodiles do not attack papyrus boats — they honour Isis's grief)
- Each piece they find is **buried on the spot** and a temple is built over it (the origin of the many Osiris tombs throughout Egypt)
- Thirteen pieces are recovered. The **fourteenth — the phallus — is eaten by a fish** (the oxyrhyncus or the phagrus)
- Isis fashions a **golden replacement phallus**

### Act VI: The Resurrection

With [[Thoth — Egyptian God|Thoth's]] wisdom and [[Anubis — Egyptian God|Anubis's]] embalming skills, Isis performs the first mummification:

1. Anubis wraps the reassembled body in linen
2. Isis fans the body with her wings
3. Thoth recites the spells
4. **Osiris is restored — but not to life as before**

Osiris does not return to the throne of Egypt. Instead, he descends to the **Duat (Tuat)** to become the **Lord of the Dead** — the judge and king of the underworld.

But before he departs, Isis — in the form of a kite — **conceives Horus** from the restored Osiris.

> *She hovered over the body of Osiris and conceived a son.*

### Act VII: The Hidden Childhood

Isis raises Horus in the **marshes of Chemmis** (the Delta), hidden from Set:
- She protects him from scorpions, snakes, and Set's spies
- [[Hathor — Egyptian Goddess|Hathor]] nurses him with her cow's milk
- Thoth teaches him wisdom
- The local god of Buto protects the marshes

### Act VIII: The Contendings of Horus and Set

When Horus comes of age, he demands the throne of Egypt before the **Great Ennead** (the council of nine gods, presided over by [[Ra — Egyptian God|Ra]]):

**The legal case:**

| Claimant | Argument | Supporting God(s) |
|----------|----------|-------------------|
| **Horus** | Son of the murdered king — rightful heir by descent | Isis, Thoth, Osiris (from the Tuat) |
| **Set** | Stronger — "A son cannot rule while his father's murderer lives"; also, he protects the barque | Ra (initially), some of the Ennead |

**The contest drags on for 80 years** (some versions say centuries). The gods grow tired of arguing.

**The Trials:**

1. **The Hippopotamus Contest**: Both turn into hippos and dive underwater — whoever stays under longest wins. Isis throws a harpoon at Set (she intends to hit Horus to prove he's worthy by surviving; at the last moment she throws it at Set instead). Set begs for mercy; Isis heals him. Horus is enraged and **cuts off Isis's head** (she is quickly restored by Thoth).

2. **The Eye of Horus**: Set tears out Horus's eye in the night. [[Hathor — Egyptian Goddess|Hathor]] (or Thoth) restores it with gazelle's milk. The eye becomes the *Wadjet* — the most powerful protective amulet in Egypt.

3. **The Sethian Trick**: Set (in some versions) tries to sexually dominate Horus. Horus catches Set's seed in his hand and shows it to Isis. Isis cuts off Horus's hand, makes it whole again, and instructs Horus to spread his own seed on Set's food so that Set is shown to be the *receiver*, not the giver. This complex episode is a **legal argument about dominance and submission** before the gods.

**The Verdict:**

The Ennead is deadlocked for decades. Finally:
- [[Osiris — Egyptian God|Osiris]] (from the underworld) sends a letter to the Ennead: *"If I am not avenged, I will send the demons of the Tuat to devour the gods."*
- Ra relents.
- The Ennead awards the throne to Horus.

**The Resolution:**

Set is not destroyed. Ra declares:

> *"Let Set be given to me. He shall be with me as my son, and he shall thunder in the sky and be feared. He shall stand on the prow of my barque and repel the serpent."*

Set becomes the god of storms and the protector of the Solar Barque — the necessary chaos that defends order.

### Epilogue: Osiris in the Underworld

Osiris rules the dead as the "Foremost of the Westerners." Every justified soul becomes an "Osiris." Horus rules the living. The cosmic order — *[[Ma'at — Goddess of Truth|Ma'at]]* — is restored.

## The Myth in the Book of the Dead

The Book of the Dead does not recount the myth as a continuous narrative (that comes from Plutarch and the Pyramid Texts). Instead, it **presupposes the myth** at every point:

- Every deceased person is called "Osiris N." — they *participate in* Osiris's story
- The mummification follows Anubis's first embalming of Osiris
- The [[The Negative Confession — Egyptian Story|Negative Confession]] echoes [[Ma'at — Goddess of Truth|Ma'at's]] standard — the ethics that Horus fought to establish
- The [[The Weighing of the Heart — Egyptian Story|Weighing]] takes place in Osiris's hall
- The [[The Field of Reeds — Egyptian Story|Field of Reeds]] is Osiris's domain

## Theological Significance

### A. The Dying-and-Rising God
Osiris is the archetype of the dying-and-rising vegetation god — his death and restoration echo the annual cycle of the Nile's flood and retreat, the planting and harvesting of grain.

### B. Resurrection as Mummification
The myth establishes that **resurrection depends on preservation**. The body must be kept intact — hence mummification. This is why the dismemberment (14 pieces) is so horrifying: it is the un-making of the self.

### C. The Justification of Kingship
The myth is a **divine legal case** about succession. It grounds the pharaoh's authority in a chain going back to Ra himself — but it also shows that kingship must be *justified* before the gods.

### D. Chaos Contained
Set's punishment is not exile or death — it's **inclusion with a role**. Chaos is not eliminated from the cosmos; it's stationed at the front of the boat, facing forward, doing useful work.
