# Esther: For Such a Time as This

> A Jewish queen in Persia who risks her life to save her people — the only book of the Bible that never mentions God, yet shows divine providence on every page.

---

## The Story (Book of Esther)

### The Setting

King **Xerxes** (Ahasuerus) of Persia — ruler from India to Ethiopia — holds a lavish 180-day feast. Queen Vashti refuses to appear before the king's drunken guests. She is deposed.

### The Rise of Esther

A search for a new queen: **Esther** (Hadassah), a Jewish orphan raised by her cousin **Mordecai**, wins the king's favor and becomes queen — but keeps her Jewish identity secret.

### The Threat

Mordecai refuses to bow to **Haman**, the king's highest official. Enraged, Haman persuades the king to issue a decree to **annihilate all Jews** in the empire — casting lots (*purim*) to choose the date.

### The Crisis

Mordecai sends word to Esther: she must appeal to the king. She hesitates — approaching the king unsummoned is punishable by death. Mordecai's reply:

> [!quote] Esther 4:14
> "If you keep silent at this time, relief and deliverance will arise from another place — but you and your father's house will perish. And who knows whether you have not come to the kingdom for such a time as this?"

### The Reversal

Esther approaches the king — he extends the golden scepter. She invites the king and Haman to two banquets. At the second, she reveals her Jewish identity and accuses Haman. The king — enraged — orders Haman hanged on the very gallows Haman built for Mordecai.

The Jews are given the right to defend themselves. **Purim** is established as a feast of deliverance.

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Hidden providence** | God is never named — yet His hand is unmistakable in the coincidences |
| **Courage** | "If I perish, I perish" — Esther's willingness to die for her people |
| **The reversal** | The very instrument of destruction (Haman's gallows) becomes the means of his downfall |
| **Jewish survival** | The archetypal story of anti-Semitism defeated |

---

## Comparison with Islamic Tradition

Esther does not appear in the Qur'an. The story is set in the Persian period, after the Babylonian exile — a historical context the Qur'an does not narrate in detail. However:
- The theme of a righteous person in a position of power saving their people echoes the story of **Joseph in Egypt**
- **Mordecai's** insistence on principle recalls the steadfastness of prophets
- There is no direct parallel — this story is unique to the Hebrew Bible
