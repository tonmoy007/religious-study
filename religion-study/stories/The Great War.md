# The Great War

> The war for Sita's freedom — a clash of gods and demons, titans and monkeys, ending with the death of Ravana and the vindication of dharma.

---

## The Story (Ramayana — Yuddha Kanda)

### Building the Bridge

Rama's army of monkeys reaches the shore. The ocean cannot be crossed. Rama prays — but the ocean god does not respond. Rama threatens to dry up the sea with his arrows. The god appears: Nala and Nila (the celestial architects, reborn as monkeys) can build a bridge across the ocean — using **floating stones** that cannot sink.

In five days, the **Rama Setu** (Bridge of Rama) is built — 100 leagues long. The army crosses into Lanka.

### The Siege

The war begins. Ravana's generals attack:

- **Indrajit** (Ravana's son, a master of illusion) wounds Rama and Lakshmana with serpent-arrows. Hanuman brings the **Sanjivani** mountain — herbs that heal all wounds.
- **Kumbhakarna** (Ravana's giant brother, who sleeps six months at a time) is awakened. He slaughters thousands of monkeys. Rama kills him with a divine arrow.
- Indrajit creates an illusion of Sita being killed — Rama despairs. Lakshmana kills Indrajit.

The battle reaches its climax.

### Rama and Ravana

Ravana enters the battlefield. He and Rama fight with divine weapons — arrows that become serpents, spears that shatter mountains. Ravana repeatedly loses his heads — they grow back.

At the crucial moment, **Matali** (Indra's charioteer) drives Rama's chariot. The sage **Agastya** teaches Rama the **Aditya Hridayam** (prayer to the sun god). Filled with power, Rama shoots the **Brahmastra** — it pierces Ravana's chest and kills him.

The ten-headed king falls. The Devas shower flowers from heaven.

### Victory

Lakshmana faints from a wound — Hanuman brings Dronagiri mountain again. Rama frees Sita — but refuses to take her back in public until she proves her purity.

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Dharma restored** | Good triumphs over evil — but only through immense sacrifice |
| **Floating stones** | Faith makes the impossible possible |
| **Ravana's death** | The scholar-demon who knew all scriptures but could not conquer desire |
| **The cost of war** | Countless die on both sides — victory is not clean |

---

## Comparison with Other Traditions

The floating-stone bridge of Ramayana (Rama Setu) has parallels in other ancient narratives — the **Pons Sublicius** of Rome, the **Chinvat Bridge** of Zoroastrian tradition (the judgment bridge), and the bridge over which Bunyan's Christian must cross to reach the Celestial City. The theme of a physical bridge to salvation appears across cultures.
