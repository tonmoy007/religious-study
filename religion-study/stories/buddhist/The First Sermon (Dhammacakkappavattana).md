# The First Sermon (Dhammacakkappavattana)

> *"The Tathāgata does not teach a Dharma he has merely heard or learned — he teaches what he has directly seen, known, and realized."*
> — Aṅguttara Nikāya 4:23

---

## The Decision to Teach

After the enlightenment under the Bodhi tree, the Buddha spent seven weeks meditating on his realisation. At first, he **hesitated** to teach. The Dharma was *"profound, hard to see, hard to understand, tranquil, sublime, beyond the sphere of logic, subtle — to be understood by the wise"* (MN 26). He saw a world lost in craving, clinging to pleasures and doctrines — few would have the eyes to see the truth.

> *"What I have realized is like a garland of finest lotuses — yet the world delights in its muddy pond. Shall I speak? Who will understand?"*

According to the **Brahmāyācana Sutta** (SN 6.1), the great god **Brahmā Sahampati** appeared and pleaded with the Buddha to teach. He argued that there *were* beings with "little dust in their eyes" who would benefit — like lotuses that rise above the water unstained. Brahmā knelt, his hands folded, and implored:

> *"Let the Blessed One teach the Dharma! There are beings with little dust in their eyes — if they do not hear it, they will be lost."*

The Buddha, out of compassion for the world, surveyed it with his "Buddha-eye" and saw beings of various capacities — some sharp, some dull, some responsive, some resistant. He accepted. He would teach.

---

## The Journey to Sarnath

The Buddha decided to first teach the **Pañcavaggiyā** — the five ascetics (Koṇḍañña, Vappa, Bhaddiya, Mahānāma, and Assaji) who had served him during his ascetic years but abandoned him when he adopted the Middle Way. He walked from Bodhgaya to **Sarnath** (Isipatana, the Deer Park) — a journey of about 230 kilometres across the Gangetic plain.

On the road, he met the ascetic **Upaka** (or Upaga). Upaka saw the Buddha's radiant appearance and asked:

> *"Friend, your features are clear, your complexion pure. Who is your teacher? Whose doctrine do you follow?"*

The Buddha declared:

> *"I am the **Arahant** — the Worthy One. I am the **Sammāsambuddha** — the Fully Awakened One. I have no teacher. Alone I awakened to the truth. I go to the city of Kāsi to set in motion the wheel of Dhamma."*

Upaka shook his head: *"May it be so, friend,"* and walked away — taking a different path. The first person to meet the newly awakened Buddha declined his teaching.

---

## Meeting the Five Ascetics

When the Buddha arrived at the Deer Park in Sarnath, the five ascetics saw him approaching from a distance. They had agreed among themselves: *"Here comes the ascetic Gotama — he who abandoned austerity for luxury. We will not bow to him, not rise for him, not receive his bowl. We will call him 'Friend.'"*

But as the Buddha drew closer, his radiance overwhelmed them. They could not keep their resolve. One took his bowl, another prepared a seat, another brought water for his feet — they broke their own vow.

Still, they addressed him as "Friend Gotama." The Buddha replied:

> *"Do not address the Tathāgata as 'Friend,' bhikkhus. The Tathāgata is an Arahant, a Fully Enlightened One. Give ear — the Deathless has been attained. I will teach you."*

They protested: *"Your earlier austerities did not bring enlightenment — how could luxury and comfort have done so?"*

The Buddha answered: *"The Tathāgata does not indulge in luxury, nor has he returned to the pursuit of pleasure. He has found the Middle Way."*

---

## The Sermon

The Buddha delivered the **Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta** ("Setting in Motion the Wheel of Dhamma") — recorded in SN 56.11. The sermon has three great movements:

### The Middle Way

> *"There are two extremes, bhikkhus, which one who has gone forth should not follow: devotion to the indulgence of sense-pleasures (kāmasukhallikānuyoga) — low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, leading to no good — and devotion to self-mortification (attakilamathānuyoga) — painful, ignoble, leading to no good. Avoiding both extremes, the Tathāgata has realized the Middle Way (Majjhimā Paṭipadā) — giving vision, giving knowledge, leading to peace, direct knowledge, enlightenment, Nibbāna."*

### The Four Noble Truths (Cattāri Ariyasaccāni)

1. **Dukkha** — The Noble Truth of Suffering: Birth is suffering, aging is suffering, sickness is suffering, death is suffering; association with the unloved is suffering, separation from the loved is suffering; not getting what one wants is suffering; in short, the five aggregates of clinging are suffering.

2. **Samudaya** — The Noble Truth of the Origin of Suffering: It is craving (taṇhā) — craving for sense-pleasures (kāmataṇhā), craving for existence (bhavataṇhā), craving for non-existence (vibhavataṇhā) — which leads to renewed becoming.

3. **Nirodha** — The Noble Truth of the Cessation of Suffering: The complete, passionless cessation of that same craving — its forsaking, relinquishing, releasing, letting go.

4. **Magga** — The Noble Truth of the Path Leading to the Cessation of Suffering: The Noble Eightfold Path.

### The Noble Eightfold Path (Ariyo Aṭṭhaṅgiko Maggo)

| Division | Factor | Translation |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **Wisdom (Paññā)** | Sammā Diṭṭhi | Right View |
| | Sammā Saṅkappa | Right Intention |
| **Ethics (Sīla)** | Sammā Vācā | Right Speech |
| | Sammā Kammanta | Right Action |
| | Sammā Ājīva | Right Livelihood |
| **Mental Discipline (Samādhi)** | Sammā Vāyāma | Right Effort |
| | Sammā Sati | Right Mindfulness |
| | Sammā Samādhi | Right Concentration |

The Buddha taught each truth with three aspects: *"This is the Noble Truth… it should be understood… it has been understood."* With each turn of insight, his vision and knowledge became complete. At the end of this twelvefold teaching, he declared:

> *"Knowledge and vision arose in me: unshakable is the liberation of my mind. This is my last birth. Now there is no further becoming."*

---

## The Response

As the Buddha spoke, the **Dhamma-eye** (Dhammacakkhu) arose in **Koṇḍañña** — the youngest and wisest of the five ascetics, the very same brahmin who had prophesied at Siddhattha's naming ceremony thirty-five years earlier:

> *"Yam kiñci samudayadhammaṃ — sabbaṃ taṃ nirodhadhammaṃ."*
> *"Whatever is subject to origination — all that is subject to cessation."*

Koṇḍañña became the **first disciple** — the first human to directly see the truth of the Dhamma. The earth trembled, and the gods cried out:

> *"The **Dhamma-wheel** has been set in motion by the Blessed One at the Deer Park in Sarnath — by no one can it be turned back!"*

The cry echoed from the earth up through the heavens to the highest Brahmā realms — a single shout that shook ten thousand world-systems. The Buddha smiled and said:

> *"Aññāsi vata bho Koṇḍañño — Koṇḍañña has understood indeed!"*

Thus Koṇḍañña was called **Aññāsi-Koṇḍañña** — "Koṇḍañña Who Has Understood."

---

## The Founding of the Saṅgha

After the sermon, Koṇḍañña asked for ordination. The Buddha said:

> *"Come, bhikkhu. The Dhamma is well-proclaimed. Live the holy life for the complete ending of suffering."*

This **Ehi Bhikkhu** ("Come, monk") ordination was the simplest form — the Buddha's invitation itself constituted full ordination. Over the following days, the remaining four ascetics also attained the Dhamma-eye as the Buddha continued teaching, and each was ordained.

Thus the **Triple Gem** — Buddha (the teacher), Dhamma (the teaching), and Saṅgha (the community) — was complete for the first time. Within two months, all five had become arahants — fully enlightened — and the Buddha sent his first sixty disciples into the world:

> *"Go forth, bhikkhus, for the good of the many, for the happiness of the many, out of compassion for the world."*

---

## Comparison: First Revelations Across Traditions

The First Sermon is Buddhism's founding proclamation — the moment a private realisation became a public teaching. It parallels other "first revelation" moments in world religion:

| Tradition | Event | Key Figure | Content |
|-----------|-------|------------|---------|
| **Buddhism** | First Sermon (Sarnath, 528 BCE) | The Buddha | Middle Way, Four Noble Truths, Eightfold Path — a diagnosis and prescription for suffering |
| **Christianity** | Sermon on the Mount (c. 28 CE) | Jesus | Beatitudes — "Blessed are the poor in spirit…" — the kingdom turned upside down |
| **Islam** | First Revelation (Cave of Hirā, 610 CE) | Muhammad | *"Iqra!"* (Read/Recite) — the command that began the Qur'anic revelation |
| **Hinduism** | Krishna's Bhagavad Gita | Krishna | *"Do your duty without attachment to the fruits"* — dharma as liberation |

All four are **founding discourses** — teachings that define their tradition's core message. But the First Sermon is unique in being **diagnostic** — it does not begin with a command from a deity but with an empirical observation: *"There is suffering. Here is its cause. Here is its end. Here is the path."*
