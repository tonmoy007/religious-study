# Mahāprajāpatī's Ordination

> The founding of the Bhikkhunī Saṅgha — an epic of devotion, grief, persuasion, and the most controversial decision of the Buddha's ministry. Recorded in the Cullavagga (Vinaya Piṭaka, Khandhaka X).

> *"Women, when rightly taught and properly ordained, are capable of realizing the highest fruit of the Dhamma."*

---

## The First Request

**Mahāprajāpatī Gotamī** — the Buddha's stepmother and aunt. She raised Prince Siddhattha after Queen Māyā died seven days after his birth. When the Buddha returned to Kapilavatthu after his enlightenment, Mahāprajāpatī listened to his teaching and attained stream-entry. She was the first woman in the Sakyan clan to do so.

After King Suddhodana's death, she saw her path clearly. She approached the Buddha and asked:

> *"Lord, let women go forth from home into homelessness in the Dhamma and Vinaya proclaimed by the Tathāgata."*

The Buddha refused: **"Enough, Gotamī — do not ask for this."**

She asked a second time. Again: **"Enough — do not ask for this."**

She asked a third time. The Buddha's answer did not change.

Three times she requested; three times he refused. No reason was given. She bowed, withdrew, and wept.

---

## The Journey

Mahāprajāpatī did not accept the refusal. She cut off her hair, put on ochre robes — without being formally ordained — and gathered around her **five hundred Sakyan women** who had also lost their husbands and wished to join the homeless life.

They set out on foot from Kapilavatthu — a 150-mile journey to Vesālī, where the Buddha was staying at the Mahāvana monastery. They walked through the heat of the plains, their heads shaved, their feet swelling and bleeding from the long road. When they arrived, exhausted and covered in dust, they stood at the gate of the monastery.

Mahāprajāpatī stood outside, her feet swollen, her face wet with tears. She had walked every step for the chance to ask one more time.

---

## Ānanda's Intervention

The Venerable **Ānanda** — the Buddha's attendant — found her weeping outside the monastery gate. He asked why she was crying.

> *"Because, Ānanda, the Lord does not permit women to go forth from home into homelessness in the Dhamma and Vinaya proclaimed by the Tathāgata."*

Ānanda, moved by compassion, entered the Buddha's dwelling and made his own plea.

**First plea:** "Lord, is Mahāprajāpatī Gotamī worthy of going forth? She has been of great service — she raised you, nursed you, gave you milk when your mother died. Please let women ordain."

The Buddha: **"Enough, Ānanda — do not ask."**

**Second plea:** Ānanda pressed again, using the same argument: her service, her devotion, the sincerity of her renunciation.

The Buddha refused again.

**Third plea:** Ānanda then shifted strategy. He asked directly:

> *"Lord, are women capable — when they go forth from home into homelessness in the Dhamma and Vinaya proclaimed by the Tathāgata — of attaining stream-entry, once-return, non-return, and arahantship?"*

The Buddha answered: **"Yes, Ānanda — women are capable."**

That single admission was the turning point. If women could awaken — could reach the same highest goal as men — how could they be denied the path?

---

## The Eight Garudhammas

The Buddha then said: **"If Mahāprajāpatī Gotamī accepts the Eight Garudhammas (Heavy Rules or Principles of Respect), that itself shall be her ordination."**

The Eight Garudhammas:

1. **Seniority subordination** — A nun of a hundred years' standing must greet, rise for, bow to, and perform duties for a monk ordained that very day.
2. **Residence** — A nun must not spend the rains retreat in a district where there is no monk available for guidance.
3. **Uposatha** — Every fortnight, the nuns must request the date of the Uposatha (observance day) and come for instruction from the Saṅgha of monks.
4. **Pavāraṇā** — After the rains retreat, a nun must hold her invitation (*pavāraṇā*) before both the Saṅgha of monks and the Saṅgha of nuns.
5. **Penance** — A nun who commits a major offense must undergo penance before both Saṅghas.
6. **Novice ordination** — A female novice must train for two years in the six precepts before requesting full ordination from both Saṅghas.
7. **Monastic deference** — A nun must never insult or revile a monk.
8. **Admonition** — From this day forward, the rule is set: a nun's admonition of a monk is not permitted, but a monk's admonition of a nun is permitted.

The Buddha presented these rules to Ānanda: If Mahāprajāpatī accepts, let her ordination be considered granted.

---

## The Acceptance

Ānanda returned to Mahāprajāpatī and recited the Eight Garudhammas.

She did not hesitate. She answered:

> *"Just as a woman or man, young and fond of adornment, would accept a garland of flowers — so too do I accept the Eight Garudhammas. I will never transgress them as long as I live."*

Her ordination — and the ordination of the 500 Sakyan women — was thus accomplished. The Bhikkhunī Saṅgha was born.

Mahāprajāpatī became the first bhikkhunī, declared by the Buddha as **foremost among the nuns in long standing** (*etadaggaṁ bhikkhunīnaṁ rattaccānaṁ*). In time, many of the 500 women became arahants.

---

## The Cost

But the Buddha also spoke a prophecy — one that has haunted the tradition for 2,500 years:

> *"If women had not gone forth from home into homelessness, Ānanda, the pure Dhamma would have endured for a thousand years. Because women have now gone forth, the Dhamma will endure only five hundred years."*

This is the most debated passage in the Vinaya. Interpretations range from literal (a historical decline triggered by institutional tension) to compassionate (the Buddha knew the sexism of the surrounding society would weaken the Saṅgha's reputation) to metaphorical (the Dhamma's full flowering was too demanding for the world to bear).

What is not debated: the Buddha **did allow it**. Despite the warning, despite the risks, he gave the ordination. The Bhikkhunī Saṅgha flourished for centuries — producing nuns like **Mahāprajāpatī, Kisā Gotamī, Paṭācārā, Bhaddā Kuṇḍalakesā, and Somā**, whose verses fill the Therīgāthā. The prophecy stands alongside the decision, unresolved.

## Comparison — Women's Religious Leadership Across Traditions

| Figure | Tradition | Role |
|--------|-----------|------|
| **Mahāprajāpatī Gotamī** | Buddhism | Founded the Bhikkhunī Saṅgha; first ordained nun |
| **Mary Magdalene** | Christianity | First witness of the resurrection; "apostle to the apostles" |
| **Miriam (Miryam)** | Judaism / Torah | Prophetess; led women in song after the Red Sea crossing |
| **Hājar (Hagar)** | Islam | Mother of Ismāʿīl; her search for water between Safā and Marwa is re-enacted in the Hajj |

**Mahāprajāpatī** mirrors **Mary Magdalene** most closely — both were the first women in their tradition to receive a previously male-exclusive spiritual commission. Mary was sent by Jesus to tell the apostles of the resurrection; Mahāprajāpatī was the first to receive full ordination, opening the monastic path for all women after her.

**Miriam** led public worship — a prophetic and liturgical role rare for women in ancient Israel. Her song at the Red Sea (Exodus 15:20–21) is the oldest poetic passage in the Bible.

**Hājar** was not a formal religious leader, but her story — abandoned in the desert, running between Safā and Marwa seven times in search of water — became the central ritual of the Hajj, performed by millions every year. Like Mahāprajāpatī's walk to Vesālī, it is a story of a woman's struggle and God's response.

The common thread: **women found a way**. The path was refused, blocked, or unimaginable — and yet each of these women opened it anyway, through persistence, faith, and an insistence that the sacred could not be limited to men.
