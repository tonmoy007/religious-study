# The Four Sights

> The four encounters that shattered Prince Siddhartha's palace idyll — an old man, a sick man, a corpse, and a wandering ascetic — and set him on the path to Buddhahood. Recorded in the Ariyapariyesanā Sutta (MN 26) and the Nidānakathā.

---

## The King's Strategy

King **Suddhodana**, on hearing the prophecy that his son would renounce the world if he saw four signs, resolved to prevent it. He built three palaces — one for each season — and filled them with youth, beauty, and pleasure. The old, the sick, the dead, and the holy were banished from the prince's sight. Every street Siddhartha might travel was swept clean; only the young and healthy were permitted near him. The king's strategy was not cruelty but fear — he wanted his son to be a world-ruler (*cakkavatti*), not a wandering mendicant.

---

## The First Sight — Old Age

Prince Siddhartha, now a young man, desired to see the city outside the palace walls. Suddhodana ordered the streets beautified, but the devas (gods) had other plans. They sent a **decrepit old man** — bent, grey-haired, leaning on a staff — to appear before the prince.

Siddhartha turned to his charioteer **Channa**:

> "What has happened to this man? His hair is not like others — his body is broken."

Channa replied: "This is **old age** — *jarā*. It comes to all beings. No one escapes it."

The prince was shaken. "How can I delight in youth," he thought, "when old age is the certain end of it?" He returned to the palace, but his heart was no longer in its pleasures.

---

## The Second Sight — Sickness

On a second outing, the devas sent a **sick man** — wasted, feverish, lying in his own filth, unable to stand.

> "What is this, Channa?"

"This is **sickness** — *vyādhi*. Disease strikes without warning. The body, though cherished, is subject to decay at any moment."

Siddhartha had known sickness in the abstract, but the sight struck him like a blow. The body — so carefully adorned, so proud of its strength — was a vessel for disease. He saw then that health is a temporary reprieve, not a possession.

---

## The Third Sight — Death

On the third journey, the devas revealed a **corpse** — a funeral procession carrying a body wrapped in white cloth, followed by weeping mourners.

> "Even this, Channa? Even this comes to everyone?"

"This is **death** — *maraṇa*. It ends all life. Rich and poor, king and slave — all must die."

The prince stared at the corpse with an intensity that alarmed even Channa. If the body dies, if everything we love will be taken — what is the point of pleasure? What is the point of anything?

> "Is there no way out of this?" Siddhartha whispered.

---

## The Fourth Sight — The Ascetic

On the fourth outing, the devas sent not a vision of suffering but a vision of **answer** — a **wandering renunciant** (*samaṇa*): shaven-headed, wearing a ochre robe, walking with a bowl, his face calm and his eyes at peace.

> "Who is this, Channa — so different from all others?"

"This is one who has gone forth (*pabbajita*). Seeing the danger in birth, old age, sickness, and death, he has left home to seek liberation. He lives on alms, possesses nothing, and devotes himself to finding what is true."

Siddhartha's heart leaped. Here was a path. The first three sights had shattered his world; the fourth showed him a way beyond it.

---

## The Aftermath

Siddhartha returned to the palace, but a fire had been lit. The palace pleasures — the dancers, the music, the gardens — now seemed hollow. He could not unsee what he had seen.

Suddhodana, sensing the change, doubled the palace pleasures: more dancers, finer wine, stricter guards at the gates. But the prince lay awake that night, watching the dancers fall asleep in grotesque poses, snoring, drooling — bodies no different from the corpse he had seen.

That night, he made his decision. He would leave. The **Great Renunciation** — the leaving of home — was only hours away.

## Comparison

### The Three Messengers of Indian Thought

The first three sights correspond to the **"three messengers"** (*devadūta*) described in the Devadūta Sutta (MN 130) — old age, sickness, and death — sent by the gods to wake humans from their heedlessness. In the later Buddhist tradition, these are not merely sights but divine warnings: gods take the forms of the old, sick, and dead to remind the living of impermanence (*anicca*). The fourth sight — the ascetic — is the unique Buddhist addition: suffering is not final; there is a way out.

### Parallel: The Parable of the Prodigal Son

A loose but illuminating parallel exists with the Christian parable of the **Prodigal Son** (Luke 15:11–32). In both stories, a young prince (or son) leaves a sheltered home to undertake a difficult journey. But the contrasts are sharper:

| Aspect | The Four Sights | The Prodigal Son |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Motivation** | Seeing universal suffering | Personal restlessness |
| **Departure** | Renunciation — leaving wealth behind | Demanding inheritance — taking wealth with him |
| **Destination** | Spiritual liberation (Nibbāna) | A distant country → a pigsty (rock bottom) |
| **Ending** | Full enlightenment, return to teach | Humbled return, feast, reconciliation |
| **Father figure** | Suddhodana tries to keep him | The father welcomes him back |

Where the Prodigal Son's journey is a **return** to the father's house, Siddhartha's is an **irreversible departure** — he never returns to the palace as a prince. The Prodigal Son story is about forgiveness and restoration within a familial order; the Four Sights are about transcendence of all worldly orders.

### Parallel: The Cave Allegory

The Four Sights also echo Plato's **Allegory of the Cave** (Republic VII). The palace is the cave — a constructed world of shadows and illusions. Each sight is a step toward the sun: first the painful realization that the shadows are not real (old age, sickness, death), then the turning of the soul toward the source of light (the ascetic). Both stories present enlightenment as an initially painful but ultimately liberating process of seeing what is real.
