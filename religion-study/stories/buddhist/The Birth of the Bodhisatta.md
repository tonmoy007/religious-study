# The Birth of the Bodhisatta

> A radiant white elephant descending from Tusita — a dream that shook heaven and earth. The Bodhisatta's birth is one of the most lavishly told miracle-narratives in all of Buddhism, found in the Mahāpadāna Sutta (DN 14), the Acchariyabbhutadhamma Sutta (MN 123), and the Nidānakathā.

---

## The Prophecy

The story begins not in Lumbinī but in **Tusita Heaven**, the realm of delight where Bodhisattas reside before their final life. The future Buddha — then a _devaputta_ (celestial being) named **Setaketu** — surveyed the five portents of his impending departure: his divine garlands withered, his robes discoloured, sweat appeared in his armpits, his body grew dull, and his heavenly seat became uncomfortable.

The gods of the ten thousand world-systems begged him to be reborn. He chose **Jambudīpa** (India), the **Sākiya** clan, and **Queen Māyā** as his mother — a queen who had maintained perfect virtue for innumerable lifetimes.

> One full-moon night, Queen Māyā dreamed that a magnificent **white elephant** carrying a white lotus in its trunk descended from heaven, circled her three times, and entered her right side.

The king summoned sixty-four brahmins to interpret the dream. They declared: *"The queen will bear a son. If he remains in the palace, he will become a **Cakkavatti** (universal monarch). If he renounces the world, he will become a **Buddha**."*

The brahmins also identified the **32 marks of a Great Man** (Mahāpurisa-lakkhaṇā) on the infant's body in prophecy — a wheel on the soles of his feet, webbed fingers, a golden complexion, blue eyes, a tongue that could reach his forehead, and twenty-nine other signs that could only belong to a world-teacher or a world-ruler.

---

## The Birth at Lumbinī

As the time drew near, Queen Māyā wished to visit her family in Devadaha. On the way, she stopped at the **Lumbinī grove** — a sal forest in present-day Nepal, then ruled by the Sākiyan clan.

> The sal trees were in full blossom, bending their branches as if to bow. The queen reached up to grasp a branch — and as she stood, **the Bodhisatta emerged from her right side**, without pain, without breaking the birth canal.

According to the Acchariyabbhutadhamma Sutta (MN 123):

- Two streams of water — one cool, one warm — fell from the sky to bathe mother and child.
- The Bodhisatta was born **mindful and aware** — not as other infants are, smeared and helpless.
- He was born already **pure**, unstained by amniotic fluid, blood, or mucus — a figure of light.
- Four devas received him on a golden net.

Then the infant took **seven steps** to the north, a white parasol held above him, and in a voice that reached all worlds, declared:

> *"Aggo 'ham asmi lokassa — Supreme am I in the world.  
>  Jeṭṭho 'ham asmi lokassa — Eldest am I in the world.  
>  Seṭṭho 'ham asmi lokassa — Foremost am I in the world.  
>  Ayam antimā jāti — This is my last birth.  
>  Natthi dāni punabbhavo — Now there is no further becoming."*

The earth trembled, the ten thousand world-systems shook, and the gods sang.

---

## Asita's Visit

The hermit-sage **Asita** (also called Kāḷadevala) — the royal chaplain of King Suddhodana's father — saw the heavens rejoice and knew a Buddha had been born. He flew to Kapilavatthu.

The king presented the infant to Asita for a blessing. But when Asita saw the **32 marks**, he wept.

> *"Why do you weep, venerable sir? Is there danger for the child?"* — Suddhodana.

> *"No, great king. I weep because I am old — I will not live to see him become a Buddha. I will not hear his Dharma. That is my sorrow."*

Asita bowed at the infant's feet. The Buddha himself later remembered this moment — the first human to honour him before his enlightenment.

---

## The Naming Ceremony

On the fifth day, the brahmins held the naming ceremony. Eight chaplains examined the marks. Seven raised two fingers — *"He could be a king or a Buddha"* — but **Koṇḍañña**, the youngest and wisest, raised one finger: *"This child will surely become a Buddha. He will see the truth, not the throne."*

They named him **Siddhattha** (Siddhārtha in Sanskrit) — *"one who achieves his aim"* or *"one who has accomplished his purpose."*

Koṇḍañña himself later became the first disciple — the very first of the Pañcavaggiyā to whom the Buddha would preach the **Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta** forty years later.

---

## The Death of Queen Māyā

> Queen Māyā died **seven days** after giving birth.

The texts explain that the womb that carries a Bodhisatta is like a temple — no other being can occupy it after the Bodhisatta leaves. Her life-span fulfilled, she was reborn in **Tusita Heaven** as a male deva, where she could hear her son's Dharma from the celestial realm.

The Buddha later ascended to Tāvatiṃsa Heaven for one rains-retreat — not to his mother Māyā, but to teach the Abhidhamma to the devas, including her. This is the only recorded meeting between the Buddha and his mother after her death.

## Miraculous Births Across Traditions

The birth of the Bodhisatta is a **nativity of light** — painless, pure, witnessed by gods — and it belongs to a global pattern of miraculous conception and birth stories:

| Tradition | Figure | Miraculous Element |
|-----------|--------|--------------------|
| **Buddhism** | Siddhartha | White elephant dream, birth from the side, seven steps |
| **Christianity** | Jesus | Virgin birth, angelic annunciation, star of Bethlehem |
| **Hinduism** | Krishna | Transported across the Yamuna at birth, divine signs |
| **Zoroastrianism** | Zarathustra | Laughed at birth, halo of light |
| **Daoism** | Laozi | Carried in the womb for 62 (or 81) years, born as a sage |
| **Jainism** | Mahāvīra | Transferred from a brahmin womb to a kṣatriya womb by the gods |

The **virgin birth parallel** between Māyā and Mary is the most striking: in both, the mother conceives without ordinary union (the white elephant is a dream-vision, not a physical act), the child is born in humble surroundings, and a prophecy of greatness is proclaimed by a holy visitor. Yet the Buddhist account is unique in its **explicit declaration of non-theistic agency** — the Bodhisatta chooses his own rebirth through his own accumulated merit, not through divine intervention.
