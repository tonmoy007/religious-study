# The Enlightenment Under the Bodhi Tree

> *"House-builder you are seen! You shall build no house again!"* — The Buddha, Udāna 1:3

---

## The Journey to Uruvelā

Siddhartha left his five ascetic companions — **Kondañña, Vappa, Bhaddiya, Mahānāma, and Assaji** — after realizing that self-mortification was not the path. They scorned him: *"The ascetic Gotama has become luxurious — he has turned to abundance."*

He walked alone to **Uruvelā** (modern Bodh Gayā), a forest grove beside the Nerañjarā River. The ground was level, the water clear — a place that **felt right**.

At the village of **Senāni**, a young woman named **Sujātā** offered him **milk-rice** (kīra) in a golden bowl, believing him to be a tree-spirit who had answered her prayers. Siddhartha ate the first proper meal he had taken in six years. He took the bowl and set it afloat on the river — if it floated upstream, he would achieve enlightenment that night. The bowl **drifted against the current** then sank to the abode of VRama.

That evening, beneath a great **peepul tree** (later the Bodhi Tree), Siddhartha sat down on a seat of kusa grass spread by the grass-cutter **Sotthiya**. He made a solemn resolve:

> *"Let my skin and sinews dry up, let my flesh waste away — I will not leave this seat until I have attained supreme awakening."*

---

## Māra's Assault

**Māra** — the Evil One, the Lord of the Senses, Death personified — saw Siddhartha's resolve and trembled. His throne in the highest heaven of desire grew hot. He mounted his elephant **Girimekhala** and led his armies against the seated ascetic.

> *"Māra's army: craving, aversion, hunger, thirst, desire, sloth, fear, doubt, hypocrisy, delusion, gain, fame, and self-praise."* — Sutta Nipāta 3:2

Māra attacked with **hurricanes, floods, blazing rocks, and showers of weapons**. He sent his three daughters — **Taṇhā** (Craving), **Aratī** (Aversion), and **Rāgā** (Passion) — to seduce him.

But Siddhartha sat motionless. The hail of weapons turned to **flower petals**. The daughters of Māra withered before his compassion. Māra challenged him: *"Who will bear witness that you have earned this seat?"*

Siddhartha reached down with his right hand and touched the earth: **bhūmisparśa mudrā** — the earth-touching gesture.

> *"O Earth, bear witness — in how many lifetimes have I given my life for awakening?"*

The earth **roared**: *"I bear witness!"* — a hundred thousand times. Māra fled. His elephant knelt.

---

## The Three Watches of the Night

As the night unfolded, Siddhartha's mind, purified by years of practice, entered deeper and deeper states of meditative absorption (*jhāna*).

### First Watch (Evening — Past Lives)

He directed his mind to the **knowledge of past lives** (*pubbenivāsānussati-ñāṇa*). He saw thousands upon thousands of his own births — names, appearances, joys, sufferings, deaths — stretching back eons. He saw the **whole chain**: how he was born here, died there, was reborn, died again. The entire **saṃsāra** — beginningless and relentless.

### Second Watch (Midnight — Passing Away and Rebirth)

He directed his mind to the **knowledge of the passing away and rebirth of all beings** (*cutūpapāta-ñāṇa*). With the **divine eye** (*dibba-cakkhu*), he saw beings dying and being reborn according to their karma — the noble born in misery, the cruel reborn in hells, the generous reborn in happiness. He understood: **karma is the architect of destiny**.

> *"These beings — their conduct of body, speech, and mind — lead them to happy or miserable destinations."*

### Third Watch (Dawn — the Destruction of the Taints)

The deepest knowledge arose. He saw **Dependent Origination** (*paṭicca-samuppāda*):

> *"When this exists, that comes to be. With the cessation of this, that ceases."*

He traced suffering to its root: **ignorance** → formations → consciousness → name-and-form → the six sense bases → contact → feeling → craving → clinging → becoming → birth → **aging and death**.

And the path to its cessation: **the Four Noble Truths**:

1. **Dukkha** — suffering: birth, aging, death, separation from what we love, union with what we hate, the five aggregates
2. **Samudaya** — the origin of suffering: craving (*taṇhā*)
3. **Nirodha** — the cessation of suffering: the complete fading away of craving
4. **Magga** — the path: the Noble Eightfold Path (right view, intention, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, concentration)

As the **morning star** rose, Siddhartha's mind was completely liberated. He became the **Buddha** — the Awakened One.

## The Morning After

The Buddha sat in silence. Then he spoke this exultation — the first words of the new era:

> *"Through many rounds of birth I have run — seeking the house-builder — not finding. Painful is repeated birth. House-builder, you are seen! You will not build a house again. All your rafters are broken, your ridgepole is shattered. My mind has attained the unconditioned. The end of craving has been reached."* — Dhammapada 153–154

## The Seven Weeks After Enlightenment

For seven weeks, the Buddha remained near the Bodhi Tree, experiencing the bliss of liberation:

1. **Week 1** — Beneath the **Bodhi Tree** itself, experiencing the taste of freedom
2. **Week 2** — At the **Animisa Cetiya** (Unblinking Shrine), gazing at the tree in gratitude — never blinking
3. **Week 3** — Walking the **Ratanacaṅkama** (Jewel Walk), a promenade where flowers bloomed under each step
4. **Week 4** — At the **Ratnāgāra** (Jewel House) in the northwest, contemplating the **Abhidhamma** (higher teaching)
5. **Week 5** — Under the **Ajapāla banyan tree**, where a haughty **brahmin** asked what makes a brahmin. The Buddha answered: *"Not by matted hair, nor by birth — but by truth and Dhamma."* Here, Māra returned: *"Why not enter Parinibbāna now?"* The Buddha refused: *"Not until my teaching is established."*
6. **Week 6** — Under the **Mucalinda tree**, where a great serpent-king, **Mucalinda**, coiled seven times around the Buddha's body and spread his hood over him during a week of rains — protection for the unprotected one
7. **Week 7** — Under the **Rājāyatana tree**, where two merchants, **Tapussa and Bhallika**, offered the first meal to the Buddha and became the **first lay disciples**, taking refuge in the Buddha and the Dhamma (the Saṅgha did not yet exist)

After the seventh week, the Buddha considered: *"This Dhamma is deep, subtle, hard to see. The world delights in pleasure — they will not understand it."* Brahmā **Sahampati** appeared and begged him: *"Let the Dhamma be taught. There are those with little dust in their eyes."*

> *"The doors of the deathless are open — let those with ears release their faith."*

The Buddha looked out with the Buddha-eye and saw beings of various capacities — like lotuses in a pond: some deep under water, some touching the surface, some standing above, untouched by mud. He agreed to teach.

## Comparison

| Parallel | Figure | The Pattern |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **Resurrection** | Jesus Christ | Three days of death; three watches of the night; victory over death/ignorance |
| **Sinai Theophany** | Moses | Encounter with the divine on a mountain; the giving of the Law; forty days of preparation |
| **Night of Power** | Muhammad | Solitary vigil in the Cave of Hirā; the first revelation; the angel's command "Recite!" |
| **The Great Awakening** | Mahāvīra | Twelve years of asceticism; enlightenment under a Sāla tree; victory over the god of obstacles |

Each story describes a **threshold event** — a moment when a single human being breaks through the ordinary limits of consciousness. Siddhartha's awakening differs in its **systematic analysis**: the Four Noble Truths are not a revelation from a god but a diagnosis — a doctor's prescription for the disease of suffering.
