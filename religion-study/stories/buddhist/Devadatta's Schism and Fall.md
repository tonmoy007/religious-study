# Devadatta's Schism and Fall

> "Devadatta was a man whose psychic powers could shake Mount Meru — but whose envy burned hotter than any fire. He tried to kill the Buddha five times, split the Saṅgha, and was swallowed alive into Avīci hell." — Cullavagga (Vinaya VII)

---

## The Rise of Devadatta

Devadatta was the Buddha's cousin — born on the same day as Siddhattha, raised in the Sakyan court. After the Buddha's enlightenment, Devadatta ordained and, through diligent practice, **developed supernatural powers (*iddhi*)** — he could fly, walk on water, and manifest illusions.

But Devadatta craved **status, not liberation**. When he failed to gain the same reverence as the Buddha, his virtue curdled into ambition.

His opportunity came with **Prince Ajātasattu**, son of King Bimbisāra. Devadatta demonstrated his psychic powers to the prince — appearing as a boy with a snake girdle, then as a giant. Ajātasattu was awestruck and lavished Devadatta with wealth, building him a monastery and granting him an entourage of 500 attendants. Devadatta became "the Buddha of Gayāsīsa" in his own mind — a rival teacher with royal patronage.

---

## The Five Demands

Emboldened, Devadatta approached the Buddha with **five demands** for stricter monastic rules:

1. Monks must live **only in the forest** — never in towns
2. Monks must **live only on alms-food** — never accept invitations
3. Monks must **wear only rag-robes** — never accept cloth from laypeople
4. Monks must **dwell at the foot of trees** — never under roofs
5. Monks must **never eat fish or meat** — even if offered

The Buddha **refused** each one. He explained that monks could choose forest dwelling or alms-food if they wished — but making them mandatory would discourage those who needed the Saṅgha's presence in the world. The Middle Way rejects extremes of austerity as much as indulgence.

Devadatta then **publicly declared** the five rules to an assembly, claiming he was purer than the Buddha himself. The Buddha warned him: "Devadatta, do not create a schism — it is a grave offense."

---

## The Assassination Attempts

Devadatta resolved to eliminate the Buddha entirely. He made **five attempts**:

1. **Archers** — He hired assassins to kill the Buddha, but each approached, heard the Buddha's teaching, and became arahants instead
2. **The Boulder** — He climbed Mount Vulture Peak and **rolled a massive boulder** onto the Buddha. The rock split, grazing the Buddha's foot — his first and only recorded wound
3. **The Nālāgiri Elephant** — He unleashed the **fierce, man-killing elephant Nālāgiri** on the Buddha's alms-round. The beast charged — but the Buddha suffused it with *mettā* (loving-kindness). The elephant stopped, knelt, and wept at his feet

Two further attempts — sending armed men and setting ambushes — also failed. Each time, the Buddha's compassion and calm dissolved the attack.

---

## The Schism

When assassination failed, Devadatta attempted the **unforgivable** — a formal schism (*saṅghabheda*). He took **500 newly-ordained monks** from Vesālī and **led them to Gayāsīsa**, declaring himself the true Buddha.

The Buddha sent **Sāriputta and Moggallāna** — his two chief disciples — to bring them back. When they arrived, Devadatta prepared a grand sermon, drunk with pride. Moggallāna used his psychic powers to produce a dazzling display, as if the Buddha himself had come. The monks were awe-struck.

Then **Sāriputta spoke** — not of Devadatta, but of the true Dhamma. With his peerless teaching, he re-established the monks' understanding. Every one of the 500 returned to the Buddha's Saṅgha that very night.

Devadatta was left alone at Gayāsīsa — betrayed by his own ambition.

---

## The Earth Swallows Devadatta

The Vinaya records Devadatta's end in visceral terms. As he journeyed to see the Buddha one last time — perhaps seeking forgiveness — his body was **swallowed by the earth while still alive**. He fell into **Avīci**, the lowest and most terrible hell — "the hell without interval" — where beings burn in endless torment for the heaviest of karmic crimes.

Yet the tradition leaves a crack of light: Devadatta is prophesied to be a **Paccekabuddha** (Silent Buddha) in a future life, after eons of purifying his karma. Even the schismatic is not damned forever.

## Comparison with Other Traditions

| Figure | Tradition | Crime | Fate |
|--------|-----------|-------|------|
| **Devadatta** | Buddhism | Schism, attempted murder of the Buddha | Swallowed alive, Avīci hell → future Paccekabuddha |
| **Judas Iscariot** | Christianity | Betrayal of Jesus for 30 pieces of silver | Hanged himself (Matthew), field of blood (Acts) |
| **Munāfiqūn (Hypocrites)** | Islam (Qur'an 63) | Inward rejection of faith while outwardly professing it | "Lowest depths of the Fire" — eternal punishment |

All three figures represent the **betrayer-from-within** archetype — the intimate enemy who is closest to the founder, knows the teaching best, and yet turns against it. Devadatta's story stands apart in one crucial way: his motive was **not greed or doubt, but ambition and envy** — he believed he deserved to lead.

Additionally, Devadatta's error is karmic, not moral-theological. He is punished not by divine judgment but by the **natural law of cause and effect** (*kamma*). And unlike Judas or the Munāfiq, Buddhism holds out the possibility of his eventual liberation.
