# The First Buddhist Council

> Three months after the Buddha's Parinirvāṇa, 500 arahants gathered at the Sattapaṇṇī Cave in Rājagaha to recite and codify his teachings. This Council gave birth to the Pāli Canon — the oldest surviving record of the Buddha's words.

---

## The Subhadda Incident

When the Buddha passed into Parinirvāṇa at Kusinārā, most monks grieved — but one old monk named **Subhadda** rejoiced:

> "Enough, friends! Do not grieve. We are well rid of that Great Ascetic. He tormented us: 'This is fitting, this is not fitting.' Now we can do as we please."

Mahākassapa, returning from his journey, heard these words and was horrified. The Dhamma-Vinaya — the Buddha's legacy — was already at risk of distortion. If Subhadda could speak so brazenly within hours of the Buddha's death, what would happen when the generation of direct disciples passed away? The Teachings had to be preserved — exactly as spoken.

## Mahākassapa's Decision

Mahākassapa proposed a **council of 500 arahants** — all who had attained final liberation — to compile an authoritative recitation of the Buddha's entire doctrine. King **Ajātasattu** of Magadha offered patronage and sponsored the assembly at the **Sattapaṇṇī Cave** (the Cave of the Seven Leaves) on the slopes of Vebhāra Mountain near Rājagaha.

The criteria were strict: only arahants who had been personally present at the Buddha's Parinirvāṇa, who had eaten the meal offered by Cunda before it, and who were fully liberated. Of the 500, one key figure was missing.

## The Exclusion of Ānanda

**Ānanda** — the Buddha's cousin, attendant, and the monk who had heard more discourses than any other — was **not yet an arahant**. Though he had memorised tens of thousands of the Buddha's suttas, he still harboured attachment and had not realised final liberation.

Mahākassapa told him to leave. The assembly of arahants could not include one who was not fully enlightened.

Ānanda was devastated but not defeated. He spent the night in **intense striving** — walking meditation, then lying down at dawn, his head just touching the pillow — when his mind at last released all clinging. He **attained arahantship** in the very moment between waking and sleep.

The next morning, he walked back to the cave. When he arrived, he was no longer a devoted attendant but an arahant — equal to the 500. The gates opened.

> "Then I thought: 'It is not yet dawn — let me practice.' And I sat down. And as I practised, the earth quaked." — Ānanda, Udāna 8.10 (paraphr.)

## Upāli Recites the Vinaya

The council first turned to the **Vinaya** — the monastic code of 227+ rules governing the Saṅgha's conduct. **Upāli**, whom the Buddha had declared foremost in knowing the Vinaya, was called upon first (before Ānanda — the rules of discipline carried higher priority).

Mahākassapa questioned Upāli on **every single rule**:

- "Where was this rule laid down?"
- "What was the occasion?"
- "Who committed the offence?"
- "What is the offence and the penalty?"

Upāli answered each question from perfect memory. The assembly affirmed his recitation. This became the **Vinaya Piṭaka** (the Basket of Discipline).

## Ānanda Recites the Suttas

With the Vinaya secured, Mahākassapa turned to Ānanda. The Buddha had praised him: "The most learned of monks, Ānanda." Now he would prove it.

Ānanda sat facing the assembly and began each discourse with the phrase that would echo across millennia:

> **Evaṃ me sutaṃ** — "Thus have I heard."

He placed each sutta in context: the occasion, the location, the audience. He recited with such clarity and completeness that the 500 arahants confirmed every word.

> "At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Sāvatthī in Jeta's Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika's Park..."
> — opening of countless suttas

This became the **Sutta Piṭaka** (the Basket of Discourses) — the heart of Buddhist scripture.

## The Closing

The council sat for **seven months**. When the final word was spoken:

- **Vinaya Piṭaka** — the monastic discipline
- **Sutta Piṭaka** — the discourses
- **(Abhidhamma Piṭaka would come later at the Third Council)**

Mahākassapa spoke the closing words:

> "Let the Teaching shine like the full moon in a cloudless sky. Let those who follow it preserve it without addition, without subtraction."

The **Pāli Canon** (Tipiṭaka / Tripiṭaka) was born — transmitted orally for four centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka (c. 29–17 BCE).

## Comparison with Other Traditions

| Parallel | Tradition | What Happened |
|----------|-----------|---------------|
| **Council of Nicaea (325 CE)** | Christianity | Emperor Constantine convened bishops to define orthodox doctrine (the Nicene Creed) — settling disputes about Christ's divinity. Like the First Council, it aimed to fix an authoritative canon against divergent interpretations. Unlike the Buddhist council, it was an imperial, political assembly with theological debates, not a recitation from memory. |
| **Qur'an compilation (632–650 CE)** | Islam | After Muhammad's death, Abu Bakr ordered Zayd ibn Thabit to compile the Quran from scattered palm-leaf, bone, and memory sources — fearing loss of revelation as the Prophet's companions died in battle. Uthman later standardised the text and burned variant copies. The parallel is striking: both councils responded to a crisis triggered by the founder's death, both relied on companions with perfect memory, and both produced the foundational canon. |
