# The Buddha's Parinirvāṇa

> The Mahāparinibbāna Sutta (DN 16) recounts the Buddha's last days — his final journey, last meal, illness, final teachings, and passing into final Nibbāna. The most detailed account of a teacher's passing in all Buddhist scripture.

---

## The Final Journey

At 80 years old, the Buddha set out from **Rājagaha** with a large company of monks, heading north toward **Vesālī**. He stopped at **Pāṭaliputta** (modern Patna), where he prophesied the city's future greatness as a trading hub. He crossed the Ganges, passed through **Vesālī** — where he finally renounced his will to live, telling Ānanda that he would pass away in three months — and continued to the village of **Kusinārā**, a small and insignificant town of the Malla people.

---

## Cunda's Offering

At **Pāvā**, the Buddha stopped at the home of **Cunda the smith**. Cunda prepared a meal with great devotion, offering **sūkaramaddava** — a dish whose meaning has been debated for centuries:

- **"Tender pork"** — the traditional reading; Cunda was a smith, and pork was a common meal
- **"Truffles" or "pig's delight"** — a vegetable delicacy pigs dig up; some modern scholars argue the Buddha would not have eaten meat later in his life
- **"A mushroom dish"** — another vegetarian interpretation

The Buddha instructed Cunda to serve the sūkaramaddava **only to him**, warning the monks not to partake. After eating, he told Cunda to bury the leftovers — a sign he knew the meal was spoiled or fatal. He then comforted Cunda, saying the merit of this final offering equalled that of the Buddha's first meal after enlightenment.

---

## The Illness

Shortly after the meal, the Buddha was struck by a violent illness — **severe dysentery** with sharp, life-threatening pain. Yet he **bore it mindfully**, without complaint. He continued travelling despite the agony, pausing only to rest and drink water.

> "I am now old, Ānanda — my body is like a worn-out cart, held together by straps." — DN 16

He instructed Ānanda to tell Cunda that no blame attached to him — the offering should be seen as a great act of merit, not a cause of death.

---

## The Last Ānanda Dialogues

**Ānanda** — the Buddha's devoted attendant for 25 years — broke down in tears. He stepped outside, weeping: "I am still only a learner — my Teacher is about to pass away, he who was so compassionate to me!"

The Buddha called him back and **consoled** him:

> "Enough, Ānanda — do not grieve. Have I not taught you that separation from all that is dear is inevitable? For a long time you have served me with love — with kind thought, kind word, kind deed. You have done well, Ānanda. You will soon be **liberated**."

When Ānanda asked what would become of the Saṅgha after the Buddha's death, the Buddha gave a definitive answer:

> "The Dhamma and the Vinaya — **these are your teacher from now on**."

The Buddha also praised **Sāriputta** and **Moggallāna** (who had predeceased him), and announced that the Saṅgha was not dependent on a single leader — each monk should be an island unto himself, with the Dhamma as his island.

---

## The Last Teaching

A wandering ascetic named **Subhadda** came seeking the Buddha. Ānanda tried to turn him away — "The Teacher is tired" — but the Buddha overheard and allowed him in.

Subhadda asked: "Do all the other teachers — the Jains, the Brahmins, the other sectarians — possess the truth?"

The Buddha answered directly:

> "In whatever Dhamma-Vinaya the Noble Eightfold Path is not found — there is no true ascetic. **Only here** is the path found — only here is true liberation."

Subhadda **immediately requested ordination** — the last direct disciple of the Buddha. He became an Arahant on the spot.

Then came the **final words**:

> **"Vaya-dhammā saṅkhārā — appamādena sampādetha."**
>
> *"All conditioned things are subject to decay — **strive on with diligence**."*

---

## The Passing

The Buddha lay down on his right side between two **sal trees** in the **Upavattana grove** of Kusinārā. Though it was out of season, the two trees **bloomed**, showering the Tathāgata with flowers as a spontaneous homage. A **great earthquake** shook the world — the eighth and greatest of the earthquakes during the Buddha's life-time. The gods of the ten thousand world-systems gathered.

The Buddha then entered the **four jhānas** in reverse and forward order, rising through the meditative absorptions:

1. First jhāna → Second → Third → Fourth
2. Then into the sphere of infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, neither-perception-nor-non-perception
3. Then the cessation of perception and feeling
4. Then back down through the jhānas to the **fourth jhāna**

From the fourth jhāna, he **passed away** into **Parinirvāṇa** — final, complete Nibbāna without remainder.

---

## The Cremation

The **Mallas of Kusinārā** honoured the Buddha's body for seven days, then carried it to the cremation grounds. When they tried to light the pyre, it would not ignite — until **Mahākassapa**, the senior monk, arrived from afar with a large following and paid his respects. Then the pyre **ignited spontaneously**.

The flames consumed the body, leaving only the **relics** (sarīra). The Mallas tried to keep them, but a war nearly broke out between seven kingdoms demanding a share. A **Brahmin named Dona** mediated, dividing the relics into **eight portions**:

- **Magadha** (Ajātasattu) → stored in Rājagaha
- The **Licchavis of Vesālī**
- The **Sakyas of Kapilavatthu**
- The **Bulayas**
- The **Koliyas**
- The **Mallas of Pāvā**
- The **Mallas of Kusinārā**
- A **Brahmin of Vethadīpa**

A ninth portion — the **urn** — was given to the **Moriyas later**. The **emperor Asoka** (3rd century BCE) later opened seven of the eight relic mounds and enshrined them in 84,000 stūpas across India. Today, portions of the Buddha's relics are enshrined in stūpas worldwide.

## Comparison with Other Traditions

| Tradition | Figure | Nature of Death | Parallel |
|-----------|--------|----------------|----------|
| **Islam** | Muhammad (632 CE) | **Peaceful**, in the lap of his wife Aisha, after a final sermon at the Farewell Pilgrimage | Both teachers delivered farewell teachings and died with loved ones present |
| **Philosophy** | Socrates (399 BCE) | **Dialogue until the end** — the Phaedo records his final discussion on the immortality of the soul, then he drinks hemlock calmly | Both faced death with serenity and used the last hours to teach |
| **Christianity** | Jesus (c. 30 CE) | **Violent** — crucified with a cry of abandonment ("Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?") | The Buddha's peaceful passing contrasts sharply — one dies in full equanimity, the other in anguish; yet both deaths became the foundation of their respective traditions |

The Buddha's death is unique in that he **chose** the time — having renounced his will to live three months earlier — and passed away in full meditative control, demonstrating the very path he taught.
