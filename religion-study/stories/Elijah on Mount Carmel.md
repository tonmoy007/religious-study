# Elijah on Mount Carmel

> The prophet versus 450 prophets of Baal — fire from heaven, a drought broken, and the most dramatic showdown in the Old Testament.

---

## The Story (1 Kings 18)

King **Ahab** and Queen **Jezebel** lead Israel into Baal worship. The prophet **Elijah** announces a three-year drought as judgment. At God's command, Elijah challenges the 450 prophets of Baal (and 400 prophets of Asherah) to a contest on **Mount Carmel**.

**The rules:**
- Two altars, two bulls
- No fire
- The God who answers by fire — He is God

The prophets of Baal prepare their sacrifice and call on Baal from morning to noon — *"O Baal, answer us!"* — but there is no answer. They leap around the altar, cut themselves with swords, and prophesy wildly. Nothing.

Elijah mocks them: *"Cry aloud — perhaps he is asleep and must be awakened!"* (18:27).

Then Elijah repairs the altar of the Lord with 12 stones, digs a trench around it, lays the bull on the wood, and drenches everything with water three times — filling the trench.

He prays: *"O Lord, God of Abraham, Isaac, and Israel — let it be known that you are God in Israel!"* (18:36). **Fire falls from heaven** — consuming the sacrifice, the wood, the stones, the dust, and even the water in the trench.

The people fall on their faces: *"The Lord, He is God!"* Elijah executes the prophets of Baal. The drought breaks — rain comes.

---

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Monotheism vs. syncretism** | The choice between YHWH and Baal — Israel's defining struggle |
| **Prophetic authority** | Elijah stands alone against king, queen, and 450 prophets |
| **Fire from heaven** | Divine authentication of the true prophet |
| **The still small voice** | After the fire — God speaks in silence (1 Kings 19:11–13) |

---

## Comparison with Islamic Tradition

Elijah (*Ilyās*) is mentioned briefly in the Qur'an (37:123–132, 6:85) as one of the prophets — "Peace upon Ilyās!" (37:130). The Qur'an does not narrate the Mount Carmel story. The contest with Baal is referenced in passing: "Indeed, Ilyās was among the messengers. When he said to his people: 'Will you not fear Allah? Do you invoke **Ba'l** and leave the best of creators?'" (37:123–125).
