# Krishna's Birth and Childhood

> The divine child born in a prison who became the cowherd of Vrindavan — a story of miracles, love, and the slaying of the tyrant Kamsa.

---

## The Story (Bhagavata Purana, Harivamsa)

### The Prophecy

**Kamsa** — king of Mathura, a tyrant — receives a prophecy: the eighth child of his sister **Devaki** will kill him. He imprisons Devaki and her husband **Vasudeva** and kills their first six children.

The seventh child — **Balarama** — is miraculously transferred to the womb of **Rohini** (Vasudeva's other wife). The eighth child arrives.

### The Birth

At midnight, in the darkness of the prison, **Krishna** is born — dark-skinned, holding a conch and discus. The prison cell fills with divine light. The doors open of their own accord.

Vasudeva carries the infant across the **Yamuna River** — the river parts for him. He exchanges Krishna with the newborn daughter of **Nanda** and **Yashoda** in Gokul. He returns to prison with the baby girl. The prison doors close. The guards wake. Kamsa arrives — the baby girl slips from his hands and ascends to heaven: "Your killer is already born — elsewhere."

### Life in Vrindavan

Krishna grows up as a cowherd in **Vrindavan**:

| Event | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Putana's death** | A demoness feeds Krishna poisoned milk — he sucks her life away |
| **The cart overturned** | A demon cart-crash — Krishna kicks it over |
| **Trinavarta** | A whirlwind demon — Krishna drags him down |
| **Govardhan Hill** | Krishna lifts the entire mountain on his little finger to shelter the cowherds from Indra's rain |
| **Kaliya's defeat** | Krishna dances on the serpent Kaliya in the Yamuna — subduing the poison |
| **The butter thief** | Krishna steals butter — the gopis complain, but Yashoda cannot stay angry |
| **Rasa Lila** | Krishna dances with the cowherd girls — the divine love play |

### The Slaying of Kamsa

Krishna and Balarama go to Mathura for a wrestling match. Krishna wrestles and kills Kamsa's champions. He leaps into the royal dais and **kills Kamsa** — fulfilling the prophecy.

---

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Leela (divine play)** | Krishna's childhood is not incidental — it is the god at play |
| **Govardhan** | Krishna sets aside Vedic sacrifice for *bhakti* — devotion over ritual |
| **The butter thief** | The god who steals hearts — even when caught, he is irresistible |
| **Kamsa's fate** | The tyrant who cannot escape destiny — his fear brings it about |

---

## Comparison with Other Traditions

Krishna's birth narrative — a tyrant kills children to prevent a prophecy, the divine child is hidden — parallels:
- **Moses**: Pharaoh drowns Hebrew infants (Exodus 1)
- **Jesus**: Herod slaughters the innocents (Matthew 2)
- **Zarathustra**: A tyrant tries to kill the prophet as a baby

The motif of the **hiding of the divine child** from a threatened ruler is a cross-cultural archetype.
