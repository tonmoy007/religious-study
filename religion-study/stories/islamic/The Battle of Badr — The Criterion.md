# The Battle of Badr — The Criterion

> 17 Ramadan, 2 AH · 13 March 624 CE · Near Medina (at the wells of Badr)

The first major battle in Islamic history — 313 poorly armed Muslims against 1,000 elite Quraysh soldiers. The Muslims won decisively. The Qur'an calls the day of Badr **Yawm al-Furqān** — the Day of the Criterion — because it separated truth from falsehood once and for all.

---

## The Background

After the Hijrah to Medina (622 CE), the Muslims had established a community but faced constant economic pressure from Mecca. The Quraysh had confiscated the property of the emigrant Muslims. The Muslims began intercepting Quraysh trade caravans as a pressure tactic.

In March 624 CE, a massive caravan led by **Abu Sufyan** was returning from Syria, laden with goods. The Prophet mobilized about 313 men to intercept it.

Abu Sufyan learned of the plan and sent a messenger to Mecca. The Quraysh raised an army of 1,000 men — their best soldiers, armed and armoured — to protect the caravan.

When Abu Sufyan escaped with the caravan via the coastal route, the Prophet could have turned back. But he asked his companions:

> *"What is your opinion?"*

The Muhajirun (emigrants) spoke, but the Prophet waited. Then Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, leader of the Ansar of Medina, said:

> *"Perhaps you are waiting for us, the Ansar? We believe in you. We affirm that what you have brought is truth. We pledge to hear and obey. Go where you wish, stay where you wish. By Allah, if you took us into the sea, we would follow you."*

The Prophet smiled. The army advanced to Badr.

---

## The Battle

### The Night

The Muslims reached the wells of Badr first. They dug a reservoir and filled it with water. The Prophet spent the night in prayer under a makeshift shelter.

> *"When you appealed to your Lord for help — He answered you: I will reinforce you with a thousand angels, rank upon rank."* — **8:9**

### The Duels

The Arabs began battles with single combats — champions duelling before the main clash. **Utbah ibn Rabi'ah**, his son **al-Walid**, and his brother **Shaybah** stepped forward from the Quraysh ranks. Three Ansar came out to meet them. The Quraysh called: "We want our own tribe!"

The Prophet sent **[[Hamzah ibn Abd al-Muttalib|Hamzah]]** (his uncle), **[[Ali ibn Abi Talib|Ali]]** (his cousin), and **Ubaydah ibn al-Harith**. In the duels:
- Hamzah killed Utbah
- Ali killed al-Walid  
- Ubaydah was wounded by Shaybah but the Muslims finished him

All three Quraysh champions were dead.

### The Charge

The Prophet gave the order. He took a handful of pebbles and threw them at the Quraysh army:

> *"May their faces be disgraced!"*

He commanded: *"Attack!"*

The Muslims charged. Allah sent angels to fight alongside them. The Quraysh were routed. **70 Quraysh were killed** and **70 captured**; only **14 Muslims died**.

### The Famous Dead

Among the Quraysh dead: **Abu Jahl** (the Prophet's arch-enemy, killed by two youths), **Utbah, Shaybah, al-Walid, Umayyah ibn Khalaf** (who had tortured Bilal), and **al-As ibn Hisham**. Abu Lahab did not participate but died of grief seven days after hearing the news.

### The Prisoners

The Prophet consulted his companions about the prisoners. **[[Abu Bakr al-Siddiq|Abu Bakr]]** said: "They are our cousins — take ransom and strengthen the Muslims." **[[Umar ibn al-Khattab|Umar]]** said: "Kill them — these are the leaders of disbelief."

The Prophet followed Abu Bakr's counsel. The ransom was set at 4,000 dirhams per prisoner, or the teaching of 10 Muslim children to read and write. This decision was later questioned in revelation:

> *"It is not for a prophet to have captives until he has bloodied the land."* — **8:67**

But the prisoners proved valuable: those who taught reading wrote the first written copies of the Qur'an.

## Key Tafsir Insights

**Yawm al-Furqān:** The Qur'an calls Badr the "Day of the Criterion" (8:41). Before Badr, Muslims were a persecuted minority; after Badr, the Quraysh never recovered their prestige, and Islam was a power to be reckoned with.

**The angels at Badr:** 8:9–17 describes angels fighting alongside the Muslims. Classical exegetes: the angels had green turbans, white turbans, or yellow — their presence was both real and symbolic. The angels served as morale reinforcement and divine confirmation.

**Why the Muslims won:**
- Strategic advantage (control of the water)
- High morale (fighting for faith)
- Quraysh overconfidence
- Divine intervention (angels, sleep cast on the enemy, sand in their eyes — 8:11, 8:17, 8:43)

**The paradox of prisoners:** The revelation 8:67 slightly rebuked the Prophet for taking ransom — suggesting capital enemy leaders should have been executed. But the ransom decision also had long-term benefits, demonstrating mercy and gaining goodwill.
