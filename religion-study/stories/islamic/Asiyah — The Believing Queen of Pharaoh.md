# Asiyah — The Believing Queen of Pharaoh

> Mentioned in Surah [[028 Al-Qasas]] · Verses 7–13, and Surah [[066 At-Tahrim]] · Verses 10–12 · Meccan & Medinan

The story of Asiyah bint Muzahim — Pharaoh's wife who defied the most powerful tyrant of her age, adopted the infant Moses, and died as a believer. The Qur'an holds her up as one of the four perfect women of Paradise, alongside Mary, Khadijah, and Fatimah.

---

## The Story

### The Adoption

When Pharaoh ordered the slaughter of the male infants of the Israelites, Moses' mother placed him in a basket and cast him into the Nile. The basket floated to the palace of Pharaoh.

> *"And the family of Pharaoh picked him up — so that he might become for them an enemy and a cause of grief. Indeed, Pharaoh, Haman, and their hosts were sinners."* — **28:8**

When the basket was opened, **[[Asiyah bint Muzahim|Asiyah]]**, the wife of Pharaoh, saw the baby. Pharaoh ordered the child killed, but Asiyah intervened:

> *"He is a comfort to the eye for me and for you. Do not kill him — perhaps he may benefit us, or we may adopt him as a son."* — **28:9**

Pharaoh relented — the tyrant was tempered by his wife's compassion. Asiyah took the child into her arms.

### The Nurse

The baby Moses refused all wet-nurses. Asiyah and Pharaoh watched helplessly as the infant rejected every breast. Then Moses' sister, Miriam, followed the basket and suggested:

> *"Shall I direct you to a family who will nurse him for you and care for him?"* — **28:12**

Thus Moses was returned to his mother — she nursed him in the palace as a paid servant, unaware that the women of the palace knew she was the true mother. The Qur'an says:

> *"Thus We returned him to his mother that her eye might be comforted and that she might not grieve."* — **28:13**

### The Believer in the Palace

Asiyah's faith grew in secret. She had seen the miracle of Moses being saved from the Nile and knew the child was no ordinary infant. As Moses grew, she believed in the God he would one day preach — the God of Abraham.

### The Martyrdom

The hadith and tafsir traditions record Asiyah's final test. When Pharaoh discovered that his own wife had believed in Moses' Lord, he subjected her to a brutal torture — tying her to four stakes and ordering a heavy millstone to be dropped on her.

As Asiyah was being tortured, the angels shaded her, and she was shown her house in Paradise. She prayed:

> *"My Lord, build for me near You a house in Paradise, and save me from Pharaoh and his deeds, and save me from the wrongdoing people."* — **66:11**

The Qur'an presents her as the model of the believer trapped in a household of disbelief:

> *"Allah presents an example for those who believe: the wife of Pharaoh, when she said: My Lord, build for me near You a house in Paradise…"* — **66:11**

## Key Tafsir Insights

**Asiyah bint Muzahim:** Her full name. She was the daughter of Muzahim, an Egyptian noble. In some traditions, she was a relative of the Pharaoh who ruled during the Exodus (not just his queen).

**The four perfect women:** The Prophet (ﷺ) said:
> *"The best women of Paradise are Khadijah bint Khuwaylid, Fatimah bint Muhammad, Asiyah bint Muzahim, and Maryam bint Imran."* — **Ahmad 2663**

**Why Asiyah is exemplary:** She is the model of faith in an environment of total unbelief. Unlike Mary who was raised in piety, Asiyah had no believing community — she was alone in Pharaoh's palace, yet she held firm. The Qur'an pairs her with Mary as the two examples for believers (66:10–12).

**House in Paradise:** Her prayer "build for me near You a house in Paradise" is remarkable — she asked for a house *near God*, not simply in Paradise. It is the only prayer in the Qur'an where someone asks to be near God Himself.

**The lesson for the Prophet's household:** Surah 66 was revealed in the context of the Prophet's wives' dispute. The pairing of Asiyah (the believing wife of a disbeliever) with Mary, alongside the pairing of Noah's wife and Lot's wife as examples of disbelieving wives of believers — makes the point that salvation is individual, not inherited through family.
