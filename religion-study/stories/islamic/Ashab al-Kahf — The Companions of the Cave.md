# Ashab al-Kahf — The Companions of the Cave

> Surah [[018 Al-Kahf]] · Verses 9–26 · Meccan

The story of the young believers who fled persecution and slept in a cave for **309 years**, emerging to find a world transformed — one of the most famous narratives in the Qur'an, and the first of the four great stories of Surah Al-Kahf.

---

## The Story

### The Flight

A group of young men lived in a city of polytheists who worshipped idols. They believed in the One God, and publicly declared their faith:

> *"Our Lord is the Lord of the heavens and the earth. We will never call upon any god besides Him — if we did, we would have spoken an outrage."* — **18:14**

When their people threatened them with death if they did not return to idolatry, the young men withdrew. They said to one another:

> *"When you have withdrawn from them and from what they worship besides Allah, take refuge in the cave. Your Lord will unfold His mercy for you and will prepare for you a way out of your ordeal."* — **18:16**

They entered the cave, and Allah caused them to sleep.

### The Sleep

The Qur'an paints a vivid picture of their long sleep:

- They slept with their dog at the entrance, its paws stretched out
- The sun entered the cave from the right in the morning and passed to the left in the evening — the cave was situated so that the sun's path kept the air fresh without burning them
- To an outside observer, they appeared awake (their eyes were open, their bodies turned) but they were in a deep, unnatural sleep
- They were turned to the right and to the left to prevent their bodies from wasting away

> *"You would have thought them awake, though they were asleep. And We turned them to the right and to the left, while their dog stretched his forelegs at the entrance."* — **18:18**

### The Awakening

After 309 years (300 solar years = 309 lunar), they awoke. One of them went into the city with a silver coin to buy food, telling the others to be discreet. But when he entered the city, everything had changed:

- The city's people were now believers
- The coin was ancient currency — the shopkeeper was astonished
- News spread of the "man from the past"

The Qur'an does not specify how many of them there were — their number was disputed (three, five, or seven with their dog). The Qur'an says:

> *"Say: My Lord knows best their number — only a few know of them."* — **18:22**

### The Aftermath

The people of the city discovered the cave. Two factions argued over what to do with the bodies:
- Some wanted to build a building over the cave
- Others wanted to build a mosque over it

Eventually, they built a place of worship at the cave's entrance.

## Key Tafsir Insights

**Length of sleep:** 309 years — the Qur'an says *they remained in their cave for three hundred years, adding nine* (v.25). The "nine" is the difference between the solar and lunar calendar.

**Why this story?** The Prophet was challenged by the Quraysh to answer three questions from the People of the Book to prove his prophethood: (1) the Companions of the Cave, (2) Dhul-Qarnayn, and (3) the Spirit. The Qur'an answers all three. — [[Asbab al-Nuzul]]

**The dog:** The only dog mentioned in the Qur'an. Islamic tradition holds it was a yellow or spotted hunting dog that guarded the entrance. Unlike the rest of the cave, the dog is named in some traditions: *Qitmir*.

**Historical context:** The story is believed to be a Christian legend from Ephesus (the "Seven Sleepers of Ephesus"), dating to the Decian persecution (249–251 CE). The Qur'an retells it with its own theological purpose: proving the resurrection of the body.

**Number ambiguity:** "They will say: Three, the fourth their dog; and they will say: Five, the sixth their dog. Say: Only my Lord knows their number." (v.22) — the Qur'an deliberately refuses to satisfy idle curiosity, redirecting attention to the story's moral.
