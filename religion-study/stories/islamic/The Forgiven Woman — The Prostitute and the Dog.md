# The Forgiven Woman — The Prostitute and the Dog

> Sahih al-Bukhari 3467 · Sahih Muslim 2245

Perhaps the most beloved and frequently told hadith story — the woman who was forgiven by Allah for an act of mercy toward a thirsty dog. It is the Qur'anic principle of *the weight of a mustard seed of good* made concrete in a single, unforgettable story.

---

## The Story

> *"A prostitute of the Children of Israel saw a dog moving around a well on a hot day, panting from thirst. She took off her shoe, filled it with water, and gave the dog to drink. She was forgiven for that."*

The Prophet (ﷺ) narrated this story and then said:

> *"A woman entered Hell because of a cat — she tied it up, neither fed it nor let it loose to eat from the vermin of the earth."*

The two stories form a contrasting pair: a prostitute — who by the standards of her society was among the worst of sinners — entered Paradise because of mercy to a dumb animal. A wealthy woman — who had every opportunity to do good — entered Hell because of cruelty to a cat.

---

## Key Tafsir Insights

**"She was forgiven" (*ghufira lahā*):** The hadith does not say "her sins were erased" or "she was saved" — it simply says she was forgiven. The Arabic *ghufira* implies complete pardon. Her entire past was wiped clean by a single act of pure compassion.

**Why a prostitute?** The story breaks every social expectation. The most "unworthy" person imaginable — a woman engaged in the worst sin — becomes the example of divine mercy. The point: no one is beyond forgiveness. A single sincere act of compassion can outweigh a lifetime of sin.

**The contrast with the cat-owner:** The cat story is the inverse — a woman who had the means to do good (she owned a cat) was condemned for withholding basic mercy. Cruelty, not sin, is the real danger.

**The lesson about animals:** In Islamic ethics, kindness to animals is a path to Paradise and cruelty a path to Hell. The Prophet said: "There is reward in doing good to every living thing." (Bukhari, Muslim)

**How the story differs from the Qur'anic emphasis:** The Qur'an emphasizes *tawhid* (faith) and *shirk* (polytheism) as the ultimate divide. This hadith emphasizes that even without explicit tawhid, an act of pure mercy brings forgiveness — showing the universality of God's mercy.
