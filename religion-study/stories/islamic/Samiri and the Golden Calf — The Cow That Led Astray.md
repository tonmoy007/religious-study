# Samiri and the Golden Calf — The Cow That Led Astray

> Surah [[020 Taa-Haa]] · Verses 83–98 · Meccan

The Qur'an uniquely names a figure — **Samiri** — as the maker of the golden calf. Unlike the biblical narrative which places the blame on Aaron, the Qur'an exonerates Aaron completely and identifies a shadowy figure who led the Israelites astray while Moses was on Mount Sinai.

---

## The Story

### Moses on the Mountain

Moses had departed to Mount Sinai to receive the tablets, leaving his brother Aaron in charge. He promised to return in 40 days. But the people grew restless.

### The Calf Emerges

Among the Israelites was a man named **[[Samiri]]**. He had collected dust from the tracks of the horse of Jibril (Gabriel) — the very dust of the archangel's mount, which had the power to give life to inanimate forms.

He gathered the people's gold ornaments and cast them into a fire. From the molten gold, he fashioned a **calf** — a lifeless statue that, through Samiri's sorcery, appeared to make a sound.

> *"And he produced for them a calf — a body that lowed. So they said: This is your god and the god of Moses — he has forgotten."* — **20:88**

The people worshipped the calf. Aaron cried out:

> *"O my people, you are being tested by it. Your Lord is the Most Merciful — follow me and obey my command."* — **20:90**

They refused:

> *"We will not cease worshipping it until Moses returns to us."* — **20:91**

### Moses Returns

When Moses returned and saw the people worshipping the calf, he threw down the tablets in anger. He seized his brother by the head, dragging him closer:

> *"Aaron, what prevented you, when you saw them going astray, from following me? Did you disobey my command?"* — **20:92–93**

Aaron explained:

> *"Son of my mother, do not seize me by my beard or my head. I feared you would say: You have divided the Children of Israel and did not observe my word."* — **20:94**

Moses understood. The blame was not on his brother.

### The Confrontation

Moses turned to Samiri:

> *"And what is the matter with you, Samiri?"* — **20:95**

Samiri replied:

> *"I saw what they did not see. I took a handful of dust from the messenger's footprint and threw it. Thus my soul prompted me."* — **20:96**

Moses pronounced judgment:

> *"Then go! In this life, you shall be exiled, saying: Do not touch me. And there is a promise of punishment for you that will not be broken. Now look at your god — the calf to which you devoted yourself — we will burn it, then scatter it into the sea."* — **20:97**

Samiri was banished from the community — unable to be touched, a living outcast — as punishment for leading the people astray.

> *"Your god is Allah alone — there is no god but Him. He encompasses all things in knowledge."* — **20:98**

## Key Tafsir Insights

**Who was Samiri?** The name *Samiri* (السامري) is unique to the Qur'an. Interpretations:
- He was a man from the tribe of **Samira** — an ancient Israelite or Copt
- He was a **Samaritan** — though the Samaritans as a distinct group emerged much later (after the Assyrian exile, 7th c. BCE), so this is anachronistic
- Most likely: *Samiri* derives from a foreign name, perhaps an Egyptian or Arabian sorcerer who accompanied the Israelites
- In some traditions, he was a **Copt** who converted outwardly but kept his Egyptian sorcery

**Aaron exonerated:** The Qur'an's version is significantly different from the Bible (Exodus 32) where Aaron builds the calf. Here, Aaron is firmly on the side of truth — he tried to stop the people but was outnumbered. This reflects the Islamic belief that prophets are protected from major sin (*'iṣmah*).

**"The messenger's footprint":** Samiri took dust from where Jibril's horse had stepped. This is deeply symbolic: he tried to use a trace of the divine for idolatry — a perversion of sacred power.

**The lowing calf:** The Qur'an says the calf "lowed" (*yahūr* — it made a sound). Did it actually move and sound? Classical exegetes: Samiri's sorcery made the wind pass through the hollow gold to create a sound. The people were deceived into thinking it was alive.

**The punishment of exile:** "Do not touch me" (*lā misās*) — Samiri's punishment was perpetual isolation. This became a proverbial curse: *lā misās* means "keep away — you are contaminated."
