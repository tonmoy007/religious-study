# Ashab al-Ukhdud — The People of the Ditch

> Surah [[085 Al-Burooj]] · Verses 4–8 · Meccan · Revelation order: 27

One of the most compressed and powerful martyrdom stories in scripture — eleven verses that tell the story of believers thrown into a trench of fire for their faith. The Qur'anic version is deliberately terse; the hadith tradition fills it with one of the most beautiful stories of faith under persecution in all of religious literature.

---

## The Qur'anic Account

> *"Slain be the People of the Ditch — the fire abounding in fuel. When they sat by it, witnessing what they were doing to the believers. And they resented them only because they believed in Allah, the Mighty, the Praised."* — **85:4–8**

The Qur'an says only this: a trench was dug, filled with fire, and believers were thrown into it — their only crime was their faith.

The punishment of the persecutors is promised in the same verses:

> *"Indeed, those who persecute the believing men and believing women and then do not repent — for them is the punishment of Hell, and for them is the punishment of the Burning."* — **85:10**

---

## The Story in Hadith (The Believing Boy and the Magician)

The fullest account comes from the hadith of the **"Believing Boy and the Magician"** (Sahih Muslim 3005), which the Prophet (ﷺ) narrated as the fullest background to the People of the Ditch:

### The Magician's Apprentice

There was once a king who employed a powerful magician. As the magician grew old, he told the king: "I am old. Send me a young boy to teach magic." The king sent him a clever boy.

On his way to the magician each day, the boy passed by a monk. He stopped and listened to the monk's teachings, and was deeply moved. One day, he arrived late for the magician, who beat him. The boy complained to the monk, who told him: "When you fear the magician, say: My family kept me. And when you fear your family, say: The magician kept me."

### The Miracle

One day, the boy found a huge beast blocking the road. He thought: "Today I will know — is the monk better or the magician?" He picked up a stone and said:

> *"O Allah, if the monk is more beloved to You than the magician, then kill this beast."*

He threw the stone and killed the beast. The people said: "Who killed it?" The boy answered: "The monk." The monk's fame grew.

### The Blind Man

The boy began healing people. A blind man approached and said: "If you are a servant of God, restore my sight." The boy prayed, and the blind man was healed. News spread.

### The Boy's Trial

The king heard of the boy's miracles. He summoned him and demanded he renounce his faith. The boy refused. The king ordered soldiers to take him to a mountain and if he did not recant, to throw him off.

*But the mountain threw the soldiers off instead.*

The king ordered the boy to be taken out to sea and drowned if he did not recant. *But the sea drowned the soldiers instead.*

Finally, the boy said: "You cannot kill me unless you tie me to a palm tree, gather the people, take an arrow from my quiver, place it in the bow, and say: In the name of the Lord of the boy, then shoot."

The king did exactly this. As the arrow struck the boy's temple, the boy said:

> *"Allah is my Lord."*

Then he died.

### The People of the Ditch

The king, enraged and terrified, ordered a trench dug and filled with fire. He gathered the people and said:

> *"Whoever returns to his former religion (of disbelief) will be spared. Whoever does not, will be thrown into the fire."*

They threw them in. One woman came carrying her infant. She hesitated. The infant spoke:

> *"O mother, be patient — for you are on the truth."*

This is the tradition behind the Qur'an's condensed words: "Slain be the People of the Ditch."

## Key Tafsir Insights

**"Slain be" (*qutila*):** This is not an imprecation but an expression of wonder or condemnation — "Damn them!" or "Cursed be!" It expresses the Qur'an's outrage at the injustice.

**Historical identification:** The story is associated with the persecution of Christians in **Najran** (modern Yemen) around 520 CE by the Jewish Himyarite king **Dhu Nawas**. He forced the Christian population of Najran to convert or be killed, and a famous trench of fire was used. The incident is well-attested in Christian martyrdom literature ("The Martyrs of Najran").

**Dhu Nawas (Yusuf As'ar Yath'ar):** The last Himyarite king of Yemen, who converted to Judaism and persecuted Christians. He was overthrown by the Christian kingdom of Axum (Ethiopia) in 525 CE.

**The speaking infant:** This is one of only a few instances in hadith where an infant speaks miraculously — the Prophet said there were only three such infants in history: the infant of the People of the Ditch, the infant of Surah Maryam (Jesus speaking from the cradle), and the infant of the story of Jurayj the monk.
