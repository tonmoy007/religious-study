# The Magicians of Pharaoh — From Sorcery to Martyrdom

> Surah [[007 Al-A'raaf]] · Verses 113–126 · Surah [[020 Taa-Haa]] · Verses 56–76 · Meccan

The story of Pharaoh's magicians — the elite sorcerers of Egypt — who, after witnessing Moses' miracle, converted on the spot, refused to renounce their newfound faith under torture, and were martyred with prayers of forgiveness on their lips. One of the most dramatic conversion stories in the Qur'an.

---

## The Story

### The Challenge

Pharaoh and his court refused to believe Moses' message. When Moses threatened them with divine punishment, Pharaoh responded:

> *"And what is the Lord of the worlds?"* — **26:23**

Moses replied with the testimony of God's sovereignty over creation. Pharaoh, dismissing him, said:

> *"If you take any god besides me, I will surely put you among the prisoners."* — **26:29**

Moses then demonstrated his signs: the staff turned into a serpent, and his hand shone white. Pharaoh cried:

> *"This is nothing but magic learned from the old — a sorcerer who wants to drive you from your land!"* — **20:57–58**

Pharaoh summoned the greatest magicians of Egypt to the palace. He gathered them in a vast public contest — a spectacle to prove that Egyptian magic could overcome Moses' God.

### The Contest

The magicians gathered. They threw their ropes and staffs — by their sorcery, the people saw them moving like snakes. The crowd was amazed. Pharaoh's court smiled.

Then Moses threw his staff.

> *"And We inspired Moses: Throw your staff. And behold, it swallowed up their falsehood!"* — **7:117**

The staff became a massive serpent and **consumed** every snake the magicians had conjured.

The magicians recognized instantly what had happened. This was not magic — magic could be learned, studied, and replicated. This was something else. They fell to the ground in prostration:

> *"And the magicians fell down in prostration. They said: We believe in the Lord of the worlds — the Lord of Moses and Aaron."* — **7:120–122**

### The Pharaoh's Rage

Pharaoh was livid. He threatened them:

> *"You believed in him before I gave you permission! This is a plot you have devised in the city to drive out its people. You will soon know!"* — **7:123**

He promised to cut off their hands and feet on opposite sides, then crucify them all.

### The Martyrs' Reply

The magicians — who had been the most powerful sorcerers in Egypt, men who had studied magic their entire lives — did not waver:

> *"We will never prefer you over the clear proofs that have come to us, nor over He who created us. So decree whatever you will decree — you can only decree concerning this worldly life."* — **20:72**

Then they spoke one of the most beautiful prayers in the Qur'an:

> *"Our Lord, pour upon us patience, and cause us to die as Muslims."* — **7:126**

They were executed — hands severed, crucified — but they died as believers.

---

## Key Tafsir Insights

**Why did they convert so instantly?** The magicians were experts in sorcery. They knew the difference between illusion (*sihr*) and reality. When Moses' staff consumed their snakes — not by superior magic but by divine power — they knew it was from God. Their expertise made them uniquely qualified to recognize the miracle.

**"We will never prefer you":** The magicians' defiance of Pharaoh is a model of prophetic boldness. They had everything to lose — status, wealth, life — and they knew it. Yet they chose truth over survival.

**The parallel with Asiyah:** In the same court, two conversions: the queen in secret and the magicians in public. Both died for their faith.

**Why the magicians were martyred while Moses survived:** The magicians had already lived their lives under Pharaoh's power; they converted at the peak of their careers. Their martyrdom sealed their faith forever.

**The prayer for patience:** *Rabbanā afrigh ʿalaynā ṣabran* — "Our Lord, pour upon us patience" — uses the verb *afrigh* (to pour out), as if patience is a liquid that must be poured over them to cover them entirely. A request for complete, overwhelming patience.
