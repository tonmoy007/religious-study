# The Battle of the Trench — The Confederates Repelled

> Shawwal, 5 AH · March 627 CE · The siege of Medina

The third major engagement — a coalition army of **10,000 men** — the Quraysh alliance at its peak — besieged Medina for nearly a month. The Muslims dug a trench (a tactic never before used in Arabian warfare), endured a siege, and were saved when Allah sent a windstorm that scattered the Confederates. The Qur'an calls this the moment when "the believers were tested and shaken with a mighty shaking."

---

## The Background

After Uhud, the Quraysh were still not satisfied. [[Abu Sufyan]] formed an alliance with the Jewish tribe of **Banu Nadir** (expelled from Medina by the Prophet), the **Ghatafan** tribes, and other Bedouin confederates. The army of **10,000** marched on Medina.

The Prophet consulted his companions. **[[Salman al-Farsi]]** (Salman the Persian) suggested a tactic used in Persian warfare — digging a trench around the exposed parts of Medina. The Muslims dug for six days, while the army approached.

---

## The Siege

### The Trench

The trench was dug on the northern approach to Medina — the other sides were protected by lava rock formations and palm groves. It was about 5 meters wide and 3 meters deep.

When the Confederates arrived, they were stunned. Bedouin horsemen could not cross. The Quraysh settled for a siege.

### The Strain

The siege lasted **20–30 days**. The Muslims were outnumbered 3:1. The city's food was running low. Children cried from hunger. The **Banu Qurayza** — a Jewish tribe within Medina — began secret negotiations with the Quraysh. The Prophet learned of the betrayal. Now the Muslims faced an army outside and possible enemies inside.

> *"When they came upon you from above you and from below you, and when your eyes rolled and hearts reached the throats, and you thought thoughts about Allah. There, the believers were sorely tried and severely shaken."* — **33:10–11**

### The Climax

The crisis was severe. Some hypocrites said: "Muhammad promised us the treasures of Persia and Rome — and here we are, unable even to leave our homes." The Quraysh and Banu Ghatafan pressed the attack.

The Prophet could barely stand from hunger. He tied two stones to his stomach to suppress his hunger pangs. He prayed:

> *"O Allah, You revealed the Book, You are swift in reckoning — defeat the Confederates, shake them."*

### The Intervention

Then Allah sent:

> *"A wind and forces you could not see"* — **33:9**

A violent sandstorm erupted — the tent ropes snapped, the cooking pots overturned, the camels broke loose. The Confederates could not maintain their positions. **Khalid ibn al-Walid** later said: "The wind was so strong I could not see a single tent peg in place."

The Quraysh lost heart. Abu Sufyan declared:

> *"Go back — we are done here."*

The siege was over. The Confederates scattered without a full-scale battle.

---

## The Aftermath

### The Banu Qurayza

The Prophet turned immediately to the **Banu Qurayza** — the tribe that had betrayed the pact. They were besieged in their fortresses and surrendered. The Prophet appointed **Sa'd ibn Mu'adh** — a leader of the Ansar and a former ally of the Qurayza — as arbiter. Sa'd ruled according to Jewish law (Deuteronomy 20:10–14): adult males to be executed, women and children taken captive. The Qurayza accepted the ruling.

The Banu Qurayza's fate ended Jewish political presence in Medina and secured the city for the Muslims.

---

## Key Tafsir Insights

**The trench as innovation:** [[Salman al-Farsi]]'s suggestion to dig a trench introduced a military tactic unknown in Arabia. The Prophet accepted the counsel of a Persian companion — showing that wisdom has no nationality.

**The wind as miracle:** The Qur'an says "a wind and forces you could not see" — the wind was visible, the angels were not. The storm was both a physical phenomenon and a divine intervention.

**The Banu Qurayza judgment:** Sa'd ibn Mu'adh's ruling is controversial in modern times. Classical context: in 7th-century tribal warfare, the penalty for treason in a besieged city was typically total annihilation. The Qurayza accepted Sa'd's ruling because he was known to be moderate, and they had agreed to abide by his decision. Moreover, the ruling aligned with their own scripture (Deuteronomy 20).

**The test of faith:** The Qur'an calls the Trench a *filter* — it separated the sincere believers from the hypocrites. The prolonged stress of the siege revealed who truly trusted Allah.
