# Luqman the Wise — Wisdom for His Son

> Surah [[031 Luqman]] · Verses 12–19 · Meccan

Not a prophet — but a figure of wisdom so profound that Allah named an entire surah after him. Luqman's advice to his son is a parenting manual in eight verses, covering theology, ethics, manners, and spirituality. The Qur'an presents it as the distillation of wisdom itself.

---

## Who Was Luqman?

The Qur'an does not call [[Luqman]] a prophet. The classical exegetes debated his status:

- **Ibn Abbas:** He was a righteous servant, not a prophet; Allah gave him wisdom (*ḥikmah*)
- **Mujahid:** He was a wise man, a judge among his people, known for his sound judgment
- **Ibn Kathir:** He was a Nubian slave who attained great wisdom through God-consciousness
- **Ṭabarī:** He had the gift of right judgment — his counsel was a form of revelation

The traditions describe him as a black man from Nubia (Sudan) or Ethiopia, of humble origin, whose wisdom made him famous. He is sometimes identified with the biblical "Ezekiel" or "Lukman" of Arabic folklore, but there is no consensus.

> *"We gave Luqman wisdom: Be grateful to Allah. And whoever is grateful is grateful only for the good of his own soul."* — **31:12**

---

## The Advice

### 1. The Greatest Wrong — Shirk

> *"Remember when Luqman said to his son while advising him: O my son, do not associate anything with Allah — indeed, shirk is a great wrong."* — **31:13**

The first and most important lesson: the unforgivable sin is the one against God's oneness. *Ẓulm* (wrong/injustice) here means **the worst injustice** — giving gratitude and worship to other than the One who created you.

### 2. Honour Parents (With a Limit)

> *"And We have enjoined upon man concerning his parents — his mother carried him in weakness upon weakness, and his weaning is in two years. Be grateful to Me and to your parents. To Me is the return. But if they strive to make you associate with Me that of which you have no knowledge, do not obey them. Yet keep company with them in this world kindly."* — **31:14–15**

The only exception to filial obedience: parents cannot force you into shirk. But even then, kindness and dignity toward them remain obligatory.

### 3. The Mustard Seed

> *"O my son, if it were the weight of a mustard seed — and it were in a rock, or in the heavens, or in the earth — Allah would bring it forth. Allah is Subtle, Aware."* — **31:16**

Nothing is hidden from God. Even the smallest action, hidden in the most inaccessible place, will be brought to account. The *lutf* (subtlety) of God means His knowledge penetrates everything.

### 4. Prayer

> *"O my son, establish prayer."* — **31:17**

The first act of worship: *ṣalāh* (prayer) as the foundation of faith in action.

### 5. Enjoin Good, Forbid Evil

> *"Enjoin what is right, forbid what is wrong, and bear whatever befalls you with patience. Indeed, that is among the most resolute of commands."* — **31:17**

Luqman teaches his son social responsibility — yet immediately adds the cost: *"bear whatever befalls you with patience."* Doing good invites opposition; patience is essential.

### 6. No Arrogance

> *"Do not turn your cheek away from people, nor walk arrogantly on the earth. Allah does not love any swaggering boaster."* — **31:18**

Humility in two dimensions: (a) don't turn your face away when people address you, (b) don't walk with pride. The inner disposition manifests in outer bearing.

### 7. Walk in Moderation

> *"Be moderate in your pace."* — **31:19**

Neither too slow (lazy) nor too fast (undignified). The *golden mean* in physical movement.

### 8. Lower Your Voice

> *"And lower your voice. The most detestable of voices is the braying of donkeys."* — **31:19**

A metaphor for loud, harsh, or pointless speech. The donkey's cry is the ugliest sound — speech without substance resembles it.

## Key Tafsir Insights

**Parenting as theology:** Luqman's advice moves from the most important matter (tawhid) to the most practical (lowering your voice). The sequence is itself a lesson: ground your children in faith before ethics.

**The mother's sacrifice:** Verse 31:14 mentions the mother's weakness *upon weakness* — the physical burden of pregnancy, childbirth, and nursing over two years. Some exegetes count this as the most explicit recognition of maternal sacrifice in the Qur'an.

**Is Luqman a prophet?** The strongest evidence against prophethood: the Qur'an says "We gave him wisdom" (*ḥikmah*) — not "We sent him." The hadith say "Luqman was not a prophet, but a man of deep reflection and certainty." (Tirmidhi)

**Luqman in tradition:** He is the subject of many folk tales in the Islamic world — he outsmarts kings, advises the foolhardy, and speaks in memorable proverbs. The story of Luqman and the camel, Luqman and the donkey, and Luqman's judgment are part of Arabic literary tradition.
