# Hadith al-Ifk — The Slander of Aisha

> Surah [[024 An-Noor]] · Verses 11–20 · Medinan · c. 5–6 AH / 627 CE

The most famous and dramatic "backstory" revelation in the Qur'an — a month-long scandal in Medina that tested the bonds of the early Muslim community. The story of the slander against Aisha, the Prophet's wife, and the revelation that exonerated her remains one of the most powerful narratives about truth, justice, and community ethics in Islamic tradition.

---

## The Historical Context

The event took place during or immediately after the **Expedition of Banu al-Mustaliq** (5 or 6 AH), when the Prophet led a campaign against a tribe threatening Medina. [[Aisha bint Abi Bakr|Aisha]] accompanied him — as was customary, she was chosen by lot among the Prophet's wives.

---

## The Story

### The Lost Necklace

On the return journey, the army stopped for the night. Aisha stepped away to relieve herself. When she returned, she discovered that her necklace of Zifar beads — a gift from her sister Asma — had broken and fallen. She went back to search for it while the attendants helped her onto the camel-litter, not realizing she was absent.

The camel-litter, with its curtains drawn, was lifted onto the camel. The attendants assumed Aisha was inside. Aisha returned to find the camp empty — the army had departed, unaware she was missing.

### The Night with Safwan

Safwan ibn al-Mu'attal al-Sulami, a companion tasked with searching for anything the army might have left behind, saw Aisha alone in the dark and recognized her (he had seen her before the hijab was revealed). He said:

> *"Truly we belong to Allah, and truly to Him we shall return!"*

He brought his camel forward and stepped back — he did not touch her or speak beyond that. Aisha rode the camel while Safwan led it on foot, catching up with the army at the next halt.

### The Scandal

When they arrived, the rumourmongers began their work. The chief slanderer was **'Abdullah ibn Ubayy ibn Salul**, the leader of the hypocrites of Medina. He spread the lie: "Aisha spent the night with a strange man in the desert." The gossip spread like wildfire:

> *"Those who came with the slander are a group among you."* — **24:11**

Hassan ibn Thabit (the poet), Mistah ibn Uthatha (Aisha's cousin), and Hamnah bint Jahsh (Aisha's sister-in-law) all repeated the rumour — though Hamnah's motive was rivalry with Aisha.

### The Month of Torment

Aisha knew nothing of the rumours. She fell ill. After returning to Medina, she noticed the Prophet was unusually distant — kind but cool. She asked him for permission to stay at her mother's house to recover, which he granted.

Only there did she learn the truth: her servant Umm Mistah, while gossiping, had let it slip. Aisha wept until her parents feared she would die.

> *"I cried continuously. By Allah, I could not sleep at night, and I cried all day."*

### The Interrogation

The Prophet asked Aisha's family about her. He sent for Ali ibn Abi Talib and Usamah ibn Zayd. Usamah said only good things: "O Messenger of Allah, I have never known your household to be anything but good." But Ali said:

> *"O Messenger of Allah, women are plentiful. You can replace her. Ask her maid-servant."*

The Prophet summoned Aisha. He sat on the pulpit in the mosque and asked:

> *"Aisha, I have heard such-and-such about you. If you are innocent, Allah will declare you innocent. If you have sinned, repent — Allah accepts repentance."*

Aisha could not find words. She said:

> *"By Allah, I know you heard so much gossip that it has sunk into your soul. If I tell you I am innocent — but you don't believe me — what good would that do? And if I confess to something — and Allah knows I am innocent — I would only be admitting to what I did not do."*

Then she said the words that would become famous:

> *"I can only say what the father of Yusuf said: Patience is beautiful. Allah is the One whose help is sought against what you describe."*

She turned away and waited.

### The Revelation

The Prophet remained seated, sweating — the sign of revelation came upon him, even in winter. When the revelation lifted, he was smiling:

> *"Good news, Aisha! Allah has sent down your vindication."*

Then he recited the first verses of Surah An-Nur:

> *"Indeed, those who came with the slander are a group among you. Do not consider it bad for you — rather, it is good for you."* — **24:11**

The verses commanded:
- Four witnesses for any accusation of adultery (24:13)
- Flogging for slanderers who fail to produce four witnesses (24:4)
- The curse of Allah in this life and the next for those who persist in slander (24:7)

### The Punishment

The Prophet ordered the punishment of the slanderers:
- **Mistah and Hassan ibn Thabit** were flogged with 80 lashes each (the *hadd* for *qadhf* — false accusation of adultery)
- **Hamnah bint Jahsh** was also flogged
- **'Abdullah ibn Ubayy** — the chief hypocrite — could not be executed because the Prophet's policy was to leave hypocrites to Allah's judgment. But the revelation exposed him publicly

## Key Tafsir Insights

**Why this revelation matters:** The Ifk verses established a foundational principle of Islamic criminal law: **any accusation of sexual misconduct requires four eyewitnesses**. Without four witnesses, the accuser is guilty of *qadhf* (slander) and receives 80 lashes. This effectively makes adultery prosecutable only in the most extreme cases.

**Aisha's stature:** After the Ifk incident, Aisha's status in the community actually rose. The revelation that vindicated her was recited in mosques daily. The Prophet said: "Aisha's superiority over other women is like the superiority of *tharid* (meat-broth) over other foods."

**The lessons:** 
> *"Why did you not, when you heard it, say: It is not for us to speak of this. Exalted are You, O Allah — this is a great slander."* — **24:16**

The incident taught the community to stop gossip at the source, and to presume good faith until proven otherwise.
