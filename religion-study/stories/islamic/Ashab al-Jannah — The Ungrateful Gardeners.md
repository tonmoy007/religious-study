# Ashab al-Jannah — The Ungrateful Gardeners

> Surah [[068 Al-Qalam]] · Verses 17–33 · Meccan

A parable of greed and ingratitude — the story of the owners of a garden who planned to harvest its fruits in secret, refusing to give the poor their share, and woke to find the entire garden destroyed overnight. It is the longest continuous parable in the Qur'an outside the prophetic narratives.

---

## The Story

### The Garden

There was a garden — flourishing, productive, blessed. Its owners had been given abundant fruit. But they refused to give the poor their due share at harvest time. They said to one another:

> *"Go to your crop early in the morning, if you are going to harvest."* — **68:22**

They planned to sneak out before dawn, while the poor were still asleep, so that no one would ask for a portion.

### The Punishment

While they slept, a divine punishment descended on the garden:

> *"So a visitation from your Lord came down upon it while they slept — and by morning it was like a harvested field, stripped bare."* — **68:19–20**

### The Discovery

At dawn, they called to one another:

> *"Go to your crop if you are going to harvest."* — **68:22**

They walked along, whispering in low voices:

> *"Do not let any poor person enter the garden today."* — **68:24**

They were confident, purposeful. But when they arrived, the garden was black — destroyed, desolate. They cried out:

> *"We have certainly gone astray! No — we are deprived!"* — **68:26–27**

### The Realization

The most just among them spoke up:

> *"Did I not say to you: Why do you not glorify Allah?"* — **68:28**

They admitted their sin:

> *"Glory to our Lord! We were truly wrongdoers."* — **68:29**

Then they turned on one another, blaming each other. And then came the most painful realization:

> *"They said: O woe to us — we were insolent! Perhaps our Lord will give us something better in exchange. Truly we turn to our Lord."* — **68:31–32

But the garden was gone.

---

## Key Tafsir Insights

**The sin:** Not merely greed but *intention* — they planned to deprive the poor before they even harvested. The Qur'an emphasizes the premeditation: "If you are going to harvest" — they planned the injustice in advance.

**The "best among them" (*awsatuhum*):** The one who had warned them is described as *awsat* — meaning the most moderate, the most just, the best in character. This figure is the conscience of the group. His warning: "Why do you not *glorify* Allah?" — meaning, why do you not recognize that all provision comes from Him and must be shared?

**The punishment fits the crime:** They intended to harvest in secret, away from the poor. Their punishment: the garden was "harvested" in secret, away from them.

**Repentance and replacement:** Their hope — "Perhaps our Lord will give us something better" — saved them from utter despair. The Qur'an ends the parable with "Such is the punishment — but the punishment of the Hereafter is greater, if they only knew" (68:33). The garden parable is a warning of this-worldly punishment; the Hereafter's is greater.

**The parallel with the People of the Elephant:** In both stories, people who thought themselves secure in their resources (the elephant army, the garden's wealth) were destroyed by a divine intervention they could not have anticipated.
