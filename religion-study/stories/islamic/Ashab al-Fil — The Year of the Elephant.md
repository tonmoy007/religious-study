# Ashab al-Fil — The Year of the Elephant

> Surah [[105 Al-Fil]] · 5 verses · Meccan · Revelation order: 19

The story of the miraculous destruction of Abraha's army — an expedition sent from Yemen to destroy the Kaaba, stopped by flocks of birds carrying stones. The year of this event, **570 CE**, was so significant in Arabian memory that it became the era by which dates were marked — and it was the year the Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) was born.

---

## The Story

### The Ambition of Abraha

**Abraha al-Ashram** was the Abyssinian (Ethiopian) viceroy of Yemen, ruling on behalf of the Christian Negus (king) of Axum. A devout Christian, he built a magnificent cathedral in Sana'a called **al-Qullays** (from Greek *ekklesia*), hoping to divert the Arab pilgrimage trade from Mecca to his church. He intended it to be "the Kaaba of Yemen."

The Arabs refused to abandon the Kaaba. In fact, a man from the Kinanah tribe defiled the cathedral — some say he entered it and relieved himself. Abraha, enraged, vowed to destroy the Kaaba.

### The March to Mecca

Abraha assembled a massive army, including war elephants — the first time elephants had been seen in the Hijaz. He rode at the head of a force that included a famous white elephant named **Mahmud**.

The Arab tribes attempted to resist but were scattered. Abraha approached Mecca.

### The Quraysh's Dilemma

The Quraysh, the custodians of the Kaaba, knew they were too weak to resist. Their leader, **[[Abd al-Muttalib]]** (the Prophet's grandfather), went to meet Abraha. When Abraha asked what he wanted, Abd al-Muttalib replied:

> *"I want you to return my camels — two hundred camels that you have taken."*

Abraha was astonished: "You ask for your camels but not for your temple — the sanctuary of your religion?"

Abd al-Muttalib replied with a line that would become legendary:

> *"I am the owner of the camels — the House also has an Owner who will protect it."*

Abraha returned the camels. The Quraysh evacuated Mecca to the surrounding hills.

### The Miracle

Abraha's army prepared to march on the Kaaba. The elephant **Mahmud** refused to move toward Mecca — it knelt and would not rise, even when beaten. But when turned toward Yemen, it immediately got up and marched.

Then Allah sent:

> *"Flocks of birds (abābīl), pelting them with stones of baked clay (sijjīl). So He made them like eaten straw."* — **105:3–5**

The birds flew from the coast, each carrying three stones — one in its beak, two in its claws. The stones pierced the soldiers' bodies. The army was destroyed, fleeing in panic. Abraha himself was struck, his body falling apart piece by piece until he died near Sana'a.

> *"Have you not seen how your Lord dealt with the owners of the Elephant?"* — **105:1**

## Key Tafsir Insights

**The year of the Elephant:** 570 CE — the Prophet's birth year. The Arabs began to use "the Year of the Elephant" as a date marker, showing how deeply the event entered Arabian consciousness. It was in this year that the Prophet was born, as if the Kaaba's protection heralded the one who would restore it.

**Abābīl:** A word that occurs only once in the Qur'an. The classical exegetes understood it as flocks of birds coming in waves. Some modern scholars interpret *abābīl* as referring to swarms of mosquitoes or locusts carrying disease (perhaps a plague epidemic), while others see it as a specific type of swallow-like bird.

**Sijjīl (baked clay):** The same word used for the stones that destroyed the people of Lot (11:82) and the city of Iram (51:33). The word is of Persian origin (*sang + gil* = stone + clay), referring to hardened clay pellets.

**The elephant's refusal:** The hadith record that the elephant Mahmud refused to advance toward Mecca — a clear sign of divine protection. The phenomenon is widely attested in Islamic tradition.

**Historical verification:** The event is attested outside Islamic sources. The Abyssinian expedition against Mecca is mentioned in Byzantine histories of the period, and the Sabaean inscriptions confirm Abraha's rule over Yemen and his military campaigns. The collapse of Abyssinian power in Yemen shortly after (c. 575 CE) fits the timeline of the army's destruction.

**The Kaaba never destroyed:** The Kaaba has survived every attempt at destruction across fourteen centuries — fulfilling the promise implied in this surah.
