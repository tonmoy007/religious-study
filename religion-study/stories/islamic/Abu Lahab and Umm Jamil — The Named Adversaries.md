# Abu Lahab and Umm Jamil — The Named Adversaries

> Surah [[111 Al-Masad]] · 5 verses · Meccan · Revelation order: 6

The only two people in the entire Qur'an to be condemned by name — the Prophet's own uncle and aunt who opposed him with relentless hostility. Surah Al-Masad is unique: it curses specific living individuals who were contemporaries of the Prophet, and its prophecy was fulfilled in their lifetimes.

---

## The Story

### The Uncle Who Refused

**Abu Lahab** — whose real name was **Abd al-Uzza ibn Abd al-Muttalib** — was the Prophet's paternal uncle. He was wealthy, influential, and fiercely proud of the Quraysh's pagan heritage.

When the Prophet began to preach publicly, he was commanded:

> *"And warn your nearest kindred."* — **26:214**

The Prophet climbed Mount Safa and called out: "O people of Quraysh!" When they gathered, he said:

> *"If I told you that an army was behind this mountain about to attack you, would you believe me?"*

They replied: "We have never known you to lie."

He said: *"Then I warn you of a severe punishment."*

Abu Lahab was the first to respond — he cried out:

> *"May you perish! Is this why you gathered us?"*

From that moment, Abu Lahab became the Prophet's most vocal opponent. He followed the Prophet everywhere, telling people: "He is a liar. He has abandoned the religion of his fathers."

### The Curse Revealed

Surah Al-Masad was revealed:

> *"May the hands of Abu Lahab perish — and perish he! His wealth did not avail him, nor what he earned. He will burn in a flaming fire — and his wife, the carrier of firewood — around her neck a rope of palm fiber."* — **111:1–5**

Abu Lahab heard the revelation and was enraged. But he never converted — not to spite the Qur'an, not to disprove it. He died a polytheist.

### The Wife Who Carried Thorns

His wife, **Umm Jamil** (Arwa bint Harb), was equally hostile. She would place thorns and sharp objects in the Prophet's path at night. She spread slander against him in the streets. When she heard the surah, she went to the mosque carrying a stone to strike the Prophet. Allah caused her to see only Abu Bakr, and she said: "Tell your companion I have a stone to crush him."

She never embraced Islam. She died — according to tradition — screaming in her house, abandoned, a rope around her neck.

## Key Tafsir Insights

**The prophecy fulfilled:** The surah predicted that Abu Lahab would die in a state of disbelief — "perish he!" — and that his wealth would not save him. This was a dangerous prophecy: if Abu Lahab had simply *pretended* to convert, the Qur'an's credibility would have been shattered. He had 10 years to do so. He never did. This is considered among the strongest proofs of the Qur'an's divine origin.

**Why name names?** The Qur'an is generally restrained about naming its opponents. Abu Lahab is the exception. The condemnation of an individual by name is a *challenge* — a prophecy that this man will die in rejection and be punished. The fact that Abu Lahab, despite every worldly incentive to disprove it, remained an enemy to his death is evidence of the prophecy's truth.

**"Carrier of firewood" (*ḥammālata al-ḥaṭab*):** A double meaning:
1. Literal: Umm Jamil carried thorny wood to throw on the Prophet's path
2. Figurative: "carrying wood" is an Arabic idiom for carrying gossip and slander — stirring up conflict

**"A rope of palm fiber" (*masad*):** The word *masad* appears only here. It means a twisted rope of palm fiber — the cheapest, roughest material. It was also a punishment: she was so wealthy and proud, she would be humiliated with the lowest form of restraint.

**Abu Lahab's sons:** Abu Lahab had two sons, **Utbah** and **Utaybah**, who were married to the Prophet's daughters (Ruqayyah and Umm Kulthum). After Surah Al-Masad was revealed, Abu Lahab ordered them to divorce the Prophet's daughters. They complied — showing their hostility even at the cost of family ties.

**Different from the Bible:** No prophet in the Bible invokes a curse by name on specific living opponents. The Qur'an does it once — and stakes its credibility on the outcome.
