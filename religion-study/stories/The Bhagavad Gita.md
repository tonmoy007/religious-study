# The Bhagavad Gita

> The Song Celestial — Krishna's discourse to Arjuna on the battlefield of Kurukshetra. The most sacred text of Hinduism after the Vedas; the philosophical core of the Mahabharata.

---

## The Setting (Mahabharata — Bhishma Parva)

The two armies face each other on the field of **Kurukshetra**. Arjuna asks Krishna — his charioteer — to drive his chariot between the armies. He sees his grandfather **Bhishma**, his teacher **Drona**, his cousins, his uncles — all arrayed to fight.

Arjuna **breaks down**:

> "I see no good in killing my own kinsmen. I do not want victory — not even the kingdom. What is victory if those I love are dead?" — Gita 1:31

He throws down his bow: "I will not fight."

---

## The Teachings (Key Themes)

### 1. The Immortal Self (Chapters 2–3)

> "The soul is never born, never dies. It is not slain when the body is slain." — 2:20

Krishna's first teaching: the *Atman* (Self) is eternal. Wounding the body is not the ultimate concern — the Self cannot be killed. Arjuna should not grieve for what is eternal.

### 2. Yoga of Action (Karma Yoga) (Chapters 3–4)

> "You have a right to perform your duty — but not to the fruits of your action." — 2:47

Krishna teaches **detached action**: do your duty without attachment to success or failure. Act for its own sake — not for reward.

### 3. Yoga of Devotion (Bhakti Yoga) (Chapters 7–11)

> "Fix your mind on Me — be devoted to Me. I will deliver you from all sins." — 18:65

Krishna reveals his **universal form** (Vishvarupa) — a terrifying, infinite vision of the god who contains all worlds, all time, all death. Arjuna trembles: "I see all the gods in your body — I see the universe in your limbs."

### 4. Yoga of Knowledge (Jnana Yoga) (Chapters 13–15)

> "Know the field and the knower of the field — that is true knowledge." — 13:1

Krishna teaches the distinction between the body (the field) and the Self (the knower). Liberation comes through discrimination — seeing the One in all.

### 5. The Three Gunas (Chapter 14)

All nature consists of three qualities:
- **Sattva** — purity, wisdom, harmony
- **Rajas** — passion, activity, restlessness
- **Tamas** — darkness, inertia, ignorance

### 6. The Final Teaching (Chapter 18)

> "Abandon all dharmas — surrender to Me alone. Do not grieve — I will liberate you." — 18:66

Arjuna: "My delusion is destroyed. I will act according to your word."

---

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Dharma of the warrior** | Arjuna's duty is to fight — not for hatred but for dharma |
| **Detachment** | The path of action without attachment to results |
| **Bhakti** | Devotion to God as the highest path |
| **Vishvarupa** | The terrifying vision — God is not only love but also destruction |
| **Three yogas** | Karma (action), Bhakti (devotion), Jnana (knowledge) — three paths to the same goal |

---

## Comparison with Other Traditions

The Bhagavad Gita's teaching of **detached action** — "do your duty without concern for the outcome" — has parallels with:
- **Stoicism** (Epictetus): focus on what is within your control
- **Zen Buddhism**: act without the trace of ego
- **Christianity**: "Not my will, but yours be done" (Luke 22:42)

The **Vishvarupa** — God as both creator and destroyer — parallels the Qur'an's description of Allah as "the Compeller" and the Book of Revelation's vision of the Lamb on the throne.
