# The Sacrifice: Isaac or Ishmael?

> Abraham's greatest test, retold in two scriptures — with the identity of the son changed. The divergence is small (one name) but the theological consequences are vast.

---

## The Two Accounts: Side by Side

| Element | **Genesis 22:1–19** (Bible) | **Qur'an 37:100–113** |
|---------|---------------------------|----------------------|
| **Who is tested?** | God tests Abraham | Abraham sees a dream/vision |
| **The son named?** | **Isaac** — "Take your son, your only son Isaac, whom you love" (v.2) | Not named — "a forbearing son" (*ghulām ḥalīm*) |
| **The son's awareness** | Isaac asks: "The fire and wood are here, but where is the lamb?" | The son says: "Father, do as you are commanded — you will find me patient" |
| **The binding** | Abraham binds Isaac, lays him on the altar, raises the knife | Both submit — no binding described; the focus is on mutual surrender |
| **The ram** | A ram caught in a thicket replaces Isaac | A great ram (*dhibḥ ʿaẓīm*) replaces the son |
| **The blessing** | "Your descendants will possess the gate of their enemies" | "We left [blessings] upon him among later generations" |

---

## What is Shared

1. **The core event:** A father is commanded to sacrifice his son as a test of faith. At the last moment, a ram is substituted.
2. **The location:** Mount Moriah / the place of sacrifice — both traditions locate it near Jerusalem (or Mina for the Ḥajj).
3. **The outcome:** The son lives; the father passes the test; the covenant is reaffirmed.
4. **The ram:** Both agree a divinely-provided animal replaces the human sacrifice.

---

## What is Changed

### 1. The Identity of the Son

This is the single most important theological divergence between Judaism/Christianity and Islam regarding Abraham.

| Tradition | Son sacrificed | Evidence |
|-----------|---------------|----------|
| **Judaism** | Isaac | Genesis 22:2 — "your only son Isaac" (Ishmael had been sent away, Gen 21:14) |
| **Christianity** | Isaac | Inherits Jewish tradition; Isaac as type of Christ (Hebrews 11:17–19) |
| **Islam** | **Ishmael** | The Qur'an never names the son; Islamic tradition (Ṭabarī, Ibn Kathīr) identifies him as Ishmael. The "blessing" on the son in 37:112 (Isaac's birth announced *after* the sacrifice) suggests the sacrificed son was the elder — Ishmael |

**Why it matters:**
- **Judaism:** Isaac is the son of the covenant — the line through whom the Jewish people are chosen. Ishmael is sent away.
- **Christianity:** Isaac prefigures Christ — the beloved son, the willing sacrifice, the ram = the cross.
- **Islam:** Ishmael is the Arab patriarch — the ancestor of Muhammad (ﷺ). The substitution of Ishmael establishes the Arabs as inheritors of the Abrahamic covenant through the line of Ishmael, not Isaac.

### 2. The Son's Awareness

The Qur'an uniquely depicts the son giving **consent**: 

> *"O my father, do as you are commanded. You will find me, Allah willing, among the patient."* — **37:102**

This transforms the story from a test of Abraham alone into a test of **both father and son** — a family of faith. It also makes the narrative more explicitly about *submission* (islām), the core virtue of Islam.

### 3. The Structure of the Test

In Genesis, God's command is direct: "Take your son... and offer him as a burnt offering." In the Qur'an, Abraham receives the command **in a dream** (37:105: "You have fulfilled the vision"). This subtle difference:
- Protects Abraham from the implication of divinely commanded human sacrifice
- Makes the test more interpretative — a vision requires discernment
- Aligns with the Islamic concept of true dreams as a form of prophecy

---

## Type of Connection

**Genealogical** — The Qur'an explicitly retells the Abraham story, assuming the audience knows the biblical version. The modifications are intentional theological corrections: clarifying that the son was Ishmael (not Isaac), that the son consented, and that the command came through a dream (not a direct order).

---

## Theological Stakes

| Lens | What's at stake |
|------|-----------------|
| **Abraham's role** | Jew, Christian, and Muslim all claim him as "father of the faithful" — but which children count? |
| **Sacrifice theology** | The ram replaces Isaac → Gospel of substitutionary atonement. The ram replaces Ishmael → mercy outweighs even the greatest commands |
| **Lineage politics** | The sacrificed son determines which descendants inherit the covenant — Jews (Isaac) or Arabs (Ishmael) |
| **Human sacrifice** | Both traditions emphatically reject it — but the Qur'an is more explicit: "Allah does not want the sacrifice of your sons. He wants the *willingness*." |
