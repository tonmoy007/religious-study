# Prahlada and Narasimha

> The man-lion avatar of Vishnu — a demon king who thought himself invincible, a son whose devotion could not be broken, and the impossible moment when neither man nor beast, neither day nor night, neither inside nor outside, became the death of evil.

---

## The Story (Bhagavata Purana)

### The Boon of Hiranyakashipu

**Hiranyakashipu** — king of the Asuras — wants immortality. He performs penance for 10,000 years, standing on one leg, until Brahma appears. Brahma cannot grant immortality (it would violate cosmic order), but Hiranyakashipu asks:

- Not killed by **man or beast**
- Not **inside or outside** (any dwelling)
- Not **day or night**
- Not on **earth or in the sky**
- Not by any **weapon**, living or inanimate

Brahma: "Let it be."

### The Tyrant

Hiranyakashipu conquers the three worlds. He forbids the worship of Vishnu: "Only I am God."

But his own son — **Prahlada** — is devoted to Vishnu. He chants Vishnu's name constantly. Hiranyakashipu tries everything:

- Poisoning him — the poison turns to nectar
- Throwing him off a cliff — Vishnu catches him
- Elephants trampling him — they refuse
- Serpents biting him — they worship him
- Setting him on fire — the fire is cold

Hiranyakashipu's sister **Holika** — immune to fire — sits with Prahlada in a bonfire. The boon reverses: Holika burns, Prahlada walks out unharmed.

### The Question

Hiranyakashipu: "Where is this Vishnu you worship? In this pillar?"

Prahlada: "He is everywhere."

Enraged, the demon king smashes a pillar. The pillar cracks.

### Narasimha

From the broken pillar emerges **Narasimha** — half-man, half-lion. He is:
- **Neither man nor beast** (man-lion)
- **Neither inside nor outside** (on the threshold of the doorway)
- **Neither day nor night** (twilight)
- **Not on earth or in the sky** (on Hiranyakashipu's lap, on the threshold)
- **Not by any weapon** (with his claws)

Narasimha tears Hiranyakashipu apart with his claws.

---

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Devotion conquers all** | Prahlada's *bhakti* is unshakable — no threat can break it |
| **God is everywhere** | The pillar proves it — God is in even the most inert matter |
| **The limits of boons** | No boon can outsmart God — every loophole is closed |
| **The terrifying form** | Narasimha is not gentle — God's destructive power is awesome |

---

## Comparison with Other Traditions

The story of a child who remains faithful in the face of a tyrant's threats recalls the **Binding of Isaac** (Genesis 22) and the **People of the Ditch** (Qur'an 85). In all three, a young faithful is tested by a powerful persecutor — and either delivered or rewarded.

The **impossible condition** (the loophole that cannot be loopholed) has parallels in the story of **Ahikar** (ancient Near Eastern wisdom) and the **riddles that save** in folklore worldwide.
