# Jonah and the Great Fish

> The prophet who ran from God — and the fish that brought him back. A story about reluctant prophecy, divine mercy, and a God who cares even for Nineveh.

---

## The Story (Book of Jonah)

God commands **Jonah** to go to **Nineveh** — the capital of Assyria, Israel's enemy — and cry out against its wickedness. Jonah flees in the opposite direction, boarding a ship to Tarshish.

God hurls a great storm. The sailors — pagan — pray to their gods, throw cargo overboard, and draw lots: Jonah is the cause. He tells them: *"I fear the Lord, the God of heaven, who made the sea and dry land"* (1:9). He tells them to throw him overboard.

They hesitate, then comply. The sea calms. The sailors **fear the Lord** and offer sacrifices.

God appoints a **great fish** to swallow Jonah. Three days and three nights in its belly. Jonah prays — a psalm of thanksgiving from the depths. The fish vomits him onto dry land.

Jonah goes to Nineveh — a city of three days' walk — and preaches: *"Forty more days and Nineveh will be overthrown!"* The people **repent** — from the king to the cattle — in sackcloth and ashes. God relents.

But Jonah is **angry**: *"I knew you are a gracious God, slow to anger, abounding in love — a God who relents from calamity!"* (4:2). He sits east of the city, hoping for destruction. God grows a plant to shade him, then sends a worm to kill it. Jonah is miserable over the plant.

God's closing question: *"You pity the plant — and should I not pity Nineveh, that great city, in which there are more than 120,000 people who cannot tell their right hand from their left, and also many animals?"* (4:10–11).

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **God's universal mercy** | God cares even for Israel's enemies |
| **Reluctant prophet** | Jonah's prejudice vs. God's compassion |
| **Repentance works** | Nineveh is saved by genuine repentance — the only foreign mission in the OT that succeeds |
| **The sign of Jonah** | Jesus references Jonah's three days in the fish as a sign of His death and resurrection (Matthew 12:39–41) |

## Comparison with Islamic Tradition

The Qur'an mentions **Yūnus** (Jonah) in multiple places (4:163, 6:86, 10:98, 21:87–88, 37:139–148, 68:48–50). Key differences:

| Element | Bible | Qur'an |
|---------|-------|--------|
| **Why he fled** | Disobedience — he didn't want to preach to Nineveh | He fled without permission — became frustrated |
| **The fish** | "Great fish" (*dag gadol*) — not specified as whale | "Fish" (*ḥūt*) — swallows him |
| **Duration** | Three days and three nights | Only "until the Day of Resurrection" — ambiguous |
| **Jonah's prayer** | A psalm of thanksgiving (Jonah 2) | "There is no god but You — Glory to You — I have been among the wrongdoers" (21:87) |
| **What saved him** | He prayed, the fish vomited him | His **glorification of God** before the prayer — "Had he not been among those who glorify Allah, he would have remained in its belly" (37:143–144) |

> [!tip] The Qur'an uniquely emphasizes that Jonah's deliverance was due to his *constant habit of glorifying God* — a subtle but profound difference.
