# The Golden Rule Across Traditions

> Part of [[Cross-Scripture Connections MOC]]

"Treat others as you wish to be treated" surfaces — independently and by
diffusion — in nearly every major tradition. It is the strongest evidence that
different scriptures converge on a **shared ethical core** (see
[[Religious Ethics and Meaning]]).

## The passages, verbatim

> [!quote] Christianity — positive form
> "Therefore **all things whatsoever ye would that men should do to you, do ye even so to them**: for this is the law and the prophets."
> — [[40 Matthew|Matthew 7:12]]

> [!quote] Judaism — love of neighbour
> "…thou shalt **love thy neighbour as thyself**: I am the LORD."
> — [[scriptures/bible-kjv/03 Leviticus|Leviticus 19:18]] (quoted by Jesus in [[41 Mark|Mark 12:31]] as the second greatest commandment)

> [!quote] Islam — love for your brother
> "**None of you believes until he loves for his brother what he loves for himself.**"
> — [[04 Jami\` at-Tirmidhi|Jami\` at-Tirmidhi 2585]] (also Bukhari & Muslim)

> [!quote] Confucianism — negative form (*shu*, reciprocity)
> "Is not **RECIPROCITY** such a word? **What you do not want done to yourself, do not do to others.**"
> — [[The Analects of Confucius|Analects]], Book XV

> [!quote] Buddhism — the self as measure
> "All men tremble at punishment, all men love life; remember that thou art like unto them, and **do not kill, nor cause slaughter**."
> — [[The Dhammapada|Dhammapada]] 129–130

## Positive vs. negative form
A key analytic distinction:
- **Positive ("do unto…")** — Matthew 7:12. Demands active goodness.
- **Negative ("do not do…")** — the Analects, and Hillel's famous summary ("What is hateful to you, do not do to your neighbour"). Demands restraint.

The two forms are close but not identical: the negative rule forbids harm; the
positive rule obliges help. Comparative ethicists treat this difference as
significant, not cosmetic.

## What kind of connection?
Mostly **independent convergence with some diffusion**:
- Matthew, Leviticus, and the Hadith share a genuine **genealogical** stream (Abrahamic).
- Confucius (5th c. BCE) and the Buddhist tradition arose **independently** of the Abrahamic world — yet reached the same principle. This is the classic case *against* assuming every parallel means borrowing (see [[Connections Between Religious Texts]]).

That so many separate traditions land here suggests the rule reflects something
basic about human social life — a candidate "ethical universal."
