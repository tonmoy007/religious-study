# The Prodigal Son

> The greatest short story ever told — a father, two sons, and the radical nature of grace.

---

## The Parable (Luke 15:11–32)

A man has two sons. The younger demands his inheritance — early. The father divides his living. The son travels to a far country and **wastes everything** on reckless living.

A famine hits. The son hires himself to a pig farmer — he is so hungry he craves the pods the pigs eat. *"No one gave him anything"* (15:16).

He **comes to himself**: *"How many of my father's hired servants have bread enough — and I perish here with hunger! I will arise and go to my father, and say: Father, I have sinned against heaven and before you — I am no longer worthy to be called your son. Make me like one of your hired servants."*

While he is **still a long way off**, his father sees him — and **runs** to him, embraces him, and kisses him. The son begins his confession — but the father interrupts:

> *"Bring quickly the best robe — put it on him. Put a ring on his hand and shoes on his feet. Bring the fattened calf and kill it — let us eat and celebrate. For this my son **was dead and is alive again** — he was lost and is found!"*

The **older brother** — working in the field — hears the celebration. He is **angry**: *"I served you all these years and never disobeyed — yet you never gave me even a young goat to celebrate with my friends. But this son of yours — who devoured your property with prostitutes — you kill the fattened calf for him!"*

The father's response: *"Son, you are always with me — all that is mine is yours. But it was fitting to celebrate and be glad — for this your brother was dead and is alive; he was lost and is found."*

---

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Grace without merit** | The son does nothing to earn the forgiveness — the father runs to him |
| **The father's love** | God's love is extravagant, undignified, unconditional |
| **The older brother** | Self-righteousness is as dangerous as sin — it misses the heart of the father |
| **Lost and found** | The three parables of Luke 15 (lost sheep, lost coin, lost son) — God seeks the lost |
| **"He came to himself"** | Repentance as coming to one's senses — returning home |

---

## Connection with Islamic Tradition

The Qur'an emphasizes God's mercy in similar terms:

> *"Say: O My servants who have transgressed against themselves — do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins. He is the Forgiving, the Merciful."* (39:53)

The closest hadith parallel is the story of the man who killed 99 people, then was guided by a learned man to a village of righteous people — he died on the way, but God forgave him (Bukhari, Muslim). The theme of **a sinner whose repentance is accepted despite the gravity of their sins** runs through both traditions.
