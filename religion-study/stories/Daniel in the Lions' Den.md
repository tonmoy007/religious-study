# Daniel in the Lions' Den

> The faithful servant who would rather face lions than stop praying — a story of integrity, envy, and divine protection.

---

## The Story (Daniel 6)

**Darius the Mede** rules Babylon. **Daniel** — now an old man, having served through the Babylonian and Persian courts — is one of three administrators over the kingdom. His exceptional character makes him stand out. The other administrators and satraps are jealous.

They trick the king into issuing an irrevocable decree: for 30 days, anyone who prays to any god or man except the king shall be thrown into the lions' den.

Daniel learns of the decree — and continues to pray three times a day toward Jerusalem, as was his custom, with his windows open.

The conspirators report him. The king is **distressed** — he wants to save Daniel but cannot break his own law. Daniel is thrown to the lions. The king says: *"Your God, whom you serve continually, will deliver you"* (6:16).

The king spends a sleepless night. At dawn, he runs to the den: *"O Daniel, servant of the living God — has your God been able to deliver you from the lions?"* Daniel replies: *"My God sent his angel and shut the lions' mouths"* (6:22). He is lifted out — no wound found.

The conspirators and their families are thrown in — the lions crush them before they hit the floor.

Darius issues a decree: *"In every part of my kingdom, people must fear and reverence the God of Daniel — for he is the living God"* (6:26).

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Faithful in exile** | Daniel serves a foreign king — but never compromises his worship |
| **Prayer as resistance** | The open window is a deliberate act of witness |
| **Divine deliverance** | God shuts the mouths of lions — vindicating His servant |
| **Witness to the nations** | The pagan king himself acknowledges the living God |

---

## Comparison with Islamic Tradition

Daniel (*Dānyāl*) is **not named in the Qur'an**, though some Islamic traditions regard him as a prophet. He is mentioned in **hadith** (e.g., Abu Hurairah reports that the Prophet ﷺ said Daniel was a prophet who was buried, and his body was preserved). The lions' den story is known in Islamic tradition but does not appear in the Qur'an.
