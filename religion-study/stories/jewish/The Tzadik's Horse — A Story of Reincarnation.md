# The Tzadik's Horse — A Story of Reincarnation

A mysterious horse that refuses to obey, a soul trapped in animal form, and the compassion of the [[Ba'al Shem Tov]] that frees it. This story teaches the Hasidic understanding of *gilgul neshamot* — the transmigration of souls — and the power of a tzadik's loving-kindness.

## The Story

The [[Ba'al Shem Tov]] once acquired a horse for his wagon. But the horse was strange: it refused to move, stood trembling in the traces, and wept — actually wept — tears rolling down its great brown eyes. The drivers beat it, but it would not budge.

The Besht came out and looked into the horse's eyes. He saw something that made him tremble. He ordered the horse to be unharnessed and led to a quiet stable. There, the Besht sat beside the animal for an entire night, speaking softly to it.

In the morning, the horse was found lying peacefully in the straw — its soul had departed. The Besht emerged with tears on his face. He told his students that the horse had once been a man — a miser who had hoarded his wealth and refused to give charity, who had mocked the poor and turned away orphans. His soul had been reincarnated into the body of a beast, condemned to experience the suffering he had ignored.

"Had he remained in that form for his full seventy years," the Besht said, "perhaps he would have been purified. But when I looked into his eyes, I saw that his teshuvah had begun. His tears were the tears of a soul remembering its origin. I completed his repair with my compassion, and he returned to his Source."

## Jewish Significance

- Illustrates *gilgul neshamot* — the kabbalistic doctrine of soul transmigration, later adopted by Hasidism
- The tzadik (righteous one) has the power to perceive the hidden history of souls and accelerate their repair
- Compassion — not punishment — is the force that fuses the broken vessels of a soul
- The story teaches that no state is beyond redemption: even a horse may hold a human soul on its journey back to God
