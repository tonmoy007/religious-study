# The Khmelnytsky Massacres (1648–49)

The Cossack uprising led by Bohdan Khmelnytsky swept through Ukraine and Poland, destroying hundreds of Jewish communities in a wave of violence unprecedented in its scale. The massacres reshaped Ashkenazi consciousness forever — and their memory still echoes in the Yom Kippur memorial prayers.

## The Story

In 1648, the Cossacks of the Zaporozhian Sich, led by the Cossack hetman Bohdan Khmelnytsky, rose against the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth. The Jewish population — caught between the warring powers — became a primary target. The Cossacks believed the Jews were agents of the Polish nobility and slaughtered entire communities without mercy.

The chronicler Nathan Nata Hanover, himself a survivor, recorded the horrors in his book *Yeven Metzulah* (The Deep Mire). Town after town fell: Nemyriv, Tulchyn, Polonne, Ostrog. In Nemyriv, Jews sought refuge in the fortress, but the Cossacks tricked them by waving Polish flags. When the gates opened, the killing began. Thousands were murdered, many choosing to sanctify God's name by leaping into the flames rather than accepting baptism.

Over three hundred communities were destroyed. Estimates of the dead range from 40,000 to over 100,000 — a catastrophe that, proportionally, rivaled the destruction of the First and Second Temples. The survivors fled westward, and the center of Ashkenazi life shifted from Poland-Lithuania to the German lands.

## Jewish Significance

- Called *Gezeirot Tach Ve-Tat* (the decrees of 1648–49) — one of the greatest tragedies in pre-modern Jewish history
- The massacres inspired a vast literature of lamentation, including special Yom Kippur memorial prayers (*Yizkor*)
- Deepened the messianic yearning that fed the Sabbatean movement in the following decades
- Reshaped Ashkenazi demographics and shtetl culture for centuries to come
- The martyrs of Khmelnytsky are included in the list of [[The Ten Martyrs]] liturgies
