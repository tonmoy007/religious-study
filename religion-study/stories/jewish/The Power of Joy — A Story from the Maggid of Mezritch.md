# The Power of Joy — A Story from the Maggid of Mezritch

A story that defined the Hasidic attitude toward life: a man who lost everything discovers that joy is not the absence of suffering but the refusal to let suffering have the last word. [[The Maggid of Mezritch]] taught that serving God with joy (*simcha*) is the highest form of worship.

## The Story

A wealthy merchant came to [[The Maggid of Mezritch]]. His ships had sunk, his warehouses had burned, his children had fallen ill. "Rebbe," he wept, "I have lost everything. How can I serve God when my heart is filled with bitterness?"

The Maggid did not answer. He called to his students: "Bring me the fiddle."

They brought him an old, battered fiddle. The Maggid began to play. The melody was so pure, so full of longing and hope, that the merchant's tears turned to wonder. The Maggid played on — and the room filled with light.

When he finished, the Maggid said: "God does not need your wealth or your health to be served. The Holy One needs only your heart. If you have nothing left but a broken vessel, give that. But give it with joy — because the joy itself is the repair."

The merchant left transformed. Years later, when his fortunes had been restored, he returned to the Maggid. "Rebbe," he said, "I learned something that day. When I had nothing, I had everything — because in giving my broken heart to God, I discovered that joy is not the absence of sorrow. It is the presence of something greater."

## Jewish Significance

- The foundational Hasidic teaching on *simcha* — joy as a spiritual discipline
- [[The Maggid of Mezritch]] systematized the Besht's teachings, making joy central to divine service
- Joy breaks the vessels of melancholy and opens the channel for divine blessing to flow
- The story embodies the Hasidic rejection of asceticism in favour of the joyful affirmation of life as service to God
