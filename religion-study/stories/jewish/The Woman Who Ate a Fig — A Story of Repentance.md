# The Woman Who Ate a Fig — A Story of Repentance

The tale of Rabbi Elazar ben Durdaya, who learned that no one — no matter how far they have fallen — is beyond the reach of return. His story is one of the most powerful teachings on *teshuvah* (repentance) in the entire Talmud.

## The Story

Rabbi Elazar ben Durdaya was known as a man of enormous appetites. The Talmud tells us that he visited every prostitute in the world — and there were no fewer than four hundred, each of whom he satisfied. One day he crossed the sea to be with a particular woman. As they sat together, she sighed and said: "Just as this breath does not return to its place, so Elazar ben Durdaya will never be accepted in repentance."

Those words pierced him. He went and sat between two mountains, calling upon the mountains to beg mercy for him. "We need mercy ourselves," they replied. He called upon heaven and earth, upon the sun and moon, upon the stars and constellations — but each answered that they could not plead for him; they needed mercy for themselves.

Then Elazar ben Durdaya placed his head between his knees and wept so bitterly that his soul departed. A heavenly voice rang out: "Rabbi Elazar ben Durdaya is invited to the World to Come."

When [[Rabbi Yehudah haNasi]] heard this, he wept and said: "There is one who acquires his world in many years, and one who acquires his world in a single moment."

## Jewish Significance

- The ultimate proof that *teshuvah* is always available — even at the very last moment of life
- Establishes that sincere repentance, even without a lifetime of good deeds, can redeem a soul
- The woman's casual remark becomes the instrument of his salvation — no one is a lost cause
- [[Rabbi Yehudah haNasi]]'s response shows that the late repentant is sometimes greater than the lifelong righteous
