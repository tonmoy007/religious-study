# The Golem of Prague

The most famous kabbalistic legend in Jewish folklore tells of Rabbi Judah Loew ben Bezalel — the Maharal of Prague — who created a living being from clay to defend the Jewish community of Prague from blood libels and persecution. In the 16th century, the Jews of Prague were often accused of using Christian blood for Passover matzah, leading to riots, arrests, and executions. The Maharal, the greatest scholar of his generation, received a mystical dream instructing him to create a protector.

Using the *Sefer Yetzirah* (Book of Creation) and the power of the divine name, the Maharal fashioned a figure of clay from the banks of the Vltava River. Together with his son-in-law and his leading student, he walked around the figure seven times, reciting combinations of the letters of God's name until the Golem opened its eyes. The Golem, called Joseph (or sometimes just "the Golem"), was mute — for the divine name gave it life but not speech — and it served the Maharal as a shammash (attendant) and protector. Each Friday evening, the Maharal would remove the *shem* (the parchment with God's name) from the Golem's mouth so it would rest on Shabbat.

One Friday afternoon, the Maharal forgot to remove the *shem*. The Golem went into a violent rampage through the Jewish quarter. The Maharal struggled with the creature and finally tore the *shem* from its mouth in the middle of the synagogue. The Golem crumbled to dust — and the Maharal never recreated it. The Golem's body is said to still lie in the attic of the Old New Synagogue in Prague, waiting perhaps for the day it is needed again. The story explores the limits of human creation: even the righteous may not fully control what they bring into being, and the power of the divine name is not to be handled casually.
