# The Ba'al Shem Tov and the Shepherd's Prayer

A story that embodies the Hasidic revolution: an illiterate shepherd who can only recite the alphabet offers a prayer more precious to God than the most elaborate liturgy of the greatest scholar. The simple heart reaches higher than the learned mind.

## The Story

The [[Ba'al Shem Tov]] (the Besht), founder of Hasidism, was once walking in the fields with his students when he came upon a young shepherd tending his flock. The boy looked troubled and was weeping.

"What troubles you?" asked the Besht.

The shepherd explained that he dearly wanted to pray, but he had never learned the holy words. He knew only the letters of the alphabet — *aleph, beit, gimel, dalet* — and nothing more. Every day he felt shame that he could not recite the morning blessings, the *Shema*, the *Amidah* like the other Jews.

The Besht smiled and placed a hand on the boy's shoulder. "My child," he said, "do you love the Holy One, blessed be He?"

"With all my heart!" the shepherd cried.

"Then go up on that hill," said the Besht, "and say the letters. Say them with all your strength and all your love, and let God arrange them into the proper prayers."

The shepherd ran to the hilltop. He threw his arms open to heaven and shouted the alphabet — *aleph, beit, gimel, dalet* — over and over, his voice cracking with longing. And the Besht, standing below, saw a pillar of fire rising from the spot where the boy stood. The angels themselves paused to listen.

## Jewish Significance

- The foundational Hasidic teaching on prayer: sincerity and *kavanah* (intention) outweigh erudition
- Undermines the rabbinic elite's monopoly on holiness — the unlettered Jew has direct access to God
- Demonstrates *devekut* (cleaving to God) as the essence of prayer, not the recitation of fixed texts
- The alphabet itself becomes a vessel for the soul's longing when spoken with love
