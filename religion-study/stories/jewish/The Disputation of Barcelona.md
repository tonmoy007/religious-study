# The Disputation of Barcelona

In 1263, the great Spanish rabbi Moses ben Nachman — Nachmanides (Ramban) — was compelled to participate in a public religious disputation before King James I of Aragon. His opponent was Pablo Christiani, a Jewish convert to Christianity who argued that the Hebrew Bible itself testified to the truth of Christianity — particularly that the Messiah had already come in the person of Jesus. The debate was held in the royal palace of Barcelona, and the Jewish community of Catalonia watched with terror, knowing that a loss could mean mass conversions or violence.

Nachmanides argued with brilliance and courage. He accepted the terms that only the Hebrew Bible would be used as evidence, rejecting the Christian reliance on the New Testament and later interpretations. He demonstrated that the prophecies Christians claimed as messianic — particularly from Isaiah 53 — described the suffering of the people of Israel, not an individual savior. When Pablo Christiani argued that the Messiah must have come because the Temple was destroyed, Nachmanides replied that the destruction was a punishment for sin, not evidence of messianic arrival. He pointed to the continued exile and suffering of the Jewish people as proof that the messianic age had not begun: if the Messiah had come, why was the world still unredeemed?

King James I awarded Nachmanides a monetary prize and acknowledged that he had never heard "an unjust cause so nobly defended." However, the Dominican order — led by the same Pablo Christiani — continued to pressure the king, and Nachmanides was eventually forced to flee Aragon. The *Sefer ha-Vikkuach* (Book of the Disputation), Nachmanides' own account of the debate, became a foundational text of Jewish polemics and a source of pride for generations of Jews facing theological pressure from the Church.
