# Nachum Ish Gamzu — Everything for the Best

Nachum Ish Gamzu was known for his unshakable declaration in the face of any misfortune: **"Gam zu le-tovah"** (*גַּם זוֹ לְטוֹבָה* — "This too is for the good"). The Talmud (Taanit 21a) tells that he suffered extreme afflictions: he was blind in both eyes, his hands and feet were amputated, and his entire body was covered with boils. He lay in a dilapidated house whose legs were propped in bowls of water to keep the ants from climbing up to eat him. Yet never did his faith waver.

One day his students came to visit and found his house collapsing. They began to remove his bedding, but he stopped them: "First take out the utensils," he said, "then my bed. For you can be certain that as long as I am in the house, it will not fall." They did as he said, and only after they removed him did the house collapse. His students asked: "Master, if you are so righteous, why do you suffer so?" He replied that once, while transporting a chest of gems for a poor man, he had been too slow in setting down the chest to rest, and the delay caused the poor man to die of hunger. His punishment was measure for measure — and he accepted it with love.

Nachum Ish Gamzu was the teacher of Rabbi Akiva — the greatest sage of the next generation. His radical trust in Providence became the foundation of Akiva's own faith, including Akiva's famous teaching that everything God does is for the good. The name "Ish Gamzu" itself became a permanent monument to his motto: "This man of Gamzu — this too is for the good."
