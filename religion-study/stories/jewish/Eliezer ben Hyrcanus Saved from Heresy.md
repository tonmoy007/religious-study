# Eliezer ben Hyrcanus Saved from Heresy

The "Oven of Akhnai" (*Tanuro shel Akhnai*) is the most famous story in the Talmud about the nature of rabbinic authority. Rabbi Eliezer ben Hyrcanus, the greatest sage of his generation, finds himself alone in a halakhic dispute over whether a certain oven can become impure. Confident in his position, he invokes supernatural proofs: a carob tree uproots itself and flies across the field, a stream flows backward, and the walls of the study hall themselves begin to fall. Yet the Sages are unmoved.

When a Heavenly Voice (*Bat Kol*) declares, "Why do you argue with Rabbi Eliezer? The law is always with him," Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananyah stands and thunders: **"It is not in heaven"** (Lo baShamayim hi — Deuteronomy 30:12). The Torah has been given to human beings at Sinai, and Heaven no longer has a vote. The majority of Sages decide the law — even against a Heavenly Voice and the greatest sage alive.

The Sages voted to excommunicate Rabbi Eliezer. That day — the day Rabbi Eliezer was banned — the Talmud says that every place he cast his eyes was burned, and Rabban Gamaliel, the Nasi who led the vote, was later drowned at sea as a consequence. Rabbi Eliezer's wife, Ima Shalom (sister of Rabban Gamaliel), had been the only one who knew why her brother came to harm, and she understood the tragic cost of the Sages' triumph. The story enshrines the principle of human reason and majority rule in Jewish law — and the price one righteous individual may pay for the system to endure.
