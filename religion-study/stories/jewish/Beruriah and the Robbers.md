# Beruriah and the Robbers

Beruriah, the wife of Rabbi Meir and daughter of Rabbi Chananiah ben Teradyon, is the only woman consistently cited in Talmudic legal and theological debate. The Talmud (Berakhot 10a) relates that a group of robbers lived in Rabbi Meir's neighborhood and caused him great distress. In his anguish, Rabbi Meir prayed that they should die. Beruriah overheard him and corrected his prayer with a profound scriptural insight.

She cited Psalm 104:35: "Sins shall cease from the earth, and the wicked shall be no more." She read the verse carefully: "Let **sins** cease" (יִתַּמּוּ חַטָּאִים), not "let **sinners** cease." The Hebrew word *chata'im* can be read as either "sinners" or "sins." "Pray, then," she told her husband, "that they repent — not that they perish. If they repent, the wicked will indeed be no more, because their wickedness will have ended."

Rabbi Meir prayed for their repentance, and the robbers indeed repented. Beruriah's lesson — that prayer should seek the annihilation of sin, not the sinner — became a foundational Jewish ethical teaching. Her exegesis demonstrates that the Torah's language, carefully read, always points toward compassion and human transformation rather than destruction. She remains the Talmud's premier female intellect: a scholar who taught from the rooftops, debated sages on the nature of women's ritual obligations, and died a martyr alongside her father in the Hadrianic persecutions.
