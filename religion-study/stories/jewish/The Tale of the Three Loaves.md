# The Tale of the Three Loaves

King Munbaz (Monobaz II), the Jewish king of Adiabene who converted to Judaism, spent his entire personal fortune — and the treasuries inherited from his ancestors — to feed the poor during a devastating famine. His relatives and ministers rebuked him: "Your ancestors stored up treasure and added to the treasures of their fathers, but you are squandering everything on the poor!" The Talmud (Bava Batra 10a–11a) records King Munbaz's extraordinary reply.

"My ancestors stored up treasure on earth," he said, "but I store up treasure in heaven. My ancestors stored treasure where hands can reach it; I store treasure where no hand can reach it. My ancestors stored treasure that bears no fruit; I store treasure that bears fruit — as it is written: 'Your righteousness shall go before you' (Isaiah 58:8). My ancestors stored up money; I stored up souls. My ancestors stored for others; I stored for myself. My ancestors stored in this world; I stored for the World to Come."

The Sages, including Rabban Gamaliel, cited King Munbaz's example in their debates about the nature of wealth and charity. The story illustrates the rabbinic principle that charity (*tzedakah*) is not a donation but an act of justice — the wealthy are merely trustees of God's resources. King Munbaz's "three loaves" (a reference to the three meals he gave away, or the three coins he spent) became a lasting symbol of radical generosity rooted not in guilt but in the confident knowledge that what is given to the poor is deposited in an account that cannot be exhausted.
