# The Zealots at Masada

The last stand of the Jewish rebels atop the mountain fortress of Masada — 960 men, women, and children who chose death over enslavement. The story, recorded by the historian Josephus, has become the defining symbol of Jewish resistance and the rallying cry "Masada shall not fall again."

## The Story

After the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE, a band of Zealot rebels — the Sicarii — fled Jerusalem and took refuge in Herod's mountaintop palace at Masada, overlooking the Dead Sea. For three years they held out under the leadership of [[Eleazar ben Yair]].

In 73 CE, the Roman governor Flavius Silva marched the Tenth Legion to Masada. The Romans built a massive siege ramp of earth and stone — portions of which remain visible today — and breached the fortress wall with a battering ram.

Seeing that the end was near, Eleazar gathered the community. In a speech preserved by Josephus, he declared: "Since we long ago resolved never to be servants to the Romans, nor to any other than God Himself, the only true and just Lord of mankind, the time is come that obliges us to make that resolution true in practice."

The men embraced their wives and children, then killed them with their own hands. Ten men were chosen by lot to kill the remaining 950. Then one man killed the other nine, set fire to the fortress, and fell on his own sword. When the Romans entered at dawn, they found only silence and the bodies of the dead — "they congratulated none of the others for so courageous an act."

## Jewish Significance

- The ultimate expression of *Kiddush Hashem* — the sanctification of God's name through martyrdom
- Became a central symbol of Zionist ideology: "Masada shall not fall again" (*Shem Yisrael*)
- The story raises profound questions about suicide, martyrdom, and the ethics of killing one's own family
- Modern archaeology (Yadin's 1963–65 excavations) has confirmed many details of Josephus' account
- Debated within Jewish tradition: some halakhic authorities condemn the mass suicide as forbidden under Jewish law
