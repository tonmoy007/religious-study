# The Rabbi's Son Who Left — A Breslov Story

A father's love for his lost son is the most powerful image of God's love for the distant Jew. This Breslov story captures the paradox that the one who has wandered farthest may be the closest of all.

## The Story

There was a great rabbi whose son abandoned the path of Torah and travelled to a faraway land. The years passed. The father prayed every day, but no word came.

At last, the son returned — ragged, thin, his face marked by suffering. He stood in the doorway of his father's study, ashamed to enter. The rabbi looked up, and his face broke into a smile of pure joy.

"My son," he said, embracing him, "you have come home."

But the son pulled away. "Father," he whispered, "you do not understand. I have done terrible things. I have broken every commandment. I am not worthy to be called your son."

The rabbi took his son's hands. "Do you know what I thought," he said, "every moment you were gone? I thought: he may be far, but he is my son. That has never changed. The distance is only in your mind. Your soul has never left my house."

The son wept. And the father said: "The [[Ba'al Shem Tov]] taught that there are two kinds of return. One is the return of the righteous who have never sinned — they return from closeness to closer. But the other is the return of the one who has fallen — and that return is higher, because the distance itself becomes the vessel for the light."

## Jewish Significance

- The core Breslov teaching on *teshuvah*: the descent is for the sake of the ascent
- The father's love mirrors God's unconditional love — no sin can sever the bond between a Jew and the Holy One
- [[Rebbe Nachman]] of Breslov taught that the place of the *Ba'al Teshuvah* (master of return) is higher than the place of the completely righteous
- The story reframes shame not as a barrier but as the opening for divine compassion
