# Rabbi Amnon of Mainz and Unetanneh Tokef

The story of Rabbi Amnon of Mainz is one of the most haunting martyr narratives in Jewish literature. According to the 13th-century work *Or Zarua*, Rabbi Amnon was a prominent and respected Jewish leader in 11th-century Mainz, a center of Ashkenazi Jewish learning. The local bishop repeatedly pressured him to convert to Christianity. Finally, worn down by the relentless demands, Rabbi Amnon asked for three days to consider — and immediately was seized with horror at his own hesitation. He did not return to the bishop at the appointed time.

When the bishop's guards brought him by force, the bishop asked why he had broken his word. Rabbi Amnon replied that his tongue should be cut out for speaking as he did — a tongue that had wavered in its loyalty to God. The bishop, however, ordered a more terrible punishment: Rabbi Amnon's hands and feet were cut off, joint by joint, as he was given one final chance to convert. Through each amputation, Rabbi Amnon refused.

On Rosh Hashanah, mortally wounded, Rabbi Amnon asked to be carried into the synagogue. As the congregation stood in the silent *Amidah* prayer, he asked the cantor to wait. With his last strength, he recited a new prayer: **Unetanneh Tokef** (*וּנְתַנֶּה תֹּקֶף* — "We shall tell the power"). The prayer describes the heavenly court in session on the Day of Judgment: "Who shall live and who shall die, who by fire and who by water, who by sword and who by beast..." As he finished the final line — "Repentance, prayer, and charity avert the severe decree" — his soul departed. The prayer was immediately incorporated into the High Holy Day liturgy and is recited in synagogues worldwide to this day.
