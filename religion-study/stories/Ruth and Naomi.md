# Ruth and Naomi

> A story of loyalty, redemption, and the Moabite woman who became the great-grandmother of King David — and an ancestor of Jesus.

---

## The Story (Book of Ruth)

### The Setting

A famine drives **Elimelech** and **Naomi** from Bethlehem to Moab. Elimelech dies. Their two sons marry Moabite women — **Ruth** and **Orpah** — then both sons die. Naomi decides to return to Bethlehem.

She urges her daughters-in-law to stay in Moab and remarry. Orpah leaves — but **Ruth clings to her**:

> [!quote] Ruth 1:16–17
> "Where you go, I will go; where you lodge, I will lodge. Your people shall be my people, and your God my God. Where you die, I will die — and there will I be buried."

### The Harvest

In Bethlehem, Ruth gleans in the fields of **Boaz**, a wealthy relative of Elimelech. Boaz notices her loyalty, protects her, and instructs his workers to leave extra grain for her.

Naomi sees an opportunity: Ruth should go to Boaz at the threshing floor and ask him to be her **kinsman-redeemer** (*go'el*) — a levirate-like marriage that would preserve the family line.

### The Redemption

Boaz is willing — but there is a closer relative who has the first right. At the city gate, Boaz negotiates: the other relative declines. Boaz marries Ruth.

They have a son: **Obed** — the father of Jesse, the father of **David**. Ruth — a Moabite, a foreigner, a convert — becomes the great-grandmother of Israel's greatest king.

---

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Hesed (covenant loyalty)** | Ruth's loyalty echoes God's faithful love |
| **Inclusion of the outsider** | A Moabite (from a nation cursed in Deuteronomy 23:3) enters the covenant community |
| **Kinsman-redeemer** | The *go'el* foreshadows Christ as redeemer |
| **Providence in the ordinary** | God works through everyday acts of kindness and loyalty |
| **Genealogical significance** | Ruth is one of four women named in Matthew's genealogy of Jesus — alongside Tamar, Rahab, and Bathsheba |
