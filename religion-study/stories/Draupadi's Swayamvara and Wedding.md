# Draupadi's Swayamvara and Wedding

> The archer who won a queen — and the strange marriage that bound five brothers to one wife. The Pandavas' fortunes change forever.

---

## The Story (Mahabharata — Adi & Sabha Parva)

### The Escape from the Lacquer Palace

The Kauravas, led by **Duryodhana** and his uncle **Shakuni**, plot to kill the Pandavas. They build a palace of lacquer (resin) and invite the Pandavas to stay — then set it on fire. The Pandavas escape through a tunnel, flee to the forest, and wander disguised as brahmins.

### The Swayamvara

In **Panchala**, King **Drupada** holds a Swayamvara for his daughter **Draupadi**. The challenge: string a massive bow and shoot five arrows through a revolving wheel at a fish's eye — looking only at its reflection in a pool of water below.

Hundreds of kings fail — including **Karna** (Draupadi refuses him: "I will not marry a charioteer's son"). Arjuna, disguised as a brahmin, steps forward. He strings the bow and shoots all five arrows through the wheel. The assembly erupts.

### The Bride Who Married Five

The Pandavas return home with Draupadi — calling to their mother Kunti: "Look what we have brought!" Without looking, Kunti says: "Share it among yourselves — equally."

A mother's word is law. Arjuna — who won her — must share Draupadi with his four brothers. Draupadi marries all five Pandavas.

### The Division

The news reaches Hastinapura. Dhritarashtra offers to divide the kingdom: the Pandavas get **Khandavaprastha** (a barren wilderness). The Pandavas transform it into **Indraprastha** — a golden city. Yudhishthira becomes king, Draupadi his queen.

---

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Arjuna's skill** | The greatest archer in the world — proved in the sight of all kings |
| **Polyandry** | Draupadi's marriage to five men is unique — explained as destiny from a previous birth |
| **Kunti's word** | The mother's command — the foundation of dharma in the family |
| **Indraprastha** | Merit turns wilderness into paradise — the Pandavas earn their kingdom |
