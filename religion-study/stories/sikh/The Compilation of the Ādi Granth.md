# The Compilation of the Ādi Granth

> *"In the True Lord's court this is the acceptable prayer: to read, to understand, and to live the Word. The Guru's Word is the Guru himself — there is no difference between them."*
> — **Gurū Arjan**, Guru Granth Sāhib, Rāga Mājh, p. 41

The **Compilation of the Ādi Granth** (1601–1604 CE) is the single most important literary event in Sikh history — the moment when the scattered hymns of four Gurus, fifteen Bhagats (devotional saints), and eleven Bhātts (bardic poets) were gathered, edited, authenticated, and bound into a single volume that would become the **eternal Guru** of the Sikhs. It was the work of **Gurū Arjan** (1563–1606), the fifth Sikh Guru, and his chief scribe **Bhāī Gurdās** (1551–1637).

Unlike the scriptural codifications of other traditions — the Councils of Valabhī or Pāṭaliputra in Jainism, the Council of Nicaea in Christianity, or the canonisation of the Qur'an under Caliph Uthmān — the compilation of the Ādi Granth was not a **reactive** act of salvage after crisis. It was a **proactive** act of foundation: a Guru who saw that the Sikh community needed a permanent, authenticated scripture while the voices of the Gurus were still alive in living memory, and before rival claimants could corrupt the tradition.

---

## The Crisis of Authenticity

By 1581 CE, when Gurū Arjan became the fifth Guru, the Sikh community had grown into a pan-Punjabi movement. Four Gurus — Nānak, Aṅgad, Amar Dās, and Rām Dās — had composed hymns preserved in *pothīs* (manuscript collections). But authenticity was in doubt:

| Issue | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Spurious compositions** | Poets and rivals composed hymns in the early Gurus' names |
| **Rival collections** | **Prithi Chand** (Gurū Arjan's elder brother) produced his own *pothīs* with interpolated hymns |
| **Regional variations** | Different Sikh communities preserved different recensions |
| **Oral drift** | Hymns changed as singers forgot exact wordings or embellished them |

### The Rival: Prithi Chand

**Prithi Chand** (1558–1618), Gurū Arjan's elder brother, had been passed over for the Guruship. He established a **rival Guruship** at Hehar with his own following and his own hymn collection — the **Miharbān Janam Sākhī tradition** (named after his son). His faction spread rumours: that Arjan had altered the Gurus' hymns, that the true teachings of Nānak survived only in Prithi Chand's lineage. This was not merely political rivalry — it was a **scriptural crisis** that threatened the unity of the Sikh tradition.

---

## Gurū Arjan's Vision

Gurū Arjan was uniquely qualified for the task. A poet of remarkable range — his compositions include the **Sukhmani Sāhib**, the **Bārahmāha**, and the **Lāvān** marriage hymns — he was also a scholar fluent in Braj Bhasha, Punjabi, Persian, and Sanskrit, with deep knowledge of both the Hindu bhakti tradition and Islamic Sufism. No earlier Guru had attempted a definitive compilation.

Around **1601 CE**, he made the decision: he would gather every hymn attributed to the Gurus, compare every version, authenticate every line, and produce a single, definitive anthology. The work would be done **openly**, in the Harimandir Sāhib precincts in **Amritsar** (then under construction).

---

## Bhāī Gurdās: The Scribe

**Bhāī Gurdās** (1551–1637) was, after Gurū Arjan himself, the most important figure in the compilation. A distant relative of the Guru family, he was a scholar of the Vedas, Purāṇas, and Qur'an — and a poet whose **39 Vārs** (ballads) are considered the first systematic commentary on Sikh theology. Gurū Arjan chose him as scribe (*lipikār*) for his perfect Gurmukhī script and his theological depth.

The compilation followed a meticulous six-step process:

1. **Collection** — Trusted Sikhs gathered every *pothī* across northern India, authenticating each manuscript's chain of transmission
2. **Recitation** — Memorised hymns were recited before the Guru for confirmation or rejection
3. **Authentication** — Each hymn was tested for theological alignment with earlier Gurus, stylistic consistency, and unbroken transmission
4. **Comparison** — Multiple versions of the same hymn were compared; variant readings resolved by the Guru's authority
5. **Dictation** — Gurū Arjan recited the approved text; Bhāī Gurdās transcribed it in Gurmukhī
6. **Organization** — Hymns were arranged by rāga (musical mode), then by author, then by length

Hundreds of Sikhs gathered daily to witness the work in what is now called the **Granth Ghar** (House of the Book). The compilation was a **public, liturgical act** — not a secret editorial project.

---

## The Structure of the Ādi Granth

The Ādi Granth is a **carefully structured liturgical collection** organised by rāga:

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **33 Rāgas** | Musical sections (Āsā, Gūjrī, Sūhī, Bilāval, Rāmkālī, Mājh, etc.) |
| **Author groups** | Within each rāga: Gurus in order, then Bhagats, then Bhātts |
| **Length groups** | *Chaupadas* (4-stanza), *Asta-padīs* (8-stanza), longer compositions |
| **Prefatory sections** | Mūl Mantra, Japjī Sāhib, So Dar, So Purakh |
| **Closing sections** | Slokas of Gurū Nānak, **Mundāvaṇī** (the Seal declaring completeness) |

### The Languages

The Granth is multi-lingual — **Sant Bhāṣā** (blended Punjabi-Braj-Hindi) predominates, but Punjabi, Braj, Persian, Multani, and even traces of Marathi appear.

### The Authors

| Type | Names | Count |
|------|-------|-------|
| **Sikh Gurus** | Nānak, Aṅgad, Amar Dās, Rām Dās, Arjan | ~4,600 hymns |
| **Hindu Bhagats** | Kabīr, Nāmdev, Raidās, Trilochan, Pīpā, Benī, Rāmānand, Dhannā, Sain, Sūrdās, Parmanand, Bhīkhan, Jaidayv | ~600 hymns |
| **Muslim Bhagats** | Bābā Farīd (Chishtī Sufi) | ~200+ hymns |
| **Bhātts (Bards)** | Kalhār, Kaṅgā, Bhīkhā, Gaṅgā, and others | ~100+ hymns |

The inclusion of **non-Sikh saints** was a radical theological act: Gurū Arjan declared that truth is not the property of any single religion. A saint like Kabīr (a Muslim weaver), Raidās (a Hindu tanner), or Bābā Farīd (a Muslim Sufi) could speak the Word of God if their teaching aligned with the One Nām.

> *"Kabīr says: listen, O seeker — do not ask about caste. The True Lord is contained in every heart."*
> — Bhagat Kabīr, Guru Granth Sāhib, Rāga Sūhī, p. 729

The selection was **theologically rigorous**: hymns promoting idolatry, caste hierarchy, or religious exclusivism — even from revered saints — were excluded. The Ādi Granth thus became a **universal scripture** authenticated by the Guru's spiritual discernment.

---

## The Controversy with Prithi Chand

Prithi Chand attempted to subvert the compilation through infiltration (sending agents to overhear which hymns were included), public accusation (claiming Arjan altered Nānak's words), theft (attempting to steal manuscripts), and rival *pothīs* (the Miharbān Granth). Gurū Arjan's response was characteristic: he did not retaliate. He continued openly, trusting the authentic voice of Nānak would be recognised.

One tradition recounts the **Test of the Shoe**: when a Sikh presented a hymn claimed to be Nānak's, Gurū Arjan immediately declared it inauthentic. When pressed for proof, he placed a shoe on the manuscript — the humblest object — saying: *"What is true needs no stamp of authority. What is false cannot be made true by any seal."*

---

## The Role of Mīān Mīr

**Mīān Mīr** (1550–1635), a renowned Qādirī Sufi saint of Lahore, was Gurū Arjan's close friend. Their relationship is one of the most powerful symbols of interfaith harmony in Sikh tradition.

According to Sikh accounts, when the **Harimandir Sāhib** was being built, Gurū Arjan invited Mīān Mīr to **lay its foundation stone** in December 1588 — an extraordinary gesture: the holiest Sikh site was blessed by a Muslim saint. The Sufi and the Guru embraced before thousands of Sikhs, Hindus, and Muslims.

Mīān Mīr's connection to the Granth compilation was threefold:

1. **Spiritual endorsement** — His presence at the Harimandir — where the Granth was being compiled — gave the project interfaith legitimacy
2. **Sufi inclusion** — The presence of Bābā Farīd's hymns in the Granth was the textual counterpart to Mīān Mīr's presence at the Harimandir
3. **Bhakti-Sufi synthesis** — The Ādi Granth is the **textual culmination** of three centuries of North Indian Bhakti-Sufi dialogue

> *"Mīān Mīr came, and the Guru rose to greet him. They embraced — the Sufi and the Sikh — and the whole assembly saw that there is no difference between those who love God."*
> — **Gurbilās Chhevīn Pātshāhī**

---

## Completion and Installation (1604)

After **three years** of work, the Ādi Granth was complete. The **Kartārpurī Bīr** — the original manuscript — was bound between wooden covers, written entirely in Gurmukhī.

The final hymn — the **Mundāvaṇī** (The Seal) — declared the treasury complete:

> *"The plate contains three things: truth, contentment, and contemplation. The Nām, the Name of the Lord, is also placed upon it. One who eats and savours this is saved. This treasure can never be exhausted."*

In **August 1604** (Bhādon Sudī 1st, Samvat 1661), the Granth was installed at the **Harimandir Sāhib** in a public festival. Gurū Arjan placed it on a throne (*takht*) and declared:

> *"Let this be the Guru of the Sikhs. Where the Word is, there is the Guru. The Word, the Guru, and the congregation are one."*

He bowed before it — the first act of reverence toward the scripture as the embodiment of the Guru's presence.

From that day, the protocol was established: the Granth is uncovered with a *chaurī* (ceremonial whisk), opened each morning (*parkash*), closed each evening (*sukhasan*), and daily verses (*hukamnāmā*) are read as the Guru's command. No one sits higher than the Granth; Sikhs prostrate before it as before a living Guru.

---

## Significance

### A Closed Canon

The Ādi Granth of 1604 was a **closed canon** — the Mundāvaṇī explicitly sealed it. Later, the hymns of **Gurū Tegh Bahādur** (the ninth Guru) were added by **Gurū Gobind Siṅgh** (the tenth), creating the **Guru Granth Sāhib** as it exists today (1,430 pages). Gurū Gobind Siṅgh did not add his own hymns; he composed the separate **Dasam Granth**. And in **1708**, he declared that after him there would be no human Guru — the Guru Granth Sāhib would be the **eternal Guru**:

> *"Sab sikkhāṇ kō hukam hai: Gurū māṇiyo Granth."*
> *"All Sikhs are commanded: recognise the Granth as the Guru."*

The compilation thus set in motion the **absorption of the Guru into the Word** — almost unique in world religious history.

### Comparative View

| Tradition | Event | Date | Outcome |
|-----------|-------|------|---------|
| **Sikhism** | Compilation of Ādi Granth | 1601–1604 CE | Living Guru compiles canon; canon replaces living Guru |
| **Jainism (Śvetāmbara)** | Council of Valabhī | c. 453–467 CE | Council fixes written canon after centuries of oral transmission |
| **Christianity** | Councils of Nicaea/Carthage | 325–397 CE | Canon debated; finalised by episcopal authority |
| **Islam** | Uthmānic recension | c. 650 CE | Authoritative Qur'anic text established; variants destroyed |

What makes the Sikh case unique: the canon was compiled by a **living spiritual authority** whose discernment was considered infallible — not by a council debating manuscripts.

### The Legacy

The Ādi Granth **preserved the authentic voices** of the first five Gurus within living memory of them, sparing Sikhism the crisis of lost scripture that affected so many traditions. It became the **foundation of Sikh identity** — shaping theology through its hymns, music through its rāgas, ethics through its teachings, and worship through its presence in every Gurdwārā.

And as a scripture that includes Hindu and Muslim saints, it remains a **model of interfaith openness**: a tradition both firm in its own identity and generous toward the truth in other traditions.

> *"Recognise the light in all; do not ask about caste or creed. The true Guru has shown me that the same Lord is everywhere."*
> — **Gurū Arjan**, Guru Granth Sāhib, Rāga Bhairon, p. 1133
