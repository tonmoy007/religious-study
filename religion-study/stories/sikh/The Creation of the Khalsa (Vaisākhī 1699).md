# The Creation of the Khalsa (Vaisākhī 1699)

> *"I shall call my followers the Khalsa. They shall be saints and soldiers — sant-sipāhī. They shall be the army of the Eternal One (Akal Purakh kī Fauj), and the sovereignty shall pass to them."*
> — **Gurū Gobind Siṅgh**, from the *Bachitar Nāṭak* tradition

On the first day of **Vaisākhī 1699** (30 March 1699 by the Julian calendar, 1 Vaisākh 1756 Vikram Samvat), the greatest transformation in Sikh history unfolded on the plains of the Punjab. A nine-year-old boy — who had watched his father beheaded in Delhi — had grown into a thirty-two-year-old warrior-saint. And on that spring morning, in the hill town of **Anandpur Sāhib** (the City of Bliss), Gurū Gobind Siṅgh took the Sikh Panth and broke it open — poured it into a new mould of steel and sweetness — and released it into history as the **Khalsa** (the Pure Ones).

What follows is the story of that day: the drama of the five head-offerings, the baptism of the double-edged sword, the covenant of the five K's, and the theological earthquake that still reverberates across the Sikh world three centuries later.

---

## The Road to Vaisākhī — Why the Khalsa Was Necessary

The creation of the Khalsa was not a spontaneous inspiration. It was the culmination of a century of pressure, persecution, and theological evolution.

| Phase | Period | Event | Impact on Sikh Identity |
|-------|--------|-------|------------------------|
| **Founding** | 1469–1539 | Gurū Nānak's ministry | Established a monotheistic, egalitarian, bhakti-based path distinct from both Hinduism and Islam |
| **Institutionalisation** | 1539–1606 | Gurus Aṅgad through Arjan | Created a scriptural canon (Ādi Granth), pilgrimage centres (Amritsar), and a growing community with its own customs |
| **First Martyrdom** | 1606 | Execution of Gurū Arjan by Emperor Jahāngīr | Transformed the Panth from a pacifist community into one that recognised the necessity of political self-defence |
| **Mīrī-Pīrī** | 1606–1644 | Gurū Har Gobind wears two swords | Established the doctrine of *Mīrī* (temporal power) and *Pīrī* (spiritual authority) — the Guru was now both a saint and a sovereign |
| **Second Martyrdom** | 1675 | Execution of Gurū Tegh Bahādur by Emperor Aurangzeb | Established the Guru as *Hind kī Chādar* — the shield of all faiths; demonstrated that the Mughal Empire would not tolerate Sikh sovereignty |
| **Preparation** | 1675–1699 | Gurū Gobind Siṅgh's early Guruship | Fortified Anandpur, composed martial poetry, fought defensive battles at Bhangāṇī (1688) and Nadaun (1691), built a standing army of Sikh warriors |

By 1699, the Sikh community was a paradox. It had spiritual authority (Pīrī) in abundance — the hymns of the Gurus, the sanctity of the gurdwārās, the growing devotion of the saṅgat. But it lacked institutional sovereignty. It lacked a visible, binding identity that could resist both Mughal coercion and the gravitational pull of caste Hinduism. The Sikhs were still, in many ways, a collection of Khatri and Jat communities with Guru-devotion — not yet a **nation** with a shared body, banner, and blood oath.

Gurū Gobind Siṅgh had already written — in his autobiographical *Bachitar Nāṭak* (The Wonderful Drama) — that he had been sent by God for a specific purpose. The passage, now canonical, reads:

> *"I shall take birth again and again, and I shall protect the saints and destroy the tyrants. This is why the Eternal One sent me into this world. For the sake of the faith, I shall wield the sword. I shall crush the evil-doers and raise the humble."*

The creation of the Khalsa was the moment that purpose took visible form.

---

## The Setting: Anandpur Sāhib, Vaisākhī 1699

Anandpur Sāhib — the City of Bliss — sat in the foothills of the Śivālik range, a natural fortress surrounded by hills, rivers, and forests. It had been founded by Gurū Tegh Bahādur in 1665 and greatly expanded by his son. By 1699 it was a walled town with a formidable **kaṛhā** (fortress) and a large open ground at the foot of the hill — the **Keshgarh Sāhib** platform — where the Guru held his assemblies.

The **Vaisākhī** festival had long been a harvest celebration and a major gathering for the Sikhs. For weeks before the event, word had spread that the Guru would make a momentous announcement. Sikhs poured into Anandpur from across northern India — from Lahore and Multan in the west, from Delhi and Agra in the south, from Kashmir and the hill states in the north, and from Bengal and Bihar in the east. Estimates of the crowd range from ten thousand to eighty thousand. The Guru had ordered that a large tent be pitched on the open ground and that a high platform (*dharmāsana*) be erected for the day's proceedings.

The morning of **30 March 1699** dawned clear and warm. The Guru emerged from his residence at **Takht Srī Keshgarh Sāhib** dressed in full warrior regalia — a blue tunic, a steel helmet, a sword at his waist, and a shield on his back. A golden *kaṛā* (steel bracelet) gleamed on his wrist. His long black beard was untrimmed. His eyes — by every account — blazed with a fire that the crowd had never seen before.

---

## The Five Head-Offerings — The Call

The Guru ascended the platform, drew his sword — a gleaming **khāṇḍā** (double-edged sword) — and stood in silence before the multitude. The crowd, which had been buzzing with conversation, fell into a hush so deep that the only sound was the wind moving through the hills.

Then the Guru spoke — and his voice carried across the entire assembly:

> **"Is there any Sikh here who will offer his head to the Guru? Is there any true Sikh who will give his life for the faith?"**

The crowd gasped. A murmur rippled through the thousands. Men looked at each other, uncertain. The Guru had called for volunteers before — for military service, for dangerous missions — but never for a head-offering. No one moved.

The Guru called a second time — the same words, the same demand. The silence was now unbearable. Mothers clutched their children. Warriors looked at their feet.

A third time — louder, more urgent:

> *"Is there any true Sikh of the Guru who will give his head?"*

On the third call — or the fourth, depending on the account — a man stood up from the crowd. He was **Dayā Rām**, a Khatri (merchant-caste) Sikh from Lahore — a handsome man in his thirties, well-dressed, a respectable householder. He walked forward through the parted crowd, knelt before the Guru, and said:

> *"My head is yours, O Guru. It was never mine to keep."*

The Guru took him by the hand and led him into the tent. The crowd watched in stunned silence. A moment later — a sound that no one who heard it ever forgot: the thud of a heavy blade, the crack of bone, the wet sound of something heavy falling to the earth.

The Guru emerged from the tent alone. His sword was dripping with blood.

He called for a second head.

The crowd was in turmoil. Some wept. Some turned to flee. Some stood rigid, unable to process what they had just seen. But a second man — **Dharam Dās**, a Jat farmer from Hastināpur — rose, walked forward, and entered the tent. The same sound. The same blood.

A third call. **Mohkam Chand**, a tailor (Chhīmba caste) from Dwarka in Gujarat — a man of low social rank by the standards of the time — stood up and walked into the tent. The blow. The blood.

A fourth call. **Sāhib Chand**, a barber (Nāī caste) from Bidar in the Deccan — lower still in the caste hierarchy — came forward.

A fifth call. **Himmat Rāi**, a water-carrier (Jhīvar caste) from Jagannath Puri in Odisha — the lowest of the low by Brahminical standards — stepped forward.

Five men had entered the tent. Five times the sword had fallen. Five times the Guru had emerged with blood on his blade. The crowd was now in chaos — many believed the Guru had gone mad, that he had slaughtered his own Sikhs. Some mounted their horses and rode away, never to return. Others wept openly, convinced the Guru had lost his mind.

---

## The Revelation — The Pañj Piāre (Five Beloved Ones)

Then the Guru did something that changed everything.

He walked back into the tent, and when he emerged, he was **not alone**. Behind him walked all five men — alive, intact, unharmed. The Guru had not killed them. He had slaughtered **five goats** inside the tent, and the blood on his sword was animal blood. The five volunteers had been tested — tested to the very limit of human loyalty — and they had passed.

The Guru brought them forward and addressed the stunned assembly:

> *"These are the **Pañj Piāre** — the Five Beloved Ones. They have given me their heads. Now I give them my head. They are the first of the Khalsa. They are the body of the Guru. Where five Sikhs gather, the Guru is present."*

The five men stood before the multitude:

| Name | Original Name | Caste Background | Home Region | Key Significance |
|------|---------------|------------------|-------------|------------------|
| **Bhāī Dayā Siṅgh** | Dayā Rām | Khatri (merchant) | Lahore, Punjab | First to volunteer; became the Guru's messenger; carried the *Zafarnāmā* to Aurangzeb |
| **Bhāī Dharam Siṅgh** | Dharam Dās | Jat (farmer) | Hastināpur, Delhi region | Second to volunteer; known for his steadfastness and physical strength |
| **Bhāī Mohkam Siṅgh** | Mohkam Chand | Chhīmba (tailor) | Dwarka, Gujarat | Third to volunteer; low-caste background made his offering revolutionary; martyred at Chamkaur (1705) |
| **Bhāī Sāhib Siṅgh** | Sāhib Chand | Nāī (barber) | Bidar, Karnataka | Fourth to volunteer; also low-caste; martyred at Chamkaur (1705) |
| **Bhāī Himmat Siṅgh** | Himmat Rāi | Jhīvar (water-carrier) | Jagannath Puri, Odisha | Fifth to volunteer; the lowest caste of the five; martyred at Chamkaur (1705) |

The revolutionary significance of the Pañj Piāre cannot be overstated. They came from **five different castes** — the highest (Khatri) and the lowest (Jhīvar), with Jat, tailor, and barber in between — and from **five different regions** covering nearly the entire geography of the Indian subcontinent: Lahore in the northwest, Delhi in the north, Dwarka in the west, Bidar in the south, and Puri in the east. The Guru was making a statement: **the Khalsa would know no caste, no region, no hierarchy.** In the Khalsa, the Brahmin and the water-carrier would drink from the same bowl and die on the same battlefield.

---

## The Baptism Ceremony — Khaṇḍē dī Pāhul

The Guru now performed a ritual that had never been seen before — a baptism that would become the definitive initiation for every Khalsa Sikh: **Khaṇḍē dī Pāhul** (the baptism of the double-edged sword).

The ceremony unfolded with careful symbolism:

1. **The Vessel**: An iron bowl (*baṭā* or *sarābloh*) was placed on the ground — iron, the metal of the sword, the metal of the warrior. No clay, no brass, no silver — iron, pure and unadorned.

2. **The Water**: Clean water was poured into the bowl. In some accounts, the Guru's wife — **Māta Jito** (also known as **Māta Sundarī** or **Māta Sāhib Devān**, depending on the tradition) — added sugar crystals (*patāse*) to the water. The *amrit* (nectar of immortality) was thus a marriage of opposites: the **steel** of the sword and the **sweetness** of devotion, the sharpness of justice and the gentleness of compassion.

3. **The Stirring**: The Guru took the **khāṇḍā** — the double-edged steel sword — and stirred the water with it, reciting the **five Banīs** (sacred compositions) that remain the core of the Sikh baptismal liturgy:

| Banī | Composition | Author | Length | Theme |
|------|-------------|--------|--------|-------|
| **Japjī Sāhib** | The foundational Sikh prayer | Gurū Nānak | 38 stanzas | The nature of God, the path to liberation, the triumph of grace |
| **Jāp Sāhib** | The praise of the Divine | Gurū Gobind Siṅgh | 199 verses | The attributes of the Eternal One; a litany of divine names |
| **Tīā Sāhib** (Tvā Prasād) | The wisdom of the sword | Gurū Gobind Siṅgh | 4 sections | The praise of the Creator who wields the sword of justice |
| **Baintī Chaupāī** | The supplication | Gurū Gobind Siṅgh | 8 stanzas | A prayer for strength, protection, and victory over evil |
| **Anand Sāhib** | The song of bliss | Gurū Amar Dās | 40 stanzas | The state of spiritual ecstasy and union with the Divine |

4. **The Sanctification**: As the Guru recited, he stirred the water with the khāṇḍā, chanting with such intensity that the tradition says the bowl seemed to glow. When the five Banīs were complete, the water had been transformed — no longer ordinary water but **Amrit** — the nectar of immortality, infused with the spiritual power of the Gurus' word and the martial power of the sword.

5. **The Administration**: The Guru offered the Amrit to the five men out of the same iron bowl — **each drank from the same vessel** — symbolising the complete abolition of caste distinctions. (In later practice, the Amrit is administered into the cupped hands of the initiate, who drinks it five times, and is sprinkled on the eyes and hair.)

6. **The Mutual Baptism**: Then — in a moment of breathtaking egalitarianism — the Guru knelt before the **Pañj Piāre** and asked **them** to baptise **him**. One of the five — Bhāī Dayā Siṅgh — recited the Banīs while the others stirred the water, and the Guru received Amrit from his own disciples. He had already declared: *"These Five are the Guru. Where they are, I am."* Now he made it literal. He was no longer **Gobind Rāi** — he became **Gobind Siṅgh** (Siṅgh = lion). The disciples became **Siṅgh** as well — the Guru and the Khalsa were **one body**.

This act remains the most radical institutional innovation in the history of South Asian religion: a spiritual leader voluntarily accepting initiation from his own followers, thereby establishing that **the Guru resides in the collective body of the Khalsa**, not in any individual — including himself.

---

## The Giving of the Five K's (Pañj Kakār)

With the Amrit ceremony complete, the Guru proclaimed the **visible identity** of the Khalsa — five articles of faith that every baptised Sikh must wear at all times. These are the **Five K's** (*Pañj Kakār*), so called because each begins with the letter **K** (ਕ) in Gurmukhī script:

| K | Article | Gurmukhī | Description | Purpose |
|---|---------|----------|-------------|---------|
| **1** | **Keś** | ਕੇਸ | Uncut hair and beard, typically bound in a turban (*dastār*) | Acceptance of God's will in creating the human body as it is; a visible marker of Khalsa identity; rejection of caste-based tonsure practices |
| **2** | **Kaṅghā** | ਕੰਘਾ | A small wooden comb worn in the hair | Cleanliness and discipline; a symbol of order and care for the body as a gift from God |
| **3** | **Kirpān** | ਕਿਰਪਾਨ | A ceremonial sword (from *kirpā* = grace, *ān* = honour) | Defence of the weak, the innocent, and the faith; the duty to protect justice with one's life; the sovereignty of the Khalsa |
| **4** | **Kachh** | ਕਛ | Short undergarment/breeches reaching to the knee | Modesty, readiness for action, sexual restraint; contrasts with the flowing robes of ascetics and the tight garments of Mughal nobles |
| **5** | **Kaṛā** | ਕੜਾ | A steel bracelet worn on the right wrist | Bound to the Guru and to God; the circle with no beginning and no end — symbol of the Infinite; restraint of the hand from evil actions |

The Five K's were not arbitrary. They encoded a complete theological anthropology:

- **Keś** represents the **spiritual** dimension — submission to the Divine will, acceptance of the body as God-given.
- **Kaṅghā** represents the **intellectual** dimension — cleanliness, order, and the discipline of the mind.
- **Kirpān** represents the **ethical** dimension — the duty to protect and the willingness to use force in defence of justice.
- **Kachh** represents the **physical** dimension — readiness, modesty, and the sanctity of the body.
- **Kaṛā** represents the **social** dimension — belonging to the Khalsa brotherhood, bound by a common oath and a common purpose.

Every Sikh who wears the Five K's is, in effect, a walking scripture — a visible embodiment of the Khalsa covenant. The turban (*dastār*) that binds the Keś is itself a crown of sovereignty: in the Khalsa, every initiated man and woman is a king or queen before God.

---

## The Rehit — The Code of Conduct

Baptism and outward symbols alone could not sustain the Khalsa. The Guru also laid down a comprehensive **Rehit** (code of conduct) — a set of prohibitions, obligations, and practices that would define Khalsa behaviour in every sphere of life.

### The Four Cardinal Prohibitions (*Kurahit*)

These four actions are strictly forbidden to a Khalsa Sikh on pain of excommunication (becoming a *patit* — a fallen one who must be re-baptised):

1. **Keś-nashī** (cutting or trimming any bodily hair) — violation of the first K
2. **Halālī** (eating meat prepared through the Muslim halal method of ritual slaughter) — the Khalsa eats *kuṭṭhā* meat (killed instantly with a single stroke) or *jhaṭkā* (killed without ritual), rejecting the religious significance of the killing act
3. **Dākū-bajārī / Paradārishanī** (adultery or any form of sexual misconduct with a married person) — fidelity to one's spouse and respect for others' families
4. **Tambākū** (using tobacco in any form) — a modern addition summarised in the larger principle of avoiding intoxicants (though the Guru himself prohibited all addictive substances)

### The Positive Obligations (*Rehit*)

The Khalsa was also bound to specific daily practices:

| Practice | Gurmukhī | Description |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Rise early** | *Amrit-velā* | Wake before dawn for meditation and prayer |
| **Daily recitation** | *Nitnem* | Recite the five prescribed Banīs daily — Japjī Sāhib, Jāp Sāhib, Tīā Sāhib, Baintī Chaupāī, Anand Sāhib |
| **Worship One God** | *Ik Oaṅkār* | No idols, no demigods, no saints as intercessors — only the One Formless Creator |
| **Serve the poor** | *Sēvā* | Engage in selfless service, especially at gurdwārās — cleaning, cooking, serving food |
| **Share earnings** | *Vaṇḍ chakṇā* | Give one-tenth of one's income (*dasvandh*) to the community |
| **Defend the weak** | *Dharam-yuddh* | Fight — physically if necessary — against tyranny and injustice, even at the cost of one's life |
| **Practice equality** | *Sarbat dā bhālā* | Recognise all humans as equal regardless of caste, creed, gender, or social status |
| **Respect women** | *Izat* | Honour women as equals; no polygamy, no purdah, no sati, no female infanticide |

### The Communal Structure

The Guru also established the institutional framework for Khalsa decision-making. Any gathering of five or more baptised Sikhs — a **Pañj Piāre** (not the original five, but any constituted body of five) — could represent the Guru's authority. Decisions reached by a **Gurmatā** (the Guru's intention) — passed by majority vote in the presence of the Gurū Granth Sāhib — were binding on the entire community. This was **theocracy without a king** — a direct rule of the saint-soldier collective under the sovereignty of the scripture.

---

## The Declaration of Khalsa Sovereignty — Rāj Karegā Khālsā

Perhaps the most electrifying moment of the day came when the Guru addressed the assembled Khalsa and proclaimed:

> **"Rāj karegā Khālsā — ākī rahe na koi. Khwār hovē sabbh milāinge — bachē śrān jō hōi."**
>
> *"The Khalsa shall rule — no opponent shall remain. Divided, they shall perish — united, the kingdom shall be theirs."*

This couplet — known as the **Gurū Rām Dās kī Chaukā** (the proclamation of sovereignty) — is traditionally attributed to Gurū Gobind Siṅgh at this gathering (though some sources attribute it to Gurū Bābā Nānak or trace its origins to the earlier Gurus). Regardless of its precise authorship, it encapsulates the theological revolution of Vaisākhī 1699: **the Khalsa was not merely a spiritual brotherhood but a sovereign community with a divine mandate to rule.**

The declaration meant:

1. **Political Sovereignty**: The Khalsa was not a subject community under the Mughal Empire. It was a *mulk* (realm) with its own territory, its own flag (*nishān sāhib*), its own law (*rehit*), and its own sovereign — who was none other than the Guru embodied in the collective Khalsa.

2. **Eschatological Destiny**: The Khalsa was the army of the Lord (*Akal Purakh kī Fauj*). History was moving toward the triumph of justice, and the Khalsa was the instrument of that victory. The *Rāj* (rule) of the Khalsa was not a political ambition but a **divine necessity**.

3. **The Abolition of Intermediaries**: There was no emperor above the Khalsa, no priest mediating between the Khalsa and God, no caste hierarchy determining status within the Khalsa. Every Sikh was a sovereign individual directly connected to the Eternal One.

4. **The Guru-Panth Doctrine**: The Guru declared that after him, the Guru would reside in two forms: the **Gurū Granth** (the scripture) as the spiritual Guru, and the **Gurū Panth** (the Khalsa community) as the temporal Guru. The Khalsa was now the living body of the Guru.

---

## The Third Stage — The Amrit of the Sword

The Guru did not stop with the five initiates. He ordered the **Pañj Piāre** to prepare more Amrit and to begin baptising the willing members of the crowd. Thousands stepped forward — men, women, and children. The baptismal ceremony was repeated throughout the day and into the night, as the Guru stood by and watched the Khalsa multiply before his eyes.

Sikhs who did not undergo the Amrit ceremony remained **kesdhārī Sikhs** (Sikhs with uncut hair who follow the Gurus but are not formally baptised) or **sahajdhārī Sikhs** (slow-adopters who follow the Guru's teachings but do not yet keep the full Khalsa discipline). Both were — and remain — respected members of the Panth, though the Khalsa holds a special status as the visible embodiment of the Guru's will.

The Guru also formally adopted the surname **Siṅgh** (lion) for all Khalsa men and **Kaur** (princess) for all Khalsa women — a radical act of equal naming that erased caste, clan, and regional markers at a stroke. From that day forward, every Khalsa Sikh shared a single family name, a single identity, and a single loyalty.

---

## Theological Meaning — The Khalsa as Covenant

The creation of the Khalsa is the single most theologically significant event in Sikhism after the founding ministry of Gurū Nānak. It can be understood on multiple levels:

### 1. The Khalsa as Covenant (*Bahutā*)

Like the covenant at Sinai in Judaism or the establishment of the Saṅgha in Buddhism, Vaisākhī 1699 was a **founding event** — a moment when a community was ritually reborn through a collective act of commitment. The five head-offerings functioned as a **sacrifice** — not of goats or bulls, but of the will itself. The volunteers had to be willing to die. The Guru had to be willing to appear to kill. The community had to be willing to witness the impossible. All three parties — the Guru, the initiates, and the saṅgat — entered into a covenant sealed by the iron bowl and the double-edged sword.

### 2. The Khalsa as the Guru's Body (*Gurū Panth*)

The most radical theological innovation of Vaisākhī 1699 was the doctrine of **Gurū Panth** — the Guru embodied in the community. By submitting to baptism from his own disciples, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh established that the Guru was no longer an individual but a **collective reality**. Where five baptised Sikhs gather in the presence of the Gurū Granth Sāhib, the Guru is present. The Khalsa is not a church that worships the Guru — it **is** the Guru in visible form.

### 3. The Khalsa as Sant-Sipāhī (Saint-Soldier)

The Khalsa united two identities that most religious traditions keep separate: the **saint** (who meditates, prays, and turns the other cheek) and the **soldier** (who fights, kills, and dies in battle). The Khalsa Sikh is both. The *Kirpān* and the *Kaṛā* are not optional accessories — they are as essential as the Banī recitation and the meditation on the Name. **Spirituality without the capacity for self-defence is incomplete; violence without spiritual discipline is barbaric.**

### 4. The Khalsa as Eschatological Army

The phrase **Akal Purakh kī Fauj** (the Army of the Timeless One) is not merely metaphorical. In Sikh theology, the Khalsa is an **eschatological reality** — the instrument through which God will establish righteousness on earth. The *Rāj Karegā Khālsā* declaration is not a prediction but a **decree**: the Khalsa will rule because it must rule, because that is the divine plan. Every Khalsa Sikh is, in this sense, a soldier in the army of the age to come.

### 5. The Khalsa as Total Inclusivity

The abolition of caste — enacted through the five volunteers from five castes and five regions — was not a reform but a **destruction**. The Guru did not say "caste should matter less." He made it impossible for caste to exist at all within the Khalsa. The shared surname, the shared baptismal bowl, the shared discipline of the Five K's — all erased the distinctions that had divided Indian society for millennia. In the Khalsa, the water-carrier and the prince were one.

### 6. The Khalsa as Personal Transformation

At the individual level, the Amrit ceremony was (and is) a death and rebirth. The initiate dies to their old self — their caste, their clan, their regional identity, their attachments — and is reborn as a Siṅgh or Kaur, a child of the Guru, a member of the Khalsa family. The Five K's are the uniform of this new identity. The Rehit is the discipline of this new life. The daily Banī recitation is the breath of this new being.

---

## The Legacy of Vaisākhī 1699

The creation of the Khalsa did not immediately overthrow the Mughal Empire or establish Sikh rule. The Guru would fight for another nine years, losing all four of his sons to martyrdom, before being assassinated in 1708. His disciple **Banda Siṅgh Bahādur** would briefly establish a Sikh state in the Punjab (1710–1716), only to be captured and tortured to death. The Khalsa spent much of the eighteenth century as a scattered confederacy — the **Misl** system — fighting for survival against Mughals, Afghans, and rival Sikh factions.

But the covenant of Vaisākhī 1699 held. The *Rāj* did come — under **Maharaja Raṇjīt Siṅgh** (1780–1839), who united the Misls and created the Sikh Empire, the first and only fully sovereign Sikh state, which stretched from the Khyber Pass to the Sutlej River. The empire was lost to the British in 1849, but the Khalsa never dissolved.

Today, the Khalsa is the heart of the Sikh tradition. Every year on **Vaisākhī** (13–14 April), Sikhs around the world gather in gurdwārās to hear the story of the five head-offerings, to witness or undergo Amrit baptism, and to reaffirm the covenant of the Five K's. The Nishān Sāhib — the saffron flag of the Khalsa bearing the *khaṇḍā* (double-edged sword), the *chakkar* (circle), and two *kirpāns* — flies over every gurdwārā as a declaration that the Khalsa has not forgotten its sovereignty.

> *"The Khalsa is my very self. In the Khalsa, I live. In the Khalsa, I act. In the Khalsa, I am victorious."*
> — Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, attributed tradition

The creation of the Khalsa was not an event that happened in 1699 and ended. It is a **permanent act** — a covenant renewed with every Amrit baptism, every Gurmatā decision, every Sikh who rises before dawn to recite the Banīs and fights against injustice with the Kirpān of truth. The five men who walked into the Guru's tent on the plains of Anandpur are still walking. And the sovereignty that was declared that day is still being claimed.
