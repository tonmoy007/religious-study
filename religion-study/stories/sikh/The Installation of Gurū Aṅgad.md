# The Installation of Gurū Aṅgad

> *"The light of Nānak has merged with the light of Aṅgad. As Nānak was, so now is Aṅgad. The Guru is one, though the body changes."*
> — **Bhāī Gurdās**, Vār 1, Paurī 46

The succession of **Gurū Aṅgad** (1504–1552 CE) is the hinge on which the entire Sikh tradition turns — the moment when a community founded around a single teacher learned that the Guru did not die with the Guru's body. That the **light** (*jot*) could pass from one vessel to another. That the **Word** (*śabad*) was continuous, unbroken, and would not cease so long as there were ears to hear it and hearts to live it.

The story begins not with Aṅgad but with **Lehnā** — a humble Khatri of the Trehaṇ clan, a devotee of the goddess Durgā, a man who set out to find a teacher and found instead his own death and rebirth.

---

## Lehna Before Kartarpur

Before he was Gurū Aṅgad — before he was even a follower of Nānak — **Lehnā** was a prosperous but restless man. He was born in 1504 CE in the village of **Harike** (now Matte-dī-Sarāī, near Ferozepur, Punjab), the son of a wealthy Khatri trader named **Pherū Mall** and his wife **Rāmo Devī**. The family were of the **Trehaṇ sub-caste** — merchants and landowners who had done well under the Lodi Sultanate.

Lehnā grew up in comfort, inheriting his father's trading business, marrying a woman named **Khir Devī**, and fathering two sons and a daughter. By worldly standards, he was a success. But from his youth, Lehnā was troubled by a question that wealth could not answer: *What is the purpose of this life?*

He sought refuge in the worship of **Durgā**, the great goddess of Hindu tradition. Every year, he would lead a pilgrimage to the temple of **Durgā at Bhaṭiāl** (in present-day Himachal Pradesh), a journey of some 130 kilometres on foot, carrying offerings and hymns. He was sincere — the Janam Sākhīs attest that his devotion was genuine — but the goddess gave him no peace.

> *"I have bowed before the stone. I have offered the flowers. I have chanted the mantras. And I am still hungry. There is a fire in my soul that no ritual can quench."*

In the year 1532 CE, on one of his pilgrimages to Durgā Bhaṭiāl, Lehnā happened to pass through the village of **Kartarpur** — the settlement on the banks of the Ravi River where Gurū Nānak had settled after his long journeys and where he now lived with his family and a growing community of disciples.

As Lehnā passed by the Gurū's house, he heard a hymn being sung:

> *"There is but One God. His name is Truth. He is the Creator, without fear, without enmity, timeless, unborn, self-existent — known by the grace of the Guru."*

The voice — Nānak's voice — stopped Lehnā in his tracks. He stood on the road, tears streaming down his face, unable to move. He had never heard anything like this — not in any temple, not in any scripture, not from any teacher. This was not doctrine. This was *direct address* — as if the words had been spoken to his soul alone.

He abandoned his pilgrimage. He never reached Durgā Bhaṭiāl. He entered Kartarpur and sat at the feet of Gurū Nānak — and from that moment, he never left.

---

## The Service of Lehna

Lehnā's conversion was not a single event but a process — and its engine was **service** (*sevā*). Unlike the intellectual conversion of a Brāhmaṇa scholar debating doctrines, Lehnā's path was one of **humble, bodily work** — the work of hands, feet, and back. He did not ask questions. He simply served.

Every morning, Lehnā would rise before dawn, draw water from the well, and prepare the langar (the communal kitchen) for the day's visitors. He swept the courtyard. He washed the dishes. He fetched firewood. He tended to the sick. He did the work that no one else wanted to do — and he did it with a joy that drew the attention of the entire community.

The other disciples noticed. *Who is this man?* they whispered. He had arrived as a stranger, a wealthy merchant who had abandoned his business — yet he acted like a servant.

Nānak watched him carefully. The Gurū tested Lehnā not with philosophy but with menial labour — and Lehnā passed every test before he even knew it was a test.

---

## The Tests

The tradition records three principal tests — episodes that reveal not merely Lehnā's obedience but his complete dissolution of ego.

### The Test of the Hot Water

One bitterly cold winter morning, Gurū Nānak woke his household with an unusual request. He wanted hot water for his bath — but the fire had gone out, and the kitchen was freezing. He called for Lehnā.

Lehnā took the cold pot, set it on the hearth, and tried to light the fire. But the wood was damp and would not catch. He tried again and again — nothing.

Then, without hesitation, Lehnā picked up the heavy pot and held it against his own chest. He wrapped his arms around it, pressed it to his body, and stood there in the freezing dark until his own body heat had warmed the water enough that it was no longer icy. He then lit what little flame he could coax from the damp wood and finished the job — purely by the force of his will and devotion.

When he brought the warm water to Nānak, the Gurū asked: *"How did you light the fire, Lehnā?"*

> **Lehnā:** "Gurū-ji, the fire of my love for you is hotter than any flame. My body is your wood. My devotion is your spark. The water is warm because my heart burned for you."

Nānak said nothing. But he smiled.

### The Test of the Stones

Another day, Nānak was walking in the fields near Kartarpur when he stopped at a muddy spot and announced: *"I want to build a well here for the travellers."*

The disciples gathered stones and began to build. But Nānak, instead of directing the work, began throwing the stones into the mud — apparently undoing everything they had done.

The disciples were confused. Some grew frustrated. A few muttered: *"What is the Gurū doing? He asks us to build, and then he destroys."*

Only Lehnā kept working. When a stone was thrown into the mud, he waded in, pulled it out, cleaned it, and placed it back on the wall. When another was thrown, he retrieved it again. He did this for hours — muddy, exhausted, but utterly at peace. He did not ask why. He did not complain. He simply served.

Finally, Nānak stopped. He looked at Lehnā — covered in mud, breathing hard, his eyes shining — and said:

> *"The world builds walls, Lehnā, and the world knocks them down. Only the one who serves without asking why builds something that lasts. You have understood."*

### The Test of the Vomit

The hardest test — the one that shatters the ego more brutally than any other — is the test of the vomit.

A visitor came to Kartarpur who was suffering from a severe illness. He had been vomiting — foul, bile-stained matter — and the stench was unbearable. No one would go near him. The disciples avoided his corner of the langar. The women covered their faces and stepped away.

Nānak approached the group and asked: *"Who will clean this brother?"*

The disciples fell silent. Some looked at the ground. Others stepped back. No one volunteered.

Lehnā stepped forward.

He went to the sick man, cleaned his face, washed his soiled clothes, and scrubbed the floor where the vomit had pooled. He did it with the same calm, steady hands with which he had drawn water from the well. When he was finished, he helped the man drink fresh water and sat with him until he fell asleep.

When he returned to Nānak, the Gurū asked: *"Lehnā, was it not repellent?"*

> **Lehnā:** "Gurū-ji, in the body of that sick man, I saw only you. How could I be repelled by you? The filth I cleaned was my own ego. The vomit I washed away was my own pride. You gave me a chance to serve the Lord in his most humble form — and I am grateful."

Nānak embraced him.

---

## Nānak's Two Sons

The question of succession hung over Kartarpur like a gathering cloud. Gurū Nānak had two sons — **Sri Chand** (born 1494) and **Lakhmi Das** (born 1497) — both of whom were by now grown men. In any normal father-to-son tradition, one of them would inherit the mantle of leadership.

But Nānak was not a normal father, and the Guruship was not a normal inheritance.

**Sri Chand** was the elder — intelligent, handsome, spiritual. Early in his youth, he had been deeply impressed by the ascetic tradition, and he gravitated toward the life of a *sannyāsī* — a celibate renunciate who wandered without possessions, depending entirely on alms. He founded the **Udāsī** sect (from *udās*, meaning "detached" or "sad"), whose followers wore ochre robes, grew their hair long, and lived as celibate monks. They were sincere and devout — but their path was one of withdrawal from the world, and this stood in direct opposition to everything Nānak had taught.

**Lakhmi Das** was the younger — practical, worldly, interested in the family's business affairs. He saw the Guruship as a position of honour and authority, a throne to be inherited. He had little interest in the spiritual substance of his father's mission; he wanted its visible prestige.

Nānak loved both his sons. But he saw clearly that neither could carry his work:

| Son | Inclination | Why Passed Over |
|-----|------------|-----------------|
| **Sri Chand** | Ascetic withdrawal (*udās*); founded the celibate Udāsī order | The Guru's path is the householder's path — not renunciation of the world but transformation within it |
| **Lakhmi Das** | Worldly ambition; sought the Guruship as a position of power | The Guru is a servant, not a ruler — authority in the Sikh tradition is the authority of the towel and the washbasin, not the crown |

The tradition says that when the sons realised their father would pass them over, they reacted characteristically. Sri Chand was hurt but accepted the decision with dignity — he continued his Udāsī path and later became a founder of an important Sikh ascetic lineage. Lakhmi Das was angry. He demanded his rights. He argued, pleaded, and finally withdrew from his father's community, settling in his own village and living as a wealthy landowner.

The door was open. And it led to **Lehnā**.

---

## The Appointment of Lehnā

The formal appointment of Lehnā as Gurū Nānak's successor is remembered in Sikh tradition as a ceremony of profound simplicity and power.

It took place in **Kartarpur** in the late summer or autumn of **1539 CE** — Nānak was seventy years old, his body failing, his work complete. He called the disciples together and said:

> *"I have completed my work. The Lord now calls me home. But the Word does not die. The truth does not end. The Guru does not leave. I shall place my light in another vessel — and that vessel is Lehnā."*

There was murmuring among the disciples. Some were shocked. Some were doubtful. *Lehnā?* The newcomer? The merchant who had appeared only seven years ago? What of Sri Chand? What of Lakhmi Das? What of the older disciples who had been with Nānak since the Udāsīs?

Nānak silenced them with a gesture.

### The Ceremony

Nānak called Lehnā before him. He asked for a coconut and five paise (the traditional offerings made to a teacher) and placed them before Lehnā. Then he bowed — the Gurū himself bowed — before the one he had chosen.

The community gasped. The Gurū bowing to a disciple? This was unthinkable.

But Nānak explained:

> *"I do not bow to Lehnā. I bow to the light of the One Lord that shines in him. The same light that was in me is now in him. From this day, Lehnā is dead. He is **Aṅgad** — the limb of my body. As a limb serves the whole, so shall he serve the Sangat. As a limb cannot be separated from the body, so shall he not be separated from the Word."*

Nānak then placed his hand on Lehnā's head and gave him his blessing. The name **Aṅgad** — from *aṅga* (limb) and *gada* (to be attached) — was conferred. Lehnā was no more. A new Guru had been born.

The details of the ceremony vary across sources:

| Source | Key Detail |
|--------|-----------|
| **Bhāī Gurdās**, Vār 1 | Nānak placed five paise and a coconut before Aṅgad and bowed — symbolising the complete transfer of authority |
| **Gurbilās Chhevīn Pātshāhī** | Nānak said: *"The Word is the Guru. The Guru is the Word. All the spiritual powers I have received from the Lord are now in Aṅgad."* |
| **Sikh oral tradition** | Aṅgad was asked to circumambulate (perform *parikramā*) around Nānak three times — and then Nānak himself bowed to Aṅgad, confounding all present |

The most poignant element of the ceremony is Nānak's bow. In a tradition where the Guru is the highest human authority, the Guru bowing to his disciple was a radical act — it declared that the Guruship was not a personal possession but a **trust**. The light belonged to the Lord, not to Nānak. And it could pass to whomever the Lord chose.

---

## Nānak's Final Instructions

After the ceremony, Nānak gave Aṅgad his final charge — instructions that would define the Sikh tradition for centuries to come:

> *"The Word (*śabad*) is the Guru. Let the Sangat (the community of devotees) be your family. Serve the Lord through service to the Sangat. Teach the people to repeat the Nām — the Name of the One Lord. Let no distinction be made between high and low caste, between Hindu and Muslim, between rich and poor. All are equal in the court of the Lord."*

He continued:

> *"Do not sit in a high place. Do not wear the clothes of a prince. The Guru is not a king — the Guru is a servant. The towel of the kitchen is your crown. The dust of the Sangat's feet is your perfume. Langar — the communal meal — is your temple. Feed the hungry before you preach to the faithful."*

And finally:

> *"When I am gone, do not fall into the worship of this body. The body of Nānak will rot. The body of Aṅgad will also rot. But the Word will not rot. The Truth will not die. The Name will not end. You are not the successor of Nānak the man — you are the successor of Nānak the light. And the light is the Lord's."*

### The Two Nānaks

One of the most famous incidents from Nānak's final days illustrates this teaching with unforgettable power.

After the installation, a disciple came to Kartarpur looking for Gurū Nānak. He saw Aṅgad sitting in Nānak's place, surrounded by the disciples. Confused, he asked:

> *"Where is Gurū Nānak? I have come to see the Guru."*

Aṅgad smiled and said: *"Nānak is here. But Nānak is also there. Go into the next room."*

The disciple entered the inner room and found Nānak lying on a simple bed, his body failing. He bowed and said:

> *"Gurū-ji, I see you here, and I see the one you have placed outside. Which of you is the Guru?"*

Nānak replied:

> *"The Guru is the light, not the body. The same light that is in me is now in Aṅgad. If you seek Nānak, seek the Word — for the Word is Nānak. From today, there are not two Gurus — there is one Guru in two bodies. Go and serve the one who sits outside, for he is Nānak."*

This is the foundation of the Sikh doctrine of the **continuity of the Guruship** (*Gurū-Panthī*): the Guru is not a person but a light that passes from vessel to vessel. The ten human Gurus are, in essence, **one Guru** — one *jot* — appearing in ten bodies.

---

## The Death of Gurū Nānak

On **22 September 1539 CE**, a few days after the installation of Aṅgad, Gurū Nānak's body finally gave out. He was seventy years old.

The Janam Sākhīs record that his final moments were peaceful. He lay surrounded by his disciples — Aṅgad at his head, Mardānā at his feet. He was silent for a long time. Then he opened his eyes, looked at Aṅgad, and said:

> *"Aṅgad, I am going now. The Lord is calling. Do not weep. The Word is with you. The Sangat is with you. The Lord is with you. Wheresoever two or three are gathered in the Nām, there I am also."*

He closed his eyes. And he was gone.

A dispute immediately arose between the Hindu and Muslim followers of Nānak over what to do with his body. The Hindus wanted to cremate him according to their tradition. The Muslims wanted to bury him according to theirs. Aṅgad — now Gurū Aṅgad — intervened:

> *"The body of Nānak is no longer with us. His light has returned to its source. Do with this shell what you will — it cannot contain him anymore."*

The tradition says that when the dispute was at its peak, the cloth covering Nānak's body was lifted — and only a heap of flowers remained. The Hindus took their flowers and cremated them. The Muslims took theirs and buried them. Both claimed the body. Neither was wrong. The Guru had become what he always was: the Word without a body.

---

## What Aṅgad Inherited

When Aṅgad accepted the Guruship, he inherited a movement — not an institution. Nānak had left no written scripture, no formal organisation, no designated headquarters beyond the village of Kartarpur. What he left was:

- **The Word** — a body of hymns and teachings, memorised by disciples and sung in the Sangat
- **The Sangat** — a community of devotees, bound by love and service, not by creed or hierarchy
- **The Langar** — the communal kitchen, where all ate together without distinction of caste or religion
- **The Nām** — the practice of repeating the divine Name as the central spiritual discipline

Aṅgad's task was to give this movement a **shape**. He moved the centre of the community from Kartarpur to **Khadūr Sāhib** (a village on the opposite bank of the Ravi), where he established a new settlement. He standardised the **Gurmukhī script** (literally, "from the mouth of the Guru") — the alphabet in which Punjabi would be written and in which the Guru Granth Sāhib would eventually be recorded. He collected Nānak's hymns and began the process of preserving them in written form. He continued the langar, the daily *kīrtan* (hymn-singing), and the practice of *sevā* (service) as the foundation of Sikh life.

He also selected his own successor — **Gurū Amar Dās** — continuing the chain of transmission that would run unbroken for 170 years and ten human Gurus.

## The Significance of the Succession

The installation of Gurū Aṅgad established several principles that define Sikhism to this day:

| Principle | Established by the Succession of Aṅgad |
|-----------|----------------------------------------|
| **The Guru is the Word, not the body** | The light passes from vessel to vessel; the physical body of a specific Guru is not the object of devotion |
| **Succession is by merit, not birth** | Nānak's own sons were passed over in favour of the most devoted and humble disciple — blood lineage does not convey spiritual authority |
| **The householder path is supreme** | Unlike Sri Chand's celibate asceticism, Aṅgad married, had children, and worked as a householder — affirming Nānak's vision of spirituality in the world |
| **Service is the foundation of discipleship** | Aṅgad was chosen not for his learning or his miracles but for his *sevā* — his willingness to do the dirtiest work without complaint |
| **The community is the Guru's body** | Nānak's bow to Aṅgad was also a bow to the Sangat — the Guru serves the community; the community is the living vessel of the Guru's presence |

The succession of Aṅgad is not merely a historical event. It is a **template** — the pattern by which Sikhism understood itself as a tradition of transmitted light rather than linear descent. The Guru did not die in 1539. The Guru simply changed bodies.

> *"The river flows from the mountain to the sea. The mountain does not move — it is the river that moves. Nānak is the mountain. Aṅgad is the river. The water is the same."*
> — traditional Sikh saying

## Comparative Connection: Jain Gaṇadharas and Sikh Succession

The installation of Aṅgad finds a revealing parallel in the Jain tradition of the **Gaṇadharas** — the eleven chief disciples of Mahāvīra who received, organised, and transmitted his teaching after his nirvāṇa.

| Element | Sikh Succession (Nānak → Aṅgad) | Jain Gaṇadharas (Mahāvīra → Gautama et al.) |
|---------|--------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| **Type of transmission** | Succession of a single leader (one Guru at a time) | Collective transmission by eleven leaders who together formed a teaching council |
| **Method of selection** | Personal appointment by the living Guru | Spontaneous conversion through debate and recognition — no formal appointment |
| **Role of the founder** | Nānak lived alongside Aṅgad after appointing him — a period of overlap | Mahāvīra remained the supreme authority throughout; the Gaṇadharas were always subordinate |
| **After the founder's death** | Aṅgad became the sole Guru — the line continues as one Guru per generation | The Gaṇadharas functioned collectively; after Mahāvīra's nirvāṇa, the saṅgha was led by the most senior surviving Gaṇadhara |
| **Transmission of scripture** | Aṅgad collected Nānak's hymns; the canon grew over ten Gurus | The Gaṇadharas organised the twelve Aṅgas immediately; the canon was fixed within Mahāvīra's lifetime |
| **Attitude to the founder's body** | Nānak's body disappeared (flowers remained); no relic veneration | Mahāvīra's body was cremated; relics were enshrined and worshipped, particularly by the Digambara tradition |

The parallel is structural — both traditions faced the crisis of succession after the death of a founding teacher and solved it by creating a formal mechanism for preserving and transmitting the teaching. The difference lies in the Sikh emphasis on **singular succession** (one Guru per generation) versus the Jain emphasis on **collective transmission** (eleven Gaṇadharas, later a lineage of heads of orders).

Both, however, affirm a principle that the Indian religious traditions share: **the teacher does not die with the body**. The truth the Guru embodies outlasts the Guru's physical form.
