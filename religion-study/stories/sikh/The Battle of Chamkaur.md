# The Battle of Chamkaur

> *"I have given my sons in the service of the Lord. What is this to me? The Khālsā is my real family — the Khālsā is the living form of my Guru."*
> — Gurū Gobind Siṅgh (traditional saying)

The **Battle of Chamkaur** (21–22 December 1705) is the most harrowing episode in Sikh history — a story of betrayal, impossible odds, and the martyrdom of all four of Gurū Gobind Siṅgh's sons on the same day. It is a **last stand**: forty Sikhs against a Mughal army of tens of thousands, in a mud-and-brick haveli, fighting until ammunition runs out, food is gone, and the only thing left to give is life itself.

---

## Background: The Siege of Anandpur (1705)

By 1705, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh (1666–1708) had been at war with the Mughal Empire for over a decade. Since the **creation of the Khālsā** at Vaisākhī 1699, the Sikh community had transformed into a sovereign military brotherhood. Emperor **Aurangzeb 'Ālamgīr** (r. 1658–1707) viewed the Khālsā as an existential threat, and the hill rajas of the Shivalik range — Bilaspur, Kahlur, Jaswan — enlisted Mughal support to crush the Guru.

In early 1705, a combined Mughal–hill army under **Khānzāda Khān** and **Rājā Ajmer Chand** laid siege to **Anandpur Sāhib**, the Guru's headquarters. The siege lasted months. Supply lines were cut; the Sikhs ground tree bark for flour, boiled dead grass for sustenance, and stood guard on the walls with re-fletched arrows. The four **Sāhibzāde** — **Ajit Siṅgh** (18), **Jujhār Siṅgh** (14), **Zorāwar Siṅgh** (9), and **Fateh Siṅgh** (7) — stood on the ramparts alongside their father.

Unable to breach the city, the besiegers resorted to deception. They swore a sacred oath — on the **Qur'an** for the Mughals, on the **cow and Ganges** for the hill rajas — that if the Guru evacuated Anandpur, he and his family would be granted safe passage. The Guru, seeing his people starving, reluctantly agreed.

---

## The Evacuation and the Tragedy at the Sarsa River

On **20 December 1705**, the Guru and his family evacuated Anandpur under cover of darkness. They crossed the frozen **Sarsa River** (a Sutlej tributary) in the December night — and the Mughal army attacked them in mid-crossing.

The river was in spate, the current strong, the water icy. The hill rajas had broken their oath and attacked from the rear; Mughal forces struck from the flanks. The crossing became a massacre.

In the chaos, the Guru's family was separated:

- **Gurū Gobind Siṅgh** crossed with the two elder Sāhibzāde — **Ajit Siṅgh** and **Jujhār Siṅgh** — and forty Sikhs, including several of the **Pañj Piāre** (the Five Beloved Ones). They made their way to the village of **Chamkaur**.
- **Mātā Gujrī** (the Guru's mother, aged 85) was swept downstream with the two younger Sāhibzāde — **Zorāwar Siṅgh** (9) and **Fateh Siṅgh** (7). Disoriented and separated from any Sikh protection, they took shelter with a Brāhmaṇa cook named **Gaṅgū Rām** in the village of **Kherī**, near **Morinda**. Gaṅgū Rām, who had once worked in the Guru's langar (community kitchen), initially welcomed them — but greed overtook loyalty. He calculated that the Mughal reward for the capture of the Guru's family would be enormous, and betrayed them to the Mughal authorities of **Sirhind**.

The Mughals arrested the younger Sāhibzāde and Mātā Gujrī in Gaṅgū Rām's house. They were bound, separated from their grandmother, and taken to the court of **Nawāb Wazīr Khān**, the Governor of Sirhind, for trial.

---|

## The Martyrdom of the Younger Sāhibzāde at Sirhind

The two younger princes were brought before **Nawāb Wazīr Khān**, the Governor of Sirhind, in the **Diwān-i-'Ām** of the Sirhind Fort. They were offered a choice: embrace Islam, or die.

Zorāwar Siṅgh, aged nine, answered:

> *"We are the sons of Gurū Gobind Siṅgh. We do not fear death — we fear only the dishonour of abandoning our faith."*

Some nobles — **Nawāb Sher Muhammād Khān** and **Mīrzā Beg** — pleaded for clemency: *"They are children. Spare them."* But Wazīr Khān was adamant: *"The child of a snake is also a snake. They must die."*

The sentence was **walled alive** — sealed inside a brick-and-mortar chamber and left to suffocate. On **26 December 1705**, Zorāwar Siṅgh and Fateh Siṅgh were taken to the execution ground. According to tradition, they recited the **Japjī Sāhib** as the bricks rose around them. When the wall was completed, the sounds of their prayers could still be heard — faint, then fainter, then silence.

**Mātā Gujrī**, learning of their fate, refused food and water in captivity and died on the same day, aged 85. The three are commemorated together at **Fatehgaṛh Sāhib**, the gurdwārā built at the execution site in Sirhind.

---

## The Haveli of Chamkaur

Meanwhile, the Guru arrived at Chamkaur on **21 December 1705**. Two local Sikh brothers, **Budhī Chand** and **Pīpā Chand**, offered their modest mud-brick **haveli** (a fortified manor house) as shelter. The haveli had a central courtyard, a single gate, and a flat roof with parapet walls — not a fort, but defensible.

The Mughal army — between **7,000 and 30,000** men, with musketeers, cavalry, and artillery — was approaching. The Guru could have fled into the forest paths, but he chose to stay. His reasoning, preserved in the **Bachitar Nāṭak** (his autobiographical composition), was twofold: flight would destroy the morale of the Khālsā, and the haveli offered a chance to inflict disproportionate casualties on the Mughals.

The forty Sikhs barricaded the gate, manned the parapets with bows and a dozen muskets, and counted their ammunition: barely enough for a few hours.

---

## The Battle: 21–22 December 1705

The Mughal army surrounded the haveli at nightfall. The first assault came under cover of darkness — infantry with axes and battering rams. The Sikhs, hidden behind the parapets, released volleys of arrows and musket fire. The front ranks fell. The assault faltered. The Mughals charged again — and were repelled again.

This pattern repeated through the night. The Mughals had overwhelming numbers but could only bring a fraction of their force to bear against a single gate in a narrow lane.

### The Death of Ajit Siṅgh

By dawn, ammunition was almost exhausted. **Ajit Siṅgh**, the Guru's eldest son, asked for permission to lead a counterattack:

> *"Father, give me your blessing. Our arrows are gone — but our swords are still sharp. Let us go out and meet them face to face."*

The Guru embraced him and gave him his own sword. Ajit Siṅgh and five Sikhs threw open the gate and charged directly into the Mughal ranks. They cut through the first line — Ajit Siṅgh was seen to cut down a standard-bearer and engage an elephant-mounted commander. But the numbers were overwhelming. He fell, surrounded, fighting to the last.

### The Death of Jujhār Siṅgh

When Ajit Siṅgh did not return, **Jujhār Siṅgh** — just fourteen — knelt before his father:

> *"Father, my brother has gone to the court of the Lord. Do not keep me behind. Let me join him."*

The Guru placed his hand on his son's head: *"You are named Jujhār — 'the Warrior.' Now prove your name."*

Jujhār Siṅgh led the second sortie, charging into the Mughal horde with five Sikhs. He fought with extraordinary ferocity — killing several soldiers before being overwhelmed. His body was found near his brother's.

### The Escape

With the two Sāhibzāde dead and the ammunition gone, only eight men remained: the Guru, the **Pañj Piāre**, and three others. The Guru prepared for his own death, reciting the **Jāp Sāhib** in meditation.

But the five Sikhs surrounded him and issued an ultimatum:

> *"O Guru, you gave us the gift of the Khālsā. Now you must do what we command. You must leave — immediately. We will hold the gate. If you die here, the Panth dies with you."*

The Guru refused. The Sikhs insisted. Finally, he consented.

The Guru and three companions — **Bhāī Madan Siṅgh**, **Bhāī Kharak Siṅgh**, and **Rāṅghā Siṅgh** — abandoned their Sikh dress, shaved their beards and hair, and disguised themselves as **Uchchī Piīrs** (Muslim holy men). The Guru adopted the name **Uchchī Piīr Nāth**.

While the Pañj Piāre continued to fight — chanting **Wāhegurū** to make the Mughals believe the Guru was still inside — the Guru escaped through a rear passage into the **Machhīwārā jungle**. The five Sikhs fell one by one, the last captured alive and tortured for the Guru's whereabouts. He revealed nothing.

When the Mughals stormed the haveli, they found only corpses. The Guru was gone.

---

## The Zafarnama: The Epistle of Victory

About a month after the battle, while staying in the village of **Dīnā** (near Bathinda), Gurū Gobind Siṅgh wrote his most famous work: the **Zafarnāmā** (Persian: "The Epistle of Victory") — a poem of **111 Persian couplets** addressed to Emperor Aurangzeb.

The letter was delivered by two Sikh messengers — **Bhāī Dayā Siṅgh** and **Bhāī Dharam Siṅgh** — who travelled to the Mughal camp in the Deccan and read it aloud before the emperor. It is a document unlike any other in Mughal history: a letter from a defeated rebel leader to the emperor of the world's most powerful empire, not pleading for mercy but **declaring moral victory**.

| Theme | Couplets | Content |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Rebuke** | 1–30 | Denounces Aurangzeb for the false oath at Anandpur and the attack on a peaceful evacuation |
| **Witness** | 31–60 | Describes the martyrdom of the four Sāhibzāde — not as a defeat but as a testimony to truth |
| **Warning** | 61–80 | Warns that empires built on treachery will not last: *"When faith is broken, even a king's throne trembles"* |
| **Defiance** | 81–100 | Declares the Khālsā cannot be destroyed: *"What can you do to me? Death is but the gateway to the Beloved"* |
| **Challenge** | 101–111 | Challenges Aurangzeb to meet in person to debate religion face to face |

The most quoted couplet:

> *"Chī kardam-āyad ba man, Jānā-i-man? / Ān kardam-āyad ba tū, Īmāna-i-man."*
> ("What can you do to me, O my soul? / What you have done to me, O my faith.")

According to tradition, Aurangzeb was deeply shaken by the letter. He is said to have wept at the account of the Sāhibzāde's martyrdom and ordered an inquiry into Wazīr Khān's actions. But Aurangzeb died in **February 1707** — just over a year after Chamkaur — before any meeting could take place. The Zafarnāmā became the foundational text of Sikh political theology: the declaration that oppression will be answered with defiance even when all hope of military victory is gone.

---

## The Reunion at Damdamā Sāhib and the Legacy

After Chamkaur, the Guru marched south to **Talwandi Sabo** (now **Damdamā Sāhib**), where he spent nine months recovering and rebuilding. He **recited the complete Ādi Granth from memory** (his copy had been lost in the Sarsa River) and dictated it to **Bhāī Manī Siṅgh**. He composed the **Zafarnāmā** and trained new warriors who had flocked to him after hearing of Chamkaur.

The Guru had lost his mother, his four sons, and his most trusted companions. But the Khālsā was stronger than ever.

### The Four Sāhibzāde as Theological Categories

| Sāhibzādā | Age | Place of Death | Mode | Spiritual Meaning |
|-----------|-----|---------------|------|-------------------|
| **Ajit Siṅgh** | 18 | Chamkaur | Killed in battle (sortie) | The warrior's sacrifice — dying in full strength for righteousness |
| **Jujhār Siṅgh** | 14 | Chamkaur | Killed in battle (sortie) | The disciple's sacrifice — fighting alongside his brother |
| **Zorāwar Siṅgh** | 9 | Sirhind | Walled alive | Innocence sacrificed — dying for faith without understanding politics |
| **Fateh Siṅgh** | 7 | Sirhind | Walled alive | Purity sacrificed — the youngest, standing firm |

Together they represent the totality of sacrifice: the Khālsā gives everything — its young, its old, its warriors, its children — rather than submit to oppression.

### The Guru as the Willing Father

The most difficult theological question: why did the Guru **allow** his sons to die? Sikh tradition answers through **chardi kalā** (eternal optimism) — the Guru consented to their sacrifice because the truth of the Khālsā was worth more than their lives.

This directly parallels the **Binding of Isaac** (Akedah) — but with a crucial difference. In the Akedah, God intervenes and substitutes a ram. At Chamkaur and Sirhind, there was no substitute. The sons died. Sikh theology does not promise that God will rescue the faithful from death — it promises that dying for the truth is a victory, not a defeat.

When Sikhs wept for his lost sons, the Guru replied:

> *"Do not weep. The Khālsā is my true lineage — the sons born of my soul. As long as the Khālsā lives, my lineage lives."*

### Commemoration

The Battle of Chamkaur is commemorated annually on **7–8 Poh** (December). The names of the four Sāhibzāde are recited in the **Ardās** (the daily Sikh congregational prayer). The key sites:

| Site | Location | Significance |
|------|----------|--------------|
| **Gurdwārā Chamkaur Sāhib** | Rupnagar, Punjab | The main shrine at the battle site — the haveli where forty Sikhs made their stand |
| **Gurdwārā Fatehgaṛh Sāhib** | Sirhind | Where Zorāwar Siṅgh and Fateh Siṅgh were walled alive — one of the most sacred Sikh sites |
| **Gurdwārā Burj Māta Gujrī** | Sirhind | Where Mātā Gujrī died in captivity |

Within a decade, **Banda Siṅgh Bahādur** — the Guru's appointed general — would march on Sirhind, capture the city, and execute Wazīr Khān on the same ground where the younger Sāhibzāde had been walled alive. Within a century, the Sikhs would rule Punjab. But the memory of Chamkaur — of the forty against the thousands, of the four Sāhibzāde, of the Guru who escaped to write a letter of defiance — has outlasted every empire since.

> *"The Sikhs' backs were to the wall. Their arrows were gone. Their sons were dead. And they fought harder than ever — because they had nothing left but the Name of God, and the Name was enough."*
> — Traditional Sikh teaching
