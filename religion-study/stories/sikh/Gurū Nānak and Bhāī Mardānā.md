# Gurū Nānak and Bhāī Mardānā

> *"Mardānā, play the rabab. The Lord's word is sweet. Sing the truth, and let the false be far."*
> — Purātan Janam Sākhī, paraphrasing the bond between the two companions

Of all the relationships in Sikh history, none is more intimate, more enduring, or more moving than the bond between **Gurū Nānak** (1469–1539) and **Bhāī Mardānā** (1459–1534). For over three decades — through four great journeys spanning thousands of kilometres across India, Tibet, Persia, and Arabia — they walked together, sang together, and faced every trial as one.

Mardānā was not merely a disciple. He was a **companion** — the only human who shared the entirety of Nānak's public mission. He played the *rabāb* (a stringed instrument) while Nānak sang divine hymns. He carried the Guru's message when Nānak could not speak. He cooked, begged, walked, and — in the end — died in the Guru's arms.

This is their story.

---

## How Mardāna Met Nānak

The meeting of Gurū Nānak and Bhāī Mardānā is recorded in the **Purātan Janam Sākhī** tradition with a simplicity that carries the weight of destiny.

Mardānā was born in 1459 CE in the village of **Talvaṇḍī** (now Nankana Sāhib, Pakistan) — the same village where Nānak would be born ten years later. He was a **Mirāsī** by caste — a Muslim hereditary musician of the bardic class, whose family served as singers and entertainers. His father's name was **Baddrā**, and his mother's name was **Lakho**. From childhood, Mardānā was skilled with stringed instruments, particularly the rabāb, a lute-like instrument with a resonant, plaintive voice.

Their first encounter is narrated in several Janam Sākhīs. Young Nānak, already showing signs of his spiritual depth, heard Mardānā playing the rabāb and singing folk songs. He approached the Mirāsī boy and said:

> *"Brother Mardānā, your fingers know the strings, but your heart does not yet know the word. Let me teach you a song that will never end."*

Mardānā, according to the tradition, was struck by the authority in the boy's voice — an authority that came not from age or station but from something deeper. He handed the rabāb to Nānak, who struck a single note and sang a verse of such sweetness that Mardānā fell to his knees.

> *"Whatever you sing,"* Mardānā said, *"I will play. Wherever you go, I will follow."*

This was the beginning of a bond that would last thirty-five years.

---

## The Two Become One

The early years of their relationship are less documented than the later period, but the Janam Sākhī traditions agree on one thing: after Nānak's disappearance into the **Vaiṇ Gaṅgā** river (sometimes called the Kali Bein) in 1499 CE — the three-day submersion from which he emerged as the Guru — Mardānā was the first person he sought out.

The Purātan Janam Sākhī describes the scene:

> *Nānak went straight to Mardānā's hut. Mardānā was sitting with his rabāb unstrung, weeping — he had not slept or eaten for three days, sensing that something had changed in the universe.*
>
> *"Why do you weep?"* Nānak asked.
>
> *"Because the music has gone out of the world,"* Mardānā replied.
>
> *"Then restring your rabāb,"* Nānak said. *"For the Lord has sung a new song, and I need you to play it."*

Mardānā restringed his instrument. From that day on, he never left Nānak's side.

---

## The Rabāb and the Divine Word

The most distinctive feature of the Nānak–Mardānā relationship is the way they **made music together**. Nānak composed hymns in Punjabi, infused with the experience of the divine as *Ik Oaṅkār* (One Creator). Mardānā set them to music on the rabāb, improvising melodies drawn from the folk traditions of the Punjab.

This was not decorative accompaniment. In the Sikh understanding, **music was a vehicle for *nām* (the divine Name)**. The rabāb's sound, when played in devotion, was not separate from the hymn — it *was* the hymn, vibrating in the air, carrying the word to the ears and hearts of listeners.

The *Ādi Granth* (later compiled as the Gurū Granth Sāhib) preserves the **rāgs** — the musical modes — in which Nānak's hymns were composed. Many of these (such as *Rāg Āsā*, *Rāg Gūjrī*, *Rāg Sūhī*) were performed by Mardānā. The tradition remembers that Mardānā would sit at Nānak's feet, his fingers moving across the rabāb's strings, his voice harmonising with the Guru's, creating a sound that witnesses described as *"the voice of the One made audible."*

> *"The rabāb is not an instrument of entertainment,"* Nānak once said to Mardānā. *"It is the voice of the Beloved speaking through your hands. Play as if the universe is listening — for it is."*

---

## The Four Journeys

Mardānā walked every step of Nānak's four great *Uḍāsīs* (journeys), each lasting several years and covering thousands of kilometres.

### First Journey (c. 1500–1506): The East

They travelled eastward through the Punjab, into present-day Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Bengal, and Assam. Mardānā carried the rabāb and a bundle of provisions. They lived on alms, slept under trees or in wayside shrines, and sang wherever people would listen.

In **Sultanpur Lodhi**, the town where Nānak had worked as a storekeeper before his enlightenment, Mardānā watched as the Guru settled accounts with his former employer and distributed his savings to the poor. *"We will never need money again,"* Nānak told him. *"The Lord will provide."*

They travelled to **Hardiālī** (Haridwar), where Nānak observed Brāhmaṇas throwing water toward the rising sun as an offering to their ancestors. The Guru famously splashed water westward. When mocked, he said: *"If the water can reach your ancestors in heaven, it can reach my fields in the Punjab."* Mardānā laughed — the first laughter of a journey that would be filled with both.

### Second Journey (c. 1506–1513): The South

They went south into Rajasthan, Gujarat, and as far as the Deccan plateau. In **Kāṅkarhā** (Kankhal), near Haridwar, they encountered the **Siddhas and Nāths** — yogic ascetics who tested Nānak with riddles and metaphysical questions. Mardānā sat at the edge of these debates, playing soft melodies on the rabāb as Nānak replied to the Siddhas in verse — verses that would later be recorded in the **Siddh Goṣṭ** section of the Gurū Granth Sāhib.

In **Gujarat**, they passed through the region of **Sorath**, where Nānak composed hymns describing the soul as a bride separated from her divine spouse — imagery that Mardānā's rabāb, with its melancholy tone, brought to aching life.

### Third Journey (c. 1514–1518): The Himalayas

They turned north into the mountain kingdoms, travelling through Kashmir, Ladakh, and into **Tibet**. The cold was brutal; the passes were treacherous. Mardānā, accustomed to the plains of the Punjab, suffered terribly in the altitude.

The Janam Sākhīs record that at one point Mardānā collapsed from exhaustion and hunger. Nānak held him in his arms and fed him with his own hands.

> *"Guru,"* Mardānā wept, *"I am not strong enough for this path."*
>
> *"You are strong enough,"* Nānak replied, *"because you are not walking it alone."*

In the mountains, they encountered Tantric Buddhists and Bön shamans. Mardānā's music — foreign to Tibet — drew crowds of curious monks. Nānak sang of the One beyond all forms, and the Tibetans listened with wonder.

### Fourth Journey (c. 1518–1521): The West

The most famous journey was the last: westward into the Islamic world — through the Sindh, into Balochistan, Afghanistan, Persia, and Arabia.

Mardānā, a Muslim by birth, served as both companion and cultural interpreter. He knew the customs of Islam, the etiquette of mosques, the rhythm of the daily prayers. When Nānak encountered Sufi saints, Qāḍīs, and pilgrim caravans, Mardānā bridged the gap — not as a theologian, but as a fellow traveller who understood both worlds.

---

## Episode: Sajjan Thakur

The most famous of the Sajjan Thakur stories — and one that showcases the complementary roles of Nānak and Mardānā — is the encounter with the robber-baron **Sajjan Thakur** (also called **Sajjan the Trusted**).

Sajjan was a wealthy man who built a temple and a mosque side by side, presenting himself as a man of all faiths. But his true business was robbery and murder. He would welcome travellers, feed them, offer them a place to rest — and then kill them in the night and take their possessions.

When Nānak and Mardānā arrived at Sajjan's estate, they were welcomed with the usual hospitality. Sajjan, seeing two wandering ascetics — one a Hindu? one a Muslim? — assumed they had nothing worth stealing, but they would make good servants or, if they resisted, silent corpses.

That night, Sajjan prepared for the murder. But as he approached the travellers' quarters, he heard music.

Mardānā was playing the rabāb.

And Nānak was singing:

> *"Bronze is bright and shining, but when rubbed, its blackness appears.*
> *The body is outwardly fair, but within is filled with evil passions.*
> *The sinner's heart is like a rusty vessel — it cannot be cleansed without the fire of the Name."*
> — Gurū Nānak, Rāg Sūhī, Gurū Granth Sāhib 729 (paraphrased)

The hymn pierced Sajjan like a sword. He stopped, trembling. These men were not travellers — they were seers. They *knew*.

He fell at Nānak's feet and confessed everything: the thefts, the murders, the hypocrisy. Mardānā stopped playing and watched as Nānak lifted the weeping man.

> *"The Lord's mercy is greater than your sin,"* Nānak said. *"Serve the poor. Feed the hungry. Let the temple and mosque you built become houses of true worship: where the hungry are fed and the stranger is safe."*

Sajjan Thakur became a disciple. He dismantled his criminal enterprise and turned his wealth into a free kitchen (*laṅgar*) that lasted for generations.

Mardānā, according to the tradition, remarked afterward: *"Guru, you gave him a new life with just one song."*

Nānak replied: *"The song was yours as much as mine. The rabāb opened his heart before the words could enter."*

## Episode: The Kaaba Miracle

The most famous — and most controversial — of the Nānak–Mardānā episodes is the **Kaaba miracle**, recorded in the Miharbān and Bālā Janam Sākhī traditions.

During their journey to Arabia, Nānak and Mardānā reached **Mecca**. They arrived during the Hajj, when thousands of pilgrims were circling the Kaaba, the cube-shaped shrine at the centre of Islam's holiest mosque.

Nānak entered the mosque and, exhausted from the journey, lay down to rest with his feet pointing toward the Kaaba. This was a grave offence in Islamic etiquette — one's feet must never point toward the house of God.

A Qāḍī (judge) named **Jīvan** (some traditions call him **Ruknuddīn**) saw this and was outraged. He kicked Nānak and shouted:

> *"Wretch! Who are you to point your feet toward the house of God?"*

Nānak replied calmly:

> *"Then turn my feet toward the direction where God is not."*

The Qāḍī, furious, grabbed Nānak's feet and rotated them. But wherever he turned them, the Kaaba moved to face them. He spun Nānak in a full circle — and the Kaaba followed.

Mardānā, watching from the edge of the courtyard, laughed and played a joyful melody on the rabāb.

> *"Brother,"* he called to the Qāḍī, *"God is not confined to a stone cube. He is wherever His name is sung."*

The Qāḍī was stunned. He fell at Nānak's feet and begged forgiveness. Nānak replied:

> *"There is nothing to forgive. You acted out of love for your faith, and that love, if purified, becomes love for the One."*

The Janam Sākhīs record that the Qāḍī became a follower — not of Nānak's tradition, but of his truth: that the One is not confined to any direction, any book, or any building.

---

## Episode: The Blessing of the Starving Family

A lesser-known but deeply moving episode from the **Purātan Janam Sākhī** concerns a poor **Rai Sikh** (a lower-caste farming community) family that Nānak and Mardānā encountered near **Eminābād** (in present-day Pakistani Punjab).

The family was starving. The father had sold everything; the mother had not eaten in three days to keep their children alive. When Nānak and Mardānā arrived at their mud hut, the parents wept with shame — they had nothing to offer the travellers, not even a cup of water.

Nānak turned to Mardānā: *"Play."*

Mardānā played a slow, aching raga — the raga of separation, of longing, of the soul crying out to God. Nānak sang:

> *"The Lord who creates also provides. The Lord who gives hunger also gives bread. Trust in the One who never abandons."*

As he sang, a neighbour arrived with a basket of grain — sent, the neighbour said, by a stranger who had been told in a dream to bring food to this house. A merchant came with cloth. A farmer brought milk.

By evening, the family's hut was filled with provisions.

The father fell at Nānak's feet. *"You are God,"* he sobbed.

Nānak lifted him. *"I am not God. I am a servant of God. And God does not need a miracle to feed His children — He only needs someone to carry the food."*

Mardānā, wiping his eyes, said: *"Guru, today I learned that music is not just for the wealthy who can afford to listen. It is a prayer that moves the world."*

## The Dialogues Between Nānak and Mardānā

The Janam Sākhīs preserve a number of dialogues between the two companions — some playful, some profound, all revealing of their unique bond.

### The Question of Caste

One day, as they walked through a village, Mardānā was refused alms because of his Mirāsī caste. He returned to Nānak with an empty bowl, his pride wounded.

> *"Guru,"* Mardānā said, *"they will not give me food because my ancestors were musicians. They say Mirāsīs are unclean."*
>
> *Nānak looked at him. "And what do you say?"*
>
> *"I say a hungry man is clean enough to eat."*
>
> *"Then listen to your own hunger, not their words. Caste is not made by birth, but by deeds. A Brāhmaṇa who cheats is lower than a Mirāsī who prays. The Lord does not ask who your father was — He asks who you are."*

Mardānā later asked: *"Guru, why do people cling to caste when you teach against it?"*

Nānak replied: *"Because it is easier to judge by the body than to see the soul. The body is a borrowed garment — it will be shed. The soul is the truth that remains."*

### The Question of the Long Road

At the end of a long day's walk, with blistered feet and empty stomachs, Mardānā asked:

> *"Guru, we have walked through a hundred villages. We have sung in a hundred temples and mosques. How many have truly listened?"*
>
> *Nānak smiled. "If one soul in each village has heard, the journey is worth it. The seed does not see the tree. It only knows that it must break open."*
>
> *Mardānā: "But I am tired, Guru. My fingers ache from the strings. My feet ache from the road."*
>
> *Nānak: "Then rest. The Lord does not need your exhaustion — He needs your love. A tired servant who works with love is greater than a rested one who works without it."*

### The Question of Death

As the years passed and Mardānā's body began to fail, he asked Nānak the question that haunted him:

> *"Guru, when I die, where will I go?"*
>
> *Nānak: "Where the notes of your rabāb go when the song ends."*
>
> *Mardānā: "They disappear into silence."*
>
> *Nānak: "No, brother. They disappear into the One who hears all songs. The silence that receives a true note is the same silence that receives a true soul."*

---

## Mardānā's Death and Nānak's Grief

After more than thirty years of wandering, Mardānā's health finally gave way. He was in his mid-seventies — an extraordinary age for a man who had walked the length of Asia, through deserts and mountains, on a diet of begged bread and river water.

The final scene is recorded in multiple Janam Sākhī traditions with a heart-wrenching intimacy.

They were in the town of **Kartarpur** — the community Nānak had founded on the banks of the Ravi River. Mardānā lay on a simple cot, his rabāb beside him. Nānak sat at his head, holding his hand.

> *"Guru,"* Mardānā whispered, *"I can no longer play. My fingers are cold."*
>
> *"Then let me play for you,"* Nānak said.
>
> He took the rabāb — an instrument he had rarely touched in all their years together — and drew from it a single, pure note.
>
> *"That is the note of love,"* Nānak said. *"It does not need strings. It needs a heart."*

Mardānā smiled. His breaths grew shallow. The disciples gathered around, weeping.

> *"Mardānā,"* Nānak said, his voice steady but his eyes wet, *"you have been my brother, my companion, my voice when I had no words. The Lord gave me one gift above all others: a friend who understood without explanation."*

Mardānā's last words were: *"Guru, I am not afraid. I have walked with the Eternal One all my life. Now I will rest in Him."*

He closed his eyes. The rabāb fell silent.

The tradition records that Nānak — who had comforted thousands, who had faced emperors and yogis without fear — **wept**. He did not weep for Mardānā, who had departed in peace. He wept for himself, who had lost a companion that no one could replace.

> *"The world is empty,"* Nānak said. *"The music has gone out of it."*

He refused to eat for a day. The next morning, he took the rabāb and hung it on a peg in the laṅgar — the community kitchen — saying:

> *"Let this instrument remind us that true music is not in the strings but in the heart that plays them. Mardānā taught us that the lowest-born can be the highest-loved. He taught us that the singer and the song are one."*

---

## Mardānā's Place in Sikh Memory

Mardānā died in **1534 CE**, five years before Nānak himself departed this life. He was buried — or, according to some traditions, cremated — near Kartarpur. The exact location is disputed, but his memory is not.

In Sikh tradition, Mardānā occupies a unique position:

- He is **Bhāī** (Brother) — not a Guru, not a saint, but a **companion** who walked the path alongside the Guru and was elevated by that proximity.
- His **rabāb** became a symbol of music as spiritual practice — the idea that the divine Name can be carried on the strings of an instrument as truly as on the tongue of a devotee.
- His **relationship with Nānak** is the model for the Guru–disciple bond: not one of domination and submission, but of mutual love, shared hardship, and complementary gifts.

The *Ādi Granth* preserves hymns that were sung by Nānak and accompanied by Mardānā. When a Sikh recites *Japjī Sāhib* or sings *Āsā Dī Vār*, they are — in a very real sense — continuing the music that Nānak sang and Mardānā played.

---

## The Legacy

The bond between Nānak and Mardānā is more than a historical curiosity. It embodies a core Sikh teaching: that **spiritual companionship** is the vehicle of spiritual growth. Nānak did not walk alone. He walked with a Mirāsī Muslim whose fingers knew the strings of the rabāb and whose heart knew the love of the One.

In a world divided by caste, religion, and status, Nānak and Mardānā demonstrated that the path of truth is open to all — and that those who walk it together are stronger than those who walk alone.

> *"In the company of the holy,*  
> *The name of the Lord is sung.*  
> *There, in that gathering,*  
> *The darkness of the mind is dispelled."*
> — Gurū Nānak, *Rāg Mājh*, Gurū Granth Sāhib 95

Mardānā's rabāb hangs silent now — but the music it played has never stopped. Every recitation of the Gurū Granth Sāhib, every *kīrtan* in a Gurudwārā, carries the echo of that first string plucked by a Mirāsī boy who became the Guru's brother.
