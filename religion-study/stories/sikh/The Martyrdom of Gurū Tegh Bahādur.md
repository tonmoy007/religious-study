# The Martyrdom of Gurū Tegh Bahādur

> *"Sir dīyā sir dīyā, par dharam na dharam chhoṛā."*
> *"I give my head, I give my head, but I will not give up my faith."*
> — **Gurū Tegh Bahādur**, his last words according to the *Śahīdī Bilaś*

On the morning of **11 November 1675**, a 54-year-old man was brought in chains to the **Chandnī Chauk** square of Mughal Delhi — the great public plaza just outside the Red Fort where emperors displayed their power and criminals met their ends. He was not a rebel, not a thief, not a traitor. He was a saint who had spent twenty years in silent meditation, a poet whose 115 hymns would become scripture, and a Guru who had walked deliberately toward his own death for months, knowing exactly what awaited him. His name was **Gurū Tegh Bahādur** — the Ninth Sikh Guru — and before the sun set that day, Delhi would witness one of the most consequential executions in Indian religious history.

Behind him, three of his closest disciples — **Bhāī Matī Dās**, **Bhāī Satī Dās**, and **Bhāī Dayālā** — had already been tortured to death in front of him, their screams intended to break his resolve. In front of him stood the executioner's blade. And in the crowd were the people he had come to save: a delegation of Kashmiri Pandits, watching in horror as the man who had offered himself in their place met his end.

The Sikh tradition would remember this day as the martyrdom of **Hind kī Chādar** — the Shield of India — and it would change everything.

---

## Emperor Aurangzeb and the Crisis of the Hindu Faiths

To understand why Gurū Tegh Bahādur died, one must first understand the man who ordered his death: **Muhi-ud-Dīn Muhammad Aurangzeb** (1618–1707), the sixth Mughal Emperor.

| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Emperor** | Aurangzeb Alamgir (reigned 1658–1707) |
| **Religious orientation** | A devout Sunni Muslim who rejected the pluralist policies of his great-grandfather Akbar |
| **Key policies** | Reinstituted *jizya* (poll tax on non-Muslims) in 1679; destroyed Hindu temples at an unprecedented scale; banned construction of new temples; dismissed non-Muslims from high office; imposed sharia-based governance; forcibly converted prisoners of war |
| **Target region** | **Kashmir** — a predominantly Hindu region under Muslim rule — became the epicentre of persecution |
| **The crisis** | By the 1670s, Aurangzeb's governors across the empire were enforcing conversions with increasing zeal. In Kashmir, the Muslim Governor **Iftikhār Khān** delivered an ultimatum to the Kashmiri Pandits (the Hindu Brahmin community): convert to Islam or face execution |

Aurangzeb's reign marked a sharp rupture from the religious pluralism that had characterised the Mughal Empire under Akbar (1556–1605). Where Akbar had abolished the jizya, patronised Hindu temples, married Hindu princesses, and founded the syncretic **Dīn-i-Ilāhī**, Aurangzeb saw his empire through the lens of orthodox Sunni Islam. The emperor was not a fanatic in the crude sense — he was a deeply pious, personally austere man who prayed five times daily, memorised the Qur'an, and lived on simple food and coarse cloth. But his piety took the form of a rigid enforcement of sharia that left no room for the religious diversity that had made the Mughal Empire possible.

For the Hindu communities of northern India, particularly the Brahmins who were the traditional custodians of temple worship, scriptural knowledge, and community identity, Aurangzeb's policies created an existential crisis. The Kashmiri Pandits — an ancient Brahmin community with a continuous presence in the valley for over two millennia — were among the hardest hit. Faced with the choice of death or conversion, many fled into the mountains. But flight could not last forever. They needed someone who could stand before the Emperor and say what they could not say themselves.

---

## The Delegation of the Kashmiri Pandits

In the spring of **1674**, a group of desperate men arrived at the hill town of **Anandpur Sāhib**, in the foothills of the Himalayas. They were Kashmiri Pandits, led by **Pandit Kīrpa Rām** (also known as Kīrpa Datta) — a learned Brahmin who had exhausted every avenue of appeal. They had travelled for weeks, through snow and mountain passes, to reach the court of the Ninth Sikh Guru.

The Sikh tradition records that when the Pandits entered the Guru's presence, they broke down weeping. They poured out their story: the ultimatum from Iftikhār Khān, the destruction of their temples, the desecration of their sacred texts, the families torn apart by forced conversions. They had already travelled to Delhi and petitioned Aurangzeb himself. The Emperor's answer was the same: convert or die.

Gurū Tegh Bahādur listened in silence. He was a man of few words — his entire life had been a preparation for stillness. He had spent twenty years in a subterranean cell at Bakālā, meditating day and night. He had composed hymns that spoke of the world as a shadow, life as a bubble, and the Divine Name as the only reality. But now the world had come to his door, and stillness was no longer an option.

According to the *Sūraj Prakāś Granth* and the *Śahīdī Bilaś*, the Guru sat for a long time in contemplation, his face troubled. His young son, **Gobind Rāi** — only nine years old — noticed his father's distress and asked what was wrong. When the Guru explained the Pandits' plight, the boy — the future **Gurū Gobind Siṅgh** — is said to have replied with words that have become legendary in Sikh tradition:

> *"Father, there is no one greater than you who can protect their faith. You must go to Delhi. You must offer yourself. If you do not, who will?"*

The Guru looked at his son — the same son who would one day create the Khalsa — and saw that the universe had already decided. He turned to the Pandits and spoke:

> *"Return to your homes. Go to Aurangzeb. Tell him: 'If you can convert **Gurū Tegh Bahādur** to Islam, then all the Hindus will follow.' I will be in Delhi soon."*

He was not negotiating. He was not bluffing. He was offering himself as a substitute — a living sacrifice that would absorb the violence of the empire and break its power over the innocent.

---

## The Journey to Delhi

In **May 1675**, Gurū Tegh Bahādur left Anandpur Sāhib and began his journey toward Delhi. He knew he was walking to his death. Every step was a step toward the executioner's sword. Yet he did not travel in haste, nor in despair, nor with an army. He travelled as a pilgrim — slowly, deliberately, visiting Sikh congregations (*sangats*) along the way, blessing those who came to see him, and teaching as he had always taught.

He was accompanied by three devoted disciples who had chosen to share his fate:

| Companion | Role | Method of Execution |
|-----------|------|---------------------|
| **Bhāī Matī Dās** | A learned Sikh, a scholar of the scriptures | **Sawn alive** between two wooden planks (the *chirā* torture) |
| **Bhāī Dayālā** | A devoted Sikh from the Sāṅdhū Jatt clan | **Boiled alive** in a cauldron of water |
| **Bhāī Satī Dās** | A Sikh of the Chhīmbā (tailor) caste | **Wrapped in cotton and burned alive** |

These three men are rarely mentioned outside Sikh tradition, but their deaths were an integral part of the martyrdom. They were executed first, in front of the Guru, specifically to frighten him into submission. Each was killed by a different method, as if the Mughal executioners were displaying their full repertoire of terror. The Guru watched them die, one by one, and did not waver.

The party travelled through the Punjab plains, crossed the Sutlej and Yamuna rivers, and made their way through the villages of what is now Haryana. At each stop, Sikhs gathered to pay their respects. Many begged the Guru to turn back, to take shelter, to raise an army. He refused. He was not going to Delhi to fight. He was going to Delhi to bear witness.

Near the village of **Mālakhpur** (in present-day Haryana, about 80 km north of Delhi), they were intercepted by Mughal forces under the authority of the governor of Sirhind. The Guru and his companions were arrested, bound in chains, and transported to Delhi. They were imprisoned in the **Bassī Bāgh** pavilion — a walled garden near the Red Fort that now houses the **Gurdwārā Śahīd Gañj** (the Martyrs' Shrine).

---

## The Trial Before the Emperor

The imprisonment lasted several weeks. During this time, the Guru was brought before Aurangzeb's court for a show trial. The proceedings followed the standard Mughal template for refractory religious leaders:

1. **The Demand for Conversion**: The Emperor's judges offered the Guru wealth, land, and high office if he would accept Islam. The Guru refused. He had not walked to Delhi to trade his faith for silver.

2. **The Demand for a Miracle**: The court *qāzīs* (Islamic judges) demanded that the Guru perform a miracle to prove his divine favour — a standard test for holy men who claimed spiritual authority. Displaying supernatural power would have established his credentials in the Mughal framework of prophetic legitimacy. The Guru replied: *"I am a servant of the One God. He alone works miracles. I have no power of my own. If you want a miracle, look at the sun and the moon — they are miracles enough."*

3. **The Final Demand — Submission**: The Emperor ordered the Guru to submit to imperial authority, cease his teachings, and acknowledge the supremacy of Islam in the Mughal domains. The Guru refused.

> *"Why will you die for these Hindus?"* Aurangzeb is reported to have asked. *"They are not even your own people."*

The Guru's reply — preserved in Sikh tradition — is one of the most important theological statements in the history of religious freedom:

> *"I am a protector of the weak. Their path and my path both lead to the same God. If I abandon them, I abandon my own dharma. I did not come here to save Hindus or Sikhs — I came here to save the truth itself."*

The qāzīs pronounced the death sentence. The Guru was to be executed publicly, as an example to all who would defy the empire's religious policy. The date was set: **11 November 1675**.

---

## The Execution at Chandnī Chauk

The morning of 11 November 1675 dawned over Delhi like any other — the calls to prayer from the Jama Masjid, the bustle of Chandnī Chauk's markets, the dust and noise of the Mughal capital. But there was an unusual tension in the air. Word had spread that the Sikh Guru was to be executed, and crowds gathered in the square — Sikhs who had come to pray, Mughal officials who had come to witness the display of imperial power, Hindus who had come to mourn, and curious onlookers who had come to see a man die.

The Mughals staged the execution as a carefully choreographed spectacle of terror. The Guru's three companions were brought out first:

**Bhāī Matī Dās** was stripped and bound between two wooden planks. The executioners took a saw and, slowly, began to cut him in half — from the head downward — while he was still alive. The Sikh tradition records that not a single cry of pain escaped his lips. He died reciting the Mūl Mantra — the foundational Sikh prayer declaring the Oneness of God.

**Bhāī Dayālā** was brought next. He was placed in a cauldron of boiling water — or, in some accounts, boiling oil — and immersed until he was dead. The Guru watched.

**Bhāī Satī Dās** was wrapped in cotton, doused with oil, and set on fire. He burned alive before the Guru's eyes.

The crowd was silent. The Guru had not flinched.

Now the Guru himself was brought forward. His chains were removed. The executioner — a man named **Jalālu-d-dīn** according to some accounts — ordered him to kneel before the Emperor's representative. The Guru refused.

> *"I bow only to the One who created the heavens and the earth,"* he said. *"I do not bow to any man."*

He was placed in an iron cage to restrain him. The executioner raised his sword. And then, according to the *Śahīdī Bilaś*, the Guru spoke his final words — words that have echoed through Sikh history ever since:

> **"Sir dīyā sir dīyā, par dharam na dharam chhoṛā."**
> *"I give my head, I give my head, but I will not give up my faith."*

The sword fell. The Guru's head was severed from his body in a single stroke.

A great cry went up from the crowd — not the roar of a mob satisfied, but the wail of a people who had just witnessed something they could not yet comprehend. The Mughal officials ordered the Guru's head to be displayed on a spike at the gate of the Red Fort as a warning. His body was left in the square, forbidden to be claimed.

---

## The Recovery of the Body — Lakkhī Shāh's Sacrifice

The Mughals intended to leave the Guru's body exposed as a trophy and a warning. But a fierce dust storm descended upon Delhi that evening — a storm so violent that it plunged the city into an unnatural darkness. In the chaos, the body could not be guarded effectively, and a wealthy Sikh merchant of Delhi named **Lakkhī Shāh Bāṇī** (also known as Lakhī Shāh) saw his opportunity.

Lakkhī Shāh was a Khatrī trader who had secretly remained devoted to the Guru despite living under Mughal rule. He crept to the Chandnī Chauk square, wrapped the Guru's headless body in a white cloth, and carried it to his home at **Rākāb Gañj** (near what is now the Parliament House of India). But he could not simply cremate the body in the open — if the Mughals discovered it, they would seize and desecrate the remains.

Lakkhī Shāh made an extraordinary decision. He built a funeral pyre inside his own house, laid the Guru's body upon it, and set it ablaze. When Mughal soldiers arrived at his door, alerted by neighbours, they found the house in flames. Lakkhī Shāh — rather than allow the Guru's body to be violated, and rather than face arrest and torture himself — **immolated himself** on the same pyre. He died with his Guru, a double sacrifice that Sikh tradition honours as one of the most profound acts of devotion in its history.

The site of this cremation is now **Gurdwārā Rakāb Gañj Sāhib** — one of Delhi's most sacred Sikh shrines, located just a stone's throw from the Indian Parliament. It stands as a silent witness to the price of religious freedom.

---

## The Journey of the Head — Bhāī Jaitā's Race to Anandpur

While Lakkhī Shāh recovered the body, the Guru's severed head — which had been placed on a spike at the Red Fort gate — was retrieved by another devoted Sikh: **Bhāī Jaitā** (later known as **Bhāī Jagat Rām**).

According to Sikh tradition, when the dust storm struck, Bhāī Jaitā crept to the Red Fort, climbed the gate, and took the Guru's head from its spike. He wrapped it in a cloth — the same cloth that would later be used to wrap the Guru Granth Sāhib — and began an epic journey. He carried the head across hundreds of kilometres of Mughal territory, through the storm and the night, northward toward Anandpur Sāhib.

The journey took him through villages where Sikhs sheltered him, across rivers swollen by the storm, and through Mughal patrols that would have killed him on sight if they had discovered what he carried. For days he walked, barely stopping, driven by a purpose that transcended exhaustion.

When he finally arrived at Anandpur Sāhib, the Guru's son — **Gobind Rāi**, now the Tenth Guru — received his father's head. The Sikh tradition records that the young Guru wept when he saw it, but then lifted it high and declared:

> *"This is the seed from which the Khalsa will grow."*

He performed the cremation rites with full honours at Anandpur, at the site that is now marked by **Takht Srī Keshgarh Sāhib** — the very platform where, twenty-four years later, he would baptise the first five members of the Khalsa and transform Sikhism forever.

## Theological Meaning — "Hind kī Chādar"

The martyrdom of Gurū Tegh Bahādur entered Sikh memory under a title that no other Guru bears: **Hind kī Chādar** — the Shield of India. The phrase captures the unique character of his sacrifice and has shaped Sikh theology in profound ways.

| Dimension | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Universal defence of religious freedom** | Tegh Bahādur died not for Sikhs but for Hindus — for the right of any community to practise its faith without coercion. This elevates his martyrdom from a sectarian event to a universal principle: the Guru is the protector of all who are persecuted for their beliefs |
| **Substitutionary sacrifice** | The Guru deliberately offered himself in place of the Kashmiri Pandits — a vicarious death. He did not merely protest; he took the blow meant for others upon himself |
| **The marriage of śakti and bhakti** | The Guru who had spent 20 years in silent meditation (bhakti — the path of devotion) showed that the same person could walk to Delhi without an army and face death without resistance (śakti — the power of non-violent witness). This is a different kind of heroism from his father Gurū Har Gobind's four battles, yet it requires no less courage |
| **The defeat of the tyrant** | Aurangzeb killed the Guru's body but could not kill his faith. Within a generation, the Guru's son would create the Khalsa (1699) and shatter Mughal power in the Punjab. The martyrdom seeded the destruction of the empire that ordered it |
| **The model of Sant-Sipāhī** | The martyrdom demonstrated that the saint-soldier (the Sikh ideal) is not merely a warrior who also prays — he is equally willing to **die without fighting** if that is what the situation demands. Courage is not the same as violence, and the willingness to die is sometimes more powerful than the willingness to kill |

### What Makes This Martyrdom Unique

Gurū Tegh Bahādur is unique among the world's martyred religious figures in one crucial respect: **he died for the faith of others**. Most martyrs — Socrates, Jesus, Gurū Arjan, Imām Ḥusayn — die for their own convictions. They refuse to recant or compromise their own beliefs. Tegh Bahādur died to protect the beliefs of a **different community entirely** — the Hindu Pandits of Kashmir, who were not even Sikhs.

| Figure | Died For |
|--------|----------|
| **Socrates** | His own philosophy and integrity |
| **Jesus** | His own identity as the Messiah and his mission |
| **Gurū Arjan** (1606) | His own faith — the integrity of the Sikh scripture |
| **Imām Ḥusayn** (680 CE) | His own community — the restoration of the Caliphate to the Prophet's family |
| **Gurū Tegh Bahādur** (1675) | **Others** — the Kashmiri Pandits, a different religious community facing forced conversion |

This places him in a category of his own: the **altruistic martyr** — one who takes the place of the threatened other, who makes his own body a shield for strangers. In Sikh theology, this is the highest expression of **sarbat dā bhala** (the welfare of all) — the principle that the Sikh community's duty of protection extends beyond its own members to all who suffer injustice, regardless of their religion.

### The Enduring Symbol

The sword that killed Gurū Tegh Bahādur became the symbol of his sacrifice. The *tegh* (sword) in his name — **Tegh Bahādur** (the Sword of the Sword) — was the weapon of his death, but in Sikh iconography it represents not violence but the defence of the innocent. Every Sikh Gurdwārā flies a *nishān sāhib* (the saffron flag) bearing the *khaṇḍā* — a double-edged sword that evokes both the Guru's name and his sacrifice.

And every year on **24 November**, Sikhs around the world observe **Śahīdī Divas** (Martyrdom Day) of Gurū Tegh Bahādur. At **Gurdwārā Śīś Gañj Sāhib** in Chandnī Chauk — the exact site of the execution — thousands gather to remember the day a saint gave his head so that no one else would have to give up their faith.
