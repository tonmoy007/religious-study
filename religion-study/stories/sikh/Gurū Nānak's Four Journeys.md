# Gurū Nānak's Four Journeys

> *"There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim."*
> — Gurū Nānak, emerging from the Vein River, Sultānpur Lodhī, 1499 CE

> *"I am neither a pilgrim to Mecca nor a dweller at a Hindu shrine. I serve the One who is beyond both — and I bow where the One is worshipped."*
> — ādi Granth, Mārū Rāga (adapted)

The four great journeys of **Gurū Nānak** — the *Udāsīs* — are the axis on which the Sikh tradition turns. Between 1499 and 1521 CE, Nānak walked an estimated thirty thousand kilometres across the known world: from the Brahmaputra delta in the east to Baghdad in the west, from the Tibetan plateau in the north to the shores of Sri Lanka in the south. He travelled not as a conqueror, not as a merchant, not as a pilgrim to any one shrine, but as a bearer of the divine *Śabad* (Word) — a wandering teacher sent from the court of God with a single commission: to repeat the *Nām* (the divine Name), to teach others to repeat it, and to abide by the truth in all things.

The Udāsīs are preserved in the **Janam Sākhī** tradition — especially the Purātan (oldest) Janam Sākhī, the Bālā Janam Sākhī, and the Miharbān Janam Sākhī — and though scholars debate their precise historicity (the four-journey framework may be a later hagiographic structuring), their symbolic geography is the living map of Sikh universalism. Every encounter — with Siddha yogīs and Sufi pīrs, Hindu pandits and Muslim qazīs, Buddhist monks and Tantric adepts — is a parable of the one truth: that the Divine is not the property of any religion, and that the Name is accessible to all who call upon it with sincerity.

What follows is the story of those four journeys, told in their traditional sequence, woven from the Janam Sākhī accounts, the hymns of the Gurū Granth Sāhib, and the living memory of the Sikh tradition.

---

## The Commission at the Vein River

The story of the Udāsīs begins not on the road but by a riverbank in the town of **Sultānpur Lodhī**, in the Punjab of the Lodi Sultanate. Nānak was thirty years old — a married man with two sons, employed as steward-in-charge (modī) of the royal granary under the Muslim governor Daulat Khan Lodi. He was known as a quiet, generous man who gave away grain to the poor, but nothing about him suggested that he was about to change the world.

One morning — the traditional date is Vaisākhī, 1499 CE — Nānak went to the **Vein River** for his ritual bath. He undressed, entered the water — and did not return. His clothes lay on the bank; search parties found nothing. For three days he was gone.

The Janam Sākhīs describe what happened in those three days. Nānak was taken to the **Darbār of Akāl Purakh** (the Timeless One) — the divine court beyond time and space. There he was given a cup of the *Amrit* (nectar) of the *Nām* and commanded:

> *"Go into the world to repeat the Nām, teach others to repeat it, and abide by the truth in all things."*

On the third day he reappeared at the same spot, emerged from the water, and was silent for a full day. When he finally spoke, his words were:

> *"Nā kōī hindū, nā kōī musalmān"* — There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim.

The declaration was not a statement of religious identity politics. It was a metaphysical announcement: before the One, all labels fall away. The Hindu and the Muslim are categories of the mind — the soul, which belongs to God, has no religion. This was not ecumenical tolerance in the modern sense. It was the proclamation of a radical unity that transcended and dissolved all sectarian divisions.

From that moment, Nānak was the Gurū — the bearer of the divine Word. He gave away everything he had, resigned his post, and stepped onto the road. With him went **Bhāī Mardānā**, a Muslim rebāb-player from a low-caste family of mirāsīs (musicians), whose instrument and voice would accompany the Gurū's hymns across four continents of the soul.

---

## The First Udāsī: The Eastern Journey (c. 1499–1506)

The first journey took Nānak and Mardānā eastward — down the Gangetic plain, through the heartland of Hindu pilgrimage and Tantric practice, into the delta of the Brahmaputra and the kingdoms of Assam and Bengal.

### Through the Sacred Cities

Leaving Sultānpur, the pair walked through the Punjab into the upper Doab, passing through **Delhi** — then the seat of the Lodi Sultanate — where Nānak preached in the bazaars and the mosques. From Delhi they moved south-east to **Kanpur** and **Allahabad** (Prayāg), the confluence of the Ganges and Yamuna, the most sacred site in Hindu geography. At the Triveṇī Saṅgam, where the mythical Sarasvatī is said to join the two rivers, Nānak saw thousands of pilgrims bathing for the remission of sins. He told them:

> *"The true purifier is the Nām, not the water of the Ganges. The soul is cleansed by truth, not by ritual. The river of mercy flows through the heart — if you cannot see it, you have bathed in vain."*

From Prayāg they continued east to **Banaras** (Vārānasī), the city of a thousand temples, the seat of Brāhmaṇical learning and Śaiva devotion. There Nānak encountered the Pandits of the city — the custodians of the Vedas and the scholarly guardians of orthodoxy. A famous Janam Sākhī episode describes his encounter with the chief Pandit:

The Pandit asked: *"Who are you? A Hindu? A Muslim? A yogī? A sannyāsī?"*

Nānak replied: *"I am the servant of the One who is beyond all these. The Vedas and the Qur'an speak of the same truth, but you have bound the truth with the rope of words and forgotten its meaning."*

He then composed the hymn that would become the **Japjī Sāhib** — the morning prayer of the Sikhs — a meditation on the nature of the One, the process of creation, and the path of the Nām.

### Into the East: Bengal and Assam

From Banaras, Nānak and Mardānā travelled deeper east — into **Bihar**, past the ancient ruins of Nālandā (the great Buddhist university sacked a century earlier), and into **Bengal**. In Bengal, the Gurū encountered the **Sahajiyā** tradition — a Tantric Buddhist-Vaiṣṇava synthesis that emphasised spontaneous, natural devotion over ritual. He debated with the Sahajiyā adepts, affirming their focus on inner experience while redirecting it from esoteric sexual yogic practices to the discipline of the Nām.

In **Assam**, Nānak met practitioners of the **Śākta Tantra** — the left-hand path of the Goddess, with its rituals of blood sacrifice, magic, and power. The Janam Sākhīs describe his encounter with a Tantric yoginī who demonstrated her powers by levitating. Nānak is said to have responded:

> *"The power to fly is a small thing. The real miracle is to conquer the ego. Can you fly to the court of the One? That flight requires no wings but the Nām."*

The Tantric practitioners, impressed by his fearlessness and his evident spiritual authority, became his students.

### The Return

The eastern journey lasted approximately seven years. Nānak returned to the Punjab around 1506 CE, pausing briefly at his family home before setting out again. His two sons, Srī Chand and Lakhmī Dās, had grown into young men during his absence — but the Gurū had no intention of settling. The road was calling again, this time to the south.

---

## The Second Udāsī: The Southern Journey (c. 1506–1513)

The second journey took Nānak and Mardānā southward — through Rajasthan and Gujarat, down the western coast of India, into the Deccan, and across the sea to Sri Lanka.

### Rajasthan and Gujarat

From the Punjab, the travellers passed through the desert kingdoms of **Rājasthān** — the land of the Rajputs — where Nānak visited the temples of the Jains and the shrines of the Bhakti saints. At **Ajmer**, he paid homage at the dargāh of **Mu'īnuddīn Chishtī**, the great Sufi saint of the Chishtī order, and is said to have recognised in the Sufi tradition a path parallel to his own: the annihilation of the self in the love of the Beloved.

In **Gujarat**, Nānak visited the temple of **Somanāth** — the great Śaiva shrine that had been plundered by Mahmūd of Ghaznī five centuries earlier and rebuilt multiple times since. Standing before the liṅga, the symbol of Śiva, Nānak is said to have remarked:

> *"If Śiva dwells here, he dwells equally in every stone and every heart. Do not confine the Infinite to a single pillar."*

### The Deccan and the Tamil Land

Travelling down the western coast, Nānak and Mardāna passed through **Maharashtra**, visiting the cave-temples of **Ellorā** and **Ajanta**, and the pilgrimage centre of **Pandharpur** — the heart of the Vārkarī tradition, the Bhakti movement of the Marathi saints (Jñāneśvar, Tukārām, Nāmdev). Here Nānak found kindred spirits: the Vārkarīs were householders who combined devotion to Viṭṭhal (a form of Kṛṣṇa) with a radical rejection of caste hierarchy.

In the Tamil country, Nānak visited the temple of **Śrīraṅgam** — the great Vaiṣṇava temple on an island in the Kāverī River — and the city of **Madurai**, the centre of Tamil Śaiva Siddhānta. At **Rāmeśvaram**, at the tip of the Indian peninsula, he stood on the shore looking across the sea to Sri Lanka. The Janam Sākhīs record his encounter with the Brāhmaṇa priests of the famous temple:

> *"Why do you perform rituals for the dead?"* Nānak asked them. *"Can the dead be saved by the living? Each soul must walk its own path. Feed the hungry instead — that is the ritual that truly serves the departed."*

### The Crossing to Sri Lanka

From Rāmeśvaram, according to the Miharbān Janam Sākhī and the Purātan tradition, Nānak and Mardānā crossed the sea to **Sri Lanka** — the island kingdom of the Sinhalese, a stronghold of Theravāda Buddhism. They landed at the port of **Mannar** and travelled to **Kandy**, where Nānak is said to have visited the Temple of the Tooth and debated with the Buddhist monks.

The Sri Lankan king, a devout Buddhist, tested Nānak by offering him a seat in the royal court. The Gurū refused to bow, explaining that he bowed only to the One. The king asked: *"If your God is everywhere, why can't you see him?"* Nānak replied by asking the king to bring a cup of milk, then a cup of ghee (clarified butter). He poured the ghee into the milk and asked: *"Can you separate them now?"* When the king said no, Nānak said: *"The One is in the world like ghee in milk — invisible to the eye that does not see, but present in every drop. The One is not seen because the eye is impure. Purify the heart with the Nām, and you will see."*

The tradition holds that the king became a follower and gave Nānak land for a *dharamsālā* — a place of worship.

### The Return

The southern journey lasted approximately seven years. Nānak returned to the Punjab around 1513 CE, having walked the length and breadth of the Indian subcontinent. But he did not stay long. A third journey was calling — this time to the north, into the high Himalayas.

---

## The Third Udāsī: The Northern Journey (c. 1513–1518)

The third journey was the most physically demanding — a trek through the **Kashmir valley**, across the **Pir Panjal** range, into **Ladakh**, and possibly as far as **Tibet**. Here, in the high altitudes where the air thins and the landscape opens into vastness, Nānak encountered the **Siddha yogīs** of the Nath tradition — the most austere ascetics of the Indian religious world — and the **Buddhist monks** of the Mahāyāna and Vajrayāna schools.

### The Encounter with the Siddhas

The most famous episode of the northern journey — and one of the most celebrated in the entire Janam Sākhī canon — is Nānak's encounter with the **Siddhas** (perfected yogīs) at **Achhal Bataālā**, a site in the Himalayan foothills near the source of the Ganges. The Siddhas were the masters of the **Nath** tradition — the followers of **Gorakhnāth** and **Matsyendranāth** — who claimed mastery over the body, the breath, and the elements through *haṭha yoga* and *kuṇḍalinī* practice.

The leader of the Siddhas, **Bharatharī** (or, in some versions, **Gorakhnāth** himself), saw Nānak approaching and recognised that this was no ordinary pilgrim. The Siddhas were seated in their meditation cave, surrounded by the paraphernalia of yogic power: the horn (siṅgī), the patched coat, the earrings, the staff, the begging bowl. They had been waiting for such a one — a sign that the age of true teaching was returning.

A dialogue followed — preserved as the **Siddh Goṣṭ** (the Discourse with the Siddhas) in the Gurū Granth Sāhib (Rāga Rāmakalī, pp. 938–946). In this famous exchange, the Siddhas pressed Nānak on the central question of the renunciant tradition:

> **Siddhas:** *"You wear the white clothes of a householder. You carry no staff, no begging bowl. You have not cut your hair. How can you claim to be a teacher of the spiritual path? The world is a burning fire — how can you cross the ocean of saṃsāra without renouncing the world?"*

> **Nānak:** *"The world is indeed a burning fire — but the fire is in the mind, not in the world. The true renunciant is one who lives in the world but is not of the world — like the lotus that grows in water yet remains untouched by it. The true staff is the Nām. The true begging bowl is humility. The true earrings are the ears that hear the divine Word. The true patched coat is contentment. The true horn is the call of the Truth that awakens the sleeping soul."*

> **Siddhas:** *"What is the path to liberation?"*

> **Nānak:** *"The path is one: the Nām. There is no other. Repeat the Name. Serve the One. Share what you have. This is the path. The cave of the heart is the true Himalayan retreat."*

The Siddhas, impressed by his fearlessness and the depth of his wisdom, bowed to Nānak as an enlightened being. The Siddh Goṣṭ remains one of the foundational texts of Sikh philosophy — a dialogue that defines how the Sikh path differs from the classical Indian renunciant tradition: not by rejection of the world, but by *transformation* of it.

### Kashmir and Ladakh

From the Siddha cave, Nānak and Mardānā continued north into the **Kashmir valley** — the paradise of the Mughal gardens, the seat of Kashmiri Śaivism and of a thriving Sufi culture. In Śrīnagar, Nānak visited the temple of **Śiva at Harī Parbat** and the shrine of the Sufi saint **Shaikh Nūruddīn Nārnawī**. He debated with the Pandits of the Kashmir valley, who were masters of the Pratyabhijñā (recognition) school of non-dual Śaivism. Nānak told them:

> *"You speak of the recognition of the self as Śiva. I speak of the recognition of the self as the servant of the One. The difference is only in words — the truth is the same."*

Crossing the **Zozi La** pass — a treacherous high-altitude traverse — Nānak and Mardānā entered **Ladakh**, the land of the high passes and the stronghold of Tibetan Buddhism. Here Nānak encountered Buddhist monks of the **Drikung Kagyu** order, who were amazed that this Indian teacher, so far from home, spoke of the One with such authority. The Janam Sākhīs record that Nānak visited a Buddhist monastery where he was shown a *thangka* (a religious scroll painting) of the Buddha. He touched his forehead to it and said:

> *"The light that illuminated the Buddha illuminates all beings. The lotus may open in different colours, but the sun that opens it is one."*

### Tibet

Some Janam Sākhī traditions — particularly the Purātan and some later versions — claim that Nānak reached **Tibet** itself, possibly as far as the sacred lake of **Mānasarovar** at the foot of Mount Kailāś, the most sacred site in both Hindu and Buddhist cosmology. Here, at the roof of the world, Nānak is said to have entered a deep state of samādhi (absorption) lasting several days, emerging with a new understanding of the universality of the divine Word — that the same Truth that had been revealed in the Vedas, the Qur'an, and the Buddhist sūtras was now being spoken in the language of the people of the Punjab.

The northern journey lasted approximately five years. Nānak returned to the Punjab around 1518 CE.

---

## The Fourth Udāsī: The Western Journey (c. 1518–1521)

The fourth and final journey was the boldest — a journey beyond the Indian subcontinent entirely, into the heart of the Islamic world: **Mecca**, **Medina**, and **Baghdad**. This was not a pilgrimage in the conventional Muslim sense — Nānak was not observing the rites of the Ḥajj in the prescribed manner — but a *confrontation* with Islam at its most sacred centres, a demonstration that the One of the Gurū was the same as the God of the Qur'an, but that the One could not be contained by any religion's forms.

### Through Sindh and Balochistan

Leaving the Punjab, Nānak and Mardānā travelled westward through the **Sindh** region (modern southern Pakistan), passing through the ancient port of **Thatta** and the city of **Multan** — a centre of both Sufi piety and Hindu pilgrimage (the temple of the Sun God, Surya). At Multan, Nānak met the Sufi pīrs of the city, who asked him:

> *"Are you a Muslim or a Hindu? Your clothes are of a householder, but your speech is of a sage."*

Nānak replied:

> *"I am neither. Nor am I a thousand other things I could name. I am a servant of the One who has no name, and who has every name. Call me what you will — the One knows what I am."*

From Sindh, they crossed into **Balochistan** through the **Bolān Pass**, entering the rugged highlands of what is now the Pakistan-Afghanistan border region. They passed through **Kandahār** (ancient Arachosia) and **Ghaznī**, the city from which Mahmūd had launched his invasions of India centuries earlier.

### Into Persia

Crossing the Iranian plateau, Nānak and Mardānā travelled through **Herāt** (in modern Afghanistan), then into **Khurāsān** (eastern Persia), passing through the cities of **Nīshāpūr** and **Rayy**. In the Persian cities, Nānak encountered a sophisticated Islamic scholarly culture — Shī'a and Sunnī, philosophers and mystics, the legacy of Avicenna and al-Ghazālī. The Janam Sākhīs describe his debates with Persian scholars:

> **The scholar:** "How can you, a man from India, teach us about God? We have the Qur'an. We have the Sunna. We have the commentaries of the great scholars."

> **Nānak:** *"The Qur'an is a book of paper and ink. The true Qur'an is written in the heart of the believer. If you have read the book but have not read your own heart, you have read nothing. The One who revealed the Qur'an is the same One who reveals the Nām — the name that is beyond all names."*

### Mecca and Medina

The climax of the fourth Udāsī — and the most famous single episode in the Janam Sākhī tradition — is Nānak's arrival in **Mecca**, the holiest city of Islam.

According to the Janam Sākhī account, Nānak and Mardānā arrived in Mecca during the Ḥajj season. The city was crowded with pilgrims from across the Muslim world, all dressed in the *ihrām* (the white pilgrim garment). Nānak, wearing his characteristic mixture of Hindu and Muslim dress — a Kashmiri shawl and a conical cap — sat down in the precinct of the Great Mosque, the Masjid al-Ḥarām, and began to sing a hymn in praise of the One.

A **Qāzī** (Islamic judge) named **Raḥmatullāh** approached him and said angrily: *"Remove your shoes! You are in the house of God!"*

Nānak looked at the Qāzī calmly and replied: *"My shoes have never been in an unclean place. But if you insist — is the house of God only this building? Is the earth outside not also God's creation? Where is the God of this mosque? Is he confined within these walls?"*

Enraged, the Qāzī grabbed Nānak's shoes. Then a miracle occurred: when the Qāzī tried to put the shoes down, the *direction* of the prayer (the qibla) shifted — the entire mosque turned so that whichever way Nānak's feet pointed, the Ka'aba was before him. The Qāzī, terrified, fell at Nānak's feet and begged forgiveness. Nānak said:

> *"God is not in the East or the West. God is not confined to the Ka'aba or the temple. God is everywhere — and God sees everything. The true mosque is the heart that has been purified by love."*

The Janam Sākhīs also record that Nānak visited **Medina**, the city of the Prophet Muḥammad, where he stood before the Prophet's tomb and recited the *Shahāda* (the Islamic declaration of faith) in his own way: *"Lā ilāha illallāh"* (There is no god but God) — a declaration that was fully orthodox in wording but radically inclusive in interpretation.

### Baghdad

From Arabia, Nānak and Mardānā travelled north to **Baghdad**, the former capital of the Abbasid Caliphate and one of the great centres of Islamic learning and Sufi mysticism. In Baghdad, Nānak is said to have established a *dharamsālā* on the banks of the Tigris — a place where the Nām was sung, where Hindus and Muslims sat together, and where the free kitchen (langar) was open to all.

The tradition preserves a conversation between Nānak and **Shaikh Bahlol Dānīyāl**, a prominent Sufi of the Qādiriyya order:

> **Shaikh Bahlol:** "Why have you come to Baghdad from the distant land of Hind?"

> **Nānak:** *"I have come to tell the people of Baghdad what I have told the people of every city: that the One is one, that love is the only path, and that all divisions — Hindu and Muslim, Arab and Persian, Turk and Indian — are the inventions of those who have not understood. I have come to call the lovers of God to the unity that already exists but is not seen."*

The Shaikh is said to have recognised Nānak as a saint and become his disciple.

### The Return

The western journey lasted approximately three years. Nānak and Mardānā returned to the Punjab around 1521 CE by a northern route — through Persia again, then through **Kabul** and the **Khyber Pass**, descending into the Peshawar valley and finally back to the plains of the Punjab.

Nānak had been away for over twenty years. He was now in his early fifties. The period of wandering was over. The next phase — the settling, the founding, the institutionalisation — was about to begin.

---

## After the Journeys: The Settlement at Kartarpur

When Nānak returned from his fourth Udāsī, he did not return to his family home in Talwaṇḍī. Instead, he settled on the west bank of the **Ravi River**, on land gifted by a wealthy follower. He called the settlement **Kartarpur** — "the City of the Creator."

Here, Nānak institutionalised the three pillars of Sikh practice:

1. **Nām Japo** — meditating on the divine Name, individually and congregationally
2. **Kirat Karo** — earning an honest living by one's own labour
3. **Vaṇḍ Chakko** — sharing with others, especially through the *langar* (free communal kitchen)

The Udāsīs became the template for the Sikh ideal of the *sant-sipāhī* (saint-soldier) — not a figure of militant conquest, but one who travels to spread truth, who confronts falsehood wherever it is found, and who establishes centres of light in the darkness. The four journeys are remembered not merely as historical events but as a spiritual geography of the soul: the four directions of the world are the four directions of the human heart, and the Nām is the one guide that can orient the traveller in all of them.

---

## The Theological Meaning of the Udāsīs

The four journeys are not simply a travel diary. They encode a profound theological statement about the nature of the Sikh path:

| Journey | Direction | Religious Ecology Encountered | Theological Lesson |
|---------|-----------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| **First** (East) | Gangetic plain → Assam, Bengal | Hindu orthodoxy, Tantra, Sahajiyā | The Nām is above all ritual |
| **Second** (South) | Rajasthan → Tamil Nadu → Sri Lanka | Bhakti, Śaiva Siddhānta, Theravāda Buddhism | The Nām is the path for all peoples |
| **Third** (North) | Kashmir → Ladakh → Tibet | Nāth Siddhas, Kashmiri Śaivism, Tibetan Buddhism | The Nām transcends all renunciation |
| **Fourth** (West) | Sindh → Persia → Arabia → Baghdad | Sunnī Islam, Shī'a Islam, Sufism | The Nām is the essence behind all revelation |

Together, the four journeys demonstrate that the Nām — the divine Name — is the *sine qua non* of spiritual life: it is prior to all scriptures, all rituals, all ascetic practices, and all religious identities. The Hindu pandits and the Muslim qāzīs, the Siddha yogīs and the Buddhist monks, the Tantric adepts and the Sufi pīrs — all had their ways of approaching the One, but all were incomplete without the direct experience of the Nām, which is the Name that the One gives to itself.

Nānak's journeys thus constitute a kind of *negative theology* of religious geography: every place he visited, every shrine he stood before, every tradition he encountered, he honoured for its partial truth — and then transcended. The Udāsīs are the map of a soul that has found the universal in the particular, the infinite in the finite, the One in the many.

---

## Legacy

The four Udāsīs became the founding narrative of the Sikh missionary imagination. In later centuries, Sikhs would remember their Gurū not as a settled teacher but as a wandering *sant* who walked the world to deliver a message. The Udāsīs inspired the **Udāsī** ascetic order — founded by Nānak's elder son Srī Chand — whose members continued the tradition of itinerant preaching across the Indian subcontinent, carrying the Nām into regions the Gurū himself had visited.

The places Nānak visited on his journeys became sites of pilgrimage: the **dharamsālās** he established in Baghdad, in Sri Lanka, and in the Himalayan foothills were centres of Sikh devotion long before the formalisation of the Sikh tradition. The encounters recorded in the Janam Sākhīs — with the Siddhas, with the Qāzī of Mecca, with the Sufi pīrs of Multan and Baghdad — became parables taught to Sikh children as the foundational examples of how the Gurū's truth confronts and transforms the world's partial truths.

And the declaration that began it all — *"There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim"* — echoes through the centuries as the most radical statement of religious universalism ever uttered by a religious founder. It is the seed from which the entire Sikh tradition grows: a tradition that affirms the Absolute as beyond all names while insisting that the Nām — the Name that God calls by — is the only path to that Absolute.

The four journeys of Gurū Nānak are not over. They continue in every Sikh who travels to spread the truth, who sits in the saṅgat regardless of caste or creed, who feeds the hungry in the langar, who repeats the Nām in the morning and the evening, and who lives by the three pillars of Nām Japo, Kirat Karo, and Vaṇḍ Chakko.

> *"As the many rivers flow into the sea and become one, so the many names of God flow into the Nām. The rivers are many. The sea is one."*
> — Gurū Nānak, Rāga Mājh (adapted)
