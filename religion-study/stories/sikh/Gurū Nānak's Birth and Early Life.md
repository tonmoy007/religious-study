# Gurū Nānak's Birth and Early Life

> *"He who was born in the house of Kalu, the Khatri, was the Gurū of the world, the true Guru. He came from the court of the Lord; he was sent into this Dark Age (Kaliyug) as the true Guru."*
> — **Purātan Janam Sākhī**, Sākhī 1

The birth of **Gurū Nānak** on 15 April 1469 CE marks the beginning of Sikhism — not merely a new religious tradition, but a radical re-imagination of what it means to be spiritual in the world. Unlike the founders of many faiths who renounced family and society, Nānak was born into the humblest of circumstances — a village accountant's son in a remote Punjabi village — and from his very first breath, the Janam Sākhīs (birth testimonies) tell us, the universe itself began signalling that something extraordinary had arrived.

The stories of Nānak's birth and early life are preserved primarily in the **Janam Sākhī** traditions — the oldest of which, the **Purātan Janam Sākhī** (c. 1635 CE), contains 57 sākhīs (episodes) spanning Nānak's entire life. These are not biographies in the modern sense but **hagiographic narratives** — theological documents in story form, designed to reveal the nature of the Gurū through the lens of his miraculous birth, his childhood signs, and his gradual awakening to his divine mission.

What follows is the narrative of that early life — from the cosmic portents preceding his birth to the three-day disappearance in the Vein River that would transform a married householder into the Gurū of the ages.

---

## The Village of Talwaṇḍī

Nānak was born in the village of **Talwaṇḍī**, a small settlement in the Punjab region of the Delhi Sultanate, about 65 kilometres southwest of Lahore in what is now the Punjab province of Pakistan. The village lay on the floodplain of the Ravi River, surrounded by fields of wheat, barley, and sugarcane — a landscape of mud-walled houses, village wells shaded by ancient banyan trees, and the steady rhythm of agrarian life.

Talwaṇḍī was a village of no particular importance — a place where Hindus and Muslims lived side by side, where the call of the *ādhān* (the Muslim call to prayer) mingled with the ringing of temple bells, where caste and religious identity shaped every interaction but daily life required pragmatic coexistence. This was the world into which Nānak was born — a world he would later challenge at its very foundations.

At the time of Nānak's birth, Talwaṇḍī was under the jurisdiction of **Daulat Khan Lodi**, a Muslim chieftain who held the surrounding lands as a *zamīndār* (landholder) under the Lodi Sultanate. Daulat Khan would later become Nānak's employer at Sultānpur — a relationship that the Janam Sākhīs treat with remarkable respect, portraying the Muslim governor as a man of discernment who recognised the Gurū's spiritual authority.

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| **Date of birth** | 15 April 1469 CE (Vaisākhī, full moon of the month of Vaisākh) |
| **Place** | Talwaṇḍī, Punjab (now Nānkaṇā Sāhib, Punjab, Pakistan) |
| **Father** | Kalu Mehta (also known as Kalu Bedī or Kalu Chand), a village accountant (*pātvarī*) |
| **Mother** | Triptā Devī (or Triptā), a devout Hindu woman |
| **Sister** | Bebe Nānakī (1464–1518), elder sister and lifelong companion |
| **Caste** | Bedī sub-caste of the Khatri clan — traditionally warrior-administrators turned accountants and traders under Muslim rule |
| **Astrological sign** | Gemini (Mithuna) according to some Janam Sākhī traditions; born under the influence of Mercury (Buddha) |

## The Family

**Kalu Mehta**, Nānak's father, was a man of modest means and considerable anxiety. As a *pātvarī* — a village accountant who maintained land records and collected revenue for the zamīndār — he occupied a position of some local importance but precarious security. The Janam Sākhīs portray him as a practical man, worried about his son's future, frustrated by Nānak's apparent indifference to worldly affairs, and constantly trying to push him toward a respectable livelihood. He is not a villain in these stories — he is a father who cannot understand why his brilliant son refuses to be normal.

**Triptā Devī**, Nānak's mother, is portrayed as a woman of deep devotion and quiet strength. She recognised her son's extraordinary nature from the beginning and supported him with the steady, unobtrusive love of a mother who trusts that her child is guided by a higher hand. Her death is not recorded in the Janam Sākhīs, but she appears in several early episodes as a figure of tenderness and faith.

**Bebe Nānakī** — Nānak's elder sister by five years — is one of the most important figures in his early life. The Janam Sākhīs say that from the moment of Nānak's birth, his sister felt an unusual bond with him. She was the first to recognise his spiritual radiance, the first to defend him against his father's complaints, and later, the one who took him into her home in Sultānpur where his divine commission would occur. She married **Jai Rām**, an official in the service of Daulat Khan Lodi at Sultānpur, and it was through this connection that Nānak would eventually find employment in the royal granary.

## The Horoscope and the Astrologers

The Janam Sākhīs record that, as was customary, a Brāhmaṇa astrologer was summoned to cast Nānak's horoscope at his birth. The most famous version of this episode comes from the **Purātan Janam Sākhī**:

> *"The astrologer, whose name was Hardyal, came and cast the child's horoscope. He said: 'This child is born under the influence of the planet Mercury (Buddha). He will be a great teacher — one who will show the path to all the world. He will be neither a Hindu nor a Muslim. He will be a devotee of the One Lord, and his name shall be spread in all directions.' When Kalu heard this, he was distressed, thinking: 'What will become of my family line? Who will carry on my work?' But the astrologer said: 'Do not grieve. This child is not born for worldly things. He is born to save the world.'"*

The astrological details vary across Janam Sākhī traditions:

| Janam Sākhī | Astrologer | Prediction |
|-------------|------------|------------|
| **Purātan** | Hardyal (Brāhmaṇa) | Born under Mercury; will be a teacher of all the world; neither Hindu nor Muslim |
| **Bālā** | Hardyal (though some versions say the astrologer was from Kashi/Banaras) | Similar prediction; adds that the child will drink the milk of divine knowledge and reject the milk of worldly illusion |
| **Miharbān** | A Jain astrologer (in some versions) | Emphasises the child's destiny to reconcile Hindus and Muslims; the horoscope shows both Vedantic and Sufi influences |
| **Ādi Janam Sākhī** | Hardyal | The child has all 32 royal marks (*lakhṣaṇas*) of a great being — he could have been a cakravartin emperor, but chose instead to be a spiritual king |

The horoscope episode establishes a pattern that runs through all Nānak narratives: the world's categories cannot contain him. He is not born to be a Khatri accountant like his father, nor a Hindu householder, nor a Muslim mystic — he is born to be a **Gurū**, and the stars themselves announce it.

## The Cobra and the Shade

The most famous of all Nānak childhood stories — and the one that appears in every Janam Sākhī tradition — is the story of the cobra:

> *"One day, when Nānak was a small child, his mother laid him down to sleep in the shade of a banyan tree while she worked in the fields. The sun moved across the sky, and soon the shade had shifted, leaving the infant exposed to the burning rays of the midday sun. A cobra — a deadly serpent — came and spread its hood over Nānak's face, shielding him from the sun like a canopy. When Triptā returned and saw the cobra standing over her child, she cried out in terror. But the cobra, hearing her, quietly slithered away without harming the child. Nānak slept on, undisturbed."*

| Element | Symbolic Meaning |
|---------|------------------|
| **Cobra** | In Indian tradition, the cobra (*nāga*) is a symbol of divine protection and spiritual power. That a cobra — the most feared creature of the Punjab plains — would protect rather than harm the infant signals that Nānak is under the protection of a power greater than nature |
| **Banyan tree** | The *barghaḍ* (banyan) is the tree of immortality and wisdom in Indian tradition, associated with the yogis and rishis who meditate beneath it. Nānak's birth beneath its shade (he was born in the courtyard of his home, but the cobra episode occurs under a banyan) links him to the ancient lineage of Indian seers |
| **Moving shade** | The shifting shade represents the passage of time and the inevitability of change — but also the providence that arranges protection even when human foresight fails |
| **Child's undisturbed sleep** | His utter peace in the presence of death is the first sign of the *sahaj* (effortless equanimity) that would define his teaching |

This story is often compared to similar accounts in other traditions — the infant Kṛṣṇa protected by the serpent Kāliya (though Kṛṣṇa *subdues* the serpent, while Nānak is *protected* by it), the infant Buddha protected by the shade of a tree, or the infant Muḥammad whose chest was cleansed by angels. The pattern is universal: the future spiritual master is marked by nature itself as one who is not subject to its ordinary laws.

## Childhood Miracles and Early Signs

The Janam Sākhīs gather around Nānak's childhood a cluster of miraculous and semi-miraculous episodes, each designed to reveal a different aspect of his nature:

### The First Words

According to the **Bālā Janam Sākhī**, when Nānak was only a few days old, his mother was nursing him when he suddenly spoke:

> *"O mother, what is this milk you give me? It is not the milk of this world. I have come from the court of the True Lord, and I drink the milk of the Nām (the Divine Name)."*

Triptā, understandably, thought she had imagined it. But the Bālā tradition insists it happened — the infant Nānak was already conscious of his divine origin.

### Refusing the Sacred Thread

When Nānak was about seven years old, his family arranged for the **janeū** ceremony — the Hindu sacred thread investiture that marks a Brāhmaṇa boy's initiation into Vedic study. The family Brāhmaṇa, **Pandit Hardyal** (the same who had cast his horoscope), prepared the sacred thread of twisted cotton. Nānak refused to wear it.

The dialogue recorded in the **Purātan Janam Sākhī** is one of the most famous in all Sikh literature:

> **Pandit Hardyal:** "This is the sacred thread, the mark of a twice-born. Without it, you are no better than a śūdra."

> **Young Nānak:** "Make me a thread of compassion for my cotton, contentment for my spinning, continence for its twisting, truth for its knot. That is the sacred thread that will not break, that will not be soiled, that will not be burnt or lost. Give me such a thread, Pandit — and I will wear it."

> *"If you have a thread of mercy, if you have a thread of contentment and self-restraint, if you have a thread of truth — then put that on me. Such a thread does not break, it does not get dirty, it does not burn, it is not lost. Blessed are those, O Nānak, who wear such a thread."*
> — Guru Granth Sāhib, Rāga Āsā, p. 471 (Nānak's own reworking of this episode)

The refusal of the sacred thread is not a rejection of Hinduism as such — it is a rejection of the idea that external symbols can confer spiritual status. The true "thread" is the inner quality of compassion, truth, and self-discipline. This episode prefigures Nānak's lifelong critique of empty ritual in both Hindu and Muslim traditions.

### The Shade of the Tree

In another commonly told episode, young Nānak was sent to graze the family's buffaloes. He sat down under a tree to meditate, and — as with the cobra episode — the tree's shade remained fixed over him throughout the day, unmoving as the sun crossed the sky. A passing traveller saw this and reported it to Kalu Mehta, who dismissed it as nonsense.

The story echoes similar tales about the Buddha (the tree that shaded him during his first meditation) and about various Hindu saints, but its function in the Nānak narrative is specific: it establishes that Nānak's meditation is not a temporary withdrawal but a state of such intense presence that nature itself responds.

### The Feeding of the Holy Men

When Nānak was a young boy, a group of wandering ascetics (*sādhus*) passed through Talwaṇḍī. Nānak's mother had prepared a meal for the family, but Nānak insisted on giving it to the holy men. His father, returning home hungry, was furious — but Nānak told him that feeding the holy was the highest form of worship. The episode establishes a pattern that would define Nānak's entire life: generosity without calculation, seeing the divine in every guest.

## Refusing School — The Teacher's Lesson

Perhaps the most psychologically rich of Nānak's childhood episodes is his encounter with formal education. When Nānak was about seven years old, his father Kalu Mehta, increasingly anxious about his son's future, sent him to the village school to learn reading, writing, and arithmetic — the skills necessary for a Khatri boy to enter the world of accountancy and trade.

The village teacher, **Pandit Gopāl Dās** (or, in some versions, simply **Maulvī** — a Muslim teacher), began by teaching Nānak the alphabet. But when the teacher started with the conventional lesson — the *alif* or the *svara* — Nānak interrupted him:

> *"Teacher, you are teaching me the letters of this world. But I want to learn the letters of the other world. The alphabet you teach — it begins with 'a' and ends with 'za' — but where is the letter that spells the Name of God? Where is the letter that does not die? Where is the letter that will save my soul?"*

According to the **Bālā Janam Sākhī**, Nānak then recited a hymn — an entire theological commentary on the alphabet — showing that each letter contained a spiritual meaning beyond its mere sound. The teacher, stunned, realised that he had nothing to teach this child. He went to Kalu Mehta and said: *"Your son is not a student. He is a teacher. Do not send him to me — send him to the Gurū who teaches the inner alphabet."*

The episode is recorded in the Guru Granth Sāhib itself, in the composition known as **Patti Likhī** (The Writing of the Alphabet), where Nānak dedicates each letter of the Gurmukhī alphabet to a spiritual truth:

| Letter | Spiritual Meaning |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Sasā** (ਸ) | *Saṅsār* (the world) is transitory — only the Name is permanent |
| **Hāhā** (ਹ) | *Harī* (the Lord) is the source of all — remember Him always |
| **Kakā** (ਕ) | *Kartā* (the Creator) made the world — do not forget the Maker |
| **Gagā** (ਗ) | *Gurū* (the Teacher) is the light — follow His guidance |
| **Chachā** (ਚ) | *Chālā* (conduct) must be pure — walk in truth |
| **Jajā** (ਜ) | *Jugat* (the way) is meditation on the Name — this is the only path |

Nānak never returned to formal schooling. He had learned the only alphabet that mattered — the alphabet of the divine Name.

## Early Signs of Spiritual Inclination

As Nānak grew into his early teens, his spiritual nature became increasingly evident. The Janam Sākhīs record several characteristic behaviours:

- **Long hours in meditation**: Nānak would leave the house before dawn, walk to the nearby forest or riverbank, and sit in meditation for hours. His family would find him motionless, his eyes half-closed, his lips moving in silent prayer. He seemed not to notice the passage of time.

- **Association with holy men**: Whenever a *sādhu*, *fakīr*, or wandering ascetic passed through Talwaṇḍī, Nānak would seek them out. He would sit at their feet, listen to their teachings, and discuss the nature of God, the soul, and liberation. He was equally at home with Hindu sannyāsīs and Muslim Sūfīs — a sign of the trans-religious vision that would define his ministry.

- **Distributing his father's goods**: One famous story recounts that Kalu Mehta gave Nānak some money to go to the market and make a profitable investment. On the way, Nānak encountered a group of hungry ascetics. Without hesitation, he spent the entire amount on food and fed them. When his father demanded an account, Nānak said: *"I made the best investment possible — I invested in the hungry. The profit will be counted in the court of the Lord."*

- **Unusual responses to questions**: When asked by villagers about his strange behaviour, Nānak would reply with riddles and paradoxical statements: *"I am not strange — you are the ones who are strange. You chase shadows and call them real. I have tasted the reality, and I cannot pretend to be satisfied with shadows."*

Kalu Mehta, by now deeply worried, consulted local holy men, doctors, and even exorcists — all of whom told him the same thing: there was nothing wrong with his son. The boy was touched by God.

## Marriage to Mata Sulakṣhaṇī

Around 1487 CE, when Nānak was about eighteen years old, his family arranged his marriage to **Sulakṣhaṇā Devī** (also known as **Chando Rānī** or, most commonly, **Mātā Sulakṣhaṇī**). She was the daughter of **Mūl Chand**, a Khatri of the *Chonā* sub-caste who lived in the village of **Batala** (in present-day Gurdaspur district, Punjab). The marriage was a conventional Khatri wedding, with all the usual ceremonies — but the Janam Sākhīs hint that even here, Nānak transformed the ritual into something spiritual.

The couple settled in Talwaṇḍī, living with Nānak's parents. The marriage produced two sons:

| Son | Birth Year | Later Significance |
|-----|-----------|-------------------|
| **Srī Chand** | 1494 CE | Became an ascetic; founded the **Udāsī** sect, which preserved and transmitted Sikh teachings but followed a celibate, renunciate path — a direct contrast to his father's householder ideal |
| **Lakhmī Dās** | 1497 CE | Lived as a householder; his descendants are known as the **Bedī** clan of the Khatris |

The Janam Sākhīs say little about Nānak's married life — characteristically, they focus on the spiritual rather than the domestic. But what they do reveal is significant: Nānak was a husband and father who did not abandon his family for spiritual pursuits. His path was not that of the renunciate but of the householder-yogī — and his marriage was part of that path, not a departure from it.

> *"I have no one — and I have everyone. I am not a householder, I am not a renunciate. I am the servant of the one Lord who has no equal."*
> — Gurū Nānak, Guru Granth Sāhib, Rāga Mājh, p. 150

Mātā Sulakṣhaṇī is honoured in Sikh tradition as the first **Mātā** (mother of the Sikh community). She endured long separations during Nānak's Udāsīs, raised their sons largely alone, and remained faithful to her husband's mission even when she could not fully comprehend it. Her quiet strength is part of the foundation on which the Sikh tradition was built.

---

## Job at Sultānpur — The Steward of the Royal Granary

By the early 1490s, Nānak's father, Kalu Mehta, had died — and the responsibility of supporting his family fell squarely on Nānak. He made some desultory attempts at farming, shopkeeping, and cattle-herding, but none of them lasted: his heart was simply not in worldly affairs.

It was **Bebe Nānakī**, his elder sister, who came to the rescue. She had married **Jai Rām**, a Khatri official who served as the *modī* (storekeeper or steward) to **Daulat Khan Lodi**, the Muslim governor of **Sultānpur Lodhī** — a town on the Vein River about 40 kilometres southeast of Talwaṇḍī. Through Jai Rām's influence, Nānak was offered a position as the steward-in-charge of the royal granary — the state storehouse that fed the governor's household and garrison.

Nānak moved to Sultānpur with his wife and children, living in the house of his sister and brother-in-law. He took up his post — and, by all accounts, discharged his duties with remarkable efficiency and honesty. But he also introduced a revolutionary innovation: he began distributing the grain to the poor, the hungry, and the destitute — anyone who came to the granary door was given a measure of grain without payment.

When the accounts were audited and the deficit was discovered, Daulat Khan Lodi summoned Nānak for an explanation. The Janam Sākhīs record the exchange:

> **Daulat Khan:** "Nānak, the accounts show a great shortage. Where has the grain gone?"

> **Nānak:** "Lord, I have stored the grain where it cannot be destroyed by fire or water, where thieves cannot steal it, where rats cannot eat it — I have stored it in the stomachs of the hungry. That grain is safer than any in your granary."

> **Daulat Khan:** "You have given away my grain to beggars?"

> **Nānak:** "Your grain? No, Khan — God's grain. He sent it, and I returned it to His children. You are only the trustee of what is already His."

Daulat Khan, according to the tradition, was not angry — he was moved. He saw that Nānak's generosity was not folly but the highest wisdom. He confirmed Nānak in his post and told him to continue his work.

Nānak lived in Sultānpur for over a decade (c. 1485–1499 CE). It was the longest settled period of his adult life — a period of domestic routine, daily work, and silent spiritual preparation. Every morning, before beginning his duties, he would bathe in the Vein River and meditate on its bank. Every evening, after work, he would sing hymns with other devotees. To the outside observer, he was a competent granary steward, a married man with two sons, a reliable employee. Inside, he was waiting — unaware that he was waiting, but the universe was not.

## The Three-Day Disappearance (1499 CE)

The axis around which all Sikh history turns is the event of **1499 CE** — Nānak's disappearance in the Vein River and his transformation from a pious householder into the Gurū of the world.

The story, recorded in all Janam Sākhī traditions, unfolds as follows:

> *"One morning, as was his custom, Nānak went to bathe in the Vein River. He took off his clothes, entered the water — and did not return. His clothes were found on the bank, neatly folded. Search parties were sent. Divers searched the riverbed. Nothing. For three days, he was gone."*

The people of Sultānpur were distraught. His wife, Mātā Sulakṣhaṇī, wept. His sister, Bebe Nānakī, fasted and prayed. Daulat Khan Lodi sent his soldiers to search the river for miles. But Nānak had vanished into another realm entirely.

### What Happened in the River

The Janam Sākhīs tell us that Nānak was taken — body and soul — into the **Darbār of Akāl Purakh** (the Timeless One's Court), the presence of God Himself. There, he was given a cup of the divine *Nām* (the immortal Name) and commanded:

> *"Nānak, go into the world. Repeat the Nām. Teach others to repeat the Nām. Abide by the truth in all things. I am with you. I have blessed you. Your name will be great. Go — and do not fear."*

The **Purātan Janam Sākhī** describes Nānak's vision of the divine court in terms that resonate with both Hindu and Muslim mystical traditions — the throne of God, the radiant light, the singing of celestial beings, the command to return to the world as a teacher. But the core of the experience is not the vision itself but the **commission**: Nānak is sent back. He is not allowed to remain in the bliss of union. His work is in the world.

### The Return

On the third day, Nānak reappeared at the same spot on the bank of the Vein River where he had entered. He emerged from the water — and was silent. For a full day, he did not speak. He looked at the world with new eyes — eyes that had seen the court of God and could never again see the world as separate from God.

When he finally spoke, his first words were:

> **"Nā kōī hindū, nā kōī musalmān."**
> *"There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim."*

This statement has been interpreted in many ways. It is not a denial of the existence of Hindus and Muslims as communities — it is a declaration that, in the presence of God, the labels that divide humanity lose their meaning. The true identity of every soul is not its religion but its relationship with the One. "Hindu" and "Muslim" are names made by humans. Before God, there is only the soul and its Creator.

From that moment, Nānak was transformed. He gave away all his possessions — his clothes, his savings, even the keys to the granary. He resigned his post. He told Daulat Khan Lodi:

> *"I can no longer serve your grain, Khan. I must serve the grain that does not perish — the Nām of the One Lord."*

He put on the simple robe of a wandering teacher. He took with him a Muslim rebāb-player named **Mardānā**, who had been his companion in Sultānpur. And he began to walk.

### The Significance of the Three Days

The three-day disappearance is the Sikh equivalent of the Buddha's enlightenment under the Bodhi tree, or Muḥammad's receiving of the first revelation in the Cave of Ḥirā. It is the moment when a pious seeker becomes a prophet. But there are crucial differences:

| Element | Nānak's Commission | Buddha's Enlightenment | Muḥammad's First Revelation |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| **Setting** | A river (water — symbol of cleansing and flow) | A tree (earth — symbol of stability and rootedness) | A cave (mountain — symbol of ascent and hiddenness) |
| **Duration** | Three days | One night (or up to seven weeks in some accounts) | One night (revelation over 23 years) |
| **Post-event silence** | One full day of silence | Seven weeks of indecision about teaching | Initial terror and doubt |
| **First words** | "There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim" | "The chain of causation has been seen" | "Recite! In the name of your Lord who created" |
| **Response** | Immediate: gave away everything and began to travel | Hesitant: persuaded by Brahmā to teach | Anxious: comforted by Khadīja and Waraqa ibn Nawfal |
| **Mode of teaching** | Songs, hymns, dialogues, journeys | Systematic discourses, monastic rules | Recited revelations, community building |
| **Social location** | Remained a householder (married, children, work) | Renounced householder life, became a monk | Remained in society as prophet-legislator |

What makes Nānak's commission unique is that he **returned to the world** — not as a renunciate who had left society behind, but as a householder-yogī who had been transformed within it. He did not abandon his wife or children, though his Udāsīs meant long separations. He did not renounce work, though he resigned his post. He entered the river as a granary steward and emerged as the Gurū — but he was still the same person, the same body, living in the same world. He had simply seen it as it truly was.

## Comparative Connections

Nānak's birth and early life invite comparison with the infancy narratives of other traditions — and reveal both striking parallels and revealing differences:

### Nānak and Kṛṣṇa

| Element | Nānak | Kṛṣṇa |
|---------|-------|-------|
| **Birth announcement** | The astrologer's prophecy; the cobra's protection | The voice from heaven; the stars at his birth |
| **Miraculous infancy** | Cobra shades him; tree shade fixed over him | Kāliya (serpent) subdued; demonic forces defeated |
| **Refusal of worldly teaching** | Refuses the sacred thread and conventional schooling | Teaches the Gītā on the battlefield (reverse pattern — Kṛṣṇa is the teacher, not the student) |
| **Childhood wisdom** | The alphabet hymn (Patti Likhī) | The eating of mud (revealing the universe in his mouth) |
| **Relation to father** | Kalu Mehta is anxious, loving, and unable to understand | Vasudeva is devoted, awestruck, and overshadowed by his son's divinity |
| **Rejection of caste/ritual** | Refuses janeū, critiques Brahminical ritual | Breaks the pot of butter (symbol of breaking social conventions); dances with the gopīs |
| **Overall tone** | Subdued, contemplative, pedagogical — the emphasis is on wisdom | Dramatic, playful, confrontational — the emphasis is on divine power |

The Nānak stories are far more restrained than the Kṛṣṇa stories. There are no demons slain, no mountains lifted, no cosmic visions. Nānak's miracles are miracles of **protection** (the cobra) and **wisdom** (the alphabet), not of power. The emphasis is on the teaching, not the miracle — and the teaching always points away from the teacher and toward the Name.

### Nānak and the Buddha

| Element | Nānak | The Buddha (as bodhisatta) |
|---------|-------|---------------------------|
| **Birth marks** | 32 royal marks (lakhṣaṇas) in some Janam Sākhīs | The 32 marks of a great being (mahāpuruṣa lakhṣaṇas) |
| **Astrologer's prediction** | Would be a teacher of the world, not a king | Would be either a cakravartin emperor or a Buddha |
| **Father's reaction** | Kalu Mehta is anxious and frustrated | King Suddhodana is protective and wants Siddhattha to be a king |
| **Meditation as a child** | Sits under a tree, shade does not move | Sits under a rose-apple tree, shade does not move (first jhāna) |
| **Marriage** | Married with two sons | Married with one son; later abandoned family |
| **Transformation** | 3-day disappearance in the Vein River | The four sights, the Great Renunciation |
| **Rejection of worldly education** | Refuses school, teaches the teacher | Masters all sciences but finds them unfulfilling |

The parallels between Nānak and the Buddha are structural — both are born into privilege (though Nānak's is modest), both are prophesied to be spiritual leaders, both are protected by nature as infants, both reject worldly education as insufficient. But the crucial difference is that Nānak does not renounce. He marries, works, raises a family, and only then — at age thirty, after a decade of domestic life — receives his commission. Nānak is the Buddha for the householder; his path is the path of *sahaj* (the natural, the effortless), not the path of renunciation.
