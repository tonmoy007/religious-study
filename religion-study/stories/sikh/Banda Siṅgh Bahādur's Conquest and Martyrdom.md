# Banda Siṅgh Bahādur's Conquest and Martyrdom

> *"Dī dehī dharam chhaṛ, hāth kare jihad; Sirhind dhaṛhā, mughal musibat chhaṛ.*"
> *"Lay down your life, abandon fear, and wage the righteous war; Sirhind shall fall, and the Mughal will be undone."*
> — Attributed to Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, instructing Banda Siṅgh Bahādur at Nāndeṛ, 1708

> *"I struck down the oppressor Wazīr Khān with my own hand in the heat of battle. The Mughals will write that I was a bandit. The Khalsa will know that I was an instrument of divine justice."*
> — Banda Siṅgh Bahādur, from the Mughal records of his interrogation, Delhi, 1716

The story of **Banda Siṅgh Bahādur** (1670–1716) is the most violent and the most controversial chapter in early Sikh history. It is not the story of a Guru — the lineage of ten human Gurus had ended with Gurū Gobind Siṅgh in 1708. It is the story of the *first Sikh sovereign*, a former ascetic who was transformed into a military commander by the dying Guru's command, who led a peasant uprising that shattered Mughal authority in the Punjab, who established the first independent Sikh state, who struck coins in the name of the Gurus, and whose savage execution in Delhi became a founding trauma — and a founding inspiration — for the Khalsa.

To the Mughals, Banda Siṅgh was a bandit and a heretic, a rebel who desecrated mosques, killed Muslims indiscriminately, and threatened the very fabric of imperial order. To the Khalsa, he was *Banda Siṅgh Bahādur* — the Brave One — the warrior who avenged the martyrdoms of Gurū Arjan and Gurū Tegh Bahādur, who levelled the city of Sirhind where the younger sons of Gurū Gobind Siṅgh had been walled up alive, and who proved that the Khalsa could not only resist the empire but defeat it.

The story of Banda Siṅgh is the bridge between the age of the Gurus and the age of the Sikh Misls — the eighteen sovereign confederacies that would ultimately bring down the Mughal Empire. It is a story of faith, fury, and the terrible price of liberation.

---

## Before Banda: The Sikh Condition in 1708

To understand what Banda Siṅgh did, one must first understand what the Khalsa had suffered.

| Event | Year | What Happened |
|-------|------|---------------|
| **Martyrdom of Gurū Arjan** | 1606 | Fifth Guru tortured to death on Jahāngīr's orders — the first Sikh martyrdom |
| **Martyrdom of Gurū Tegh Bahādur** | 1675 | Ninth Guru beheaded in Delhi for defending the Kashmiri Pandits |
| **Creation of the Khalsa** | 1699 | Gurū Gobind Siṅgh transformed the Sikhs into a baptised martial community at Vaisākhī |
| **Battle of Chamkaur** | 1705 | Gurū Gobind Siṅgh's two elder sons (Sāhibzāde Ajīt Siṅgh and Jujhār Siṅgh) killed in battle; his two younger sons (Sāhibzāde Zorāwar Siṅgh and Fateh Siṅgh) walled up alive at Sirhind on the orders of Wazīr Khān |
| **Death of Gurū Gobind Siṅgh** | 1708 | Tenth Guru assassinated at Nāndeṛ by a Pathan — the lineage of human Gurus ends |

The wounds were raw. The execution of the younger Sāhibzādes at Sirhind — **Zorāwar Siṅgh (age 9)** and **Fateh Siṅgh (age 7)** who refused to convert to Islam and were bricked into a wall alive — was the atrocity that would not heal. The Governor of Sirhind, **Wazīr Khān**, had ordered the killing. The Sikhs had not forgotten.

Meanwhile, the Mughal Empire was in transition: the great persecutor Aurangzeb had died in 1707, and a war of succession had erupted between his sons. **Bahādur Shāh** emerged victorious in 1708 — but his authority was still fragile. The Punjab, with its restive peasantry of Jat and Sikh farmers crushed by Mughal taxation, was ready to explode.

It was into this powder keg that Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, on his deathbed, would light the fuse.

---

## The Meeting at Nāndeṛ (1708)

### The Ascetic from the Deccan

Before he was Banda Siṅgh Bahādur, he was **Lachman Dev** (or **Madho Dās**), born in 1670 to a **Rājpūt** family in the **Rājori** region of the Jammu hills (modern-day Jammu and Kashmir). He was a devout Hindu by upbringing, and in his adolescence he became a **Bairāgī** ascetic — a celibate wandering renunciant of the Rāmānandī order, one of thousands who wandered the forests and pilgrimage sites of northern India.

For nearly two decades, he lived as Madho Dās, the ascetic — a life of meditation, fasting, and begging. He had no interest in politics, no knowledge of warfare, no ambition beyond spiritual liberation. He settled at the **Godāvarī River** near **Panḍharpur** in the Deccan, where he presided over a small ashram.

Then a visitor arrived who would change everything.

### The Guru's Commission

In 1708, Gurū Gobind Siṅgh — the tenth Guru, now in his final year — was travelling through the Deccan after his confrontations with Aurangzeb and the Mughal forces. He met Madho Dās at the banks of the Godāvarī.

The encounter is described in Sikh tradition as a moment of recognition — the Guru saw in the ascetic a warrior waiting to be awakened:

> *"I saw a spark in the Bairāgī's eyes. He had the stillness of a sage and the fire of a lion. I knew that the Khalsa needed such a man — one who feared nothing because he had already renounced everything."*
> — Attributed to Gurū Gobind Siṅgh, *Sūraj Prakāś* (adapted)

The Guru asked Madho Dās directly: **Will you take up the sword for the Khalsa? Will you lead the Sikhs against the oppressor?**

Madho Dās hesitated. He was a renunciant — he had taken vows of non-violence, chastity, and poverty. The Guru's request seemed to contradict everything he had lived for.

But Gurū Gobind Siṅgh pressed him:

> *"There is a time for meditation and a time for action. The meditation of forty years has purified you for this moment. The sword you will wield is not the sword of anger — it is the sword of justice. Wazīr Khān must answer for the blood of the Sāhibzādes. Sirhind must fall. Will you be the instrument?"*

Madho Dās bowed his head. He accepted.

### The Baptism and the New Name

Gurū Gobind Siṅgh administered the **Khaṇḍe dī Pāhul** (the Sikh baptism of the double-edged sword) to the former ascetic. He gave him the name **Banda Siṅgh** — "the Lion-Slave" (a compound of *Banda*, meaning slave/servant, and *Siṅgh*, meaning lion — the title granted to all baptised Sikhs).

He was given five arrows from the Guru's own quiver, one of his own swords, a **nishān ṣāhib** (the Khalsa banner), and a **dastār** (turban) from the Guru's own head. Most importantly, he was given a **hukamnāmā** — a written edict — addressed to the entire Sikh community, commanding them to accept Banda Siṅgh as their military leader and to provide him with men, money, and supplies.

> *"Let every Sikh who can bear arms join Banda Siṅgh. Let every Sikh who cannot bear arms support him with wealth and prayers. He is my hand. He is my sword. The oppressor will be punished. The Khalsa will be avenged."*
> — Gurū Gobind Siṅgh's hukamnāmā, 1708

The Guru's instructions were precise: **spare the weak, the unarmed, and the innocent; kill only the oppressors; and establish justice in the land**. He also commanded Banda Siṅgh to **shift his capital to the Punjab and establish a Sikh government there**.

Banda Siṅgh kissed the Guru's feet and left. He would never see his master again — Gurū Gobind Siṅgh was assassinated by a Pathan in Nāndeṛ on 7 October 1708, just months after their meeting. But the Guru's command was now Banda Siṅgh's destiny.

## The Road to the Punjab (1708–1709)

Banda Siṅgh rode north from Nāndeṛ with **only four other Sikhs**. From this handful would grow an army of thousands.

### The Journey

The journey from the Deccan to the Punjab took nearly a year. Banda Siṅgh travelled through the forests and villages of central India, gathering support as he went. The **hukamnāmā** from the Guru preceded him: wherever he stopped, Sikhs came out to meet him, bringing offerings, joining his retinue, and spreading word of his mission.

His route took him through:
- **Burhānpur** — where the local Sikh sangat (congregation) presented him with a drum and a banner
- **Kālpi** — where Jat and Sikh peasants flocked to his banner, weary of Mughal tax collectors
- **Āgra** — where he passed within sight of the Mughal capital but did not tarry
- **Mathurā** — where the local community gave him substantial funds

By the time Banda Siṅgh crossed into the Punjab in the autumn of 1709, he commanded a force of several thousand men — peasants, former soldiers, Jat farmers, and Sikhs from all walks of life. They were poorly armed, untrained, and driven by religious fervour rather than military discipline — but they were loyal to Banda Siṅgh, and they were hungry for justice.

### The Arrival

Banda Siṅgh established his first camp in the **Khatkar Kalan** region of the **Doaba** (the land between the Sutlej and Beas rivers). The local Sikh chiefs — many of whom had known Gurū Gobind Siṅgh personally — came to pay homage. The Sikh population, which had been leaderless and demoralised since the Guru's death, felt a surge of hope.

Banda Siṅgh organised his followers into a rudimentary army. He appointed officers. He established a treasury. He sent messengers to every Sikh community in the Punjab, summoning them to join the rising.

The first target was not Sirhind — the great prize — but the smaller Mughal strongholds that ringed the region. Banda Siṅgh knew that he must build momentum before taking the city that had killed the Guru's children.

---

## The First Victories: Saṃānā and the Surrender of the Doaba

Banda Siṅgh's first major action was against the town of **Saṃānā** in the Patiala region (November 1709). Saṃānā was chosen for a specific reason: it was the hometown of **Jalāluddīn of Jalandhar**, the executioner who had beheaded Gurū Tegh Bahādur in 1675. The town was also a Mughal administrative centre and a symbol of imperial authority.

The attack was swift and brutal. Banda Siṅgh's forces overwhelmed the Mughal garrison, executed the local officials, and razed the town's fortifications. The executioner's family was killed in the assault — an act that the Sikh chronicles record without apology as *avenging the Guru's blood*.

The news of Saṃānā spread across the Punjab like wildfire. **Villages that had been ground down by Mughal tax collectors rose up spontaneously.** Peasants armed with scythes and axes drove out imperial officials. Jat chiefs who had hedged their loyalties now declared for Banda Siṅgh.

### The Surrender of the Doaba

Following Saṃānā, Banda Siṅgh marched into the **Doaba** — the rich agricultural heartland between the Sutlej and Beas. The Mughal faujdār (military governor) of the region, **Khān Bahādur** (a grandson of the Mughal general who had fought the Sikhs at Chamkaur), initially attempted to negotiate. When negotiations failed, he tried to resist — but his troops, hearing of Banda Siṅgh's victories and the fervour of his followers, melted away.

Within weeks, the Doaba was under Banda Siṅgh's control. The **entire territory from the Sutlej to the Ravi** — an area of several thousand square miles — had fallen to the Khalsa without a major battle.

The Mughal court in Delhi, preoccupied with the ongoing succession crisis, did not yet realise the magnitude of the threat. Emperor Bahādur Shāh had left the Punjab for the Deccan, where he was still consolidating power. The imperial forces in the region were fragmented, demoralised, and leaderless.

Banda Siṅgh, meanwhile, was preparing for the main event.

## The Battle of Chappar Chiri (May 1710) and the Sack of Sirhind

### The Siege of Sirhind

By the spring of 1710, Banda Siṅgh's army had swelled to **over 20,000 men** — Sikhs, Jats, Gujjars, and Rājpūts united by hatred of Mughal rule. He marched on **Sirhind**, the capital of the Sirhind province (modern-day Fatehgarh Sāhib) — the city where the Sāhibzādes had been martyred, where Wazīr Khān ruled, and where the Mughals had their strongest garrison in the eastern Punjab.

Wazīr Khān was not caught unprepared. He had fortified Sirhind, brought in reinforcements from Delhi, and sent urgent messages to Emperor Bahādur Shāh begging for assistance. The Emperor, however, was still in the Deccan and sent only promises.

Banda Siṅgh's vanguard reached Sirhind in March 1710. He did not immediately assault the city — he first sent a letter to Wazīr Khān, offering terms:

> *"You killed the innocent children of my Guru. You built a wall around them and watched them die. Now I have come, not for revenge, but for justice. Surrender the city. Pay compensation to the families of the martyrs. Accept the sovereignty of the Khalsa. I will spare your life."*

Wazīr Khān responded with contempt. He tore up the letter, executed the messengers, and prepared for battle. He is said to have declared: *"I will send Banda Siṅgh's head to Delhi in a cage."*

The siege began.

### The Battle of Chappar Chiri

The decisive engagement took place on **12 May 1710** at **Chappar Chiri**, a village about 5 kilometres from Sirhind. The Mughal army, commanded by Wazīr Khān and his deputies, marched out of Sirhind to meet Banda Siṅgh in open battle.

The two armies were comparable in size — roughly 20,000–25,000 men each. But the Mughals had the advantage in artillery, cavalry, and professional training. Banda Siṅgh's army was largely peasant infantry, inspired by faith but untrained in formal warfare.

The battle began with a Mughal cannonade. The Khalsa lines wavered but did not break. Banda Siṅgh, riding at the centre of his army, raised the Nishān Ṣāhib and shouted the Sikh war cry: **"Bole So Nihāl! Sat Srī Akāl!"** (Shout in ecstasy! Truth is Eternal!)

What happened next is recorded in both Sikh and Mughal sources. The Khalsa charged — not in disciplined formations, but with the reckless fury of men who believed that death in battle was martyrdom and victory was divine will. They overwhelmed the Mughal cannon positions. They cut through the Mughal cavalry. The battle turned into a rout.

Banda Siṅgh himself engaged Wazīr Khān in single combat. Accounts differ on the precise details, but all agree on the outcome: **Wazīr Khān was struck down and killed by Banda Siṅgh's own hand.**

| **Strength** | ~20,000–25,000 men, artillery, cavalry | ~20,000–25,000 men, mostly infantry |
| **Cavalry** | 10,000+ professional troopers | ~3,000 mounted Sikhs |

### The Sack of Sirhind

With Wazīr Khān dead and the imperial army scattered, Banda Siṅgh entered Sirhind. The city was given over to plunder — but not indiscriminate slaughter. Sikh chronicles and Mughal records agree that **the general population was largely spared**; the violence was directed at Mughal officials, troops, and the supporters of Wazīr Khān.

The fortifications of Sirhind were **razed to the ground**. The city's mosques and public buildings — symbols of Mughal authority — were destroyed. The treasures of the governor's palace were taken for the Khalsa treasury. And justice was meted out to those responsible for the Sāhibzādes' deaths:

- **Sucha Nand** (the Brāhmaṇa minister of Wazīr Khān who had urged the execution of the children) was captured and executed
- The executioners who had carried out the walling-up were put to death
- The Mughal officials who had participated in the persecution of Sikhs were tried by a Khalsa court and sentenced

Banda Siṅgh ordered the construction of a memorial at the site where the Sāhibzādes had been martyred — the **Fatehgarh Sāhib** (Fort of Victory) Gurdwara that still stands today.

> *"Sirhind was levelled on his command. The stones of the governor's palace were thrown into the river. The city that had been the seat of tyranny in the Punjab became a heap of dust. And the Sikhs wept — not for the destruction, but for the memory of the children who had died there."*
> — *Ratan Siṅgh Bhaṅgū*, *Prācīn Panth Prakāś* (adapted)

The fall of Sirhind sent shockwaves through the Mughal Empire. A provincial capital had been captured. An imperial governor had been killed in battle. A Sikh state had been established on Mughal territory. The empire, which had seemed unshakeable, had been humbled by a former ascetic and his army of peasants.

## The First Sikh State: Lohgarh (1710–1712)

### Establishing the Capital

After the victory at Sirhind, Banda Siṅgh established his capital at **Lohgarh** (the "Iron Fort") in the hills of the Siwalik range, near modern-day **Kharar** in Punjab. The location was chosen for its strategic defensibility: perched on a hilltop, surrounded by forests and ravines, it could be defended by a small garrison against a much larger army.

From Lohgarh, Banda Siṅgh administered the first **sovereign Sikh state** in history. His authority stretched from the **Jhelum River** in the west to the **Yamuna River** in the east — roughly the territory of the modern Indian state of Punjab plus parts of Haryana.

### Administrative Reforms

Banda Siṅgh's administration was guided by the principles he had received from Gurū Gobind Siṅgh:

**Land Reforms**

The most radical change was in land ownership. Banda Siṅgh issued a decree that **all land belonged to the Khalsa** — that is, to the community, not to individual landlords or the Mughal state:

> *"The land that the Mughals took by force, the Khalsa has taken back by force. This land belongs to the Guru's people. No zamīndār, no jāgīrdār, no revenue farmer shall take a single coin from the peasant. The Khalsa is the sovereign. The peasant is the master."*

This policy was revolutionary. It abolished the Mughal land-revenue system at a stroke. Peasants who had been crushed by taxes for generations were suddenly free. They paid a single tithe (the *dasvandh*, one-tenth of their produce) to the Khalsa treasury in return for protection.

**Coinage**

Banda Siṅgh struck coins in the name of the Gurus — a direct assertion of sovereignty that the Mughals regarded as high treason. The coins bore the inscription:

> *"Sikkah zad bar har do 'ālam, tēgh-i Murshid-i 'ālam; Fateh-i Gobind Siṅgh Shāh, ayat-i Nānak Wāhegurū."*
> *"Struck coin in the two worlds, the sword of the Master of the world; Victory of Gobind Siṅgh, by the grace of Nānak, Wāhegurū."*

This was more than currency — it was a declaration. **Only a sovereign strikes coins.** By issuing his own coinage, Banda Siṅgh announced to the Mughal Empire and to the world that the Khalsa was no longer a subject community: it was an independent nation.

**Judicial System**

Banda Siṅgh established Khalsa courts based on Sikh principles. **Murder, theft, and oppression** were punished severely. **Corruption** among officials was treated as treason. The courts were held in the open, under a tree or in the Gurdwara, and decisions were rendered publicly.

| Institution | Mughal System | Banda Siṅgh's Khalsa System |
|-------------|---------------|------------------------------|
| **Land ownership** | Zamīndārī; landlords collect revenue for the state | All land to the Khalsa; direct peasant tax of 10% |
| **Taxation** | Heavy: land tax (kharāj), jizya, pilgrimage tax, plus local levies | Single tithe (dasvandh) plus voluntary offerings |
| **Coinage** | Imperial Mughal coins | Khalsa coins in the name of Gurū Nānak — *Guru Gobind Siṅgh* |
| **Judiciary** | Mughal Qazīs applying sharia | Khalsa courts applying Sikh principles |
| **Capital** | Delhi / Lahore | Lohgarh (the Iron Fort) |
| **Sovereignty** | Mughal Badshāh | Khalsa (the Guru-Panth) |

### The Response of the Rural Population

The peasantry — Jats, Gujjars, Rajputs, and even Muslims — responded with overwhelming enthusiasm. The abolition of Mughal taxes was a liberation. For the first time in living memory, a farmer could keep what he grew. Banda Siṅgh's army swelled further as peasants flocked to his banner.

But the revolution also produced excesses. Some of Banda Siṅgh's followers — particularly the more radical elements of the Jat peasantry — engaged in attacks on Muslim villages and mosques. While Banda Siṅgh himself attempted to restrain these excesses and issued orders protecting civilians regardless of faith, the violence was not always under his control. Reports reached Delhi of forced conversions and desecration of Muslim holy sites — claims that the Mughal chroniclers amplified and the Sikh chronicles minimise. The truth lies somewhere between, but the effect was the same: **the Mughal court now had a casus belli that could unite the empire.**

## The Mughal Counter-Campaign (1710–1712)

### The Emperor's Response

When news of Sirhind's fall reached Emperor **Bahādur Shāh** in the Deccan, he understood immediately that the Sikh rebellion was not a local disturbance — it was an existential threat to Mughal authority in the Punjab. If the Khalsa state consolidated, other regions might follow. The empire could not afford to appear weak.

The Emperor personally marched north with a massive army — contemporary Mughal sources place its strength at **over 60,000 men** — including the elite imperial artillery, the finest cavalry in Asia, and veteran commanders hardened by decades of warfare. By October 1710, Bahādur Shāh had reached the Punjab and established his camp at **Lahore**.

### The Fall of Lohgarh (December 1710)

The Mughal strategy was simple: concentrate overwhelming force against Banda Siṅgh's capital at Lohgarh. The Mughal army approached in three columns, converging on the fort simultaneously.

Banda Siṅgh knew he could not hold the fort against such odds. He evacuated the women and children, dispersed his army into the surrounding villages, and prepared for a guerrilla war. When the Mughals finally stormed Lohgarh in December 1710, they found the fort nearly empty. Banda Siṅgh had escaped into the forests of the Siwaliks.

|-------|------|-------------|
| Fall of Lohgarh | Dec 1710 | Mughals capture the Khalsa capital; Banda Siṅgh escapes into the hills |

### The Guerrilla Phase

For the next four years (1711–1715), Banda Siṅgh waged a guerrilla war against the Mughals. He moved constantly, striking at supply columns, tax convoys, and isolated garrisons. He found refuge in the hill kingdoms of the Siwaliks, whose Rājpūt rulers were suspicious of Mughal power and willing to shelter the Sikh leader.

The Mughals, for their part, responded with savage reprisals against the Sikh civilian population. Villages suspected of sheltering Banda Siṅgh were burned. Sikhs captured in the field were executed on the spot. The Emperor offered a reward of **one hundred gold mohurs** for Banda Siṅgh's head — dead or alive.

But Banda Siṅgh continued to elude capture. His network of spies and supporters was extensive. The peasantry, grateful for the land reforms he had enacted, protected him with their silence.

### The Changing of the Emperor

In February 1712, Emperor Bahādur Shāh died. His death triggered a renewed succession struggle among his sons, and Mughal attention was temporarily diverted from the Punjab. Banda Siṅgh used the respite to rebuild his forces. By 1713, he had re-emerged from the hills and re-established a base of operations in the **Jalandhar Doaba**.

But the new Emperor — **Jahāndār Shāh** (r. 1712–1713) was weak, and his successor **Farrukhsiyar** (r. 1713–1719) was determined to crush the Sikh rebellion once and for all. He appointed **Abdus Samad Khān**, a veteran Mughal commander, as Governor of the Punjab with orders to hunt down Banda Siṅgh.

---

## The Siege of Gurdās Nangaḷ (1715–1716)

### The Fort in the Lake

Banda Siṅgh's last stand took place at **Gurdās Nangaḷ**, a small village in the **Gurdaspur** district (near the modern border between India and Pakistan). The site was a natural fortress: a hill surrounded by a swampy lake, accessible only by a narrow causeway.

Here, Banda Siṅgh built a crude fort of mud and brick. His army, reduced by years of guerrilla warfare, numbered **perhaps 2,000–3,000 men** — including women and children who had followed him into the fort for protection.

The Mughal army, commanded by **Abdus Samad Khān** and his deputy **Zakariyā Khān** (the son of the late Wazīr Khān — note the personal dimension of the vengeance), arrived in the spring of 1715. They surrounded the fort and began a siege that would last **eight months**.

### The Conditions

Life inside the fort was catastrophic. Food ran out within weeks. The defenders ate grass, bark, and the leather of their shoes. The lake water was contaminated — disease spread rapidly. Women and children died of starvation and illness at appalling rates.

The Mughals, aware that the fort could not be taken by storm without heavy casualties, chose to starve the defenders into submission. They blocked the causeway, prevented any supplies from reaching the fort, and shelled the defences with artillery daily.

> *"The Khalsa inside Gurdās Nangaḷ became skeletons. Their eyes were hollow. Their bones pushed against their skin. They had no food, no water, no medicine, no hope — except the hope of dying with their faces toward the enemy."*
> — *Śāhīd Bilās* (adapted)

Banda Siṅgh refused to surrender. He told his followers:

> *"The Guru did not send me to win. He sent me to fight. Winning is in the hands of Wāhegurū. Fighting is in ours. We will fight until we cannot fight anymore."*

### The Breach and the Capture

After eight months of siege, the Mughals finally breached the fort's walls in December 1715. The final assault was savage. Of the 2,000–3,000 defenders, **most were killed in the fighting**. Only Banda Siṅgh and **about 200–300 survivors** — including his infant son, his wife, and several of his senior commanders — were taken alive.

The Mughals chained their captives and marched them toward Delhi — a procession of emaciated, wounded prisoners that took nearly two months to reach the capital. Along the way, Sikh villagers who tried to help them were executed. Banda Siṅgh was displayed in cages at every major town — a warning to anyone who might think of rebellion.

---

## The Trial and Execution in Delhi (1716)

### The Arrival

The prisoners arrived in Delhi in **February 1716**. The city had been prepared for a spectacle. Thousands lined the streets as Banda Siṅgh, chained and seated on a camel (a deliberate reversal — the camel was the Mughal mode of transporting criminals), was paraded through the city.

The Emperor **Farrukhsiyar** was eager to make an example of the rebel. He ordered a public trial in the Diwān-i-ʿĀm (the Hall of Public Audience) of the Red Fort — the same hall where Emperor Shāh Jahān had once received ambassadors from the world.

### The Interrogation

The Mughal chronicler **Muhammad Qāsim Lāhorī** records the interrogation in detail. Banda Siṅgh was asked:

**Mughal Qāzī:** *"Why did you rebel against the Emperor? Why did you kill Muslims? Why did you destroy mosques?"*

**Banda Siṅgh:** *"I did not rebel against the Emperor — I rebelled against tyranny. Your emperor killed my Guru. Your governor killed innocent children. Your officials crushed the peasantry under taxes and then crushed them again under the jizya. I did what any man of conscience would do: I fought back."*

**Qāzī:** *"Do you repent? If you convert to Islam, the Emperor may spare your life. You are a brave man — the Emperor has use for brave men."*

**Banda Siṅgh:** *"I was a Bairāgī ascetic for forty years. I gave up the world. Then my Guru gave me a sword and told me to fight for justice. I have done what he asked. I do not repent. I will not convert. I am ready to die."*

The Emperor, watching from behind a screen, was reportedly impressed by Banda Siṅgh's composure — but not moved to mercy.

### The Execution

The execution was scheduled for **9 June 1716** (some sources give **13 June**). It was designed to be as cruel as possible — a spectacle of imperial vengeance that would terrorise any potential rebels into submission.

The sentence dictated that Banda Siṅgh should be killed slowly, by degrees:

| Stage | Punishment | Detail |
|-------|------------|--------|
| 1 | **Public parade** | Banda Siṅgh was paraded through Delhi on a camel, his face blackened, pelted with stones by the crowd |
| 2 | **Witnessing the deaths of his followers** | His infant son was **torn from his arms and killed first** — the child's heart was brought on a platter and thrust into Banda Siṅgh's mouth, then the head was thrown in his face |
| 3 | **Witnessing the executions of his commanders** | His senior officers were beheaded one by one in front of him — each head placed at his feet |
| 4 | **Execution by dismemberment** | Banda Siṅgh was told he would be cut apart joint by joint. The executioner asked: *"Which part shall I cut first?"* Banda Siṅgh replied: *"The hand that struck Wazīr Khān."* |
| 5 | **Death** | His right arm was severed at the elbow, then his left arm, then his legs. The shin-bone was crushed. He did not cry out. He recited Japjī Sāhib until he could no longer speak. |

According to Sikh tradition, Banda Siṅgh's last words were the **Mūl Mantar** (the foundational Sikh prayer) — *"Ik Oaṅkār, Sat Nām, Kartā Purakh, Nirbhao, Nirvair, Akāl Mūrat, Ajūnī, Saibhaṅg, Gurprasād."* — "One God, Truth is His Name, the Creator, Fearless, Without Enmity, the Timeless Being, Unborn, Self-Existent, by Guru's Grace."

The executioners, unable to break his composure, became enraged and mutilated his body further. They cut open his chest and pulled out his heart. Finally, they cut off his head.

> *"He died not as a bandit but as a saint. Not as a rebel but as a martyr. His body was broken piece by piece, but his spirit never bent. The Mughals had conquered his flesh. They had not conquered his faith."*
> — Sikh tradition

### The Aftermath

The Mughals hung Banda Siṅgh's remains from the gates of Delhi — the head on one gate, the body on another — as a warning. But the effect was not what they intended. The martyrdom of Banda Siṅgh had the same effect as the martyrdom of Gurū Arjan and Gurū Tegh Bahādur before him: **it hardened Sikh resolve.**

The surviving prisoners — about 100 men — were offered the choice: convert to Islam or die. All chose death. They were executed by decapitation outside the Turkman Gate in Delhi. The Mughal authorities confiscated all Sikh property in the capital and banned Sikhs from entering Delhi.

But the Khalsa did not disappear. The Sikhs retreated to the hills and forests, reorganized, and within a generation had established the **eighteen sovereign Misls** (confederacies) that would eventually conquer Lahore in 1765 and rule the Punjab until the arrival of the British. Banda Siṅgh's execution had not ended the Sikh struggle — it had sanctified it.

## Banda Siṅgh in Sikh Memory

### A Contested Legacy

Banda Siṅgh's legacy is complex. Within the Sikh tradition, he is honoured as a **martyr and liberator** — the one who avenged the Sāhibzādes and established the first Sikh state. But there has also been ambivalence:

- **The Issue of Violence**: Some Sikh scholars have questioned whether the destruction of Sirhind and the attacks on Muslim civilians were consistent with Guru Nānak's teachings. Banda Siṅgh's defenders argue that he was fighting a defensive war against a genocidal empire.
- **The Issue of Authority**: Banda Siṅgh was a military leader, not a Guru. After his capture, some Sikhs questioned whether his authority had been legitimate or whether the hukamnāmā from Gurū Gobind Siṅgh had been genuine (though the consensus of Sikh historical scholarship affirms its authenticity).
- **The Issue of Succession**: Banda Siṅgh's death left the Khalsa without a clear leader. The subsequent fragmentation into the Misls was partly a consequence of his loss.

### The Standard View

Despite the complexities, the standard Sikh view of Banda Siṅgh is overwhelmingly positive:

| Aspect | Sikh Tradition | Mughal Sources |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| **Identity** | A divinely commissioned liberator | A rebel and heretic (mufsid) |
| **Motivation** | Justice and vengeance for the martyred Sāhibzādes | Ambition and plunder |
| **Victories** | Miraculous — the Guru's blessing | Temporary — the result of imperial weakness |
| **Governance** | Just and righteous — the first Sikh state | Tyrannical and lawless — a reign of terror |
| **Death** | Martyrdom — the supreme sacrifice | Execution — the punishment of a criminal |
| **Legacy** | The founder of Sikh sovereignty | A warning against rebellion |

### Banda Siṅgh Bahādur in the Sikh Calendar

Banda Siṅgh's martyrdom is commemorated in the Sikh calendar on **9 June** (the date of his execution). The Gurdwara of **Banda Siṅgh Bahādur** in Delhi marks the site of his martyrdom. The Gurdwara of **Fatehgarh Sāhib**, built on the spot where the Sāhibzādes were walled up, stands as a permanent monument to the events that Banda Siṅgh avenged.

## The Meaning of the Story

The story of Banda Siṅgh Bahādur is the story of the **Khalsa coming of age** — a transition from a persecuted religious community to a sovereign military power. It is a story that raises profound questions about violence, justice, and the ethics of liberation.

### The Ascetic as Warrior

The transformation of Madho Dās (the Bairāgī ascetic) into Banda Siṅgh Bahādur (the Khalsa commander) is the single most striking narrative arc in Sikh history after the Gurus themselves. It mirrors, in a different key, the transformation of the Sikh community from a quietist following of Gurū Nānak's hymns into the armed Khalsa of Gurū Gobind Siṅgh.

Banda Siṅgh proved that **renunciation and violence were not opposites** in the Sikh worldview. The man who could renounce the world most completely — who feared nothing because he owned nothing — was also the man who could wield the sword most effectively. The Bairāgī's detachment became the warrior's fearlessness.

### The Avenger of the Innocent

For the Sikh community, Banda Siṅgh's defining act was the capture and destruction of Sirhind — the city where the nine-year-old Zorāwar Siṅgh and seven-year-old Fateh Siṅgh had been martyred. In the Sikh imagination, Banda Siṅgh is the **avenger of the innocent**, the one who proved that the blood of children does not go unpunished.

> *"The Mughals built a wall around the Guru's sons and watched them die. Banda Siṅgh tore down that wall and razed the city to the ground. This is the justice of the Khalsa: measure for measure, blow for blow, but only when the innocent have been wronged."*
> — Kavi Santokh Siṅgh, *Sūraj Prakāś* (adapted)

### The Beginning of Sikh Sovereignty

Banda Siṅgh's state at Lohgarh lasted only two years. His coinage circulated for less than a decade. But the idea that the Khalsa could rule — that the Sikh community did not need the permission of any emperor to govern its own affairs — never died. The Misls that followed, the **Sikh Empire of Mahārājā Raṇjīt Siṅgh** (1799–1849), and the modern Indian state of Punjab all trace their political ancestry, in some measure, to the moment when Banda Siṅgh struck a coin in the name of Gurū Nānak and declared: *"The land belongs to the Khalsa."*

### The Martyrdom That Sanctified the Sword

Finally, Banda Siṅgh's execution is the **fourth great martyrdom** of the Sikh tradition — following Gurū Arjan (1606), Gurū Tegh Bahādur (1675), and the Sāhibzādes (1705). Each martyrdom deepened the Sikh community's understanding of sacrifice:

- **Gurū Arjan** taught that the body could be destroyed but the spirit could not.
- **Gurū Tegh Bahādur** taught that one could die for another's faith.
- **The Sāhibzādes** taught that even children could face death without flinching.
- **Banda Siṅgh Bahādur** taught that the warrior who fights for justice must be ready to die in the same way — joint by joint, piece by piece, without a cry.

> *"The sword that Banda Siṅgh wielded was sanctified not by the blood of his enemies but by his own blood. The Khalsa learned from him that the true warrior is not the one who kills without fear — it is the one who dies without fear."*
> — Professor Puran Siṅgh, *The Story of Banda Siṅgh Bahādur* (adapted)
