# Paul's Conversion

> The most famous conversion in Christian history — a persecutor of Christians becomes the greatest missionary of the faith, all because of a blinding light on the road to Damascus.

---

## The Story (Acts 9, 22, 26)

### The Persecutor

**Saul of Tarsus** — a zealous Pharisee — approves of Stephen's stoning (the first Christian martyr). He **breathes threats and murder** against the disciples — entering house after house, dragging off believers, men and women, to prison. He obtains letters from the high priest to arrest Christians in Damascus.

### The Encounter

On the road to Damascus, a **light from heaven** flashes around him. He falls to the ground. A voice: *"Saul, Saul — why are you persecuting me?"*

> *"Who are you, Lord?"*  
> *"I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting. Rise, enter the city — and you will be told what to do."*

Saul is **blind** — led by the hand into Damascus. For three days, he neither eats nor drinks.

### The Healing

**Ananias** — a disciple in Damascus — is told in a vision: *"Go to the house of Judas — ask for Saul of Tarsus. He is praying. He is a chosen instrument of mine to carry my name before the Gentiles and kings and the children of Israel."*

Ananias hesitates — he knows Saul's reputation. But he goes. He lays hands on Saul: scales fall from his eyes. Saul is baptized, eats, and regains his strength.

### The Mission

Saul immediately begins preaching in the synagogues: *"Jesus is the Son of God!"* The Jews plot to kill him. The disciples lower him in a basket over the wall of Damascus at night.

He becomes **Paul** — the Apostle to the Gentiles — planting churches across Asia Minor, Greece, and Rome. He writes 13 epistles (letters) that form a third of the New Testament.

## Theological Significance

| Theme | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Grace alone** | Paul did not seek Christ — Christ interrupted him |
| **The worst sinner saved** | "I am the foremost of sinners" — Paul's conversion as proof of limitless grace |
| **Persecutor becomes preacher** | The enemy of the church becomes its greatest advocate |
| **Universalism of the gospel** | Paul's mission to the Gentiles — the faith is for all nations |

---

## Connection with Islamic Tradition

There is **no parallel** to Paul in Islam. Paul is entirely absent from the Qur'an and Hadith. This is significant because:

- Paul is the architect of **distinctively Christian doctrines** that Islam rejects — especially the divinity of Christ, substitutionary atonement, and salvation by faith alone
- Islamic tradition holds that the original message of Jesus (*Injīl*) was **corrupted** (*taḥrīf*) — Paul is often seen by Muslims as a key figure in this corruption
- The Qur'an never mentions Paul — the earliest Muslim sources refer to him as a falsifier of Jesus' message
