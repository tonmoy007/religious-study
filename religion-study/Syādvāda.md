# Syādvāda (The Doctrine of Conditional Predication)

Syādvāda (skt. *syād* = "maybe" / "in some respect" + *vāda* = "doctrine") is the Jain theory of conditional predication — the logical corollary of [[Anekāntavāda]] (the doctrine of many-sidedness). Where anekāntavāda asserts that reality has infinite facets, syādvāda supplies the linguistic and logical apparatus for making valid statements about those facets without falling into contradiction.

## The Saptabhaṅgī (Sevenfold Truth)

Syādvāda's best-known formulation is the *saptabhaṅgī* — seven modes of predication, each prefaced with the qualifier **syāt** ("in some respect" or "from a certain standpoint"):

| # | Sanskrit Formula | English Gloss |
|---|------------------|---------------|
| 1 | syāt asti | Maybe it is |
| 2 | syāt nāsti | Maybe it is not |
| 3 | syāt asti nāsti ca | Maybe it is and is not |
| 4 | syāt avaktavyaḥ | Maybe it is indescribable |
| 5 | syāt asti avaktavyaḥ ca | Maybe it is and is indescribable |
| 6 | syāt nāsti avaktavyaḥ ca | Maybe it is not and is indescribable |
| 7 | syāt asti nāsti avaktavyaḥ ca | Maybe it is, is not, and is indescribable |

The first two limbs cover simple affirmation and negation. The third combines them sequentially — a thing can be said to exist from one standpoint and not exist from another (e.g., a pot exists as a pot but does not exist as a cloth). The fourth ("indescribable") arises when affirmation and negation are simultaneous rather than sequential, producing a third state that language cannot capture directly. Limbs 5-7 add the indescribable as an additional qualifier to each of the first three.

> ⚠️ **Syāt is not scepticism.** The qualifier *syāt* does not express doubt or probability. It signals that the statement is true **under a specific standpoint or viewpoint** (*naya*). Multiple statements that would be contradictory under classical bivalent logic can all be simultaneously true when each is understood as a perspective-bound description of a complex reality.

## Philosophical Significance

Syādvāda is a direct application of anekāntavāda to the domain of logic and language. It recognizes that:

- **Reality is complex** — any entity has both eternal (*dravya*) and transient (*paryāya*) aspects, as well as infinite other qualities.
- **Standpoints matter** — a statement's truth depends on the standpoint from which it is uttered (cf. the *naya* theory of Jain epistemology).
- **Non-absolutism** — no single description exhausts the truth about anything.

Far from being radical scepticism, syādvāda aims at **maximum descriptive adequacy**: rather than forcing reality into a binary true/false grid, it expands logical space to accommodate seven truth-values.

## Key Sources

- ***Tattvārtha Sūtra*** (c. 2nd–5th cent. CE) — Umāsvāmi's foundational text briefly discusses syādvāda in the context of *pramāṇa* and *naya*.
- ***Anekāntajayapatākā*** of Haribhadra Sūri (8th cent. CE) — A major work defending anekāntavāda and syādvāda against rival schools.
- ***Syādvādamañjarī*** of Mallisheṇa (13th cent. CE) — The definitive late-medieval exposition of syādvāda, containing the classic elaboration of the saptabhaṅgī.
- ***Sanmatitarka*** of Siddhasena Divākara — A key text on the *naya* theory that underpins syādvāda.

## Comparative Note

**Buddhist catuṣkoṭi** (tetralemma): Nāgārjuna's Mādhyamaka uses a four-cornered negation — (1) it is, (2) it is not, (3) it both is and is not, (4) it neither is nor is not — to deconstruct all views and point to emptiness (*śūnyatā*). Despite the surface similarity, the purpose differs sharply: the catuṣkoṭi is a **dialectical tool** that negates every position to lead to a non-conceptual realization, whereas syādvāda is a **constructive logical system** that preserves multiple predications as simultaneously valid under their respective standpoints.

**Many-valued logic**: The saptabhaṅgī has been compared (with caution) to modern many-valued and paraconsistent logics. A seven-valued truth table indeed extends beyond classical bivalence, but syādvāda's aim is not formalisation of inference but **non-violent discourse** — a logical ethic that avoids dogmatic absolutism (*ekānta*) by acknowledging the multi-facetedness of the real.
