# 📖 Inanna's Descent to the Netherworld

The myth of the goddess Inanna's descent into the underworld, her judgment and death before the **Anunnaki**, and her eventual rescue. This is the primary text in which the **Anunnaki appear as the seven judges of the dead** — a role they maintain in later Mesopotamian tradition.

> [!important] Anunnaki Key Passages
> - **Lines 164-172**: "The Anuna, the seven judges, rendered their decision against her. They looked at her — it was the look of death."
> - **Lines 282-289**: The Anuna seize Inanna as she tries to ascend: "Who has ever ascended from the underworld unscathed?"
> - **Akkadian version (Jastrow)**: "Bring out the Anunnaki, seat them on golden thrones"

## Sumerian Version (ETCSL t.1.4.1)

### Lines 1-13: Inanna Sets Her Mind on the Underworld

From the great heaven she set her mind on the great below.
From the great heaven the goddess set her mind on the great below.
From the great heaven Inana set her mind on the great below.
My mistress abandoned heaven, abandoned earth, and descended to the underworld.
Inana abandoned heaven, abandoned earth, and descended to the underworld.
She abandoned the office of en, abandoned the office of lagar,
She abandoned the E-ana in Unug, and descended to the underworld.
She abandoned the E-muš-kalama in Bad-tibira, and descended to the underworld.
She abandoned the Giguna in Zabalam, and descended to the underworld.
She abandoned the E-šara in Adab, and descended to the underworld.
She abandoned the Barag-dur-ĝara in Nibru, and descended to the underworld.
She abandoned the Ḫursaĝ-kalama in Kiš, and descended to the underworld.
She abandoned the E-Ulmaš in Agade, and descended to the underworld.

### Lines 14-19: She Takes the Divine Powers

She took the seven divine powers.
She collected the divine powers and grasped them in her hand.
With the good divine turban, headgear of the open country, she covered her head.
She placed a wig upon her forehead.
She fastened small ... of lapis lazuli on her hands.
She grasped the ... of lapis lazuli in her hand.

### Lines 20-89: Instructions to Ninshubur

She instructed her minister Ninshubur:
"Come, my faithful minister of E-ana,
My......, my messenger of good words,
My...... who speaks fair words,
I am now descending to the underworld.
When I have come to the underworld,
Make a lament for me on the ruin mounds.
Beat the drum for me in the... of the gods.
... my eyes for me. ... my mouth for me.
... my... for me like a... .
Should you not... for me... ,
Then go to the... of the gods...
In E-ana in Unug,...
Weep before Enlil..."

### Lines 90-163: The Descent Through Seven Gates

Inanna approached the underworld.
At the gate of the underworld, she spoke to the gatekeeper:
"Open the house, gatekeeper! Open the house!
Open the house, Neti! Open the house!
I alone would enter!"

The gatekeeper went to speak to Ereshkigal, the queen of the underworld.
Ereshkigal commanded: "Let her enter. Deal with her according to the ancient decrees."

At each of the seven gates, a piece of Inanna's clothing or adornment was removed:
1. The shugurra (crown)
2. The rod of lapis lazuli (measuring rod)
3. The small lapis lazuli stones (necklace)
4. The... stones from her breast
5. The gold ring from her hand
6. The pectoral from her breast
7. The royal robe

Naked and bowed low, she entered the throne room.

### Lines 164-172: 🔴 The Judgment of the Anunnaki

> [!danger] The Anunnaki Condemn Inanna to Death
> **After she had crouched down and had her clothes removed, they were carried away. Then she made her sister Ereškigala rise from her throne, and instead she sat on her throne.**
> 
> **The Anuna, the seven judges, rendered their decision against her. They looked at her — it was the look of death. They spoke to her — it was the speech of anger. They shouted at her — it was the shout of heavy guilt.**
> 
> **The afflicted woman was turned into a corpse. And the corpse was hung on a hook.**

### Lines 173-281: Rescue by Enki

After three days, Ninshubur followed Inanna's instructions and appealed to Enlil, then Nanna, then Enki.

Only Enki responded. He fashioned two asexual beings — the *gala-tura* and the *kur-ĝara* — and sent them to the underworld with the "food of life" and the "water of life." They sympathized with Ereshkigal, who was in childbirth-like labor, and as a reward she offered them anything.

They asked for Inanna's corpse. They sprinkled it with the food and water of life, and Inanna revived.

### Lines 282-289: 🔴 The Anunnaki Demand a Substitute

> [!danger] The Anunnaki Seize Inanna
> **Ereškigala said to the gala-tura and the kur-ĝara: "Bring your queen......, your...... has been seized." Inana, because of Enki's instructions, was about to ascend from the underworld. But as Inana was about to ascend from the underworld, the Anuna seized her: "Who has ever ascended from the underworld, has ascended unscathed from the underworld? If Inana is to ascend from the underworld, let her provide a substitute for herself."**

Inanna ascends from the underworld, accompanied by demons. She searches for someone to take her place. She finds her husband Dumuzi seated on her throne, not mourning her absence. In anger, she fixes the "eye of death" upon him, and the demons seize him. Dumuzi's sister Geshtinanna mourns for him, and a compromise is reached: Dumuzi spends half the year in the underworld, Geshtinanna the other half.

## Akkadian Version — Descent of Ishtar (Jastrow translation, 1915)

The later Akkadian version replaces Inanna with **Ishtar** and adds the key scene of the **Anunnaki on golden thrones**.

### The Anunnaki Released (Reverse of Tablet)

> Ereshkigal opened her mouth and spoke,
> To Namtar, her messenger, she addressed herself.
> "Go, Namtar, knock at the strong palace,
> Strike the threshold of precious stones,
> **Bring out the Anunnaki, seat them on golden thrones.**
> Sprinkle Ishtar with the waters of life and take her out of my presence."
>
> Namtar went, knocked at the strong palace,
> Tapped on the threshold of precious stones.
> **He brought out the Anunnaki and placed them on golden thrones,**
> He sprinkled Ishtar with the waters of life and took hold of her.
> Through the first gate he led her out and returned to her her loin-cloth.
> Through the second gate he led her out and returned to her the spangles of her hands and feet.
> Through the third gate he led her out and returned to her the girdle of her body, studded with birth-stones.
> Through the fourth gate he led her out and returned to her the ornaments of her breast.
> Through the fifth gate he led her out and returned to her her necklace.
> Through the sixth gate he led her out and returned to her earrings.
> Through the seventh gate he led her out and returned to her the large crown for her head.

## Summary: The Anunnaki in This Text

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Number** | Seven (the "seven judges") |
| **Role** | Underworld judges who decree death |
| **Sumerian name** | Anuna (da-nun-na) |
| **Power** | Their look = death; their speech = anger; their shout = guilt |
| **Akkadian version** | Seated on golden thrones; serve Ereshkigal |
| **Significance** | Earliest clear depiction of Anunnaki as chthonic (underworld) deities |
