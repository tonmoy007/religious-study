# 📖 Epic of Gilgamesh — Anunnaki Passages

The Epic of Gilgamesh references the **Anunnaki** in several key passages, most notably as the **seven judges of the underworld** who cause the Flood, and in the title **"judge of the Anunnaki"** applied to Gilgamesh himself.

> [!important] Anunnaki in Gilgamesh
> - **Tablet VIII**: Gilgamesh is called "judge of the Anunnaki" (broken context)
> - **Tablet XI (Flood)**: The Anunnaki are the seven judges who set the land aflame as the storm approaches
> - **Tablet XI**: Ishtar and the Anunnaki mourn the destruction of humanity

---

## Tablet XI — The Flood Narrative (Utnapishtim's Story)

The Flood story in Gilgamesh is closely derived from the [[Atrahasis Epic]].

> The Anunnaki, the great gods, held assembly;
> They brought on the deluge...
> ...
> The gods were frightened by the deluge,
> They shrank back and went up to the heaven of Anu.
> The gods cowered like dogs crouching against the outer wall.
> Ishtar shrieked like a woman in childbirth,
> The Mistress of the Gods cried out:
> "Alas, the olden days are turned to clay,
> Because I spoke evil in the Assembly of the Gods!
> How could I speak evil in the Assembly of the Gods,
> And order the catastrophe for the destruction of my people?
> No sooner have I given birth to my dear people
> Than they fill the sea like so many fish!"
> 
> The gods — the Anunnaki — were weeping with her,
> The gods sat weeping, brought low,
> Their lips tight shut, one and all.

---

## Gilgamesh as "Judge of the Anunnaki"

In Tablet VIII (poorly preserved), Gilgamesh is given the epithet **"judge of the Anunnaki"** — a reference to his role as a judge in the underworld after death.
