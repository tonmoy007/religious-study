# 📖 The Epic of Gilgamish

*Translated by R. Campbell Thompson (1928)*

The great epic of **Gilgamish** (Gilgamesh) — king of Erech (Uruk), two-thirds divine and one-third human — is the finest surviving work of ancient Mesopotamian literature. This translation by Oxford Assyriologist R. Campbell Thompson (1876–1941) was one of the first essentially complete academic translations, drawing on both the Assyrian (Seven-Tablet) and Old Babylonian versions.

The epic traces Gilgamish's journey from tyrannical ruler to wise king through his friendship with the wild-man **Enkidu**, their adventures against **Humbaba** and the **Heavenly Bull**, Enkidu's death, and Gilgamish's desperate quest for eternal life culminating in his meeting with **Uta-Napishtim** — survivor of the Great Flood — and the tragic loss of the Plant of Youth.

> [!important] Anunnaki Key Passages
> - **Tablet VIII**: Gilgamish called "Judge of the Anunnaki"
> - **Tablet X (Col VI)**: The Anunnaki, great gods, together with Mammetum, settle destiny — "Death, aye, and Life they determine"
> - **Tablet XI**: The Anunnaki brandish torches, shrivelling the land with flames at the Deluge
> - **Tablet XI**: Ishtar and the Anunnaki weep together at the destruction of humanity

> [!info] Textual Note
> Campbell Thompson uses "Gilgamish" for the hero, "Gilgamish" being the older scholarly rendering. Later convention prefers "Gilgamesh." The translation preserves the archaic English style of the 1928 original. Square brackets `[...]` indicate damaged or restored text. Footnotes from the original edition are preserved in `> [!note]` callouts. The text includes both Assyrian (Nineveh) and Old Babylonian versions where they survive.

> [!note] Textual Note on the Twelfth Tablet
> The "Architect" is Gilgamish himself. The description of the seine and net passing through his house is a euphemism for the death of Enkidu. Having failed to learn the secret of eternal life, Gilgamish is now reduced to calling up his dead friend to learn the worst which he may expect. He asks what the mourner must do to avoid being haunted — and then proceeds to do exactly the contrary in order that Enkidu may return to haunt him. This tablet is a later addition, translated directly from a Sumerian original, and differs in style from the earlier tablets.

> [!note] Textual Comparison
> The Campbell Thompson translation preserves the archaic English of the 1928 original. For a modern rendering, see Andrew George, *The Epic of Gilgamesh* (Penguin Classics, 2003). For the Anunnaki-specific passages, see [[Epic of Gilgamesh — Anunnaki Passages]].
